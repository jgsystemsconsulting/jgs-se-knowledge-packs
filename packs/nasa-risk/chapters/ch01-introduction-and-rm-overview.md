# Chapter 1: Introduction and Risk Management Overview

## Core Idea
NASA Risk Management integrates two complementary processes, Risk-Informed Decision Making (RIDM) and Continuous Risk Management (CRM), into a single coherent framework for proactive management of performance shortfalls across safety, technical, cost, and schedule domains.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Risk Management (RM)**: The integrated framework of RIDM + CRM, mandated by NPR 8000.4A, covering all NASA organizational levels.
  - When to use: All NASA programs and projects throughout the full life cycle, from formulation through operations and closeout.
- **Risk-Informed Decision Making (RIDM)**: Addresses selection of decision alternatives to assure effective approaches to achieving objectives.
  - When to use: Key decisions involving requirements-setting or rebaselining — architecture, make-buy, source selection, budget reallocation.
- **Continuous Risk Management (CRM)**: Addresses implementation of the selected alternative to assure that performance requirements are met.
  - When to use: During implementation once performance requirements have been defined; also during formulation activities with defined cost/schedule constraints.

## Key Concepts
- **Risk**: The potential for performance shortfalls, which may be realized in the future, with respect to achieving explicitly established and stated performance requirements. In CRM it is characterised as a triplet: (a) scenario(s) leading to degraded performance, (b) likelihood(s) of those scenarios, (c) consequence(s) that would result.
- **Mission Execution Domains**: The four areas in which performance shortfalls may occur: Safety, Technical, Cost, and Schedule.
- **Performance Requirements**: Values of performance measures agreed upon between organisational units, flowing down through the NASA hierarchy from strategic goals.
- **Graded Approach**: Calibrating the depth of analysis to the stakes and complexity of the risk — avoiding unnecessary analytical cost while ensuring robust decisions.
- **Organisational Hierarchy**: NASA programmes flow requirements downward; each unit manages CRM against its own requirements and elevates decisions when those requirements cannot be met.
- **Systems Engineering Engine**: The broader NASA SE process context within which RM operates; RIDM maps to the Decision Analysis process, CRM maps to Technical Risk Management per NPR 7123.1A.
- **Proactive Risk Management**: Addressing risks before they become problems, justified by savings that exceed the cost of implementing the RM approach.

## Mental Models
- Use RIDM when trade studies are needed and the decision involves high stakes, complexity, uncertainty, multiple attributes, or diverse stakeholders — the more of these conditions present, the greater the need.
- Use CRM when implementation is underway and the goal is sustaining compliance with baselined performance requirements.
- Think of RIDM as selecting the path and CRM as staying on it; they hand off to each other and feed back throughout the project life cycle.
- Apply a graded approach: if simplified bounding analysis shows uncertainty is small and a decision is clear, detailed uncertainty analysis is unnecessary.

## Anti-patterns
- **Risk-based rather than risk-informed decision making**: Relying primarily on a narrow set of model-based risk metrics rather than integrating diverse performance measures within a deliberative process — this misses stakeholder values and non-modelled concerns.
- **Treating RIDM and CRM as sequential rather than iterative**: The two processes interact continuously; CRM products can trigger RIDM re-execution when new information changes which alternative is preferred.

## Key Takeaways
1. NASA defines risk as potential performance shortfalls across four domains — not merely safety events.
2. RIDM selects which alternative to implement; CRM manages risk during implementation — both are always active within every organisational unit.
3. The graded approach makes RM cost-effective by calibrating analysis depth to decision stakes.
4. Requirements flow down through the NASA hierarchy; each level manages CRM to its requirements and escalates when those requirements become unachievable.
5. Risk management is explicitly tied to the NASA Systems Engineering Engine; it is not a separate discipline.

## Connects To
- **NPR 8000.4A**: The governing NASA procedural requirement mandating RIDM and CRM.
- **NPR 7123.1A / NASA Systems Engineering Handbook (NASA/SP-2007-6105)**: Establishes the SE process context including Technical Risk Management and Decision Analysis processes.
- **NPR 7120.5D**: Space Flight Program and Project Management Requirements — product-oriented NPR that intersects with RM requirements.
