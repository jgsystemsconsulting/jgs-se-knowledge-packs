# Chapter 10: Enterprise and Institutional Risks, and Reference Appendices

## Core Idea
The project-centred CRM process can be extended to institutional risks (workforce, facilities, IT infrastructure), enterprise risks (acquisition, strategic, financial, operational, hazard), and agency-wide strategic risks — each shares the same fundamental RM logic but requires domain-specific adaptations; the appendices provide key reference artefacts including definitions, TBfD and RISR content guides, NASA real-world examples, and the RMP practical guidance.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Institutional Risk Management**: Application of the CRM process to risks affecting infrastructure, IT, personnel, assets, processes, and operational support that threaten mission execution capabilities.
  - When to use: When an institutional risk (e.g., workforce attrition, facility maintenance lag, software obsolescence) has a quantifiable impact on performance requirements or mission execution domains.
- **Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)**: Management of risks pertaining to the operation of NASA as an agency/corporation, categorised across acquisition, strategic, financial, operational, and hazard domains.
  - When to use: At the agency-institutional level; many project CRM processes carry over, supplemented by ERM-specific texts and tools.
- **Agency-Wide Strategic Risk Process**: Risk management against NASA strategic goals and Annual Performance Goals (APGs), using a Green/Yellow/Red performance ranking framework extended to future-oriented risk tolerability specifications.
  - When to use: Agency-level strategic planning cycles; risk management parallels project CRM with severity levels defined against APG achievement and outcome realisation.

## Key Concepts
- **Institutional Risk Definition (NPR 8000.4A)**: Risks to infrastructure, IT, resources, personnel, assets, processes, occupational safety, environmental management, or security that affect capabilities and resources necessary for mission success.
- **Enterprise Risk Domains**: Acquisition (teamwork, market power), Strategic (regulatory, technology obsolescence, reputation), Financial (commodity, credit, liquidity), Operational (human resources, machine reliability, supply chain), and Hazard (fire, theft, liability) — standard ERM taxonomy adapted for NASA.
- **Modelling Institutional-to-Project Dependencies**: The key challenge is quantifying how an institutional risk (e.g., software maintenance lag) propagates into a project performance risk (milestone delay). This requires explicit interface modelling between institutional and project organisations.
- **TBfD Content Guide (Appendix C)**: Specifies the sections of the Technical Basis for Deliberation: Technical Summary, Top-Level Requirements and Expectations, Derivation of Performance Measures, Decision Alternatives, Risk Analysis Framework and Methods, Risk Analysis Results (scenarios, pdfs, imposed-constraint risk, sensitivity studies), and Risk Analysis Credibility Assessment.
- **RISR Content Guide (Appendix D)**: Specifies the sections of the Risk-Informed Selection Report: Executive Summary, Technical Basis for Deliberation, Performance Commitments, Deliberation (organisation, contending alternatives, pros/cons, summary material), Alternative Selection (selected alternative, performance commitments, risk list, decision robustness).
- **NASA ESAS Examples (Appendix E)**: Real NASA decisions illustrating RIDM elements — Design Reference Missions for stakeholder expectations, Figures of Merit (FOMs) for objectives hierarchies, joint confidence level (JCL) for cost and schedule, probabilistic safety assessments.
- **RMP Practical Guidance (Appendix F)**: Addresses staffing resources, plan update cadence, training requirements, and the interaction between project and contractor risk management processes.

## Mental Models
- Institutional risks are project risks in disguise — if a facility degrades or a workforce skill evaporates, the project performance requirement that depends on it is at risk; model the dependency explicitly.
- Agency strategic risk management mirrors project CRM: define performance objectives (APGs), set tolerability thresholds (Green/Yellow/Red), track performance, and plan responses — the same logic applies at every level of scale.
- Use the TBfD and RISR content guides as structural checklists when producing these documents; coverage gaps found in review are almost always gaps in one of the defined sections.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating institutional risks as background noise**: Institutional risks that affect mission-critical personnel or facilities can cascade to project-level failures; they must be explicitly modelled in the risk analysis framework when dependency is significant.
- **Applying ERM frameworks without adapting them to NASA's hierarchical structure**: Commercial ERM tools assume a flat corporate structure; NASA's hierarchical requirements-flowdown requires adaptation of domain definitions and escalation protocols.

## Key Takeaways
1. The RIDM and CRM processes apply beyond project risks — institutional and enterprise risks share the same process logic and can be analysed with the same tools, though modelling institutional-to-project dependencies requires additional specification.
2. Appendix C (TBfD) and Appendix D (RISR) are the definitive structural templates for the two primary RIDM deliverables; practitioners should treat them as mandatory outlines.
3. NASA ESAS examples (Appendix E) demonstrate that real high-stakes decisions (crew exploration vehicle architecture selection, Hubble servicing AoA) used proto-RIDM methods consistent with this handbook's framework.
4. The joint confidence level (JCL) policy — budgeting cost and schedule at a joint 70% confidence level — is a concrete NASA implementation of risk-normalised performance commitments applied to cost and schedule simultaneously.
5. Agency-wide strategic risk management is an emerging domain that can leverage the same CRM infrastructure (tolerability schedules, tracking charts, escalation protocols) used for project risks.
