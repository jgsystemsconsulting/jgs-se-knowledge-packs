# NASA System Safety Handbook v2 Glossary

**Acquirer** — A NASA organisation that tasks a subordinate organisation (Provider) to produce a product or deliver a service; responsible for safety assurance and risk acceptance. (Ch 2)

**Adequate Safety** — The state in which a system simultaneously meets minimum tolerable levels of safety performance AND is ASARP; must be maintained throughout all life-cycle phases. (Ch 2)

**Allocated Requirements** — Quantitative requirements apportioned from system or subsystem level to lower levels, with the same units of measure preserved at each level. (Ch 3)

**ASARP (As Safe As Reasonably Practicable)** — A system is ASARP if further incremental safety improvement would require an intolerable or disproportionate sacrifice in technical, cost, or schedule performance. (Ch 2)

**Assurance Deficit** — Any knowledge gap that prohibits perfect confidence in a safety claim; caused by variability or lack of knowledge in data, models, inputs, or interpretations; scored 1–5. (Ch 5, 6)

**Base Claim** — A claim in the RISC not further decomposed; demonstrated true by direct and supporting evidence, with assurance deficits scored to quantify remaining uncertainty. (Ch 6)

**Confirmation Bias** — The tendency to favour evidence confirming existing beliefs; specifically countered by the RISC's dual deductive (failure scenario) and inductive (safety argument) structure. (Ch 2, 6)

**Continuous Risk Management (CRM)** — The NASA process (from NPR 8000.4A) for ongoing identification, analysis, planning, tracking, control, and communication of risks; assurance deficits from RISC evaluation convert into CRM risk statements. (Ch 5)

**Credibility Assessment Scale (CAS)** — Eight-factor evaluation framework (model verification, validation, input pedigree, results uncertainty, results robustness, use history, M&S management, people qualifications) per NASA-STD-7009 for assessing ISA model validity. (Ch 5)

**Derived Requirements** — Qualitative or quantitative requirements developed at a lower level to implement a higher-level requirement; arise from constraints, implied issues, or design choices. (Ch 3)

**Direct Evidence** — Mostly quantitative information supporting a base claim by showing risk of not meeting it is acceptably low (failure rates, PRA results, anomaly analysis, best-practice adherence). (Ch 6)

**Evaluator** — Agent of the Acquirer tasked to evaluate the RISC and generate findings on technical adequacy; must be independent from both Provider and Acquirer decision-making authority. (Ch 7)

**Goal Structuring Notation (GSN)** — Optional graphical notation for representing the RISC claims tree visually; supported by NASA's RISC Evaluation Management Tool. (Ch 6)

**Graded Approach** — Matching the depth, rigor, and cost of safety analysis to the complexity and criticality of the decision; operationalised as Priority 1/2/3 rankings. (Ch 1, 5)

**Hazard** — A state or set of conditions, internal or external to a system, with the potential to cause harm through scenarios leading to death, injury, illness, equipment loss or damage, or environmental damage. (App B)

**Hazard Analysis (HA)** — Systematic, replicable methods to identify hazards and characterise mishap risk; one component integrated into the ISA. (Ch 3)

**Importance Rank** — Evaluator's score (1–5) of how much the top RISC claim's confidence would be affected if a given base claim were false; Rank 5 implies >65% effect on top-claim confidence. (Ch 7)

**Integrated Safety Analysis (ISA)** — Scenario-based, system-level analysis that consolidates HA, FMEA, PRA, and phenomenological modelling into a single quantified safety performance characterisation; the central analytic engine of the framework. (Ch 3, 5)

**Intermediate Claim** — A RISC claim further decomposed into lower-level sub-claims; demonstrated true when all feeding sub-claims are demonstrated true with high confidence. (Ch 6)

**Key Decision Point (KDP)** — A life-cycle milestone at which the Acquirer's Decision Authority determines readiness to progress to the next phase; the RISC is submitted and evaluated at each KDP. (Ch 2)

**Key Mission Objective (KMO)** — A distinct mission phase or objective requiring a separate safety sub-case within the overall RISC (e.g., separate planetary rendezvous events, distinct flight destinations). (Ch 4)

**Known Risk** — A scenario correctly identified and accurately assessed for likelihood and severity of harm or loss. (Ch 2)

**Minimum Tolerable Level of Safety** — The safety performance floor below which a system is considered unsafe; typically expressed as a maximum probability of loss or harm. (Ch 2)

**Operational Safety Objective** — A safety objective decomposed to the level where it can be addressed by systems engineering processes; the link between policy and requirement. (Ch 3)

**Precursor Analysis** — Process for identifying near-miss events and anomalies and evaluating their potential risk significance for incorporation into ISA updates. (Ch 5)

**Probabilistic Risk Assessment (PRA)** — A structured probabilistic treatment of scenarios, likelihoods, and consequences using Bayesian uncertainty treatment; mandatory for Priority 1 missions. (Ch 4, 5)

**Provider** — A NASA or contractor organisation tasked to produce a product or service; responsible for safety ensurance and RISC development. (Ch 2)

**Qualitative Scope/Methods Review** — Second phase of RISC evaluation; compares RISC methods and results to standard approaches and comparable analyses; evaluators develop independent perspectives. (Ch 7)

**Quantitative Evaluation (Auditing)** — Third phase of RISC evaluation; independent checking of selected technical results, ranging from peer review to replication; includes sensitivity studies. (Ch 7)

**Request for Additional Information (RAI)** — Formal Acquirer mechanism for seeking clarification of a specific weakness in one or more RISC claims at any evaluation phase. (Ch 7)

**Risk Burndown** — The expected reduction in safety risk over time as mitigations are implemented, UU risks are discovered and corrected, and knowledge improves. (Ch 2)

**Risk Driver** — A significant contributor to safety performance risk; a subset of SCIs incorporating both probability and consequence, used when the safety concern involves equipment or mission loss rather than harm to humans. (Ch 3)

**Risk-Informed Decision Making (RIDM)** — A decision approach using diverse performance measures including risk metrics within a deliberative process; distinct from risk-based decision making where risk alone dictates the decision. (Ch 5)

**Risk-Informed Safety Case (RISC)** — A structured argument supported by a body of evidence providing a compelling, comprehensible, and valid case that a system is or will be adequately safe for a given application in a given environment; the central product of the framework. (Ch 2)

**Safety** — Freedom from hazards that can result in death, injury, illness, equipment loss or damage, or environmental damage. (Ch 2, App B)

**Safety Assurance** — Acquirer activity; developing confidence that safety has been sufficiently ensured by the Provider to support a risk acceptance decision. (Ch 2)

**Safety Case** — A structured argument supported by a body of evidence for adequate safety; the RISC is a specialisation of the generic safety case that incorporates risk-informed deliberation. (Ch 6)

**Safety-Critical Item (SCI)** — A system feature (hardware, software, interfaces, procedures, management practices) whose performance is necessary for system-level safety requirements to be met; identified top-down from the ISA. (Ch 3)

**Safety Ensurance** — Provider activity; the reduction and elimination of system hazards and the achievement of adequate safety performance through design, procurement, fabrication, and operation. (Ch 2)

**Safety Goal** — The maximum tolerable total loss probability after system maturity; more stringent than the safety threshold; typically ~50% of the initial known-risk loss probability. (Ch 2, 4)

**Safety Growth** — The operational reduction in loss probability as UU risks are discovered and corrected during the operational phase; analogous to reliability growth. (Ch 4)

**Safety Performance** — The complement of the probability of harm (1 minus the probability of adverse safety consequences). (Ch 2)

**Safety Performance Factor (SPF)** — The ratio of total loss probability (known + UU risks) to loss probability from known risks alone; used to derive the analytic requirement from the safety threshold. (Ch 4)

**Safety Performance Margin** — An incremental margin subtracted from the safety threshold or goal to account for UU hazard contributions; estimated from historical experience with similar technologies. (Ch 3, 4)

**Safety Risk** — The potential for shortfall with respect to safety performance requirements; only exists to the extent there is uncertainty about whether requirements will be met. (Ch 2)

**Safety Threshold** — The maximum tolerable total loss probability at first system operation; derived from applicable baselines adjusted for system differences. (Ch 2, 4)

**Staged Evaluation Process** — The three-phase RISC evaluation sequence: acceptance review, qualitative scope/methods review, quantitative auditing. (Ch 7)

**Supporting Evidence** — Mostly qualitative information providing confidence in direct evidence or demonstrating responsiveness to safety concerns (personnel qualifications, V&V of tools, documentation quality, safety culture). (Ch 6)

**System Safety** — The application of engineering and management principles, criteria, and techniques to optimise safety and reduce safety risk within constraints of operational effectiveness, time, and cost throughout all life-cycle phases. (Ch 2)

**System Safety Management Plan (SSMP)** — The Provider's comprehensive planning document linking baselined safety requirements to specific activities, roles, analysis schedules, and RISC development plans. (Ch 5)

**System Safety Requirements Analysis (SSRA)** — Early Provider activity clarifying the detailed requirements to be addressed in development; the Acquirer-Provider handshake on requirement scope and feasibility. (Ch 3, 4)

**Underappreciated Risk** — A correctly identified scenario whose likelihood and/or severity are underestimated in analysis; a major source of catastrophic accidents. (Ch 2)

**Unknown Risk** — A scenario that has not been identified at all at the time of analysis; the primary driver of catastrophic failures that evade modelled risk assessments. (Ch 2)

**Unknown and Underappreciated (UU) Risks** — The combined category of unmodelled risk; historically dominant contributor to actual loss probability relative to modelled known risks. (Ch 3)

**Value-of-Information (VOI) Analysis** — Decision-analytic method for evaluating whether the cost of obtaining additional information is justified by the expected reduction in uncertainty; structures the accept/mitigate/reduce-uncertainty choice. (Ch 7)
