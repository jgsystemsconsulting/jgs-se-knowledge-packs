# Chapter 1: Introduction

## Core Idea
Volume 2 of the NASA System Safety Handbook translates the conceptual framework of Volume 1 into implementable guidance integrated with systems engineering and risk management, covering four functional areas: safety objectives/requirements, integrated safety analysis, RISC development, and RISC evaluation.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Objectives-Driven System Safety**: Frame all safety activity around achieving defined operational safety objectives rather than producing prescribed deliverables.
  - When to use: At program/project inception, to avoid check-the-box compliance without substantive safety achievement.
- **Risk-Informed Safety Case (RISC)**: Structured argument plus evidence body demonstrating a system is or will be adequately safe.
  - When to use: At every Key Decision Point (KDP) in the system life cycle to support risk acceptance decisions.

## Key Concepts
- **Adequate Safety**: The condition of meeting minimum tolerable safety levels AND being as safe as reasonably practicable (ASARP) simultaneously throughout all life-cycle phases.
- **Integrated Safety Analysis (ISA)**: A scenario-based, system-level analysis consolidating diverse safety analyses (HA, FMEA, PRA, phenomenological modeling) into a single quantified safety performance characterization.
- **Graded Approach**: Matching the depth, rigor, and cost of safety analysis to the complexity and criticality of the decision being supported.
- **Unknown and Underappreciated (UU) Risks**: Scenarios either not identified at all (unknown) or identified but whose likelihood/severity is underestimated (underappreciated); the primary driver of catastrophic failures.
- **Model-Based System Safety**: Integrating safety analysis into model-based systems engineering rather than treating it as a separate process-based activity.
- **Safety as an Emergent Property**: System safety arises from component interactions, environment, and operators — it cannot be assessed by summing subsystem analyses alone.
- **Confirmation Bias**: The tendency to seek evidence supporting pre-existing safety beliefs; the RISC structure is specifically designed to counter this by demanding deductive as well as inductive argument.

## Mental Models
- Use the RISC framework when you need a coherent, decision-ready safety argument at a major review, not just a collection of analysis products.
- Use ISA when design trades, requirement allocation, or SCI designation decisions require risk-informed input.
- Use the graded approach when program/project resources are constrained — focus analytic depth where consequence and uncertainty are highest.
- Use an objectives-driven approach rather than a deliverable-driven approach when mission complexity outpaces the ability of pre-specified checklists to capture real safety concerns.

## Anti-patterns
- **Prescriptive deliverable compliance without substantive safety achievement**: Producing required documents without demonstrating that objectives are met; explicitly called out as inadequate.
- **Subsystem-only analysis**: Treating safety as the sum of subsystem hazard analyses; misses emergent system-level failure modes (Challenger and Columbia exemplify this).
- **Isolated safety practice**: Conducting safety analysis independently of systems engineering and risk management; creates gaps, redundancies, and disconnected decision-making.

## Key Takeaways
1. Safety is an emergent system-level property; design, integration, and operation all contribute and must be addressed holistically.
2. The principal threat to NASA missions comes from UU risks, not fully-known risks; program disciplines (budget, schedule, communication, testing) are the primary controls.
3. The RISC replaces scattered safety deliverables with a single coherent argument; it is not additional work but reorganized existing work.
4. Safety and systems engineering are overlapping disciplines; system safety ensurance is a core systems engineering input, not an add-on review.
5. The graded approach enables cost-effective implementation: match analytic rigor to criticality, not to a uniform standard.
6. Volume 2 is a 10-year transition vision, not an immediate mandate; implementation will be phased and piloted.

## Connects To
- **NPR 7123.1B (NASA Systems Engineering Processes and Requirements)**: System safety activities must map to SE common technical processes; RISC documentation requirements align with KDP entrance criteria.
- **NPR 8000.4A (NASA Risk Management Requirements)**: RISC supports continuous risk management; assurance deficits become risk statements managed under the same process.
- **MIL-STD-882**: The handbook builds on, not replaces, MIL-STD-882 by adding system-level aggregate risk perspective beyond individual hazard management.
