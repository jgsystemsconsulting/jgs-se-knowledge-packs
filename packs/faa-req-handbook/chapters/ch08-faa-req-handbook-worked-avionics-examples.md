# Chapter — Worked Avionics Examples: Isolette Thermostat, Flight Control, Flight Guidance, and Autopilot

## Core Idea

The Handbook's recommended practices would stay abstract without something to anchor them, so the appendices supply four complete, runnable-on-paper specifications that show the practices in action. They are not real products; the source is explicit that each is simplified to keep the example tractable, and that the format shown is only one of many that would work equally well. The value is pedagogical: you can see exactly *what information* a specification carries and *how it is organized*, from a one-paragraph system overview down to individual "shall" statements with latency and tolerance attached.

Two distinct teaching goals drive the choice of examples. The **Isolette Thermostat** (Appendix A) is the small, self-contained case that drives a single specification all the way down to detailed behavioral requirements — monitored and controlled variables, environmental assumptions, safety requirements, a mode machine, and per-variable requirements with rationale. The **Flight Control System** trio (Appendices B, C, D) is the large case whose entire purpose is to show *allocation*: how one system specification is decomposed and split into separate subsystem specifications — Flight Guidance and Autopilot — each of which could be handed to a different subcontractor. The FCS appendices deliberately stop at high-level requirements and skip detailed behavior and performance, because the lesson there is about partitioning across organizational boundaries, not about filling in every requirement.

Both cases are embedded, real-time control problems: a controller reads sensed inputs, decides, and drives actuators within bounded latencies. That is what makes them a sharp complement to general requirements-writing advice — the discipline they demonstrate (typed variables with ranges and units, validity status on every input, explicit timing budgets, fail-safe modes) is exactly the discipline that domain-neutral requirements guidance tends to leave implicit.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

These appendices are demonstrations rather than new frameworks, but they instantiate several specific structuring devices by name, and each appears in a fixed place in the document skeleton.

- **Context Diagram** — every appendix opens its system-context section (A.1.1, B.1.1, C.1.1, D.1.1) with a context diagram (figures A-1, B-1, C-1, D-1) that names the system, the external entities it touches directly, and the actors it touches indirectly. For the Isolette, the Thermostat sits between Temperature Sensor, Heat Source, and Operator Interface, with the Nurse and Infant reached only indirectly.
- **Use and Exception Cases** — the operational-concepts sections render behavior as numbered textual use cases with Primary Actor, Precondition, Postcondition, a Main Success Scenario, and Exception Cases / Alternate Courses. The Isolette uses six (A.2.1 through A.2.6); the FCS uses five (tables B-1 through B-5). Each step can cross-reference the requirement or function that realizes it.
- **System Goals (G-numbers)** — each system states numbered high-level goals up front (Isolette G1–G3; FCS/FGS G1–G6; Autopilot G1–G5). Recurring goals across the flight examples include providing guidance for manual flight, holding attitude automatically, preventing unsafe maneuvers, avoiding passenger-discomfort maneuvers, and keeping manufacturing and operating cost low.
- **External Entities with Monitored and Controlled Variables** — the source's variable model is shown concretely: each external entity gets tables of monitored variables (inputs the system senses) and controlled variables (outputs the system drives), each with Name, Type, Range, Units, and a Physical Interpretation. Many variables carry a paired **Status** attribute valued Invalid/Valid, and initial values are flagged.
- **Environmental Assumptions (EA-prefixed)** — assumptions about the surrounding world are pulled out as identified, rationaled statements: EA-IS-* for the Isolette enclosure, EA-TS-* for the Temperature Sensor, EA-OI-* for the Operator Interface, EA-AHS-* for the Attitude Heading System, EA-CSA-* for the Control Surface Actuators, and so on.
- **Safety Requirements (SR) tied to Hazards (H)** — Appendix A.4 derives safety requirements from an identified hazard. Hazard H1 (prolonged exposure of the infant to unsafe heat or cold) is classified catastrophic with a target probability below 10⁻⁹ per operating hour, and SR-1 and SR-2 are levied to meet it, each carrying its own allowed failure probability (below 10⁻⁵ per hour).
- **Functional decomposition with dependency diagrams** — system functions are split into subfunctions shown in dependency diagrams (figures A-2, A-3, A-5, B-2). The Thermostat splits into two logically independent functions: Regulate Temperature and Monitor Temperature, each further decomposed into Manage-Interface, Manage-Mode, Manage-actuator, and Detect-Failure subfunctions.
- **Mode / State Transition Diagrams** — modes are specified as state machines where each transition is its own numbered requirement. The Regulate Temperature mode machine (figure A-4) has INIT, NORMAL, FAILED states with transitions Req MRM 1–4; the Monitor mode machine (figure A-6) mirrors it with Req MMM identifiers. Transitions are stated to occur in negligible time.
- **Requirement IDs with Latency and Tolerance attributes** — detailed requirements use structured prefixes (REQ-TH-*, REQ-RT-*, REQ-MRI-*, REQ-MHS-*, REQ-MA-* for the Thermostat; REQ-FCS-*, REQ-FG-*, REQ-AP-*, REQ-FGS-*, REQ-APS-* for the flight systems) and, where timing matters, attach a Latency bound and a Tolerance.
- **Subsystem-allocation pattern (section 2.10 referenced)** — Appendices C and D explicitly expand a function from the parent FCS spec (the FG Function and the AP Function, B.4.2) into standalone subsystem specifications, each described as a starting point to be jointly completed by the prime contractor and the subcontractor.

## Key Concepts

- **Monitored vs. controlled variables.** The four-variable lineage from the Handbook's foundations shows up here as concrete tables. Inputs the system observes (Current Temperature, Aircraft Attitude, Pilot Inputs) are monitored; outputs it commands (Heat Control, Actuator Commands, FD Guidance, AP Status) are controlled. The specification is largely a set of rules mapping the former to the latter.
- **Validity is a first-class signal.** Nearly every sensed quantity carries a Status of Invalid or Valid. The Thermostat's mode logic folds these together: a Regulator Status definition is True only when there is no interface failure, no internal failure, *and* the Current Temperature reads Valid. Bad data is not silently used — it forces a mode change.
- **Fail-safe by construction.** When a function cannot trust itself, it drives outputs to the safe state. A failed regulator sets Heat Control Off (REQ-MHS-5); an initializing regulator also sets it Off (REQ-MHS-1); a failed monitor turns the Alarm On (REQ-MA-5). Failure latches: re-entry to NORMAL requires a power cycle, deliberately, so that transient failures cannot be hidden from the operator.
- **Explicit timing budgets.** Real-time intent is captured as numbers, not adjectives. Operator-visible responses must occur within a Max Operator Response Time of 0.5 second; the Heat Source must be switched within an Allowed Heat Source Latency of 6 seconds; the Alarm must fire within 5 seconds of an out-of-range condition. Each budget is justified — the 6-second heat latency is derived from the assumed 1°F-per-minute drift rate so the temperature cannot move past the sensor's required accuracy.
- **Hysteresis to suppress chatter.** The Manage Alarm and Manage Heat Source functions use deadbands. Inside ±0.5° of an alarm threshold the Alarm Control is held unchanged (REQ-MA-3), preventing transient alarms (figure A-7); the heater is left in its current state while temperature sits between the lower and upper desired values (REQ-MHS-4), preventing rapid cycling.
- **UNSPECIFIED as a deliberate value.** When an output is not meaningful, the spec says so explicitly rather than leaving a gap. Display Temperature is UNSPECIFIED outside NORMAL mode (REQ-MRI-5); the Desired Range is UNSPECIFIED when a Regulator Interface Failure is present (REQ-MRI-9). This tells the implementer the value may be anything, which is information, not an omission.
- **Environmental assumptions carry the safety argument.** The numbers in the EA statements are not arbitrary — the Operator Interface assumptions encode clinical limits (below 93°F risks hypothermia, above 103°F risks hyperthermia) and ordering constraints (lower alarm must sit below lower desired, upper alarm above upper desired) so the alarm cannot fire while temperature is still inside the desired band.
- **Independence for safety.** SR-1 and SR-2 demand *independent* regulator and monitor functions. The Thermostat honors this by keeping Regulate Temperature and Monitor Temperature as two separately moded, separately failing functions, each reporting its own status; the Operator Interface combines the two statuses to report overall health.
- **Allocation splits a spec along function boundaries.** The FCS decomposes into a Flight Guidance Function (computes FD and AP guidance from attitude error, sets Reference Attitude, owns the FCS Failed indication) and an Autopilot Function (turns AP guidance into Actuator Commands, reports AP Status, requests resynchronization). Appendices C and D lift each into its own subsystem spec carrying only the slice of information relevant to that subsystem.
- **Control wheel steering and resynchronization.** A recurring flight behavior: while the AP is engaged, the crew can momentarily take manual control, which decouples the AP from the actuators without disengaging it; on release the AP recouples and tracks the new Reference Attitude. The AP Guidance Request is the handshake by which the Autopilot asks the Flight Guidance side to resync the reference before engaging.

## Mental Models

- **The variable ledger.** Think of each external entity as a column of inputs (monitored) and outputs (controlled), each entry stamped with type, range, units, and a validity bit. The specification is the bookkeeping that keeps this ledger consistent — and most requirements are just rules for filling one output cell from the input cells.
- **Two independent watchdogs.** The Isolette is best pictured as two parallel machines sharing one sensor: a *regulator* that keeps temperature in the comfortable band by toggling the heater, and a *monitor* that ignores comfort entirely and only screams when temperature leaves the wider safety band. They fail independently and are wired so that a dead monitor defaults to sounding the alarm. This is the physical shape of "defense in depth."
- **Modes as a one-way trapdoor.** INIT → NORMAL → FAILED is a staircase you can only descend at runtime. There is no FAILED-to-NORMAL edge by design; the only way back up is a power cycle. Picture a trapdoor that latches shut so a flickering fault cannot quietly self-heal and hide itself from the nurse or pilot.
- **The deadband cushion.** Around every threshold, imagine a soft cushion of ±0.5° where nothing toggles. Without it the heater and the alarm would chatter on the boundary; with it, the system commits to a decision and holds until the temperature has clearly moved.
- **Nesting dolls of specification.** The FCS is the outer doll; opening it reveals Flight Guidance and Autopilot as inner dolls, each a smaller but complete specification of the same shape (overview, context, goals, external entities, functions). Allocation is the act of carving the outer doll cleanly enough that each inner doll can be built by a different shop.
- **The reference attitude as a shared baton.** In the flight examples, the Reference Attitude is a baton passed between crew and automation. Sync sets it to current attitude; the FD shows the crew how to fly toward it; the AP flies the surfaces to hold it; control wheel steering hands the baton back to the crew temporarily, then resync hands it forward again.

## Anti-patterns

The source frames these by showing the safeguard and naming, in its rationale, the failure the safeguard prevents.

- **Letting an alarm fire inside the desired band.** The Operator Interface assumptions (EA-OI ordering constraints) exist precisely to prevent a configuration where the alarm range overlaps the desired range, which would activate the alarm while the temperature is still acceptable.
- **Silently using invalid or unmeaningful data.** Forcing UNSPECIFIED for Display Temperature and Desired Range when a failure is present, and latching mode on Invalid status, are presented as the alternative to the anti-pattern of propagating untrustworthy values downstream as if they were good.
- **Auto-clearing transient failures.** The deliberate absence of a FAILED→NORMAL transition is justified by the need to keep operators aware of transient failures; auto-recovery would mask them — the rationale calls this out for both the regulator (Req MRM 3/4) and the monitor (Req MMM 3/4).
- **Chattering actuators and alarms.** The hysteresis bands are the named countermeasure to transient alarms and excessive heat-source cycling.

## Key Takeaways

- The appendices exist to make the Handbook's practices concrete; all four are deliberately simplified and present one acceptable format among many, not a mandated template.
- The Isolette Thermostat is the deep, single-system example, carried down to detailed behavioral requirements, modes, and per-variable rules with rationale; the FCS/FGS/Autopilot set is the broad example about allocating a system spec into subsystem specs for different subcontractors and intentionally stops at high-level requirements.
- The variable model is pervasive: monitored inputs and controlled outputs are tabulated with type, range, units, physical interpretation, validity status, and initial values, and the requirements are mostly rules mapping inputs to outputs.
- Real-time character is captured numerically through Latency and Tolerance attributes and named timing constants (0.5 s operator response, 6 s heat latency, 5 s alarm, 1 s init timeouts), each tied to a justifying rationale.
- Safety is engineered structurally: a catastrophic hazard with a quantified probability target drives derived safety requirements, which in turn demand independent regulator and monitor functions, fail-safe default outputs, latched failure modes, and ordering constraints on the temperature bands.
- Modes are specified as small state machines in which every transition is an individually numbered requirement, and design choices such as no automatic failure recovery and hysteresis deadbands are explicitly rationaled.
- Allocation works by carving the FCS along function boundaries (Flight Guidance vs. Autopilot) and re-emitting each function as a standalone subsystem specification containing only its relevant slice, explicitly meant as a contractor/subcontractor starting point.

## Connects To

- **Requirements-writing guidance generally:** These worked examples are the embedded/real-time, avionics-slanted complement to domain-neutral requirements writing. The structuring devices they demonstrate — typed variables with ranges and units, a validity bit on every input, explicit latency/tolerance budgets, fail-safe defaults, and rationale on each requirement — are the concrete form of the abstract qualities (complete, consistent, clear, well organized) that general requirements advice asks for but rarely shows.
- **The four-variable / SCR lineage:** The monitored/controlled variable tables and the input-to-output requirement rules are the visible payoff of the Software Cost Reduction and four-variable heritage named in the Handbook's foundations.
- **Operational concepts and use cases:** The numbered use and exception cases in each appendix are direct instances of the use-case-driven operational-concepts practice, including the cross-references from scenario steps to the requirements that satisfy them.
- **Functional architecture and allocation practices:** The dependency diagrams, the split into independent functions, and the carving of the FCS into FGS and Autopilot subsystem specs instantiate the Develop the Functional Architecture and Allocate System Requirements to Subsystems practices (section 2.10 is referenced explicitly).
- **System modes and detailed behavior:** The INIT/NORMAL/FAILED mode machines and the per-variable REQ-* statements with hysteresis and timing are the worked form of the Identify System Modes and Develop the Detailed Behavior and Performance Requirements practices.
- **Safety and certification context:** The hazard-to-safety-requirement derivation with quantified per-hour probabilities ties these examples to the avionics safety-assessment and certification framing (ARP-style safety process and the DO-178B software-assurance world) that distinguishes this material from general requirements work.
