# Chapter 3: Develop Operational Concepts and Identify Environmental Assumptions

## Core Idea
Before writing detailed requirements, describe the system as a black box that interacts with operators and neighbouring systems, then make explicit every assumption that system places on the world around it. The handbook treats these as two complementary activities: operational concepts (scenario-style scripts of how the system gets used) bridge the gap between a high-level system overview and detailed requirements, while environmental assumptions pin down the obligations the system levies back on its environment. Both keep the system at arm's length from internal design — operational concepts say nothing about internal structure, and assumptions are stated against the monitored and controlled variables at the boundary rather than against implementation details. This slant suits embedded, real-time control systems (the running example is an infant-incubator thermostat), and it complements requirements-writing rather than replacing it: it produces the validated behaviour and boundary contract that later requirements are written against.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Operational concepts (as scenario scripts)**: the handbook frames operational concepts as scripts or scenarios for how the system will be used, drawn from Hooks and Farry (cited as reference 22). When/how: applied early in the requirements engineering and management (REM) process, after a system overview exists, to view the system as a black box and surface the functions operators expect, the inputs they supply, the feedback they need, and the order in which functions are invoked under normal use.
- **Use cases**: presented as a popular technique for documenting how a system, its operators, and other systems interact. The handbook notes the literature is large and ranges from high-level requirements down to detailed design, citing well-known references (17, 18, and 19), and states its own style leans heavily on reference 17. When/how: used relatively informally early in REM to elicit understanding of needed functions and to give early validation of intended behaviour — not as a formal design notation.
- **Environmental assumptions / the NAT (natural) relationship**: assumptions the system depends on for correct operation, expressible as a mathematical relationship between controlled and monitored variables. The handbook explicitly ties this to the NAT (natural) relationship from the SCR and CoRE methodologies (cited as references 2-5). When/how: identified alongside the required behaviour itself, ranging from simple type/range/unit constraints up to a full mapping describing the environment's behaviour.
- **The precondition/postcondition contract analogy**: the handbook maps environmental assumptions to software preconditions and the system requirements to postconditions. When/how: invoked to explain how assumptions plus requirements together form a contract that lets components be developed independently.

## Key Concepts
- **Black-box view**: operational concepts describe the system with no internal structure exposed — only how it interacts with operators and other systems across its boundary.
- **Actors, primary actor**: operators, the system of interest, and the other systems it talks to are all actors; the one that kicks off a use case is the primary actor.
- **Main success scenario ("sunny day")**: the nominal dialogue in which nothing fails. The handbook is explicit that you document this first and treat failures and exceptions as later extensions, addressed during safety assessment and functional design.
- **Preconditions and postconditions of a use case**: conditions that must hold before the use case starts and after it successfully finishes; the postcondition normally captures what the use case changes in the environment.
- **Exception cases and alternate courses**: an exception case covers ways a use case fails to meet its goal or postconditions; an alternate course covers a different routine path that still satisfies the postconditions. Either may be written as an addition to the main use case rather than a wholly separate one.
- **Traceability of use cases to goals**: each use case carries the system goal(s) it helps satisfy, which both confirms the behaviour is needed and eases maintenance if goals or the use case change.
- **Linking steps to System Functions**: individual use-case steps reference the System Function they invoke, so a function can be located everywhere it is used, and a reader can drill from a step into the function's detail.
- **Use cases feed the boundary and the function list**: walking the scenarios uncovers overlooked external entities and monitored/controlled variables (e.g. the example surfaces a need to display thermostat status), and yields a preliminary set of System Functions used as input to later function definition.
- **Type, range, precision, units**: the simplest environmental assumptions; every monitored and controlled variable needs these defined, with documented rationale.
- **Rationale for assumptions**: each assumption records why it was chosen (why Fahrenheit, why this range, why this precision), giving a basis for revisiting it and for future maintenance.
- **Organising assumptions by responsible entity**: assumptions are grouped with the external entity they constrain so all obligations on that entity are visible in one place; when an assumption spans several entities, the handbook's heuristic is to associate it with whichever entity is responsible for ensuring it holds.
- **Status attribute on monitored variables**: a monitored value can be untrustworthy — never yet sensed (unknown) or not recently refreshed (stale). A status attribute whose values correspond one-to-one with distinct system behaviours captures this; its initial value must prevent use of the variable until it has been sensed at least once.

## Mental Models
- **Operational concepts are the bridge, not the destination.** They sit between the system overview and the detailed requirements; their job is to validate behaviour and expose missing functions early, in language every stakeholder can read.
- **Write the happy path first, then bolt on the failures.** Nominal behaviour comes first; exceptions and alternate courses are extensions layered on later, which keeps early scenarios simple and readable.
- **A good use case is a conversation.** Each step should be an action by a different participant. The handbook's tell-tale for a bad use case: one participant dominating the dialogue — that is a signal to revise.
- **The use case is also a discovery tool.** Because it treats the system as a black box and reuses the boundary vocabulary (external entities, monitored and controlled variables), walking through it naturally exposes oversights — new variables, new entities, new functions — and feeds them back into the boundary definition.
- **Environmental assumptions are requirements pointed outward.** They are obligations the system places on its environment. Meet the assumptions and the system is obliged to meet its requirements; this is the precondition/postcondition contract that makes independent component development possible.
- **Assumptions are reuse insurance.** Document them and you can later check, in a new environment, whether they still hold — the handbook ties undocumented assumptions to dramatic failures when systems were reused without the original developers' assumptions being known.
- **Prefer a robust system over a long list of assumptions.** Where an assumption can be eliminated by strengthening the system's own behaviour, do that; a robust system depends on its environment less than a fragile one. But no realisable system has zero assumptions — at minimum the types, ranges, and units remain.

## Anti-patterns
- **Specifying the operator interface (HMI) too early**: the handbook warns against baking physical interface details (keypad versus dial, buzzer versus blinking light) into the operational concepts. Doing so narrows the requirements to one interface and limits their applicability; HMI design should proceed as a parallel, integrated activity, and the handbook notes that poor operator-interface design has been traced to several avionics accidents. Call out the capability the operator invokes, not how they physically invoke it.
- **Incorrect facts or assumptions**: citing Hooks and Farry, the handbook names incorrect facts or assumptions as the most common form of requirements error, and ties failure to identify environmental assumptions to subsystem-integration failures (one team's assumptions not matching another's) and to reuse failures.
- **Trusting unknown or stale monitored values**: depending on a monitored variable that has never been sensed, or whose value is no longer current, is called out as the cause of several serious accidents — the motivation for the status attribute.

## Key Takeaways
1. Operational concepts are black-box usage scenarios that sit between the system overview and detailed requirements; their value is early validation and the discovery of overlooked functions, entities, and boundary variables.
2. Use cases are the handbook's preferred vehicle: document the sunny-day scenario first, make each one a dialogue, give it a goal-named title, trace it to system goals, link its steps to System Functions, and reuse common sub-scenarios via referenced use cases.
3. Keep the operator-interface decisions out of operational concepts — name the capability invoked, not the physical means — to keep requirements general and to leave HMI design to its own parallel track.
4. Environmental assumptions are the obligations the system levies on its environment; they range from type/range/precision/unit constraints to full plant models and rate-of-change limits, and each should carry a rationale.
5. Assumptions plus requirements form a contract (preconditions plus postconditions) that enables independent component development and safe reuse; group each assumption with the entity responsible for ensuring it holds.
6. Give each monitored variable a status attribute so the system can behave correctly when a value is unknown or stale, and initialise it so the variable is not trusted until sensed at least once.
7. Minimise assumptions where you can by making the system more robust, but accept that every real system retains at least the basic type/range/unit assumptions.
