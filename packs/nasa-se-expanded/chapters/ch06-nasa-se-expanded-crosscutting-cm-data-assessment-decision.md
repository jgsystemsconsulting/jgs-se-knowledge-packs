# Chapter — Crosscutting Technical Management: Configuration, Data Management, Technical Assessment, and Decision Analysis

> Source slice: *Expanded Guidance for NASA Systems Engineering* (NASA/SP-2016-6105-SUPPL), sections 6.5–6.8 (Configuration Management, Technical Data Management, Technical Assessment, Decision Analysis).
> Depth role: this chapter is the **expanded-guidance companion** to the core *NASA SE Handbook* coverage of these four processes. The handbook tells you *that* these processes exist and *what* their inputs/outputs are; this chapter carries the specifics the handbook leaves implicit — the named external standards CM rides on, the exact baseline-to-review mapping, the earned-value arithmetic, the three required leading indicators, and the full menu of formal decision-analysis techniques. Where the handbook overview would be redundant, this chapter deliberately goes one level deeper rather than restating it.

## Core Idea

These four are the *crosscutting technical management* processes — they do not produce the system directly; they keep the project honest about the system it is producing. Configuration Management (CM) controls what the product *is* and how it is allowed to change; Technical Data Management (DM) controls the information *about* the product; Technical Assessment monitors whether technical progress is actually happening on plan; and Decision Analysis governs how choices among alternatives get made and recorded. The four interlock: assessment feeds variances into decision analysis; decision analysis dispositions changes that CM then controls; and DM is the substrate that stores every work product the other three generate.

The unifying discipline is **knowing the truth about the system at any moment in its life and being able to defend how you got there.** CM ensures the documentation matches the approved product so every stakeholder works from identical data. DM ensures that data is acquired economically, protected appropriately, and retrievable. Assessment converts raw status into trends so problems surface early enough to act. Decision analysis ensures that when you act, the rationale, the alternatives, the uncertainties, and even the dissent are captured — so the choice is robust and traceable, not merely asserted.

## Key Concepts

### The four baselines and their review gates (CM, §6.5.1.2.2)
Configuration identification produces baselines, each an agreed-to, documented description of a configuration item's attributes at a point in time, against which the *next* change is measured. The four:

- **Functional baseline** — the approved documentation of system/top-level-CI performance requirements (functional, interoperability, interface) plus the verification needed to demonstrate them. Established at **SDR**; transfers to NASA control for contracted efforts.
- **Allocated baseline** — performance-oriented documentation for a CI to be developed, extending the functional baseline's top-level requirements into enough detail to start detailed design. Typically established at **PDR**; usually held by the design organization until all design requirements are verified. NASA controls it through incremental engineering-data deliveries before CDR.
- **Product baseline** — the approved technical documentation describing the CI's configuration during production, deployment, and operational support, including form/fit/function detail and production acceptance test requirements. Typically established at **CDR**.
- **As-Deployed baseline** — occurs at **ORR**; the design is considered functional and flight-ready, with all changes incorporated into documentation.

The mental hook: each baseline freezes a more concrete picture of the system, and each freeze is tied to the review that earned it. This baseline-to-gate mapping is the depth the handbook overview elides.

### Change classification and the CCB (CM, §6.5.1.2.3)
Configuration change management runs through a disciplined internal **Configuration Control Board (CCB)** chaired by someone holding program/project change authority, with members empowered to commit the teams they represent. Changes are typed:
- **Engineering change** — a baseline iteration, classed *major* (significant impact: retrofit of delivered product, or affects the baseline spec, cost, safety, interface compatibility, operator/maintenance training) or *minor* (corrects documentation/process without affecting interchangeability of system elements).
- **Waiver** — a documented, intentional release of the project from meeting a requirement. (Some Centers distinguish *deviations*, used before implementation, from *waivers* used after.)
Any change that touches an external interface requires coordination with, and sign-off from, every affected stakeholder.

### Configuration Status Accounting and configuration verification (§6.5.1.2.4–.5)
**CSA** records and reports the configuration data needed to manage CIs — current and historical configuration documentation with unique identifiers, the status of proposed changes/deviations/waivers from initiation to implementation, and the disposition of discrepancies found in audits. It serves change evaluation, historical traceability, software trouble reporting, and performance measurement. **Configuration verification** (functional and physical configuration audits — FCA/PCA) inspects documents, products, and records to confirm the product met its requirements and that its design is documented.

### CM tooling and DM tooling overlap (§6.5.1.2.4, §6.6.1.2.7)
The source gives near-parallel "critical functions" checklists for CM software and DM software: secure real-time sharing, version control and comparison, secure check-in/check-out, metric-gathering, web-based access, e-mail notification, integration with legacy databases and with drafting/modeling tools, neutral-format viewers, multi-user licensing, workflow/life-cycle management, *limited* customization, migration support across upgrades, and the ability to act as the single authoritative source for released information. The practical guidance: the CM tool can often double as the DM tool with less formality, and a separate DM tool is warranted only when access/ingestion needs cannot otherwise be met.

### CM vs. DM — the governance line (§6.6)
The two processes run side-by-side but with different change rigor: information **under configuration control** requires a Change Request (CR) and CCB approval to alter; information **under technical data management** does *not* need a CR but still needs managed authority over who may change each data type. DM additionally classifies data ("data," data call, information, technical data package, technical data management system), and explicitly *excludes* purely business data (workforce, financial transactions, personnel, communications) from its scope.

### Data protection: SBU and ITAR (§6.6.2.1)
SBU material (detailed design data, limited-rights data, source-selection data, bid/proposal info, financial data, contingency plans, restricted software) must be marked per NID 1600.55, carry a NASA Form 1686 cover sheet, and move only through secure channels — PKI-encrypted e-mail, secure fax, or tracked hand-to-hand transfer. Ordinary e-mail and WebEx are non-secure and not permitted for SBU. ITAR adds a separate, sharper control regime: technical data needed to design, produce, operate, repair, or modify USML defense articles (launch vehicles, remote-sensing satellite systems and their ground stations, related components/GSE) is export-controlled — but general scientific/engineering principles taught in schools, public-domain information, and basic marketing/system descriptions are explicitly *not* ITAR "technical data."

### The planning–status-reporting feedback loop (Assessment, §6.7.1.2.1)
Technical assessment runs a continual loop at every level of the project hierarchy: **status reporting** says where the project stands against a technical measure; **assessing** converts that status into trend and variance understanding; the result is handed to the **Decision Analysis Process**, which may then trigger corrective action. Planning and status flow *up* the hierarchy (aggregated at each level); decisions flow *down*. Good-practice principles: a single agreed set of well-defined measures, consistent reporting format at all levels, retained historical data for trend and cross-project analysis, roll-up along the WBS/PBS, quantitative risk support, and red/yellow/green alert zones.

### The technical review system (Assessment, §6.7.2)
The chapter's largest single body of depth is the catalog of life-cycle reviews and their roles. Reviews inform the **decision authority** about readiness to pass a **KDP** gate into the next phase. Space-flight KDP/milestone reviews are evaluated by an external, advisory **Standing Review Board (SRB)** that reports findings but holds no authority over content; before the SRB, the technical team runs its own internal **peer reviews** (often "tabletop" or "red team" reviews, roundtable-format, documentation-focused). The required space-flight review sequence and the gate each one earns:

| Review | Confirms / earns |
|---|---|
| MCR (Mission Concept Review) | Mission need affirmed; concept supports the customer need |
| SRR (System Requirements Review) | Requirements + concept satisfy the mission; **freezes requirements** |
| MDR/SDR (Mission/System Definition Review) | Architecture responds to requirements; requirements allocated to functional elements |
| PDR (Preliminary Design Review) | Preliminary design meets requirements at acceptable risk; **design-to baseline approved**; authorizes entry to Implementation |
| CDR (Critical Design Review) | Design mature enough for fabrication; **build-to baseline, production & verification plans approved** |
| PRR (Production Readiness Review) | Developers ready to efficiently produce multiple/similar systems (>3 or as determined) |
| SIR (System Integration Review) | Segments, facilities, personnel ready to begin integration |
| SAR (System Acceptance Review) | End products complete and compliant; authorizes shipment to launch/operational site |
| ORR (Operational Readiness Review) | System ready to assume normal operations |
| FRR (Flight Readiness Review) | Technical/procedural maturity for launch and flight authorization |
| PLAR / CERR / PFAR | Post-launch routine-ops readiness / critical-event readiness / post-flight anomaly closeout |
| DR / DRR (Decommissioning / Disposal Readiness) | Readiness to safely decommission and dispose of assets |

Non-NPR-7123.1-required but common reviews include the **DCR** (Design Certification Review — confirms qualification verification met requirements; a form of Safety and Mission Success Review), **FCA/PCA** (functional/physical configuration audits), **TRR** (Test Readiness Review — certifies test article/facility/personnel/procedures are ready and authorizes test initiation), and **PIR/PSR** (program implementation/status reviews). Tailoring is explicit: smaller mission classes (per NPR 8705.4 risk classes A–D) may collapse to a minimum trio — one review each for requirements, design, and acceptance (plus an FRR for flight systems); complex class-A work gets the full distributed set.

### Cost and schedule control — the EVM arithmetic (§6.7.2.6.1)
EVM is the disciplined way to measure "the system that produces the system." NASA requires it per NPR 7120.5; the system standard is **ANSI-EIA-748**; the **Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB)** is set in Phase B for KDP C; variances are controlled at the **control account** level (typically subsystem WBS) by a **Control Account Manager (CAM)**. The core quantities:

- **BCWS** (Budgeted Cost for Work Scheduled) — planned cost of work scheduled by time *t*.
- **BCWP** (Budgeted Cost for Work Performed = **Earned Value**) — budgeted cost of work actually produced by time *t*.
- **ACWP** (Actual Cost of Work Performed) — funds actually expended by time *t*.
- **Schedule variance** = BCWP − BCWS (negative ⇒ behind schedule).
- **Cost variance** = BCWP − ACWP (negative ⇒ overrun).
- **SPI** = BCWP / BCWS (schedule efficiency; <1 behind, =1 on, >1 ahead).
- **CPI** = BCWP / ACWP (cost efficiency; <1 negative, =1 on cost, >1 positive).
- **EAC / BAC / VAC** — Estimate at Completion vs. Budget at Completion; **VAC = BAC − EAC** (negative generally unfavorable). For a one-time-event variance, the statistical cross-check is **EAC = ACWP + (BAC − BCWP)**, reviewed against the CAM's own estimate and adjusted upward if liens/action-items/problem-difficulty are growing.

A load-bearing caution from the source: comparing *planned cost to actual cost* is only a spending indicator, never a performance indicator — performance comparisons are always made through earned value (BCWP), never against planned cost directly.

### The technical-measure hierarchy: MOEs, MOPs, KPPs, TPMs (§6.7.2.6.2)
The chapter draws the distinctions the handbook tends to blur:

- **MOEs (Measures of Effectiveness)** — *operational* success measures stated from the **customer/user viewpoint**, extracted from stakeholder expectations, solution-independent ("how well," not "how"). Failing an MOE's critical value makes the stakeholder judge the system a failure. May be quantitative or qualitative; used to rank alternatives in trade studies.
- **MOPs (Measures of Performance)** — physical/functional attributes of the end product, stated from the **supplier's viewpoint**, quantitative (speed, range, frequency). Derived from MOEs; an MOE is *validated* when its associated MOPs are achieved. One MOE may have several MOPs.
- **KPPs (Key Performance Parameters)** — the minimum set of capabilities most essential to mission success. **Failing a KPP threshold puts the project in cost/schedule/performance-risk status and can be cause for reevaluation or termination.** For R&T efforts, KPPs carry threshold and goal values tied to Technology Readiness Level (TRL).
- **TPMs (Technical Performance Measures)** — the *implementation-time* tracking measures, comparing current achievement against the value anticipated for now and projected for future dates. Considered for each KPP and selected MOEs/MOPs; a TPM outside its expected range signals evaluation and corrective action. TPMs can be *generic* (mass, reliability — meaningful to every PBS element) or *unique* (specific to one PBS element). The canonical example is **injected mass** budgeted against launch-vehicle capability and allocated down the PBS.

Two TPM reporting methods are named: the **planned profile method** (illustrated by the Chandra weight TPM with rebaseline) and the **margin management method** (illustrated by the Sojourner mass margin from SRR to launch). The SEMP is the usual home for the TPM assessment program; large programs may keep a standalone technical-metrics plan.

### Technical Leading Indicators — the three required (§6.7.2.6.3)
A **leading indicator** is a measure shown to be *predictive* of future program performance/cost/schedule, viewed against time to reveal maturity and stability. NPR 7123.1 mandates exactly three of them: **mass margin**, **power margin**, and **RFA/RID/Action-Item burndown** — gathered and reported from after SRR through SAR, at minimum at the rolled-up program/project level. The point of plotting margins against allocation and not-to-exceed (NTE) bands, with life-cycle reviews marked on the time axis, is to read **both maturity and stability**: a CDR-mature design that still has volatile requirements is *not* stable. Indicators often start as estimates (e.g., modeled mass in Phase A) and converge to measured values as the product is built.

### SE process metrics (§6.7.2.6.3)
Beyond cost/schedule, SE process metrics quantify the SE effort's progress, quality (S/Q/P categories in Table 6.7-8), and productivity — requirements volatility, TBD/TBR/TBA burndown, ECP/ECR processing, drawings planned vs. released, V&V plans/procedures status, RID processing, percentage of design validated against ConOps. Trends beat snapshots; the strongest benchmark is the same metric's trend on a *successfully completed project of the same type*. Each metric costs effort to collect, so the systems engineer balances metric value against collection cost.

### Decision Analysis — structure, triggers, and rigor (§6.8)
Decision Analysis is the framework for formulating and characterizing the alternatives that best serve the decision-maker's priorities, weighed against what that decision-maker currently knows and how uncertain that knowledge is. It spans the full life cycle, from a small informal discussion among discipline engineers for a small project to a weeks-long formal analysis for a strategic choice.

- **Decision-making structure** — the source treats board design with information-theory framing: each board member is an information source, the discussion is the communication channel, and **every piece of information the decision depends on has to be present somewhere in the membership** (missing information ⇒ noise ⇒ bias ⇒ partially informed, unintended-consequence decisions). The three structuring rules: (1) define each board's scope; (2) ensure every affected/contributing discipline (each engineering discipline, SMA, procurement, business office) is represented, including the uncertainties in scope; (3) minimize the number of boards — efficiency falls with **overlapping scopes**, which cause confusion, delay, poor quality, and a skill drain from staffing duplicate representatives.
- **When formal analysis is warranted** — four conditions raise the need (none individually required): **Complexity**, **Uncertainty** (where reducing it could change the ranking), **Multiple Attributes**, and **Diversity of Stakeholders**. Formal analysis is expensive, so its rigor should scale to consequence.
- **The process** — understand the decision in mission/system context → define decision criteria (objective, measurable, ranked; mandatory "shall-have" vs. enhancing) → identify alternatives covering the full decision space (brainstorming, literature search, pruned via a **trade tree**) → select evaluation methods/tools → evaluate against criteria with **normalization** to a common scale (with *operational definitions* — e.g., "high" = ≥67% probability) → recommend (generally a single alternative; if uncertainties could credibly flip the ranking, recommend all closely ranked options) → report.
- **Robustness and uncertainty** — a *robust* decision is based on sufficient evidence and uncertainty characterization that the chosen alternative best reflects decision-maker preferences given current knowledge. Uncertainty matters *only* when there is a non-zero probability that reducing it would change the ranking; then the source asks whether reducing it is **"net beneficial"** — explicitly including whether the project can afford the schedule slip from extra analysis.
- **The decision report** — a structured artifact (Table 6.8-1): executive summary, problem/issue, decision-matrix setup and scoring rationale, final matrix, risks/benefits, recommendation/final decision, **dissent (and how it was addressed)**, references, appendices (literature search, prior related decisions and dissent). Reports are maintained in the system archive for traceability.

### The formal decision-analysis methods (§6.8.2)
The depth payoff — a catalog the handbook only names:
- **Trade studies** — select the most suitable option from viable options within constraints. The source gives a full process (Figure 6.8-5): goals/objectives/constraints → functional & interface requirements → minimum requirement set → **selection rule** → plausible options → attributes → measurement methods (often requiring models that may need to be built, so identify them early) → data collection → compute effectiveness/performance/cost with **uncertainty ranges and sensitivity analyses** (Monte Carlo where feasible) → tentative selection → **"reality check"** (goals met? selection robust? enough data? methods discriminating? subjective aspects addressed?). A dedicated, qualified team leader runs it; **dissenting opinions that can't be resolved go to the decision-maker**; the report is signed by the whole team and folded into change control.
- **Cost-Benefit / Cost-Effectiveness / Least-Cost analysis** — cost-benefit maximizes net benefit (benefits − costs); cost-effectiveness maximizes benefit for a fixed cost cap (e.g., science data per dollar) and warns that the most cost-effective option is *not* necessarily the most effective; least-cost normalizes quality differences as cost penalties to compare technically feasible options fairly.
- **Influence Diagrams** — compact graphical/mathematical representation of a decision *state* (decision nodes, chance nodes, value nodes, arrows); good for clear communication and problem formulation; convertible into a decision tree for quantification; handles incomplete information-sharing among team members.
- **Decision Trees** — exhaustively work out consequences of all alternatives by discretizing chance nodes and weighting all outcomes; a TPM-score vector sits at each terminal branch; suffer exponential branch growth, so often started from an equivalent influence diagram.
- **Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)** — Saaty's pair-wise-comparison MCDA method; strong for deriving *criteria weights*, weaker (can bias) for selecting actual alternatives because pair-wise comparison assumes independence among factors. Six steps, reducible to the first four if used only to produce TPM weights for a Performance Index/MOE.
- **Borda Counting** — weighted-ranking vote across all criteria simultaneously; finds the option that best fits *all* criteria (often the consistent second-place finisher), capturing SME intuition without a single criterion biasing the result.
- **Utility Analysis / MAUT** — utility (measured in *utils*) maps a TPM's range onto a preference curve whose convexity (risk-prone) or concavity (risk-averse) encodes the decision-maker's risk attitude; a **value function** is the risk-neutral simplification (best end = 1, worst = 0). **Multi-Attribute Utility Theory** quantifies each alternative's *expected utility* (sometimes a Performance Index, PI) and is the best approach for significant uncertainty with a non-risk-neutral decision-maker — natural for top-level architecture decisions.

## Mental Models

- **CM as the enterprise "backbone."** The source explicitly calls CM the backbone of the enterprise structure for complex technology: it instills discipline and keeps product attributes and documentation consistent, so every stakeholder at any moment works from identical data. If the backbone fails, the project "loses configuration control" — confusion, low productivity, and unmanageable data.
- **Baselines as progressive freezes earned at gates.** Functional→Allocated→Product→As-Deployed is a ratchet: each baseline is a more concrete, agreed picture of the system, locked in at the review (SDR/PDR/CDR/ORR) that justified the freeze, and it becomes the reference against which the *next* change is judged.
- **"The system that produces the system."** Cost/schedule control (EVM) and SE process metrics measure the production *engine*, not the product. Earned value (BCWP) is the pivot: schedule and cost variance are both differences *from* BCWP, and planned-vs-actual cost alone tells you only about spending.
- **Leading vs. lagging, maturity vs. stability.** A leading indicator predicts a future state; a lagging one only records history. Mass/power margins and RFA burndown are required precisely because they are predictive. And maturity is not stability — a design can be CDR-mature yet unstable if requirements are still churning.
- **The customer/supplier viewpoint split.** MOEs are the customer's "did it work for the mission?" measures; MOPs are the supplier's "does my design deliver?" measures. KPPs are the must-not-fail subset; TPMs are how you watch the KPPs/MOPs converge during build.
- **A decision board as a communication channel.** Borrowing information theory: a board can only make a sound decision if every necessary information source is in the room. Missing or withheld information is noise; overlapping board scopes are inefficiency and a skill drain.
- **Reduce uncertainty only when it's net beneficial.** Uncertainty is worth reducing only if it could plausibly flip the ranking *and* the project can afford the time/cost of reducing it. Otherwise, decide on the knowledge you have.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these explicitly:

- **CM warning signs / red flags (§6.5.2.1)** — declaring "we don't need a specification"; failing to recognize the baseline activities that bracket design reviews; compressing change-evaluation time below what engineering/SMA/CCB members can meet; a program office that, for CCB documentation, declares "there will be no dissent in the record"; awarding a contract with CM requirements not concurred by the supporting CMO; inappropriate use of redlines on the production floor; a Material Review Board that can't distinguish critical/major/minor nonconformances; verification data that can't be traced to a released part number; parts unidentifiable due to bad markings; inability to verify latest released engineering through the contractor's CM web site; CIs that don't fit their shipping crates because the containers lost configuration control.
- **Improper redline use (§6.5.2.2)** — redlines are a controlled markup process; using them without a controlled procedure and the required hardware-manager/QA approvals is an anti-pattern. The source anchors this with the **NOAA N-Prime mishap**, where improper, unpracticed, extensively redlined procedures (requiring "jumping" from step to step) were a named precondition to the decision errors that caused the incident.
- **Inappropriate uses of technical data (§6.6.1.2.4)** — unauthorized disclosure of classified/in-confidence data; faulty interpretation from incomplete, out-of-context, or misleading data; using data for parts/maintenance procurement without at least Government-purpose rights. Countered by educating stakeholders and controlling access.
- **The cost of *not* doing CM (§6.5.2.1)** — mission failure and loss of property/life from improperly configured hardware/software; lost mission data; schedule slips and added cost; uncertified/fraudulently certified parts. The source cites the **Columbia Accident Investigation Board** finding of "unincorporated documentation changes" (though no CM issue was cited as an accident *cause*) and the X-37 fast-prototyping case where schedule took priority over recording what was done to the hardware.
- **Overlapping decision-board scopes (§6.8)** — boards with overlapping information scopes generate uncertainty, confusion, disagreement, delay, poor decision quality, or decision paralysis, plus a workforce skill drain from duplicate representation.
- **Comparing planned to actual cost as a performance measure (§6.7.2.6.1)** — explicitly flagged: that comparison indicates spending only, not performance; performance is judged through earned value.
- **AHP misuse (§6.8.2.2.3)** — using AHP to select actual alternatives (not just criteria weights) can bias results because pair-wise comparison assumes factor independence, which is often false.

## Key Takeaways

- NASA CM is not generic — it rides on **SAE/EIA-649-2's five functions and 37 principles** (and SAE/EIA 649B), giving the contractual teeth the handbook overview only gestures at.
- The **four baselines map one-to-one to review gates** (Functional→SDR, Allocated→PDR, Product→CDR, As-Deployed→ORR); know which freeze you're working under and what change authority controls it.
- **CM controls the product and requires a CR + CCB to change; DM controls the information and requires only managed change authority** — same data store often, different rigor.
- Data protection is a hard requirement, not a courtesy: **SBU (NID 1600.55, Form 1686, PKI)** and **ITAR/USML** carry distinct marking, transmission, and access rules.
- Technical assessment is a **continual up-status/down-decision feedback loop**; its review system is gated by **KDPs**, evaluated by the advisory **SRB**, and tailorable by mission class.
- Learn the **EVM arithmetic cold** — BCWS/BCWP/ACWP, SPI/CPI, EAC/BAC/VAC — and remember performance is always measured through earned value, never planned-vs-actual spend.
- Keep the **MOE/MOP/KPP/TPM** hierarchy straight (customer vs. supplier viewpoint; must-not-fail KPPs; build-time TPMs), and report the **three required leading indicators** — mass margin, power margin, RFA/RID/action-item burndown — to read maturity *and* stability.
- Decision analysis is about **defensible, traceable, robust choices**: scope the boards by information, scale rigor to consequence, reduce uncertainty only when net beneficial, and capture criteria, alternatives, uncertainties, recommendation, **and dissent** in an archived report.
- The formal-method menu — **trade studies, cost-benefit/cost-effectiveness/least-cost, influence diagrams, decision trees, AHP, Borda counting, utility analysis/MAUT** — gives the systems engineer matched tools for problems ranging from a single weighted matrix to a strategic, uncertainty-laden architecture choice.
