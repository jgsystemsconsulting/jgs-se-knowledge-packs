# Chapter — Crosscutting Technical Management: Planning, Requirements, Interface, and Risk Management

> **Depth note.** This chapter deliberately layers *practitioner mechanics* on top of the headline frameworks already summarized in the base `nasa-se-handbook` pack (chapters 27–30). The base pack tells you *what* the WBS, SEMP, network schedule, JCL, RIDM/CRM, the risk triplet, bidirectional traceability, and the IRD/ICD/IDD family are. This chapter assumes you know those definitions and instead works through *how the procedures actually run* — the six-step scheduling algorithm, the skill-mix selection logic, the full-cost double-counting trap, the change-impact toolset, the interface-document decision tree, and the scenario/initiating-event structure of a risk statement. Where a concept is only named in the base pack, here it is operated.

## Core Idea

The eight technical management processes are the load-bearing connective tissue between project management and the engineering bench. Four of them — planning, requirements management, interface management, and technical risk management — together answer a single recurring question for the systems engineer: *how do we keep a distributed, multi-organization effort converging on a system that satisfies the ConOps within cost and schedule, when the design, the budget, the team, and the environment are all changing at once?* The answer is not any one document; it is a set of disciplined, recursive bookkeeping loops. Planning scopes the work and reconciles the technical team's bottom-up cost/schedule estimate against the project's top-down allocation. Requirements management preserves the parent-child trace so that no scope is orphaned and no creep goes unbudgeted. Interface management treats every boundary as a controlled contract so integration does not surprise anyone. Risk management converts vague concern into structured scenario-likelihood-consequence triplets that can be monitored against trigger thresholds. The depth that matters is in the seams: each process feeds the others, and a change accepted in one without impact assessment in the others is how projects quietly slide out of control.

## Frameworks Introduced

The following are named in this slice of *NASA/SP-2016-6105 Rev 2* (the Systems Engineering Handbook). For each, the focus here is the operating procedure rather than the one-line definition.

- **Technical Planning Strategy (Section 6.1.1.2.1).** A pre-SEMP checklist the team works through before any plan is drafted. It is invoked at the very start of Pre-Phase A and forces explicit decisions on roughly two dozen items — communication paths (internal, up, and out), which NASA procedural requirements get tailored at each PBS level, input-document collection, work sequencing, deliverable capture, risk-identification approach, tools and training, stakeholder touch-points, contractor interface, milestone entry/success criteria, internal and external interface definition, lessons-learned pull-in and capture, technology-development incorporation, technical metrics, make/buy/reuse and COTS criteria, off-nominal mitigation, contingency and replanning playbooks, status reporting, the decision-analysis approach, and human-element management. The output is a rough draft of the SEMP.

- **Team Skill-Mix Selection (Section 6.1.1.2.1, "Team Selection").** A judgment procedure, not a formula. The team splits into subsystem engineers (cognizant over a buildable subsystem — mechanical, power, thermal) and discipline engineers (cross-cutting analyses — flight dynamics, radiation, reliability). Mix is set in consultation with experienced design leads and varies with three drivers: what the product is and demands, the class of mission, and where the project sits in its phasing. Human-rated systems add life-support and human-systems disciplines; deep-space adds planetary protection; cryogenic instruments add cryogenics support; Class A/B missions demand analyses that Class D may waive; and a single generalist in Pre-Phase A may fan out to multiple specialists by Phase B/C. The handbook's worked example (Table 6.1-1) is a Pre-Phase A robotic infrared observatory team, split into a spacecraft-bus/flight-dynamics subteam and an instrument subteam.

- **Six-Step Network Schedule Construction (Section 6.1.1.2.3, "Establishing a Network Schedule").** Applied to each lowest-available WBS element: (1) identify activities and dependencies, extending WBS elements down to every significant document, report, hardware, and software product and drawing each as a workflow diagram; (2) identify and *negotiate* external dependencies — receivables in and deliverables out — agreeing content, format, and labeling so lower schedules integrate; (3) estimate durations and write down the basis of estimate; (4) load the data into a scheduling tool to get a network schedule and element-level critical path, iterating steps 1–4 as needed and adding reserve to critical-path activities; (5) integrate lower-level schedules into a project network, folding in holidays and weekends, which is where the project critical path emerges; (6) review workforce and funding profiles over time and adjust logic and durations — adding activities, deleting redundancy, increasing workforce on critical tasks, or parallelizing serial work — until levels fall within project constraints. Replanning is described as "almost always needed."

- **Workflow Diagram Formats (Section 6.1.1.2.3).** Two graphical conventions for the same precedence logic: activity-on-arrow (the classic PERT form, products and dependencies at arrow ends) and precedence diagrams (boxes are activities, arrows are dependencies). Precedence diagrams express start-start, finish-start, and finish-finish relationships, each modifiable by a positive or negative lag; finish-start is the most common. A summary activity collapses many low-level activities by chaining start-start to the first and finish-start to the last.

- **Gantt Reporting and Float Tables (Section 6.1.1.2.3, "Reporting Techniques").** A Gantt chart summarizes schedule status with current-plan bars over thinner baseline bars, slack as a dotted line before a milestone, slips as dotted lines after current bars, the critical path highlighted, and a status date line. A companion technique is a float table that reports *how much schedule reserve key activities hold and how fast it is being consumed* — surfacing reserve burn-down that a Gantt bar alone hides.

- **Cost-Estimating Method Selection (Section 6.1.2.2.6).** Three methods mapped to program maturity: parametric (early, limited definition, historical cost-estimating relationships, least detail), analogous (a defined design with insufficient actuals, adjusting a similar program for complexity/technical/physical differences), and engineering/grassroots (bottom-up rollup, accurate but invalidated by every "what-if," so impractical for many trade iterations). Selection depends on definition adequacy, detail required, data availability, and time.

- **Life-Cycle Cost Integration Mechanics (Section 6.1.2.2.7).** The procedural traps in assembling an LCCE from multiple models: "wrapping" hardware estimates with factors for management, SE, and test; applying full-cost factors separately and carefully to avoid double-counting when some work is in-house and the model was built on all-contracted history (or the inverse under-count when the model was built on off-book in-house work); normalizing constant-year dollars; applying learning curves (resources drop a fixed percentage each time quantity doubles; unit-curve and cumulative-average forms) for repetitive manufacturing; and phasing costs with a beta curve or by resource-loading the schedule when doing a JCL.

- **Joint Confidence Level Scatterplot (Section 6.1.2.2.8).** The JCL is read off an XY scatterplot — schedule on X, cost on Y — of Monte Carlo results. A cost/schedule target splits the plot; the JCL is the percentage of simulation points at or under both targets. A frontier line of yellow points marks combinations that meet a desired joint level. Required at KDP-C for projects above $250M; KDP-B requires only probabilistic *range* estimates (high/low for cost and schedule) to "bound the problem," producible by full parametric estimates or a JCL.

- **Requirements Change-Impact Toolset (Section 6.2.1.2.5).** Three named tools used inside the CM process to assess a proposed requirement change before approval: a **Performance Margins** list (e.g., propellant available vs. needed), a **CM Topic Evaluators List** (who must review which change so all impacts are found), and a **Risk System and Threats List** (how the change shifts likelihood/consequence of existing risks or introduces new ones, with reserves prioritized against the threats list).

- **Interface Requirement Derivation from Functional Allocation (Section 6.3.2.1).** A mechanical derivation rule, using the source's own F1/F2/C1/C2 notation: when one function emits some item A to a second function, the first function maps to component C1 and the second to component C2, then an implicit interface requirement exists that the C1–C2 boundary must carry item A — whether A is a fluid, a solid, or a data message. This turns the functional architecture directly into interface requirements.

- **Interface Document Family (Sections 6.3.2.1–6.3.2.4).** A four-document taxonomy with distinct roles: the **IRD** (Interface Requirements Document — bilateral "shall" statements on functional, physical, performance, and environmental requirements at a boundary; signed by every party); the **ICD** (Interface Control Document/Drawing — the bilateral *design solution* implementing the IRD, no "shall" statements, detailing connectors, electrical parameters, mechanical and environmental specifics); the **IDD** (Interface Definition Document — a unilateral "one-sided ICD" controlled by the end-item provider describing an already-fixed interface that the other side must design to); and the **ICP** (Interface Control Plan — the process document naming organizations, responsibilities, the control forum, and the change mechanism via PIRN/IRN). The base pack names the ICD/IRD/IDD; the *distinguishing logic and the PIRN/IRN change vehicle* are the added depth here.

- **Interface Working Group (Section 6.3.1.2.4).** A standing technical team with membership from each interfacing party that owns how interface work is planned, scheduled, and carried out, plus the communication links between the interfacing systems. It may operate independently or inside a larger change control board.

- **Risk Scenario / Initiating-Event Structure (Section 6.4, opening).** Beneath the risk triplet sits the scenario-construction mechanic: a scenario begins with an initiating event that knocks the activity out of its intended state; subsequent events either mitigate or exacerbate the progression; frequencies of undesired-consequence scenarios are computed; and the aggregate of scenarios, with uncertainties, forms the system risk profile. This is the machinery the base pack's "risk triplet" abstracts over.

- **RIDM ↔ CRM Interaction (Section 6.4.1.2.3).** The post-2008 restructuring (per NPR 8000.4 and NASA/SP-2011-3422) in which Risk Management equals *two* processes, not one: RIDM informs direction-setting SE decisions by feeding risk and uncertainty into alternative selection and baseline requirement setting, while CRM manages individual risks through development and implementation. RM was formerly equated with CRM alone; the depth is in *when* each runs and how they hand off.

## Key Concepts

- **Bottom-up vs. top-down reconciliation.** The program/project plan hands down a budget and schedule *allocation*; the technical team independently generates a cost *estimate* and schedule from the actual work required. The handbook is explicit that discrepancies between the two must be reconciled *continuously* across the life cycle, not once. This reconciliation is the core tension the SEMP exists to manage.

- **Contingency planning as a named discipline.** Beyond generic reserve, the slice calls out three concrete contingency cases the team should pre-fund: unplanned software-engineering staff to troubleshoot hardware/systems test anomalies; Hardware-In-the-Loop (HWIL) debugging time, mitigable by readying debug software before the costly first hardware-software bring-up; and Human-In-the-Loop (HITL) evaluation early in design to expose nominal, off-nominal, maintenance, repair, and training issues cheaply before late changes become expensive.

- **WBS as derived from the PBS, with three failure modes.** The WBS is built from the Product Breakdown Structure by adding service elements (management, SE, integration and verification) at each branch point. The handbook names three common WBS errors that defeat its purpose: (1) describing *functions* instead of products — which makes the project manager the only party formally responsible for any product; (2) branch points inconsistent with how elements will actually integrate — e.g., splitting hardware and software that integrate at a low level as if they were system-level peers, destroying integration accountability; and (3) a WBS inconsistent with the PBS — risking that the PBS is never fully implemented.

- **Cost-account decomposition for EVM.** Under EVM a cost account decomposes into **work packages** (schedulable, budgeted units of work, preferably tied to completed products) whose budgets sum into the Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled (BCWS), and **planning packages** (future work not yet detailed enough — e.g., launch operations support in Phase B — carrying a high-level schedule and lump budget, later broken into work packages so EVM keeps running).

- **Technical work directives to Cost Account Managers (Section 6.1.1.2.6).** A specific, often-skipped Phase B activity: the technical team issues directives to CAMs (expected technical products, reporting requirements, critical-event deliverables such as a named PDR presentation, applicable standards, mandated tools, coordination instructions, and made decisions) so cost-account plans are mutually consistent and form a sound EVM baseline. Skipping it leaves CAMs guessing, and the handbook warns this reliably produces large baseline-vs-actual variances.

- **The 50–70–90 cost lock-in.** By the time the preferred architecture is selected, 50–70% of life-cycle cost is committed; by preliminary design, up to 90%. The practical consequence the slice draws out: information about alternatives is *least* certain exactly when decisions are *most* consequential, so the high-payoff move is buying better LCCE information early and pulling reliability, maintainability, supportability, and operations engineering into the process in Pre-Phase A/A/B.

- **Verification vs. validation method symmetry with asymmetric intent.** Both use the same four methods — analysis, demonstration, inspection, test — but verification proves conformance to *specified requirements* (often on controlled qualification/protoflight/flight units; "buying off a shall" on a prototype is generally unacceptable) while validation confirms satisfaction of *stakeholder expectations* (ConOps/NGOs), typically in the operational or a relevant environment, and should deliberately include off-nominal scenarios.

- **Self-derived requirements vs. gold-plating.** A requirement with no parent is either a legitimate self-derived requirement (a locally adopted good practice or a Logical-Decomposition/Design-Solution decision, for which concurrence is sought from the next level up) or gold-plating to be eliminated. Duplication across levels is also resolved: a requirement merely repeated lower and not an external constraint may not belong at the higher level.

- **Requirements creep contains real evolution.** The slice is careful that creep is not uniformly bad: some growth is enhancement-in-disguise, but some is genuinely new requirement that *could not have been anticipated* and must be honored to build a relevant system. The discipline is the formal change channel, not blanket refusal.

- **Interface change asymmetry over the life cycle.** Like requirement and schedule changes, interface changes grow costlier with maturity; bilateral interfaces require *unanimous* (all-sides) approval, and the ICP sequences interface-control readiness against milestones (approach required at MCR, preliminary at SDR, baselined at PDR).

- **The five risk domains and four risk types.** Performance shortfalls span safety, technical, cost, and schedule (plus institutional support); risk *types* are characterized as cost, schedule, technical, and programmatic. Programmatic risk is the one engineers most often dismiss — it originates outside the project (ITAR/export control, partner agreements, congressional earmarks, OMB direction, contractor restructuring) yet lands as technical/cost/schedule impact.

## Mental Models

- **The four processes are one ledger with four columns.** A change is never local. The change-impact toolset (performance margins, evaluators list, threats list) exists precisely because a requirement edit ripples into interfaces, schedule, cost, and the risk profile. Model the four processes as views into a single state, and treat any change accepted in one view without posting it to the other three as an unbalanced ledger entry that will be discovered later — expensively.

- **Estimate maturity should track design maturity.** Run cost estimation as progressive refinement: parametric when only history and a concept exist, analogous once a design is baselined but actuals are thin, grassroots once detailed scope and actuals accumulate. Pairing a precise grassroots number to an immature design is false confidence; the handbook prizes honest uncertainty (Pre-Phase A LCCE at 70% confidence "with much uncertainty expected") over spurious precision.

- **A schedule is a negotiation artifact, not a wall calendar.** Step 2 of the six-step build is literally *negotiate* external dependencies — agree content, format, and labeling of cross-WBS receivables. The schedule's value is in exposing dependencies and critical path so the team can resource-level, buffer with float, and replan; a schedule without negotiated interfaces and reserve is brittle by construction.

- **Every interface is a signed contract, and the IRD/ICD split mirrors requirement/design.** The IRD is the "shall" (what the boundary must do, signed by all sides); the ICD is the "how" (the implemented design solution, no "shall"). An IDD is the one-sided case where one provider has already fixed the interface and the other must conform. Choosing the document is choosing who holds authority over the boundary.

- **Build the risk statement from the initiating event outward.** Do not start at "the mission could fail." Start at the initiating event that departs from intended state, chain the mitigating/exacerbating events into a scenario, then attach likelihood and consequence with their uncertainties. The triplet is the *output* of this construction, not a fill-in-the-blanks template — which is why two engineers writing "the same risk" often disagree until they reconcile scenarios.

- **RIDM sets the table; CRM clears the plates.** Use RIDM where you are *choosing* — architecture, trades, baseline performance requirements — so risk and uncertainty shape the selection. Switch to CRM once you are *executing* — to drive identified risks to closure against trigger thresholds. Running CRM where RIDM belongs means managing risks inside a design you never risk-informed.

## Anti-patterns

The source explicitly names the following pitfalls:

- **WBS describes functions, not products (Error 1).** Naming the project manager as the only party formally responsible for any deliverable.
- **WBS branch points inconsistent with integration (Error 2).** Separating hardware and software that integrate at a low level as if they were system-level peers, destroying integration cost and accountability.
- **WBS inconsistent with the PBS (Error 3).** Allowing the product breakdown to go partially unimplemented and complicating management.
- **Underestimating planning resources into "continuous crisis management."** The slice states many projects historically under-resourced proper planning and were forced into perpetual change-chasing.
- **Cost-estimate credibility flags.** The handbook lists symptoms that make an estimate "suspect": WBS costs in dollars only with no other units (requirements too thin to identify processes/resources); a basis of estimate too sparse for independent verification; actuals diverging sharply from the LCCE; unplanned work causing variance; unfavorable earned-value trends; and operations costs of the human element omitted.
- **Gold-plating (orphaned requirements).** Requirements with no parent and no valid self-derivation should be eliminated, not retained.
- **Treating late changes like early changes.** Phase B/C requirement and interface changes carry far larger cost/schedule impact and demand correspondingly harder scrutiny.
- **Waiting to document lessons learned.** Because resolution may not arrive until a later phase and detailed lessons are hard for the human mind to reconstruct, deferring capture to a review or project end works against both putting lessons to use and letting practice evolve.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Run the planning-to-SEMP loop as a strategy first, document second.** The Technical Planning Strategy checklist forces the roughly two-dozen decisions whose *thinking* is the real value of the SEMP; the page count of the plan is explicitly stated to be unimportant.
2. **Set the team's skill mix against product, mission class, and phase — with experienced design leads in the room.** It is a subjective judgment, and the worked observatory example shows how human-rating, deep space, cryogenics, and mission class each pull specific disciplines onto the team.
3. **Construct schedules in six steps and expect to replan.** Negotiate external dependencies, write down every basis of estimate, integrate to discover the project critical path, resource-level against workforce and funding, and reserve float on critical-path activities.
4. **Defend the cost estimate by matching method to maturity and avoiding the full-cost double-counting trap.** Parametric → analogous → grassroots as definition firms up; wrap hardware estimates, normalize year-dollars, apply learning curves and beta-curve phasing, and reconcile in-house vs. contracted scope before integrating models.
5. **Assess every requirement change with the three named tools before approval.** Performance margins, the CM topic evaluators list, and the risk/threats list together expose cost, technical, and risk ripples; route the decision and its documentation actions through the CCB after SRR baseline.
6. **Maintain bidirectional traceability live, and distinguish self-derived requirements from gold-plating.** Trace each requirement to a parent or justify self-derivation; ensure every parent is fully allocated downward; resolve duplication across levels.
7. **Pick the right interface document for the authority structure, and control change via PIRN/IRN.** IRD ("shall," bilateral, signed) → ICD (design solution, bilateral) or IDD (one-sided, provider-controlled), all wrapped by an ICP that names the control forum; bilateral interfaces need unanimous approval.
8. **Manage risk as RIDM-plus-CRM, built from initiating events.** Use RIDM to risk-inform direction-setting choices and CRM to drive identified risks to acceptable residual levels against trigger thresholds; do not neglect programmatic risk merely because it originates outside the project.
9. **Capture lessons learned as they unfold.** Document during the event, manage across control gates, and record conclusive lessons in the LLIS at the end of every phase, major process, KDP, and review — the Chandra orbit/thermal-control lesson (#1514) shows how an environment change should trigger a fresh SE iteration from requirements.
