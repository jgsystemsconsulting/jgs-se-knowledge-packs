# Expanded Guidance for NASA Systems Engineering — Glossary

Key terms from the *Expanded Guidance for NASA Systems Engineering* (NASA/SP-2016-6105-SUPPL), alphabetical, with chapter references. This pack is the depth companion to the base `nasa-se-handbook`; entries here favour the *expanded* mechanics over the survey-level definitions.

**Acceptance** — the verification subset run on **every** flight unit to confirm workmanship and manufacturing conformance (run at operational levels, no added margin), shipped with an Acceptance Data Package. Paid once per unit. (Ch 4)

**Agency Baseline Commitment (ABC)** — the irreversible cost/schedule promise locked in near KDP C (end of Phase B); for projects above $250M life-cycle cost it extends to Congress and OMB. After it, successive refinement governs — changes are expected to be refinements, not redesigns. (Ch 2)

**Allocated baseline** — the configuration baseline (extending the functional baseline into enough detail to start detailed design) typically established at PDR. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)** — Saaty's pair-wise-comparison MCDA method; strong for deriving criteria *weights*, weaker for selecting alternatives (pair-wise comparison assumes factor independence). (Ch 6)

**As-Deployed baseline** — the configuration baseline established at ORR, with the design considered flight-ready and all changes incorporated. (Ch 6)

**AS9100** — the commercial aerospace quality-management standard that the source's Table 2.1-1 crosswalks against the 17 SE processes — the bridge artifact for any AS9100-certified Center or contractor. (Ch 1)

**Borda Counting** — a weighted-ranking vote across all criteria at once; finds the option that best fits *all* criteria (often the consistent runner-up), capturing SME intuition without one criterion dominating. (Ch 6)

**Certification** — not a test but an **audit**: the body of verification evidence is presented to a certifying authority that declares the design fit for flight. Paid once per design. (Ch 4)

**Compliance Matrix (NPR 7123.1 Appendix H)** — the auditable record listing every NPR requirement with its compliance, intent-to-comply, or relief justification, attached to the SEMP. H.1 = Center-level (OCE-approved); H.2 = program/project (Center Director-approved). (Ch 2)

**Concept of Operations (ConOps)** — the early, technical-team description (usually time-sequenced) of how the system will be used to satisfy stakeholders; distinct from the later, narrower **Operations Concept**. (Ch 3)

**Configuration Control Board (CCB)** — the disciplined internal board, chaired by a holder of change authority, that dispositions engineering changes (major/minor) and waivers; external-interface changes need all-stakeholder sign-off. (Ch 6)

**Configuration Status Accounting (CSA)** — the CM function recording current/historical configuration data, the status of changes/deviations/waivers, and audit-discrepancy dispositions. (Ch 6)

**Constraint** — a condition that must be met and typically *cannot* be moved by trade analysis (orbital mechanics, a mandated interface, regulation, technology state, budget); it sets the design boundary, separate from the trade space. (Ch 3)

**Control Account Manager (CAM)** — the owner of a control account (usually a subsystem WBS element) responsible for its EVM cost/schedule variances. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Customization** — modifying the *recommended* practices through which an SE requirement is met; changes *how* you comply, not *whether*. Needs **no** waiver or deviation (document significant cases in the SEMP). (Ch 2)

**Decision Analysis Process** — the framework for formulating, evaluating, and recording choices among alternatives; rigour scales to consequence, and the report captures criteria, alternatives, uncertainties, recommendation, and **dissent**. (Ch 6)

**Derived requirement** — a requirement generated by the chosen architecture (not present in the baselined input); both baselined and derived requirements are allocated to the architecture. (Ch 3)

**Deterministic vs. risk-informed safety requirement** — a deterministic safety requirement is a hard action/performance threshold (hardware stops, command limits, automatic shutdown); a risk-informed one is set partly from safety TPMs and their uncertainty (e.g., a P(LOC) ceiling at a confidence level). (Ch 3)

**Deviation / Waiver** — documented relief from a requirement; a deviation is sought *before* the requirement bites, a waiver *after*. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Doctrine of Successive Refinement** — design as a spiral of "create concepts" turns, each a pass of the recursive loop, moving from architecture to functional to detailed design at finer resolution. (Ch 3)

**Earned Value (BCWP)** — Budgeted Cost for Work Performed; the pivot of EVM. Schedule variance = BCWP − BCWS; cost variance = BCWP − ACWP. Planned-vs-actual cost alone measures spending only, never performance. (Ch 6)

**End-to-end testing ("test the way we fly")** — the governing V&V edict: assemble the system realistically, apply a realistic environment, and exercise every operational mode; scoped to external interfaces down to the verification-plan level. (Ch 4)

**Engineering/grassroots cost estimate** — a bottom-up cost rollup; accurate but invalidated by every "what-if," so impractical across many trade iterations. (Ch 5)

**Functional baseline** — the approved system/top-level-CI performance-requirements documentation plus the verification to demonstrate them; established at SDR. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Heritage (reuse) trap** — treating reused equipment as mature when it runs in a new architecture/environment; high heritage in a ground application can be low heritage in space. Modification turns the item into a *new design* requiring the full pipeline. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Human Systems Integration (HSI)** — assessing operators/maintainers as a subsystem with a maturity level; early HSI hedges life-cycle-cost surprises (the Shuttle's projected-vs-actual flight rate is the cautionary example). (Ch 3)

**Influence Diagram** — a compact graphical/mathematical representation of a decision state (decision, chance, value nodes); good for formulation and convertible into a decision tree. (Ch 6)

**Interface Control Document/Drawing (ICD)** — the bilateral *design solution* implementing the IRD (no "shall" statements; connectors, electrical/mechanical/environmental detail). (Ch 5)

**Interface Definition Document (IDD)** — a one-sided "ICD" controlled by the end-item provider for an already-fixed interface the other side must design to. (Ch 5)

**Interface Requirements Document (IRD)** — the bilateral "shall" statements at a boundary (functional, physical, performance, environmental), signed by every party. (Ch 5)

**Iterative** — re-running a process on the **same** product to correct a discrepancy or other departure from requirements. (Contrast *recursive*.) (Ch 1)

**Joint Confidence Level (JCL)** — the percentage of Monte Carlo points at or under both a cost and a schedule target, read off a cost-vs-schedule scatterplot; required at KDP C for projects above $250M. (Ch 5)

**Key Decision Point (KDP)** — the gate where the decision authority judges readiness to enter the next phase; passes may carry **liens** (conditional, with a clock). (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Key Driving Requirement (KDR)** — a requirement that can swing cost or schedule heavily; identifying KDRs early locates the cost/schedule leverage. (Ch 3)

**Key Performance Parameter (KPP)** — the minimum set of capabilities most essential to mission success; failing a KPP threshold puts the project in risk status and can prompt reevaluation or termination. (Ch 6)

**Leading indicator** — a measure shown to be *predictive* of future performance/cost/schedule. NPR 7123.1 mandates three: **mass margin, power margin, and RFA/RID/action-item burndown**, reported SRR→SAR to read maturity *and* stability. (Ch 6)

**Measure of Effectiveness (MOE)** — an operational success measure from the **customer's** viewpoint, solution-independent; failing its critical value makes the stakeholder judge the system a failure. (Ch 6)

**Measure of Performance (MOP)** — a physical/functional product attribute from the **supplier's** viewpoint, quantitative; an MOE is validated when its MOPs are achieved. (Ch 6)

**Operations Concept** — the later, narrower description (often by the operational team) of how flight and ground segments work together so the ConOps is reasonable. (Ch 3)

**Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB)** — the time-phased budget baseline for EVM, set in Phase B for KDP C. (Ch 6)

**Product baseline** — the approved technical documentation describing the CI during production/operation (form/fit/function, production acceptance tests); typically established at CDR. (Ch 6)

**Product Breakdown Structure (PBS)** — the tiered product hierarchy; branch length is a *dependency* map, not a timeline, and the lowest feasible decomposition level differs by product. (Ch 1, Ch 5)

**Product Realization** — the bottom-up right side of the SE engine: implementation, integration, verification, validation, transition. (Ch 4)

**Programmatic vs. technical requirement** — programmatic requirements are owned (and waivers approved) by the program/project; technical requirements by the Technical Authority. (Ch 3)

**Protoflight strategy** — qualifying on the flight unit itself (test levels above acceptance but below full qualification) instead of building dedicated qualification hardware — a cost-vs-margin trade. (Ch 4)

**Qualification** — the verification subset run at the environmental-envelope extremes plus margin, once per design, to prove design soundness. (Ch 4)

**Recursive** — re-applying the processes to design the next lower layer, realize the next upper layer, or advance a life-cycle phase. (Contrast *iterative*.) (Ch 1)

**RIDM / CRM** — Risk Management = two processes: **Risk-Informed Decision Making** feeds risk/uncertainty into direction-setting choices; **Continuous Risk Management** drives identified risks to closure against trigger thresholds. (Ch 5)

**Risk scenario / initiating event** — the construction beneath the risk triplet: an initiating event departs from the intended state, mitigating/exacerbating events chain into a scenario, then likelihood and consequence (with uncertainty) attach. (Ch 5)

**SE Engine (NPR 7123.1)** — the 17-process model in three sets (system design, product realization, technical management); processes 10–17 are **crosscutting tools** for executing 1–9, not the next sequential steps. (Ch 1)

**SE/PP&C overlap** — the structural overlap between the systems engineer's remit (technical truth, cost/schedule realism) and Project Planning & Control (programmatic constraints to protect delivery). (Ch 1)

**SEMP (Systems Engineering Management Plan)** — the living plan documenting how NPR 7123.1's practices are addressed; baselines at SRR (project / tightly coupled / single-project program) or MDR/SDR (uncoupled / loosely coupled). (Ch 2, Ch 5)

**Standing Review Board (SRB)** — the external, advisory board that evaluates space-flight KDP/milestone reviews and reports findings without authority over content. (Ch 6)

**Survivability model (threat × susceptibility = vulnerability)** — the aircraft-survivability identity applied to spacecraft/ground infrastructure, feeding a Program/Project Protection Plan iterated across KDPs. (Ch 3)

**Tailoring** — seeking relief from an NPR "shall," consistent with objectives, allowable risk, and constraints; produces deviations/waivers recorded in the Compliance Matrix. (Contrast *customization*.) (Ch 2)

**Technical Data Management (DM)** — controls the information *about* the product; needs managed change authority but not a CR/CCB (unlike CM). Excludes purely business data. (Ch 6)

**Technical Performance Measure (TPM)** — a build-time tracking measure comparing current achievement against the anticipated value; generic (mass, reliability) or unique; reported by planned-profile or margin-management method. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Threshold vs. baseline performance** — threshold = minimum acceptable (below it forces a descope); baseline = desired; the gap is deliberate trade space. Over-tight tolerances destroy solutions and inflate cost. (Ch 3)

**Trade study** — selection of the most suitable option within constraints using a selection rule, attribute measurement, uncertainty ranges, sensitivity analysis, and a final "reality check"; unresolved dissent escalates to the decision-maker. (Ch 6)

**Two-form product transition** — transition is either *up* (a lower-level product to the next integration level) or *out* (the final product to the operational user); the form drives packaging, handling, storage, training, and documentation rigour. (Ch 4)

**Utility Analysis / MAUT** — utility (in *utils*) maps a TPM range onto a preference curve whose curvature encodes risk attitude; Multi-Attribute Utility Theory computes expected utility — best for significant uncertainty with a non-risk-neutral decision-maker. (Ch 6)

**Verification** — formal confirmation (test/analysis/inspection/demonstration) that requirements are met ("built right"); traces to the requirements baseline. Paid once per design. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Validation** — confirmation that the product meets stakeholder expectations in the environment of use ("right thing built"); traces to the ConOps. Often hidden inside "functional"/"engineering" tests that can be **credited** if preplanned and recorded. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Work package / planning package** — under EVM, a cost account decomposes into schedulable, budgeted work packages (summing to BCWS) and not-yet-detailed planning packages (later broken into work packages). (Ch 5)
