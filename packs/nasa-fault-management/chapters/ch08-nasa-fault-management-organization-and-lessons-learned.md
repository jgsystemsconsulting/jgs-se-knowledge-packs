# Chapter 8 — Management Structure and NASA Lessons Learned

> **DRAFT SOURCE.** This chapter synthesizes NASA-HDBK-1002 **Draft 2 (April 2, 2012)**, an unapproved working draft. The organizational guidance (Appendix D) and the mined Lessons Learned (Appendix F) below reflect that draft and may differ from any later released edition. Lesson identifiers and mission references are reproduced as named in the draft; verify each against the current NASA Lessons Learned database before citing in formal work. Treat as guidance, not a controlling standard.

## Core Idea

Fault Management succeeds or fails on **how the project is organized**, not just on how the algorithms are designed. The handbook's Content Guide for Management Structure (Appendix D) makes a single load-bearing argument: because FM cuts across every system element and engineering discipline, it cannot be owned by a single subsystem or bolted onto a system engineer's spare time. It has to be positioned and coordinated **at the system level**, with authority that matches its responsibility, and with formal interfaces running both up-and-down the program hierarchy and sideways to the parallel organizations it depends on (engineering, Safety and Mission Assurance, integration and test, operations).

Two failure surfaces drive the chapter. The first is **structural**: vague programmatic interfaces, an unclear technical authority, and FM scoped beneath the level where it can actually drive design produce coverage gaps, late defect discovery, and cost/schedule overruns. The second is **historical**: the NASA Lessons Learned database (Appendix F), mined for FM-relevant entries, repeatedly shows the same root causes recurring across decades of missions — missing top-down systems engineering, no one continuously asking "what could go wrong," inadequate reviews, untested redundancy paths, and FM responses that misbehave in states nobody analyzed. The management structure exists precisely to stop those lessons from being re-learned the hard way.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Content Guide for Management Structure (Appendix D)** — the handbook's prescriptive guide for organizing a program/project so FM can be designed, developed, tested, and operated successfully. Its scope is explicitly programmatic: roles, responsibilities, interfaces, and authority rather than algorithms.
  - When/how used: consulted during program/project formulation and organization design, to lay down FM technical interfaces (Section 5.1) and FM roles and responsibilities (Section 5.2) before FM technical work ramps.

- **The three organizational success principles** — the handbook distills three principles a sound FM organization must satisfy: (1) an organization's **authority should be sized to its area of concern** (its responsibility); (2) the organization should have **vertical structure and interfaces** (extending to like-concerned organizations above and below it); and (3) the organization should have **horizontal integration** with the interdependent parallel organizations at its own level.
  - When/how used: applied as design criteria when allocating FM authority and wiring up boards, panels, and leadership teams. Sections D.2.1.1–D.2.1.3 elaborate each principle in turn.

- **The three recommended organizational properties** — observed in the handbook's example organization figures (Figure 13, FM roles within a *program* structure; Figure 14, FM roles within a *project* structure) and recommended for future missions: FM is acknowledged as requiring a system-level perspective that spans the engineering, safety/mission-assurance, and operations domains; one **engineering-resident team (or group of teams) is named the focal point** for analyzing, designing, and verifying-and-validating FM; and a **team, board, or panel** seated where it can see across engineering, S&MA, and operations (e.g. the Chief Engineer's Office) coordinates the separate FM areas of concern.
  - When/how used: as a checklist for whether a proposed org chart will actually support cohesive FM.

- **FM lead engineer role definition (Section D.2.2)** — a concrete role specification splitting the FM lead's duties into **Programmatic** (team leadership, cross-team coordination, representing FM in reviews and trades, owning the FM plans, reviewing acquisitions) and **Technical** (leading architecture and trade studies, applying institutional FM guidance, ensuring fault/failure analysis is complete, allocating FM requirements across flight and ground subsystems, and validating off-nominal performance through system-level scenario testing).
  - When/how used: to staff and charter the single point of FM technical leadership within a project; for a system-of-systems, a program lead engineer coordinates across level-2 organizations and the level-3 FM leads.

- **FM Functional Breakdown (Table 2)** — a decomposition of FM work into functional tasks (Analysis, Requirements, Design, Subsystem Coordination, V&V, Subsystem- and System-Level Test, Tool/Technology Development, FM Operations) so the effort can be staffed without assuming one-task-to-one-person.
  - When/how used: to size and assign the FM effort; tasks may be grouped under the FM lead or distributed to other teams depending on program size and project organization.

- **NASA Lessons Learned database mining (Appendix F)** — the handbook treats the public NASA Lessons Learned database as a source, extracting FM-relevant entries and grouping them into thematic categories so practitioners can avoid repeating past mistakes. Entries are presented as direct quotes, each carrying a NASA Lesson number, a mission title, the lesson text, and a recommendation.
  - When/how used: as a curated, categorized backstop during FM design and review — read it to ensure the project is not walking into a known historical trap.

## Key Concepts

- **FM is a crosscutting, system-level discipline.** Because FM depends on the hardware architecture, on software logic, on operational constraints and procedures, and on feedback from S&MA, it cannot live inside any one subsystem or functional component. Particular FM functions can be *assigned* to hardware, to software, to mechanisms, or to operations, but the overall approach and the org topology have to be directed and coordinated at the system level — and FM has to be able to drive the system design so it is built into the architecture rather than patched in.

- **Diffused responsibility is the default — and the problem.** The handbook observes that, across most NASA projects, no one project-level system engineer has held both the responsibility and the authority for FM. Projects tend to handle FM in one of three flawed ways: hand it to existing system engineers as an extra duty (usually to FM's detriment), spin it off as a separate engineering team or subsystem (better attention, but risks isolating FM from the broader SE effort), or push it down to subsystems with no systems-level, engineering-led coordination.

- **Authority must match area of concern.** The organization owning FM should have authority over the full set of system elements to which FM functions might be allocated and over all categories of candidate design solutions. If multiple FM teams exist, they need formal interfaces and documented roles so cognizance is complete and gaps/conflicts/inconsistencies cannot hide in the seams. This is reinforced by recommended practices to grant the FM organization adequate budgetary and resource authority, and to allocate FM resources and staffing early.

- **Vertical interfaces give issues a place to be resolved.** FM design teams should exist at each level, each one seating delegates from the level above and the level below (a system representative joins the subsystem design teams; subsystem representatives join the system-level FM team). Lacking such teams, a concern that rises from a lower level — or one pushed down from a higher level — has nowhere to be worked, which yields inconsistent resolution, overlooked concerns, and eventually gaps and overlaps in the FM that actually ships.

- **Horizontal integration prevents inter-organization seams.** At each level there are parallel organizations with related-but-distinct concerns (engineering, V&V, integration and test, S&MA, operations). They need documented interfaces *and* a coordinating organization — a system board or panel with cognizance over **all** of FM, not a subset — empowered to resolve conflicts between related teams.

- **FM as a standard, tracked engineering element.** A recommended practice is to treat FM as a first-class engineering element within the development process — for instance, giving it its own work breakdown structure beneath SE — so that complexity, cost, and schedule become realistically estimable and measurable. A historical collection of FM-engineering performance measures is needed to make future estimates credible, and a process should exist to *train* personnel into FM engineers.

- **Assess failures top-down, bottom-up, and "middle out."** A Lesson Learned in Appendix D stresses that close engagement with subsystem and S&MA engineers is essential when assessing potential system failures and defining FM requirements. Because many failures arise from interactions among several components — each of which looks "fault-free" alone — a purely bottom-up or heritage-based assessment misses a large fraction of the most dangerous faults. The handbook prescribes combining top-down and bottom-up assessments (engaging subsystem, system, S&MA, and operations engineers) with FM-engineer "middle-out" techniques such as directed-graph analysis and event-sequence failure-scenario analysis.

- **The lessons-learned categories (Appendix F).** The mined lessons are grouped by recurring theme, including: lack of top-down SE introduces risk; projects need a system-level FM engineer who keeps asking "what could possibly go wrong"; a rigorous approach with experienced designers is key for complex missions; FM-related reviews are needed for completeness/consistency/minimum risk; fault responses must handle all states and must not interrupt critical activities; fault-tolerance requirements should be unambiguous; test beds need flight spares; operations knowledge should be maximized and complexity minimized; telemetry must give enough visibility to diagnose anomalies; mission-critical events deserve extra scrutiny; all redundancy combinations must be verified; keep-out zones belong in software; single-point-failure items need scrutiny (especially under late design changes); flight computers must tolerate errors robustly; design out software "deadly embrace"; always use watchdog timers; subject vehicle interfaces to FMEA; and do not enable FM on unused components.

## Mental Models

- **Authority must equal area of concern.** If the FM organization can be held responsible for an element it has no authority over, that gap is where a coverage hole will eventually open. Draw the authority boundary to enclose every element FM functions could ever be allocated to.

- **Vertical + horizontal = no orphaned issues.** Think of the org as a grid: vertical interfaces carry an FM concern up and down the hierarchy; horizontal interfaces carry it across to S&MA, V&V, I&T, and operations. An issue with no vertical *and* no horizontal home is an issue that will be silently dropped — and then surface during system integration at maximum cost.

- **FM should be a line item, not a hidden tax.** Until FM has its own WBS, budget, and tracked performance history, its true cost stays invisible and gets absorbed as overruns elsewhere. Making FM a named, estimated, measured engineering element is what converts a recurring surprise into a managed plan.

- **Plan for the staffing bump, because it is not a surprise — it's a pattern.** FM staffing is routinely under-planned early and explodes during system-level integration and test. The handbook's example: a project planned a flat 0.5 full-time-equivalent for FM and actually peaked above 14 FTE during system-level I&T (Figure 15), with comparable discrepancies seen across many NASA projects. The generally observed pattern is FM starting as one engineer's side job, growing to a full-time role, then needing a whole team for problems, testing, launch, and early checkout. Budget for the curve, not the average.

- **Integration and test is a primary investment, not a place to cut corners.** By design, tests are meant to drive the system toward failure so its response can be observed, and that is what exposes design mistakes; system-level I&T is the best chance to characterize whole-system behavior. Because that phase sits late in the lifecycle, projects routinely cut its corners — and pay for it in late cost and schedule growth from inadequate V&V coverage. Treat I&T as a primary recipient of resources, schedule, and staffing.

- **"What could possibly go wrong?" needs a permanent owner.** Multiple lessons (e.g. Mars Climate Orbiter / ProSEDS, Lesson #1381) converge on the value of a single point of contact whose standing job is to continuously ask that question and to drive formal analyses (FTA, FMEA, FEPP). The question is only useful if someone is accountable for asking it every day, not once at a milestone.

- **History rhymes — read the database first.** The Appendix F categories are not trivia; they are the failure modes that have actually killed or crippled NASA missions. The same root causes (no top-down SE, weak reviews, untested redundancy, state-blind fault responses) recur across Mars Observer, CONTOUR, WIRE, SOHO, Galileo, MRO, Voyager, and more. The cheapest fault analysis available is reading what already went wrong.

## Anti-patterns

The handbook explicitly labels several traps. It names organizational ones as **Pitfalls** and design/operations ones as **Lessons Learned**:

- **Pitfall — poor FM/SE integration yields inadequate oversight.** Where SE plays no meaningful part in FM, FM slips into being an afterthought and system-level reviews miss critical FM issues; having no named FM technical authority compounds the problem. Even with SE engaged, oversight can still be inadequate because FM maturity lags subsystem/component maturity, causing FM reviews to trail the system review milestones.

- **Pitfall — undefined relationships with interdependent organizations.** When the relationships and the binding processes that should tie FM to the other independent organizations are left undefined, the result is trouble in FM design, implementation, and validation. The handbook singles out S&MA: a poorly specified relationship with it — or bringing it in too late — has driven sizeable cost and schedule hits and, at times, an FM design that was simply inadequate.

- **Pitfall — inadequate early, system-level FM consideration causes late cost/schedule growth.** Schedules and V&V plans tend to assume everything goes smoothly, absorbing anomalies in margin; but good testing deliberately provokes failure. Skimping on FM during planning and early development produces insufficient V&V coverage, and fixing that coverage late drives unplanned cost and schedule growth (the FM staffing "bump" is the visible symptom).

- **Lesson Learned — cavalier failure assessment does not lead to success.** One project defined the FM part of the flight system only loosely — leaning on the software architecture while specifying no FM requirements, no fault set, and no system behavior — and wound up with a system too flawed to operate; because there were no requirements to verify against, minor flaws surfaced only late, when correcting them was costly and disruptive.

- **Lessons Learned — recurring historical failure modes (Appendix F).** Among the named ones: **lack of top-down SE** in fault protection (Mars Observer #0345; ISS C&W #1063; CONTOUR #1385), including fault-protection software *never tested on the flight spacecraft before launch*; **state-blind fault responses** that interrupt critical activities (Mars Observer #0343 — fault protection that would have inhibited momentum unloading and prevented stabilization); **ambiguous fault-tolerance requirements** across conflicting documents (CALIPSO #1493); **operations/management-structure breakdowns** (SOHO #0664 — operational errors plus an erroneous decision that disabled onboard autonomous failure detection, compounded by team overload and science-over-safety emphasis; Galileo safing #0391 — a rapid, incorrect "Gyro B faulty" diagnosis that cascaded to loss of attitude control and telemetry); **untested redundancy combinations** (Mars Polar Lander uplink-loss timer #0939); **missing watchdog timers** omitted under resource pressure (#0559, mission terminated when thrusters fired past shutdown); **software "deadly embrace"** (Galileo #0369); **keep-out zones not embedded in software** (MRO articulation #2044); and **FM enabled on unused components** triggering spurious responses (Voyager gyro swap during launch #0409). Each entry in the database is paired with a concrete recommendation aimed at not repeating it.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Position FM at the system level with matching authority.** FM is crosscutting; in most NASA projects no single engineer has had both responsibility and authority for it — and that diffusion is the source of coverage gaps, late defect discovery, and overruns.
2. **Build the org to three principles:** authority matching area of concern, vertical interfaces up and down the hierarchy, and horizontal integration with the parallel organizations FM depends on — plus a coordinating board/panel with cognizance over *all* of FM.
3. **Charter a single FM lead** with explicit programmatic and technical duties, owning the FM plans and the architecture/trade work, and supported by a functional-task breakdown that is staffed deliberately rather than assumed.
4. **Make FM a tracked engineering element** — its own WBS, budget, schedule, and historical performance measures — and plan for the well-documented late-lifecycle staffing bump (the 0.5-FTE-planned-vs-14+-FTE-actual pattern), funding system-level I&T as a primary investment.
5. **Engage S&MA, subsystem, and operations engineers early** and assess failures top-down, bottom-up, *and* middle-out; late or weak S&MA involvement and purely bottom-up/heritage assessment both miss the most dangerous interaction faults.
6. **Mine the Lessons Learned database as a design input.** The same root causes recur across decades — missing top-down SE, weak reviews, untested redundancy, state-blind fault responses, omitted watchdogs — and each historical entry carries a recommendation that is cheaper to adopt than to rediscover.
