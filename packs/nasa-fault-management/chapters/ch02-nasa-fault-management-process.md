# Chapter 2: The Fault Management Process

> **DRAFT SOURCE.** This chapter is built from NASA-HDBK-1002 **Draft 2 (April 2, 2012)**, which every page marks as a draft. Treat the activity definitions, work-product names, and review labels below as draft guidance, not a released NASA standard. Verify against the final issued Handbook before citing in formal work.

## Core Idea
Fault Management (FM) is run as a discipline *inside* the systems-engineering (SE) lifecycle, not as a separate or late add-on. The Handbook's Section 4 lays out a standard FM process spanning six activities — conceptual design development, requirements development, architecture and design, assessment and analysis, verification and validation, and operations and maintenance — each mapped onto NASA mission phases (Pre-A through E) and tied to specific reviews and work products. The governing principle is that FM **matures in parallel** with the nominal system and subsystems; waiting until the system is defined and bolting FM on afterward produces brittle, hard-to-test designs.

  - When/how: use it as the artifact checklist; note that not every product is made for every mission — risk posture drives which apply.

## Key Concepts — the six activities

1. **Conceptual Design Development (Pre-A / early A).** The first step is to define the FM **boundary/scope** so the size and complexity of the FM system matches the mission's available resources and risk posture. This boundary lives in the **FM Concept Document**, which also carries the FM design principles, the FM ConOps, and gets project-management buy-in. All FM requirements should trace cleanly back to the mission concept and risk assumptions stated here. The Handbook calls out four design-principle areas the Concept Document should cover:
   - *Unique mission design characteristics* — what must be done differently from past missions, plus the mission's risk category, so FM provides all required and no unnecessary risk mitigation.
   - *Critical events (mission-critical activities)* — planned events (launch, orbit insertion, flybys, docking) or failure conditions needing a timely response; these usually demand a **fail-operational** response rather than fail-safe, and they drive the FM design because FM must survive them.
   - *Redundancy philosophy* — hardware, functional/analytic, and information redundancy are all considered. Missions with space payloads must follow **NPR 8705.4 (Risk Classification for NASA Payloads)**, which guides single-point-failure acceptability but leaves implementation to the team (fully redundant, selectively redundant, or single-string; and the level of cross-strapping).
   - *Safing strategy* — described as perhaps the most important risk-reduction decision the FM lead makes. Safing is a deliberate goal change from the current mission goal to a degraded set of goals to preserve assets; it may be autonomous, ground-initiated, or crew-initiated. The strategy must state the safe modes and objectives, which failure causes lead to safing, how safing covers all identifiable situations (even ones no fault can be hypothesized for), and that safety is not compromised by the same fault that triggered safe-mode entry.
   - New technology found during this activity is captured in the **FM Technology Plan/Assessment**, including a plan to mature it to **TRL 6 by PDR** and a fallback if it does not.

2. **Requirements Development (begins phase A, complete before CDR).** Draws from mission requirements (derived by the Mission System Engineer) and S&MA requirements. Captured in the **FM Requirements Document** as clear mission-level requirements allocated down to systems (flight, ground, payload, launch vehicle) and subsystems (hardware, software, mission operations, crew). Requirements should **not** be one-to-one with FMEA faults; instead they collapse many faults into a smaller "failure symptom set" and into the functions that must be preserved. The Handbook stresses building a system-wide **"safety net"** that catches situations the failure modes and effects analyses miss — sourced from scenario analysis, operational-mode development, engineering judgment, and lessons learned. Allocation can be whole or partial; doing it too late forfeits the chance to apply prior FM experience well.

3. **Architecture and Design (phase B preliminary; phase C detailed; final before CDR).** Preliminary design exposes interacting/interfering requirements and helps define the mission ConOps, using timelines, state-transition diagrams, or event-sequence diagrams (the latter common in PRA scenario work) to force teams to reason about how the system reacts to faults during critical events. The output is the **FM Architecture Document**, defining how all allocated FM responsibilities combine into a complete system; hardware architecture should be pinned down here because it is hard to change after phase B. Detailed design (phase C) is captured in the **FM Design Specification / FM Design Document**, growing from high-level interactions to detailed detection-isolation-reconfiguration schemes. Because FM is a **distributed function** implemented mostly by teams outside the FM team, the FM documentation takes precedence and subsystem specs must reference it. The FM lead performs active **oversight** of allocated implementations — questioning them, understanding ramifications on the system-level design, and hunting for hazardous subsystem-to-system interactions.

4. **Assessment and Analysis (phases A–C, continually refined).** The toolkit that answers the four fundamental FM questions. The Handbook lists the analysis inputs: **FMEA/FMECA**, **PRA**, **FTA**, **Event Sequence Diagrams / Event Tree Analysis**, **Hazard Analysis**, **FCR Analysis** (fault/failure containment regions), **Failure Response Analysis**, **FEPP Analysis** (failure-effect propagation paths), **Failure Detection and Isolation Analysis**, and **Failure Prognostics Analysis**. Outputs feed trade studies and update the Concept, Architecture, Requirements, and Design documents. Modeling system functions in a top-level fault tree or success tree lets the practitioner show which risks FM mitigates — and, just as importantly, which it cannot — to inform the chief engineer and project manager.

5. **Verification and Validation (starts early phase B, runs through phase D).** Governed by the **FM V&V Plan**, which sets the approach, risk posture, test planning, simulator/test-bed development, and required fidelity. A key element is the **FM Incompressible Test List** — the agreed set of tests that must pass before launch, validating core FM functionality through realistic scenarios at the highest level of integration (and noting this is the FM portion of the overall project incompressible test list). Supporting artifacts include the **FM Verification Matrix** (verification method per requirement, who is responsible, in which environment) and the **FM Validation Matrix** (failure scenarios and their validation methods). Test/demonstration follows the **"test what you fly, fly what you test"** best practice; results drive retest/regression decisions, test reports, and any necessary **requirement waivers**.

6. **Operations and Maintenance (start as early as possible; phases B–E).** FM allocations to operations should be planned early, not handed "over the fence." The FM lead works with operations staff from phase A through C to enumerate operational constraints and develop **contingency procedures** (owned by operations, with heavy FM input) — line-by-line procedures for off-nominal events. Phase D completes the operations portion of the design and produces the **FM Operations Plan** (configuration, check-out, recovery from safe modes, post-failure diagnostics, operational constraints, maintenance/repair). In phase E the FM lead supports on-orbit anomaly diagnosis and gradually transfers FM oversight from the FM team to the operations team.

## Mental Models
- **Parallel maturation, not post-facto bolt-on.** FM grows alongside the nominal system. If it lags and is added after the system is designed, it becomes an added-on capability — brittle and hard to test — instead of an integral part of the system.
- **Risk posture is the sizing dial.** The mission's risk classification (per NPR 8705.4) drives FM complexity, formality, and therefore cost and schedule — and decides which of the Table 10 work products are even produced.
- **Functions preserved, not faults enumerated.** Requirements and safing target the system *functions that must survive*, framed so they handle faults that were never explicitly hypothesized — the "safety net" idea rather than a one-to-one mapping from the FMEA.
- **FM is a distributed function with a single authoritative voice.** Most FM requirements are implemented by other teams, so the FM Requirements Document and Design Specification take precedence, and subsystem documents reference them — duplication is tolerated, contradiction is not.
- **Evidence drives the picture of risk.** The fault-tree/success-tree model shows both what FM covers and what it cannot cover, so leadership sees the residual risk explicitly.
- **Whole-lifecycle cost, not near-term milestones.** A design that is cheaper in phases B–C but hard to operate can blow up phase D–E costs through fragile procedures; operability must flow back into the design.

## Anti-patterns
The Handbook names these as **Pitfalls / Lessons Learned** (paraphrased):
- **No guiding FM philosophy.** Projects fail to define and communicate mission attributes, concepts, and function-preservation goals, so lower-level FM decisions are made without a unifying context.
- **Inheriting FM from a mismatched-complexity mission.** Reusing a heritage system from a more complex mission below the architecture level can import unnecessary complexity, inflate analysis/test time, and add verification burden to prove unwanted paths are truly removed.
- **Under-resourced test assets and fidelity.** Failing to plan enough test platforms and adequate fidelity early; by the time the shortfall is found at FM testing, it may be too late to avoid major schedule slip and cost growth. (Suggestion: build extra test assets and maximize fidelity for the FM test "hump.")
- **Lack of top-down SE in FM design.** Lesson Learned #0345 (Mars Observer) — fault protection was mostly low-level redundancy management and was never tested on the flight spacecraft before launch. Lesson Learned #1063 (ISS caution-and-warning) — absent SE forced a high-priority review to define an integrated alarm/assessment/countermeasure path.
- **Ignoring cost-benefit of FM infrastructure.** Scoping only to meet requirements and skipping infrastructure/flexibility investment that would save large downstream cost (while ensuring flexibility does not degrade mission success or become untestable).
- **"Absorbing the impact."** When FM is not driving design decisions, changes elsewhere force FM to compensate, undermining the design and making it hard to test and operate.
- **Deferring functionality to operations.** Pushing late or costly requirements onto operations without a lifecycle trade; table/script implementations are flexible but invite "always add it later" deferral.
- **Budget and schedule deficiencies.** Failing to budget for documentation, reviews, and spacecraft-level test prep, so FM lags the rest of the design.

## Key Takeaways
1. The FM process has **six activities** — conceptual design, requirements, architecture & design, assessment & analysis, V&V, and operations & maintenance — run inside the SE process across mission phases Pre-A to E.
2. **FM matures in parallel** with the system; post-facto FM is brittle and hard to test.
3. The **FM Concept Document** sets the FM boundary and the four design-principle areas: unique mission characteristics, critical events, redundancy philosophy, and **safing strategy** (the most important risk-reduction decision).
4. Requirements target **preserved functions and a "safety net,"** not a one-to-one map of FMEA faults; new technology must reach **TRL 6 by PDR**.
5. **FM is a distributed function**, so the FM Requirements Document and Design Specification are authoritative and subsystem docs reference them; the FM lead performs active **oversight**.
6. Assessment answers four questions — *what can go wrong, how often, what happens, what can be done* — using FMEA/FMECA, PRA, FTA, ESD/ETA, hazard, FCR, FEPP, detection/isolation, and prognostics analyses.
7. V&V is anchored by the **FM V&V Plan** and the **Incompressible Test List**, follows **"test what you fly, fly what you test,"** and uses verification/validation matrices.
8. Operations allocations start early; the **FM Operations Plan** and **contingency procedures** carry FM into phases D and E, with oversight handed to the operations team.
9. **Risk posture (NPR 8705.4)** drives FM complexity, formality, cost, schedule, and which Table 10 work products apply.
