# Chapter 7 — FM Fundamental Concepts and Guiding Principles

> **DRAFT SOURCE.** This chapter synthesizes Appendix C of NASA-HDBK-1002 **Draft 2 (April 2, 2012)**, an unapproved working draft. The definitions, the five-strategy taxonomy, the FM function set, and the eight guiding principles below reflect that draft and may differ from any later released or approved edition. Treat as guidance, not a controlling standard, and verify against the current NASA standard before citing in formal work.

## Core Idea

Fault Management addresses a system's off-nominal states, and the handbook's foundational claim is that this work must proceed **in parallel with the nominal system design**, not after it. The logic is straightforward: systems engineers define the functions a mission needs; each function is then examined to judge whether its risk of failure is acceptable given how it is built; and wherever that risk is unacceptable, FM engineers add capabilities to preserve the function, recover it, or fall back to an alternate goal that needs the function less (or not at all). Every function therefore has a "light side" of expected behavior and a "dark side" of potential failures, and a complete design treats both together.

The handbook grounds FM in two parent disciplines. From **systems theory and systems engineering**, it inherits the view that flight, ground, and operations form a web of interacting parts whose relationships can be analyzed — necessary because a failure in one element can propagate into seemingly unrelated elements and produce unexpected emergent behavior. From **control theory**, it inherits the framing of active failure management — detection, isolation, mitigation — as a problem of estimation and control. The rest of the chapter builds out the vocabulary, the operational functions, and the guiding principles that follow from that dual heritage.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **The Five FM Strategies (Figure 10, "FM Strategies"; Section C.1).** The handbook reduces all risk mitigation for a mission function to one of five strategies, split into two families.
  - *Failure prevention* (failures are stopped from occurring) contains two: **Design-Time Fault Avoidance** — building the function and its FM with stricter quality assurance, higher-grade parts, or more margin so a fault is unlikely; and **Operational Failure Avoidance** — predicting a future failure and acting to forestall it through repair, replacement, or operational change.
  - *Failure tolerance* (failures occur, but their effects are handled) contains three: **Failure Masking** — letting a low-level failure happen but blocking its effect from reaching the higher-level function; **Failure Recovery** — letting a failure briefly compromise the function, then responding and restoring it before a mission goal is lost; and **Goal Change** — letting the failure stand and shifting to new, usually degraded goals that remain achievable.
  - When/how used: the practitioner selects among these per failure mode and per mission phase across the full lifecycle, weighing required functionality, available resources, and accepted risk posture.

- **The Operational FM Functions (Figure 12, "Operational FM Functions"; Section C.3).** A set of functions active during the operational phase whose collective aim is to preserve as much functionality as possible. The set comprises **Detection**, **Diagnosis** (itself splitting into fault isolation and fault identification), **Decision**, **Response** (itself splitting into goal change, failure recovery, and failure masking), and **Model Adjustment**. The handbook notes that prognosis is deliberately *not* in this diagram and is not addressed in the draft. Not every function fires for every failure — a response can begin before the cause is ever pinned down.
  - When/how used: these are the run-time building blocks; a single mechanism may implement several of them at once (the handbook's example is a triple-modular-redundancy voter, which performs detection, isolation, response determination, and recovery in one stroke).

- **The Four Redundancy Approaches (Section C.2.5).** Redundancy is decomposed into four kinds, each matched to different fault classes: **hardware-identical**, **functional** (also called dissimilar or analytic), **informational**, and **temporal**. The taxonomy is introduced alongside a matching three-way classification of fault origins — design mistakes become common-mode/systemic faults, manufacturing mistakes become random part failures, and operations mistakes become operator error.
  - When/how used: each approach is selected against the fault type it actually counters, and the full FM analysis must justify the approach, its limits, and the mechanism that controls the redundancy.

- **The Eight Guiding Principles (Section C.4).** The handbook closes the appendix with eight numbered principles, each stated as a Principle plus Commentary: **Crosscutting FM Interfaces**, **FM Development as Part of Systems Engineering**, **System Boundary**, **Function Preservation**, **Asset Preservation**, **Risk Reduction**, **Design Mechanism Allocation**, and **Tailoring Redundancy**. These translate the conceptual vocabulary into design stances.
  - When/how used: applied throughout FM development to keep the discipline system-level, parallel to nominal design, and tuned to mission risk posture.

## Key Concepts

- **Failure, fault, and the cause/effect relativity.** A **failure** is the unacceptable performance of an intended function — by definition an *effect*, because performance is judged through observable, predictable states and behaviors. A **fault** is an internal cause of failure. The two are bound as cause and effect, but the handbook stresses these terms are *relative*: what is a cause from one viewpoint is an effect needing deeper explanation from another. A **root cause** sits at the head of a failure's event chain, while a **proximate cause** is the final causal step immediately preceding the failure itself. Several interacting root causes or proximate causes can combine to produce one failure (Figure 11, "Fault/Failure Event Chain"). **Contributing factors** neither represent unacceptable performance themselves nor are root causes, but they let a fault occur, make it more likely, or worsen its consequences.

- **Anomaly.** An **anomaly** is the *unexpected* performance of an intended function — distinct from a failure. Failures can be non-anomalous (the planned depletion of an expendable like a cryogenic cooler), and anomalies can be non-failures (an odd power signature that costs no functionality). Failures are FM's primary focus, but anomalies matter because they can predict future faults (a rising-but-in-range temperature nearing its limit) or trigger faults elsewhere (heat from one component overheating its neighbor). Anomalies that strike the FM functions themselves should be identified and mitigated, since FM should ideally stay independent and operational through any anomaly.

- **System and environment.** The **system boundary** marks the bound of responsibility beyond which the team need not control faults; outside lies the **environment**, which the system cannot change but must operate within. The boundary must enclose all hardware, software, procedures, and personnel needed to implement, test, and operate the mission. A subsystem owner may legitimately draw a narrower boundary at their subsystem, but the system-level FM designer must set the boundary to capture every mechanism performing FM functions. The designer also documents boundary *conditions* — physical environment, accepted risk posture, and operating constraints (e.g., time delays forcing autonomy) — because that record underpins requirements and helps contain late cost growth.

- **States, behaviors, and measurement uncertainty.** A **state** captures, at one instant, what the system's physical and logical variables are holding; **behavior** is how those states evolve over time. Detection works by comparing observed states/behaviors against expected ones drawn from formal or informal models. Detection can monitor a single-snapshot state (inspecting a photo for a crack needs no persistence counter) or, less commonly, the behavior over time (real-time systems often use a "three-strike" or persistence counter to reject false positives). Critically, **FM never observes true states directly** — every measurement carries imprecision in both value and time, and can be corrupted by failure, which is why redundant measurements are used to judge a reading's validity.

- **The four redundancies in detail.** *Hardware-identical* redundancy counters random part failures and lifetime limits (five reaction wheels where three suffice) but is powerless against a common-cause flaw shared by all strings. *Functional* redundancy uses dissimilar hardware, software, or procedures for the same function — for detection (cross-checking related parameters like voltage, current, resistance), for prevention (independent arm-and-fire mechanisms), or as a workaround (a thruster substituting for a failed reaction wheel). *Informational* redundancy adds extra data, classically error-detection-and-correction codes (EDAC) that reconstruct a message after a single-event upset flips bits. *Temporal* redundancy repeats a function across time — repeated measurements of one state variable, or computer checkpoint-rollback.

- **Failure effect propagation, FEPPs, and containment.** Failure effects spread from the fault (the FMEA "failure mode") along **failure effect propagation paths (FEPPs)**, and the physics can transform along the way — a stuck valve changes pressure, which alters fluid temperature, which throws off turbine speed, which corrupts generated power, which disables dependent avionics, and so on. A single fault can thus yield many effects with non-obvious timing. The point where a failure stops spreading is a **failure containment boundary**; a set of such boundaries defines a **failure containment region (FCR)** or zone. Placing these boundaries and defining FCRs is described as a key aspect of designing FM architecture. The handbook notes FEPP analysis has historically been underdeveloped, that FMEAs and fault tree analyses have significant gaps, and that formal methods such as directed graph modeling show promise for closing them.

- **Criticality and timing: CFE, TTC, and the race condition.** A **Critical Failure Effect (CFE)** is a failure effect that, once it occurs, irrevocably compromises one or more system or mission objectives. **Time to Criticality (TTC)** is how long the effects take, travelling the FEPPs out from the originating failure mode, to reach the CFE. FM is only effective if its responses finish *within* the TTC — making FM a **race condition** between FDIR latencies and the propagation of effects. The designer's goal is to place the containment boundary far enough upstream that the response completes before the CFE. TTC is keyed to the intermediate effect that seals the mission's fate, not the eventual visible failure (a slow propellant leak's CFE is "when too little propellant remains to meet objectives," not the later science phase). When several CFEs apply, the relevant one is whichever the effects reach soonest. Many latencies must be summed against TTC: sensor lag, transmission delay, processing cycles, detection-counter delays, decision time, and response execution time. Where humans perform FM, they are part of the mechanism and their latency counts too.

- **Fault/failure tolerance, fail-operational, and fail-safe.** **Fault tolerance** and **failure tolerance** are synonyms: the ability to perform a function despite a specified number of coincident, independent failure causes of specified types — both a count of causes and the toleration of their effects. **Fail-operational** means no loss of functionality after the specified failures; **fail-safe** means a critical subset of functions is preserved. A tolerance mechanism is valid *only against certain fault classes* — a triplex voter handles random part failures but not common-mode ones — so specifying tolerance without naming what is tolerated is incomplete and potentially dangerous. Tolerance and prevention are recursive and hierarchical: tolerance near the cause can enable prevention further downstream. The locations of tolerance mechanisms generally define FCR boundaries.

- **Automation versus autonomy.** **Automation** is about *who* implements a function — machines versus humans (fully automated, not automated, or partly automated). **Autonomy** is about the *locus of control*: the relative placement and scope of decision-making between two locations, defined with respect to a reference point. The classic comparison is flight segment versus ground segment — more decision authority in flight means more autonomy from the ground; one can equally assess a vehicle's autonomy from its own crew when the crew cannot react fast enough. FM can be designed at varying automation and autonomy levels, traded against flight/ground complexity, resource availability, response-time needs, and risk posture.

- **The five operational FM functions, defined.** *Detection* decides a failure or anomaly exists, typically via three sub-functions — a threshold separating acceptable from unacceptable, a persistence/filtering check against false positives, and notification to other functions. *Diagnosis* composes **fault isolation** (locating the causal mechanism) and **fault identification/characterization** (determining possible causes), both measured through **ambiguity groups** — sets of indistinguishable candidate components given the available detections. *Decision* determines the correct response: assessing functional compromise and propagation, finding response options and outcomes, selecting responses, and notifying the system to act; when automated, the decision is often pre-baked at design time so a detection immediately triggers a response. *Response* covers **goal change** (altering current goals, typically safing), **failure recovery** (restoring functions for existing or redefined goals, possibly including maintenance actions like a launch scrub), and **failure masking** (hiding effects from the rest of the system, forming an FCR boundary dynamically, as a voter outvoting a failed unit). *Model adjustment* updates the models on which expectations rest as the system's real behavior is learned, most obviously after an anomaly is diagnosed and reclassified as normal, degraded, or failed.

## Mental Models

- **Every function has a dark side.** The handbook's organizing image (Figure 9): the nominal "light side" of expected behavior and the off-nominal "dark side" of potential failures belong to the same function and must be engineered together. Designing FM after the fact leaves it "painted on" rather than "dyed in."

- **Cause and effect are a point of view, not an absolute.** The same event is a cause to those above it in the chain and an effect needing explanation to those below — the handbook's Columbia walkthrough shows the orbiter breakup, wing weakening, and foam strike each playing both roles. Anchoring *failure = effect* and *fault = cause* in terminology is the only defense against this relativity.

- **FM is a race, not a checklist.** Having a response is worthless if it finishes after the function is already lost. The mental shift is from "do we have a response?" to "does the whole detection-diagnosis-decision-response loop, including human latency, beat the TTC to the CFE?"

- **Tolerance is always tolerance-of-something.** No redundancy or tolerance mechanism is universal. Always pair the mechanism with the specific fault class it counters; an unqualified "single fault tolerant" claim invites false confidence.

- **Worst-case is not always worst.** The intuitively severe case can be the easy-to-detect one. A large propellant leak is obvious; a small leak may quietly drain more propellant before detection — so "worst case" must be reasoned about, not assumed.

- **Put the operator inside the box.** Humans who perform detection, decision, or response are part of the FM system, not external to it. Their judgment, their redundancy contribution, and their latency all belong inside the boundary and inside the analysis.

- **Don't protect the protection into oblivion.** Each FM mechanism adds its own failure modes and paths. The model is a net trade: FM is only worth adding when its risk reduction outweighs the complexity and new failure paths it introduces.

## Anti-patterns

The handbook calls these out explicitly as **Pitfalls** within this appendix:

- **Failure to Consider Alternatives.** Fixating on one failure type (e.g., random part failure) or one strategy (e.g., design-time fault avoidance) ignores that different strategies suit different failure modes, mission types, and lifecycle phases. Strategy and architecture selection must weigh the full lifecycle, required functionality, available resources, and risk posture.

- **Contradictory Use of Fault and Failure in Requirements.** Because cause and effect are relative, requirements written without disciplined use of "fault" (cause) and "failure" (effect) breed conflicting interpretations and can seed latent design faults that surface catastrophically in operations. The practitioner must use the terms consistently, per the handbook's definitions.

- **Worst Case Analysis.** A nominal worst-case analysis may miss the true worst case — the smaller, harder-to-detect fault that does more damage before being caught (the propellant-leak example).

- **FM Performance.** The designer must not only design but *prove* that detection, diagnosis, and response run faster than the TTC; if the loop cannot be made fast enough, the relative probabilities of FM success and failure against each effect must be determined.

- **Normalization of Deviance.** Anomalies repeatedly tolerated can drift into being treated as expected behavior, breeding complacency and masking danger signals — cited as a factor in both the Challenger and Columbia accidents. Model adjustment must be done with care precisely to avoid this.

- **Heritage FM Systems.** FM is not one-size-fits-all. Inheriting a heritage FM design without checking its applicability — at every level of concept, architecture, design, implementation, and operations — is a trap, because mission complexity and risk posture vary enormously (a 15-year flagship versus a 1-year LEO Explorer).

- **Random Part Failure Bias.** Databases attribute roughly 80–90 percent of system failures to human faults, yet designers tend to over-focus on hardware random part failure. FM scope must explicitly include software design and operational faults as likely failure causes.

## Key Takeaways

1. **FM runs parallel to nominal design.** Each function carries a failure space alongside its expected behavior, and FM is designed to be "dyed in," not "painted on" after the fact.
2. **Five strategies, two families.** Failure prevention (design-time fault avoidance, operational failure avoidance) and failure tolerance (failure masking, failure recovery, goal change) span the choices; selection is per failure mode, per phase, and per risk posture.
3. **Fault = cause, failure = effect, and the relativity bites.** Disciplined, consistent terminology is the only guard against contradictory requirements and latent design faults.
4. **Timing is the heart of FM.** Effects propagate along FEPPs toward a Critical Failure Effect; the FM loop must beat the Time to Criticality, counting every latency including human ones.
5. **Tolerance is fault-class-specific.** A mechanism valid against one fault class is not valid against all; redundancy must be tailored and modeled against the specific faults it counters.
6. **Five operational functions** — detection, diagnosis, decision, response, and model adjustment — preserve functionality at run time, and one mechanism can implement several at once.
7. **Eight guiding principles** keep FM system-level: crosscutting interfaces, development inside SE, a properly drawn system boundary, function and asset preservation, net risk reduction, mechanism allocation across hardware/software/operations, and tailored redundancy.
8. **The boundary must include the operators.** A common failure is leaving mission operators or crew "outside the system" when they in fact perform essential FM functions.
