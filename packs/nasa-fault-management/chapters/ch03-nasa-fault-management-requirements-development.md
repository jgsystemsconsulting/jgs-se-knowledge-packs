# Chapter 3: Fault Management Requirements Development

> **DRAFT SOURCE NOTICE.** This chapter is synthesized from NASA-HDBK-1002 **Draft 2 (April 2, 2012)**, which is itself an unreleased working draft. Treat every recommendation, table, and example below as provisional guidance, not as a ratified Agency standard. Verify against the current released handbook before citing in formal work.

## Core Idea
A project is no better than its fault-management (FM) requirements. Because FM is an immature discipline with historically inconsistent assumptions, vague top-level FM requirements are uniquely dangerous: they force lower subsystems to invent their own assumptions, fragment interpretation across the flow-down, and produce omissions that surface only in late integration. The handbook's thesis is that **explicit, detailed, centrally-derived FM requirements** — traced to the mission concept and risk posture — buy a clean flow-down, fewer design iterations, fewer disconnects, a more efficient V&V process, and ultimately lower life-cycle cost with higher availability and robustness. The existence of an appropriately broad FM requirement set is itself evidence that the necessary preliminary-design and ConOps thinking has been done; its absence signals under-explored design and analysis.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Five-step process for writing FM requirements** (§5.1) — *Know the Mission* → *Consider Heritage* → *Review All Categories* → *Determine Completeness* → run the *FM Requirements Checklist*.
  - When to use: as the standing procedure whenever an FM requirement set is being authored or assessed. Start by establishing mission class, duration, critical events, and autonomous (no-ground-contact) periods, then sweep categories, hunt gaps against the system design, and check each requirement.
- **FM Requirements Checklist** (§5.1.5) — eleven quality dimensions adapted from the NASA SE Handbook's "How to Write a Good Requirement," repopulated with FM-specific content: Clarity, Completeness, Compliance, Consistency, Traceability, Correctness, Functionality, Performance, Interfaces, Maintainability, Verifiability.
  - When to use: after a draft requirement set exists, to validate each statement before baselining.
- **Table 11 — FM Requirement Categories** (§5.2) — a category/sub-category taxonomy spanning Functions, Performance, Design, and Process scopes (robustness, environmental tolerance, autonomous recovery, fail-operational, fail-safe, fault/failure tolerance, allocation of FM functions, detection, diagnosis, isolation, mitigation, notification, false negatives/positives, availability, consumables, do-no-harm, operability, response time, safe mode, fault containment regions, time to criticality, parameter determination, analysis, operations, testing).
  - When to use: to drive a completeness checklist and expand candidate requirements until coverage is demonstrably broad.
- **Table 12 — FM Mission Classes and Requirement Considerations** (§5.3) — maps Mission Classes A–D to fault-tolerance posture (A: flagship, single-fault tolerant with full redundancy and extensive FDIR; D: medium-to-high risk, minimal tolerance, single-string).
  - When to use: to set the failure-tolerance ambition consistent with the mission's risk tolerance.
- **Table 13 — FM Driving Requirement Areas and Examples** (§5.3) — the small subset of requirements that completely drive the system, FM architecture, hardware, software, and operations, with class-tagged example statements (no single permanent fault plus a recoverable fault shall cause loss of mission; fail-safe outside critical events / fail-operational during them; survive a single failure without ground assistance; availability stated as a minimum uptime percent).
  - When to use: to identify and lock the few architecture-shaping requirements before lower-level derivation.
- **Test Platform Requirements taxonomy** (§5.3.1) — four test-bed requirement categories: Fault Injection, FM Functional Fidelity, FM Timing Fidelity, and FM Test Automation, each with a concept and an example "shall" statement.
  - When to use: from early phase B, supplied via the FM Verification Matrix, to set test-bed fidelity, ownership, configuration management, and availability.
- **Two top-level requirement strategies — "in-house" vs. "out-of-house"** (§5.3, §5.4) — co-located in-house teams with shared design principles may use fewer, more general requirements; subcontracted (out-of-house) work needs detailed requirement and interface-requirement documents (IRDs) with minimum ambiguity.
  - When to use: when deciding requirement granularity; if unsure, default to the out-of-house minimum-ambiguity strategy.
- **Bottom-up / mission-independent safety requirements** (§5.4.1) — define a minimal mission-independent safety requirement set so subsystem development can proceed before mission-level FM requirements are finished (cited examples: ISS SSP 50038B; JWST per-instrument and per-bus minimum safety requirements).
  - When to use: when subsystem hardware/software development must start ahead of finalized top-down FM requirements.

## Key Concepts
- **Explicit vs. implicit/general requirements.** Implicit or general requirements invite divergent subsystem interpretations, mismatched expectations, and missed functionality. Explicit requirements are the recommended default precisely because they survive flow-down intact.
- **"Failure causes" vs. "faults" wording.** If a requirement covers all causes of failure, say "failure causes"; reserve "fault" for internally-caused conditions. Word choice carries scope.
- **One concept per requirement.** Combination requirements are hard to decompose at verification time, so keep a single concept per statement and capture rationale (often lost in requirements tools) and inline definitions for terms like "critical units."
- **System-level requirements stay implementation-free — with exceptions.** Generally exclude implementation and operations specifics, but a few core FM choices (e.g., whether a separate safe-mode computer exists, or whether redundancy is "two of everything" vs. internally-redundant units) legitimately need top-down clarification.
- **Needs vs. wants and the "worst-assumption" test.** For each candidate requirement ask whether, if it were absent, the design would still work under worst-case assumptions. Many FM requirements look extraneous yet prevent drastically different and unwanted design conclusions.
- **Tolerance interrogation.** For thresholds, persistence, and tolerances, ask what the worst outcome would be if the value were doubled or tripled, and again if it were halved or cut to a tenth — to test that tolerances are defendable and cost-effective.
- **Minimum FM functions to cover.** The functionality dimension of the checklist demands coverage of four functions at the floor: detecting failures, isolating (locating) faults, containing them, and responding.
- **Driving requirements are the leverage points.** Out of a broad set, a handful of fault-tolerance, fail-safe/fail-operational, and time-to-criticality (TTC) requirements drive the entire architecture; FM mitigations must operate faster than the TTC of each protected function.
- **Possibility over probability.** Because low-probability and unconsidered items are disproportionately the ones that fail in flight, and because probability estimates (and their uncertainties) are routinely underestimated, the handbook recommends treating all electrical faults and all protectable mechanical faults as credible and protecting core health-and-safety functions regardless of computed probability. (Constellation background: LOC/LOM probability metrics used to choose which faults to monitor produced design holes.)
- **Flow-down and centralization.** One FM lead should own a single driving requirements document that flows cleanly to lower levels; minimize parroting (restating a requirement without adding justifying detail), minimize overlapping requirements (find the limiting constraint), and pay special attention to flowing FM requirements to sub-contractors.
- **Requirement counts as a sanity signal (Table 14).** Sample counts from recent NASA projects show higher mission classes and more-subcontracted projects tend to carry more FM requirements; the point is not the exact number but that more requirements usually track more detailed phase-specific scenario and robustness investigation.

## Mental Models
- **Requirements are a contract against silent over- and under-design.** Without explicit limits, an implementer can declare an open-ended design "finished" while it is substandard — or, conversely, gold-plate a low-class mission into needless complexity. Specific limits bound both failure modes.
- **Centralized top-down beats inherited bottom-up.** Heritage requirements imported without re-analysis tend to be ill-fitted; a purely bottom-up, distributed requirement set tends toward disorganization, hard V&V, and performance shortfalls. Derive top-down from the mission's risk posture and unique objectives.
- **Write the V&V venue while you write the requirement.** Documenting how and where each FM requirement will be verified, as it is authored, avoids generating the V&V matrix from scratch downstream — a major hidden cost of vague requirements.
- **A safety net has limits.** It can be appropriate to skip detecting some low-level complex failure modes if a demonstrated higher-level safety net covers them — but nets can get confused, especially under multiple or cascading failures, so give up lower-level protection cautiously.
- **Precision in language is design control.** Ambiguous terms ("single fault tolerance" with no agreed interpretation) created real project friction and contract renegotiation; inline definitions or an enforced project glossary are the remedy.
- **Carry project- or phase-specific specifics down deliberately.** Large programs with diverse subprojects can disambiguate by carrying either project-specific tolerance (Project A single-fault, Project B two-fault) or phase-specific tolerance (Phase A single-fault, Phase B two-fault) down from the top-level document.

## Anti-patterns
The handbook names these explicitly as Pitfalls:
- **Too-general / implicit requirements** ("protect against faults," "do FM"). Causes open-ended designs that can be declared done while substandard, inadequate verification with the V&V burden dumped onto validation, overlooked core driving requirements, and inconsistent subsystem designs caught only in costly system test. A *vague* requirement (more detailed than "general" but admitting many valid interpretations) is a related trap.
- **Too-specific requirements** ("have a monitor X with response Y" at the top level). Design realities shift and break over-specified requirements, flow-down degenerates into cut-and-paste, and an over-rigid design will not fit cleanly with existing heritage code and hardware.
- **Uncritical heritage reuse.** Inheriting FM requirements without re-analysis yields requirements ill-fitted to the new mission, even when hardware/software heritage is real.
- **Requirements-formulation incompleteness.** Warning signs: fewer top-level FM requirements than comparable programs, FM requirements scattered across many documents, vague/abstract statements, insufficiently detailed lower-level requirements, or FM functions not all addressed.
- **Requirement-interpretation divergence across institutions.** Different tacit assumptions about FM scope (probabilistic vs. possibility-based) produce conflicting readings of "single fault tolerance," especially when multiple institutions share FM responsibility; contractors often assume a simple safing response suffices, triggering renegotiation when the customer expected fail-operational capability.
- **Selecting faults by probability.** Produces a system less robust than expected because low-probability items fail in flight and probability values/uncertainties are underestimated.
- **Inadequate requirement flow to sub-contractors.** A documented Lesson Learned describes a spacecraft procured under the Rapid Spacecraft Development Office (RSDO) where the FM-to-ConOps disconnect surfaced only during integration and test.

## Key Takeaways
1. Derive FM requirements **top-down from the mission concept and risk posture**, sign them off at all levels, and trace architecture/design decisions back to them to control requirements creep.
2. Default to **explicit, one-concept-per-statement** requirements with captured rationale and inline definitions; explicit beats implicit/general for a clean flow-down.
3. Run the **five-step process** and the **eleven-dimension FM Requirements Checklist**; sweep **Table 11** categories to demonstrate completeness.
4. Identify the **few driving requirements** (fault tolerance, fail-safe/fail-operational, TTC/response time) that shape the whole architecture, and set tolerance using the worst-case doubling/halving interrogation.
5. Set **fault-tolerance posture by mission class** (Table 12: A flagship/single-fault-tolerant with full redundancy … D minimal/single-string).
6. Avoid both the **too-general** and **too-specific** pitfalls; choose the **in-house vs. out-of-house** strategy deliberately, defaulting to out-of-house minimum ambiguity when unsure.
7. Treat faults by **possibility over probability** — protect all electrical and protectable mechanical faults and core health-and-safety functions regardless of computed probability.
8. **Centralize** FM requirements under one lead and one driving document; minimize parroting and overlap, and flow requirements rigorously to sub-contractors.
9. Specify **test-platform requirements early** (fault injection, functional fidelity, timing fidelity, automation) via the FM Verification Matrix, and **write the V&V venue while writing each requirement**.
10. Allow subsystem work to start with a **minimal mission-independent safety requirement set** (ISS SSP 50038B; JWST per-instrument/per-bus minimums) without disturbing the top-down requirements.
