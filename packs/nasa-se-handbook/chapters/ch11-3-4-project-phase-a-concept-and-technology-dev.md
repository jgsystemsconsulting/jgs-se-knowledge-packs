# Chapter 11: 3.4 Project Phase A: Concept and Technology Development

## Core Idea
Phase A transforms a program-level mission concept into a credible, feasible, and architecturally sound technical baseline through structured mission analysis, requirements allocation, architecture development, and risk mitigation—establishing NASA's commitment to the project's technical and business viability before advancing to detailed design.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP)**: Baseline document defining how NASA systems engineering requirements and NPR 7123.1 practices will be executed across the program lifecycle.
  - When to use: Required as a mandatory Phase A product; documents governance, tailoring, and implementation of SE processes for the specific project

- **Mission Architecture Development**: Iterative process of defining system-level functions, allocating requirements to hardware/software/human elements, and demonstrating credible, feasible designs exist.
  - When to use: Core Phase A activity; involves breadboards, engineering units, physical models, and trade studies to mature the concept

- **System-Level Cost-Effectiveness Model**: Quantitative method for comparing lifecycle costs across alternative architectures and designs.
  - When to use: Refined during Phase A to enable trade study decisions; precedes final architecture selection

- **Functional and Performance Requirement Baseline**: Firm allocation of top-level system requirements to subsystems and lower-level end items, with documented interfaces and constraints.
  - When to use: Foundation for Phase B preliminary design; must be stable and configuration-controlled before advancing

- **Risk Management, Configuration Management, and Data Management Plans**: Control plans documenting verification, validation, safety assurance, software development, environmental compliance, and technical resource allocation.
  - When to use: Baseline as mandatory Phase A products; define downstream verification and operations processes

## Key Concepts
- **Concept Feasibility and Desirability**: Phase A's primary determination—confirming the suggested new system is practical, justifiable within NASA's budget, and responsive to program expectations and constraints on resources.

- **Functional Baseline**: The approved set of system-level functional and performance requirements, allocated to major end items, that establishes the firm definition of *what* the system will do.

- **Architecture Allocation**: The process of assigning system functions and performance requirements to specific hardware, software, and human elements, with internal and external interfaces explicitly defined.

- **Trade Studies**: Comparative analyses of alternative designs and architectures, performed *before* system design decisions are locked in, to optimize cost, schedule, and technical feasibility.

- **Breadboards, Engineering Units, and Physical Models**: Prototype or representative implementations used in Phase A to demonstrate feasibility, reduce technical risks, and exhibit engineering detail.

- **System-Level Cost-Effectiveness**: A quantitative model comparing lifecycle costs across alternative architectures to drive cost-conscious design decisions during the conceptual phase.

- **Human Systems Integration (HSI)**: Activities identifying how human elements (operators, maintainers, etc.) integrate into the system; includes initial evaluations of human rating and HSI plans.

- **Stakeholder Maturation**: Progressive refinement of program requirements and mission justification through active collaboration with stakeholders and subject matter experts to seek cost-effective designs.

## Mental Models
- **Use Phase A as a "go/no-go" filter**: Treat Phase A completion as proving both feasibility (can we build this?) and desirability (should we?). If the architecture cannot demonstrate credible, feasible designs or cost-effectiveness is poor, the program should not advance.

- **Think of requirements allocation as iterative loops**: System and subsystem tradeoffs "iterate back and forth" during Phase A. Allocation is not a one-way waterfall; it converges through cycles of analysis, redesign, and refinement.

- **View the functional baseline as a contract**: Once baselined in Phase A, the functional baseline becomes the reference for all downstream Phase B and beyond activities. Major changes beyond Phase A become increasingly expensive and disruptive.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Phase A establishes feasibility and commitment**: The phase proves the mission concept is credible, responsive to program requirements, and resource-constrained. By the end, NASA commits to the project's objectives at a given cost and schedule.

2. **Mission architecture is the pivot point**: Architecture development—including breadboards, trade studies, and human systems integration studies—is the core Phase A activity. It directly drives requirements allocation, cost estimation, and risk identification.

3. **Functional baseline must be firm before Phase B**: Top-level system requirements must be sufficiently detailed and stable to support Phase B's flow-down to subsystem specifications. Configuration control on the functional baseline prevents costly mid-stream changes.

4. **Trade studies precede design decisions**: Trade studies comparing alternative architectures and designs must be completed and archived before Phase B begins. This ensures decisions are grounded in analysis, not preferences.

5. **Plans are mandatory Phase A products**: SEMP, HSI Plan, Risk Management Plan, Configuration Management Plan, Software Development Plan, and specialty plans (environmental, reliability, parts management, logistics) must be baselined to prepare for downstream lifecycle management.

6. **Life-cycle thinking is mandatory**: Phase A analysis must consider all lifecycle aspects—training, operations, maintenance, safety, human factors, environmental impact—not just hardware and software development.

7. **Risk, cost, and schedule are parallel baselines**: Phase A establishes technical, cost, and schedule baselines in parallel. Cost-effectiveness modeling and technical resource estimates must be firm enough to support Phase B commitments.

## Connects To
- **NPR 7120.5**: NASA procedural requirement specifying Phase A technical activities, entrance/success criteria, and review gates (SRR, MDR/SDR).

- **NPR 7123.1**: NASA SE standard defining SE requirements and practices (including SEMP content) that Phase A must implement.

- **NPR 7150.2**: Control plan requirements for risk management, configuration management, data management, safety assurance, and software development addressed in Phase A baseline plans.

- **NPR 2810.1 and FIPS PUB 199**: Information technology security standards requiring Phase A to conduct IT system security categorization.

- **NASA-STD-8719.14**: Orbital debris assessment standard; Phase A produces initial orbital debris assessment.

- **Pre-Phase A**: Phase A builds on mission concept work from Pre-Phase A, maturing it through formal analysis and architecture development.

- **Phase B Preliminary Design**: Phase A functional baseline and architecture directly flow to Phase B, where they are decomposed into preliminary designs and detailed subsystem specifications.
