# Chapter 6: 2.7 Competency Model for Systems Engineers

## Core Idea
NASA's competency model defines eight technical management competencies and four proficiency levels to ensure systems engineers possess the skills needed to plan, manage, and execute the common technical processes across a project lifecycle.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **NASA Systems Engineering Competency Model**: Organizes SE competencies into two domains (Technical Execution and Technical Management) with eight core competencies per domain and four proficiency levels tied to role progression.
  - When to use: Staffing planning, training curriculum design, role definition, career progression frameworks, competency assessment for hiring or project assignment.

## Key Concepts

- **Technical Management Competencies**: Eight foundational competencies (SE 3.0–3.8) that focus on planning, control, and oversight of technical work rather than direct execution—including technical planning, requirements management, interface management, risk management, configuration management, data management, assessment, and decision analysis.

- **Proficiency Levels**: Four career-progression tiers defined for each competency: **Team Practitioner/Technical Engineer** (entry), **Team Lead/Subsystem Lead** (senior IC), **Project Systems Engineer** (program-level planning and oversight), and **Chief Engineer** (enterprise governance and executive counsel).

- **Technical Planning** (SE 3.2): Planning the application and management of each common technical process, identifying technical effort requirements, defining work product deliverables, establishing entry and success criteria for technical reviews, identifying technical risks, and producing the Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP).

- **Requirements Management** (SE 3.3): Managing product requirements with bidirectional traceability, establishing requirement baselines over the product lifecycle, managing requirement changes, and maintaining the approved requirements baseline.

- **Interface Management**: Establishing and maintaining formal interface definition and compliance between end products and enabling products, including interface documentation, change management, and interface control boards.

- **Technical Risk Management**: Continuously examining technical deviations from plans, identifying potential problems before they occur, and implementing risk mitigation and contingency actions when threshold conditions are triggered.

- **Configuration Management** (SE 3.5): Identifying the product configuration at various lifecycle points, controlling configuration changes, maintaining configuration integrity and traceability, and preserving configuration records.

- **Technical Data Management** (SE 3.6): Identifying and controlling product-related data throughout its lifecycle, acquiring and distributing data needed for development, operations, support and retirement, maintaining data integrity and security, and ensuring consistent use of lifecycle management best practices.

- **Technical Assessment** (SE 3.7): Monitoring technical effort progress, assessing productivity and product quality, tracking and trending technical metrics, and conducting technical reviews and peer reviews.

- **Technical Decision Analysis** (SE 3.8): Evaluating technical decision issues, identifying decision criteria and alternatives, analyzing impacts on health, safety, cost, and schedule, and recommending solutions with documented findings and corrective actions.

## Mental Models

- **Think of the eight technical management competencies as concentric rings of control**: inner ring (SE 3.1–3.2) plans what will be done and how; middle ring (SE 3.3–3.5) maintains requirements, interfaces, and configuration stability; outer ring (SE 3.6–3.8) feeds back data, assesses execution, and supports decisions.

- **Use the four proficiency levels as a skill-progression ladder within a single competency**: a Team Practitioner understands and executes a competency within a subsystem; a Team Lead coaches others and manages subsystem-level consistency; a Project SE applies the competency across the full project scope; a Chief Engineer evolves and tailors the competency model for the enterprise.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Technical management competencies are distinct from technical execution**: Mastery of the 17 common technical processes (requirements analysis, design synthesis, etc.) is separate from mastery of planning, controlling, and assessing those processes.

2. **Proficiency levels map to career progression and span of responsibility**: Each competency has four tiers; advancement requires demonstrating competence at broader scope (subsystem → project → program/enterprise).

3. **The eight technical management competencies form a system**: Requirements management feeds planning; interface and configuration management enforce requirements stability; risk management identifies problems; data management and assessment provide visibility; decision analysis consumes all outputs.

4. **SEMP and subsidiary plans are the primary output of SE 3.2 technical planning**: The Systems Engineering Management Plan operationalizes the competency model by specifying how each technical process will be applied, reviewed, and measured.

5. **Bidirectional traceability is core to SE 3.3 requirements management**: Requirements must link back to stakeholder expectations and forward to design, verification, and validation evidence; changes must propagate in both directions.

6. **Configuration management (SE 3.5) is the control mechanism for all product and data artifacts**: Baselines are established at key lifecycle points; changes are formally controlled and recorded to maintain configuration integrity and enable reproducibility.

7. **Technical assessment (SE 3.7) and decision analysis (SE 3.8) close the loop**: Metrics and reviews reveal execution health; structured decision analysis converts data into actionable choices that inform next steps and risk mitigation.

## Connects To

- **NASA Common Technical Processes (the earlier sections)**: Competency SE 3.2 requires planning the application of all 17 common technical processes; the competency model ensures skilled personnel execute those processes correctly.

- **Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP) structure**: Directly produced by SE 3.2 technical planning; the SEMP documents the tailoring and execution approach for each competency.

- **NPR 7120.5, NPR 7120.7, NPR 7120.8 (NASA program/project lifecycle and governance)**: The competency model is scaled to fit the lifecycle phases (Pre-Phase A through Phase F) and Key Decision Points.

- **Configuration Management (ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010, CM standards)**: SE 3.5 configuration management aligns with ISO configuration management principles for architecture and design artifacts.

- **Risk Management (ISO 31000, project risk standards)**: SE 3.4 technical risk management integrates with enterprise risk frameworks and feeds risk data to program-level oversight.
