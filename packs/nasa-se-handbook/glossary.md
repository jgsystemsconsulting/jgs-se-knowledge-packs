# NASA SE Handbook Glossary

**Acceptance** — Verification that flight units fully comply with requirements and are ready for integration, testing at operational levels (no added margin) to detect defects in parts, materials, and workmanship (Ch 23)
**Agency Baseline Commitment (ABC)** — The formal cost, schedule, and technical commitment for projects with life-cycle cost over $250M, approved by Congress and OMB (Ch 12)
**Analysis of Alternatives (AoA)** — Systematic evaluation of competing mission or system architectures and approaches during concept studies (Ch 10)
**Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)** — A multi-criteria decision method using pair-wise comparisons and weighted hierarchies to combine subjective and objective evaluation measures (Ch 34)
**As-Built Documentation** — Drawings, models, code listings, and records created during implementation, essential for handoff to verification and operations (Ch 21)
**Baseline** — A formally reviewed, approved, configuration-controlled set of items or requirements; once baselined, changes require formal change control (Ch 31)
**Bidirectional Traceability** — The ability to trace any requirement upward to its parent and downward to its allocated children, ensuring complete coverage and no orphans (Ch 28)
**Concept of Operations (ConOps)** — A narrative and visual description of how the system will be operated to achieve mission objectives, including nominal and off-nominal scenarios (Ch 17)
**Configuration Audit** — Functional (FCA) and physical (PCA) verification that the delivered system matches its approved baseline configuration (Ch 33)
**Configuration Control Board (CCB)** — Governance body that evaluates and approves or rejects change requests to baselined items (Ch 31)
**Configuration Item (CI)** — A physical, functional, or documentary entity selected for configuration control (Ch 31)
**Configuration Management** — Discipline for identifying, documenting, controlling, and auditing all baseline configurations and changes throughout the lifecycle (Ch 31)
**Constraint** — A condition that must be met and typically cannot be changed through trade studies (e.g., orbital mechanics, regulation, budget) (Ch 17)
**Continuous Risk Management (CRM)** — Ongoing process to identify, assess, and mitigate individual risks so residual risk stays acceptable (Ch 30)
**Contingency Action** — A reactive action plan prepared in advance and executed if a risk is realized (Ch 30)
**Control Account Manager (CAM)** — Person responsible for a control account (typically a WBS subsystem level) who plans, budgets, and analyzes variances there (Ch 33)
**Cost-Effectiveness** — The ratio of achieved system performance to total lifecycle expenditure from concept through disposal (Ch 4)
**Crosscutting Requirements** — Non-functional requirements applying across the architecture (environmental, safety, human factors, reliability, the "-ilities") (Ch 18)
**Decision Analysis Process** — A six-step workflow structuring problem formulation, criteria, alternatives, evaluation, recommendation, and documentation (Ch 34)
**Decision Matrix** — A table of alternatives (columns) against criteria (rows) populated with evaluation scores (Ch 34)
**Derived Requirements** — Requirements arising from architectural definition and design decisions, not explicitly stated in baselined stakeholder requirements (Ch 19)
**Design Driver** — Key technical parameters (launch date, orbit, duration) rooted in the ConOps that strongly shape architecture and design (Ch 17)
**Design Reference Mission (DRM)** — A time-sequenced scenario, part of the ConOps, demonstrating how the system accomplishes objectives under specific conditions (Ch 17)
**Designated Governing Authority (DGA)** — The approval body (typically Center Director or delegate) responsible for approving tailoring waivers and key documents (Ch 16)
**Discrepancy Report** — Documentation of any variance or out-of-compliance condition found during verification, distinguishing product nonconformance from procedural issues (Ch 23)
**Earned Value Management (EVM)** — Technique comparing earned value (BCWP) to planned (BCWS) and actual cost (ACWP) to detect variances and forecast cost/schedule at completion (Ch 27)
**Enabling Products** — Life-cycle support products, services, and personnel (production, test, training, maintenance, disposal) that facilitate the end product (Ch 20)
**End-to-End Testing** — Demonstration that all mission elements (flight, ground, communications, data processing) function together through realistic operational scenarios (Ch 23)
**Envelope Curve** — The Pareto frontier of designs where no alternative improves effectiveness without increasing cost or vice versa (Ch 4)
**Formulation Phase** — Establishes a cost-effective plan, allocates expectations to requirements, develops ConOps, and prepares for implementation approval (Ch 7)
**Functional Baseline** — The approved set of system-level functional and performance requirements allocated to major end items; defines what the system will do (Ch 11)
**Functional Requirements** — What functions the system must perform to accomplish objectives, derived from the ConOps (Ch 18)
**Hardware-In-the-Loop (HWIL)** — Integration of actual hardware with models simulating other system elements for high-fidelity real-time evaluation (Ch 23)
**Heritage** — A product's proven quality and reliability (time in service, quantity, MTBF, use cycles); degraded by modification or environment change (Ch 21)
**Human Systems Integration (HSI)** — Systematic consideration of human elements (operators, maintainers, support) alongside technical systems across the lifecycle (Ch 5)
**Independent Verification & Validation (IV&V)** — Third-party V&V oversight, structured for software-intensive projects (Ch 24)
**Integration Plan** — Documented optimal sequence and strategy for assembling, activating, and loading components into the complete system (Ch 22)
**Interface Control Document (ICD)** — A specification formally capturing the logical, physical, electrical, mechanical, human, and environmental characteristics of an interface (Ch 29)
**Interface Management** — Establishing and maintaining formal interface definition, verification, and change control between end and enabling products (Ch 29)
**International Traffic in Arms Regulation (ITAR)** — U.S. export control framework designating defense articles and technical data on the USML requiring controlled access (Ch 32)
**Joint Confidence Level (JCL)** — An integrated probabilistic model giving the probability of meeting both targeted cost and schedule simultaneously; required at KDP-C above $250M (Ch 27)
**Key Decision Point (KDP)** — A formal gate at which the decision authority reviews maturity evidence and approves or gates progression to the next phase (Ch 7)
**Key Design Requirement (KDR)** — A requirement with large cost or schedule impact, identified early to enable better management and tradeoffs (Ch 18)
**Key Performance Parameters (KPPs)** — Critical thresholds that, if unmet, jeopardize mission success; the top of the MOE/MOP/KPP hierarchy (Ch 33)
**Lessons Learned** — Knowledge gained from successes or failures, compiled to inform current and future project planning and risk mitigation (Ch 27)
**Logical Decomposition** — Recursive functional and structural analysis refining architecture and requirements until all system levels are defined and baselined (Ch 19)
**Make/Buy/Reuse Strategy** — Decision framework for acquiring products via in-house fabrication, commercial purchase, or reuse of existing items (Ch 21)
**Material Review Board (MRB)** — Formal review mechanism that dispositions nonconforming products during production (Ch 21)
**Measures of Effectiveness (MOEs)** — Stakeholder-centric success criteria defining what constitutes mission success and how well key objectives must be met (Ch 17)
**Measures of Performance (MOPs)** — Quantitative performance characteristics stated from the supplier perspective; multiple MOPs typically support one MOE (Ch 18)
**Mission Concept Review (MCR)** — Pre-formulation review assessing mission feasibility and alignment with NASA strategic goals (Ch 7)
**Need** — A singular problem statement that drives all downstream work, describing the problem without prescribing the solution (Ch 17)
**Needs, Goals, and Objectives (NGOs)** — A three-tier mechanism translating stakeholder desires into clear, measurable targets without prescribing solutions (Ch 17)
**Nonconformance** — A product deviation from specification, dispositioned as acceptable, reworked, or scrapped (Ch 21)
**Normalization** — Converting heterogeneous measurement bases into a common scale (e.g., 1-3-9, low/medium/high) for comparison and aggregation (Ch 34)
**Objective** — A specific, measurable, verifiable target level of system output that is aggressive but attainable and results-oriented (Ch 17)
**Objective Function (Cost Function)** — A mathematical expression combining cost, effectiveness, performance, and risk into a single metric of cost-effectiveness (Ch 20)
**Off-The-Shelf (OTS) Product** — Hardware or software with existing operational heritage requiring assessment for environmental and performance compatibility (Ch 21)
**Performance Requirements** — How well the system must perform its functions, quantitatively specified from the ConOps and scenarios (Ch 18)
**Phase A: Concept and Technology Development** — Confirms the concept is feasible, justifiable within budget, and responsive to program constraints (Ch 11)
**Phase B: Preliminary Design** — Matures requirements into a feasible preliminary design validated against ConOps, culminating in the PDR (Ch 12)
**Phase C: Final Design and Fabrication** — Matures detailed designs to the product baseline through CDR, ready for fabrication or coding (Ch 13)
**Phase D: Assembly, Integration, Test, Launch** — Builds, integrates, verifies, and validates the system end-to-end, then supports launch (AIVV) (Ch 14)
**Phase E: Operations and Sustainment** — Executes the prime mission and sustains the system within its current architecture (Ch 15)
**Phase F: Closeout** — Decommissioning and disposal; covers tailoring, the compliance matrix, and project-type classification (Ch 16)
**Pre-Phase A: Concept Studies** — Captures general ideas, justifies the mission, and produces ROM cost/schedule estimates ahead of the MCR (Ch 10)
**Preliminary Design Review (PDR)** — System- and end-item-level reviews reflecting successive refinement of requirements into a feasible design (Ch 12)
**Critical Design Review (CDR)** — Hierarchical system- and component-level reviews approving the detailed product baseline before fabrication (Ch 13)
**Product Baseline** — The complete, detailed design of all end products, approved through CDR and ready for fabrication or coding (Ch 13)
**Product Breakdown Structure (PBS)** — Hierarchical decomposition of all end and enabling products, carried to the lowest level with a cognizant engineer (Ch 3)
**Product Realization Processes** — The downstream engine half: implementation, integration, verification, validation, and transition, applied bottom-up (Ch 2)
**Product Transition** — Lifecycle process to prepare, package, document, transport, install, and verify products for delivery to the next level or customer (Ch 25)
**Programmatic Risk** — Risk from external actions outside project control (ITAR, partner agreements, congressional or OMB direction) (Ch 30)
**Qualification** — Verification that flight-unit design meets requirements under worst-case loads plus design margin, validating manufacturing and assembly (Ch 23)
**Regression Testing** — Rerunning previously passed acceptance tests (primarily software) to ensure corrections introduce no new failures (Ch 24)
**Requirements Creep** — Subtle, imperceptible growth of requirement scope, often via innocent incremental changes that collectively inflate cost and complexity (Ch 28)
**Requirements Management** — Systematic identification, control, decomposition, and allocation of requirements with bidirectional traceability and baseline change control (Ch 28)
**Residual Risk** — The risk remaining after mitigation or contingency action, monitored until deemed acceptable (Ch 30)
**Review Item Disposition (RID) / Request For Action (RFA)** — Formal mechanisms for capturing, tracking, and resolving review findings toward closure (Ch 33)
**Risk** — The potential for performance shortfalls against requirements in safety, technical, cost, schedule, or institutional domains, characterized as triplets (Ch 30)
**Risk-Informed Decision Making (RIDM)** — Decision process using risk and uncertainty information to select alternatives and set baseline performance requirements (Ch 30)
**Risk Triplet** — A three-part model of risk: scenario, likelihood, and consequence (Ch 30)
**Robustness** — A tentative selection's stability under varying reasonable input values and assumptions; a robust choice holds rank across ranges (Ch 34)
**Rough Order of Magnitude (ROM)** — High-level, preliminary life-cycle cost and schedule projections supporting early resource planning (Ch 10)
**SE Engine** — Graphical model organizing the 17 common technical processes across system design, product realization, and technical management (Ch 1)
**Sensitive But Unclassified (SBU)** — Data requiring controlled dissemination but not classified (design models, limited-rights, bid/proposal, financial data) (Ch 32)
**Stakeholder** — Any party with a vested interest in the system (customers, users, operators, maintainers, regulators), identified across all phases (Ch 17)
**Stakeholder Expectations** — The operational and business needs of customers and stakeholders; the baselined starting point for the SE process (Ch 1)
**Standing Review Board (SRB)** — Independent external reviewers assessing technical and programmatic approach and risk, advising the KDP decision authority (Ch 33)
**Successive Refinement** — The principle that design changes should be incremental refinements of baselines, growing costlier beyond Phase B (Ch 12)
**System Architecture** — The structure of components, their relationships, and the principles governing their design and evolution over time (Ch 19)
**System Design Processes** — The upstream engine half: stakeholder expectations, technical requirements, logical decomposition, and design solution definition (Ch 2)
**Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP)** — The primary top-level technical management document specifying processes, organization, cost, and schedule, tailored to the project (Ch 27)
**Tailoring** — Seeking relief from SE NPR requirements via documented deviations or waivers, consistent with objectives, risk, and constraints (Ch 16)
**Technical Assessment** — Monitoring technical progress, productivity, and quality, and conducting technical and peer reviews (Ch 33)
**Technical Data Management** — Systematic capture, maintenance, distribution, and protection of engineering data from conception through disposal (Ch 32)
**Technical Data Package (TDP)** — The evolving set of drawings, parts lists, specifications, interface definitions, V&V plans, and logistics procedures (Ch 20)
**Technical Performance Measures (TPMs)** — Critical or key parameters monitored during implementation by comparing actual achievement against anticipated values (Ch 18)
**Technical Requirements** — Functional and performance requirements derived from stakeholder expectations; the baselined contract between design and validation (Ch 1)
**Technical Risk** — Risk from design evolution and production affecting the ability to meet stakeholder expectations and technical requirements (Ch 30)
**Test Readiness Review (TRR)** — Review assessing readiness for V&V, examining test facilities, trained testers, instrumentation, and support equipment (Ch 22)
**Threshold vs. Baseline Performance** — Threshold is the minimum acceptable value for mission success; baseline is the desired level, together giving trade space (Ch 18)
**Trade Study** — A closed-loop process comparing alternative designs against criteria (cost, schedule, maturity, risk, performance) to bound cost-effectiveness (Ch 20)
**Trigger Threshold** — The risk level or metric value that initiates mitigation or contingency action; risks near thresholds get closer monitoring (Ch 30)
**Validation** — Proof that the product accomplishes its intended purpose in the intended environment and meets stakeholder expectations (built the right product) (Ch 3)
**Verification** — Proof of compliance with approved requirements via test, analysis, inspection, or demonstration (built right) (Ch 3)
**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** — A hierarchical, product-oriented decomposition of total project work, derived from the PBS and used to allocate cost and schedule (Ch 27)
**Work Package** — A unit of work within a cost account with defined scope, schedule, and budget; the basic building block for EVM (Ch 27)
