# NIST SP 800-160 (SSE) Glossary

Key terms from NIST SP 800-160 Vol 1 + Vol 2, alphabetical, with chapter references. Definitions are synthesized, not reproduced.

**Adapt (goal)** — change functions or supporting capabilities in response to predicted or actual changes in threat or environment. (Ch 5)

**Adequately secure** — secure enough, with evidence, for the stakeholders' needs and accepted risk; the realistic SSE goal given that absolute security can't be proven. (Ch 1)

**Adversity** — conditions that can cause asset loss: threats, attacks, vulnerabilities, hazards, disruptions, exposures. (Ch 1)

**Advanced Persistent Threat (APT)** — a capable, well-resourced, stealthy, adaptive adversary that pursues objectives over time via multiple vectors and seeks to maintain presence. (Ch 8)

**Anticipate (goal)** — maintain a state of informed preparedness for adversity. (Ch 5)

**Assurance** — the grounds for justified confidence that a system satisfies its security claims. (Ch 4)

**Assurance case** — a structured claim → argument → evidence artifact substantiating security claims. (Ch 4)

**Coordinated Protection (technique)** — ensure protections operate together as coordinated defense in depth. (Ch 6)

**Cyber resiliency** — ability to anticipate, withstand, recover from, and adapt to adversity on cyber-enabled systems. (Ch 5)

**Cyber-resiliency technique** — a category of capability (one of 14) that achieves resiliency objectives. (Ch 6)

**Deception (technique)** — mislead, confuse, or hide assets from the adversary. (Ch 6)

**Defense in Depth (principle)** — layered, diverse protections so no single failure is fatal. (Ch 3)

**Design principle (trustworthy secure design)** — one of Vol 1's 30+ rules for structuring a secure, analyzable system. (Ch 3)

**Design principle (cyber-resiliency)** — strategic (cross-cutting) or structural (architecture) guidance for selecting/placing resiliency. (Ch 7)

**Diversity (technique)** — heterogeneity to minimize common-mode failure and raise adversary cost. (Ch 6)

**Goal (cyber-resiliency)** — one of the four high-level aims: anticipate, withstand, recover, adapt. (Ch 5)

**Implementation approach** — a specific method within a cyber-resiliency technique. (Ch 6)

**Least Privilege (principle)** — grant each element only the privileges it needs. (Ch 3)

**Minimal Trusted Elements (principle)** — minimize the trusted computing base; less to trust, less to verify. (Ch 3)

**Non-Persistence (technique)** — generate/retain resources only as needed, for limited time, to shrink the adversary's foothold. (Ch 6)

**Objective (cyber-resiliency)** — an assessable statement (e.g. Prevent/Avoid, Continue, Constrain, Reconstitute, Understand, Transform) supporting a goal. (Ch 5)

**Problem context** — the SSE context defining protection needs, adversity, and security requirements. (Ch 2)

**Protective Failure / Recovery (principles)** — fail securely; recover to a secure state. (Ch 3)

**Recover (goal)** — restore mission/business functions during and after adversity. (Ch 5)

**Reduced Complexity (principle)** — simpler systems are more analyzable and more secure. (Ch 3)

**Redundancy (technique)** — multiple protected instances of critical resources. (Ch 6)

**Segmentation (technique)** — separate elements by criticality/trust to contain adversity. (Ch 6)

**Solution context** — the SSE context defining and realizing the security architecture, design, and trade-offs. (Ch 2)

**Substantiated Integrity (technique)** — ascertain whether critical elements have been corrupted. (Ch 6)

**Systems Security Engineering (SSE)** — specialty discipline engineering trustworthy, adequately secure systems across the life cycle. (Ch 1)

**Trustworthiness** — worthiness of being trusted to meet defined expectations; claimed then substantiated. (Ch 4)

**Trustworthiness context** — the SSE context producing assurance and the assurance case. (Ch 2)

**Unpredictability (technique)** — make changes randomly/unpredictably so the adversary can't plan around defenses. (Ch 6)

**Withstand (goal)** — continue essential mission/business functions despite adversity. (Ch 5)
