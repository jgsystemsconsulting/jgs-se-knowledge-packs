# Chapter 8 — Schedule Documentation and Communication

> Source: NASA Schedule Management Handbook, 2024 edition (Chapter 8, "Schedule Documentation and Communication"). Do not cite the superseded 2010 SP-2010-3403.

## Core Idea

Documentation and Communication are the two Schedule Management sub-functions that make every other sub-function usable. Documentation captures program/project (P/p) schedule information so that events, decisions, and the reasoning behind them are traceable, defensible, and repeatable; Communication disseminates that captured information — in writing or orally — to team members and other stakeholders so they can judge whether the P/p is meeting its objectives and commitments. The handbook treats the pair as both an "overarching linkage" running across the whole Schedule Management function and a common thread woven into each individual sub-function: planning, development, assessment/analysis, and maintenance/control all generate information that must be recorded and conveyed at the right maturity, to the right people, at the right time. The discipline rests on three things — the Schedule Database, the project's CM/DM processes, and the baselining activity of Chapter 7 — and its purpose is to ensure the intended meaning of schedule information is not only conveyed steadfastly but also received clearly.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Schedule Documentation and Communication Plan** — defines the tools, protocols, and formats used to record and disseminate schedule information. Established early in the P/p life cycle in close coordination with the SMP and Schedule Database. The handbook's Figure 8-4 lays out its components, which the P/p establishes during Schedule Management Planning; the P/p then decides which products flow through each channel and plans to produce them.
- **Schedule Management Plan (SMP)** — the controlling document that defines all forms, formats, templates, and figures used for communication, and that captures the P/p's reporting and communication requirements. Documentation and Communication are performed "according to" the SMP; the SMP is referenced throughout this chapter as the authority for protocols, cadence, lexicon, and report formats.
- **Configuration Management / Data Management (CM/DM)** — the PP&C function described as "the backbone of Documentation and Communication." Used continuously to control documentation, data, and the configuration of schedule products across the life cycle. CM supplies naming conventions, data structure, consistent coding, and change control; DM controls release of data that is not part of the configuration baseline.
- **Basis of Estimate (BoE)** — invoked as the backbone of the schedule data in the Schedule Database. Per the NASA Cost Estimating Handbook (Version 4.0), its minimum standard is a collection of information sufficient for an independent party to understand and reproduce the schedule estimate; the handbook recommends packaging it as a single information packet with a primary narrative file, constituent data, and external references.
- **SCoPe (Schedule Community of Practice)** — the Agency reference repository pointed to for common tool lists, example DRDs, Schedule Repository requirements, and the skills/competencies for this sub-function. Used as the live source of detail the handbook itself does not enumerate.
- **Schedule Repository (Agency-level)** — the formal archive (Section 5.5.9) required for all 7120.5 and 7120.8 P/ps with life-cycle cost (LCC) of $50M or greater; these P/ps must submit the IMS in native scheduling-tool formats (e.g., MS Project .mpp, Primavera P6). Maintained by the OCFO Strategic Investments Division (SID).
- **CADRe** — the Agency cost-and-data archive into which historical narrative is often captured, and one of the repositories into which schedule data packages and lessons learned are placed. Also maintained by OCFO SID.
- **Standing Review Board (SRB) data-drop framework** — drawn from SOPI 6.0 and the SRB Handbook. Defines staged "data drops" (Data Access, Data Drop 1 preliminary products, Data Drop 2 final products) governed by a Terms of Reference (ToR) so the SRB programmatic team can perform the independent assessment before a life-cycle review (LCR).
- **Decision Memorandum ("Decision Memo")** — the record produced at a Key Decision Point (KDP) that documents major decisions and their basis, the constraints and parameters the Agency and PM will operate within, and the approved outcome. Appended to the FAD or P/p Plan; the Management Agreement (MA) and, at KDP C, the Agency Baseline Commitment (ABC) are established through it.
- **Schedule Performance Reporting taxonomy (Figure 8-5)** — the chapter's organizing model splitting all schedule reports into three types: **Status Reporting** (where the schedule now stands, actual data), **Progress Reporting** (what has been accomplished, plan vs. actual, deterministic), and **Forecasting** (probabilistic projections from SRA/ICSRA techniques). Used to classify the catalog of report formats in Section 8.3.2.4.

## Key Concepts

- **Documentation vs. Communication.** Documentation records information so it can be stored, catalogued, and retrieved; Communication transfers that recorded information into one or more useful formats for management and decision making. Documentation should be the foundation of communication because it is what makes the message repeatable, traceable, and defensible.
- **Single integrated Schedule Database.** All levels of schedule reporting must be produced from one integrated database, never from separate schedule sources, so that every report is traceable back to the same authoritative data.
- **Reporting depth is not uniform.** The assumption that all WBS elements report to the same level of detail is explicitly rejected. High-dollar, high-hour, critical, or risk-bearing activities may need granular reporting; low-volume, non-critical, or level-of-effort tasks may need only summary-level reporting. Flexibility is treated as a valid, resource-aware approach.
- **The IMS is the backbone of all reports.** Every schedule-related report should trace to the Integrated Master Schedule; the Summary Schedule, for instance, is a representation of the IMS but is not itself an IMS and cannot support thorough analysis. When the IMS itself serves as a reporting product, it is delivered in its native file format.
- **Communication dimensions.** Schedule communication has many dimensions to plan for: internal vs. external; formal vs. informal; vertical vs. horizontal; official vs. unofficial; and written vs. oral (including verbal inflection and nonverbal cues).
- **Status / Progress / Forecast.** Status reports are actual-data snapshots with no metrics, trends, or projections. Progress reports introduce comparison or trend (variance, EVM) but stay deterministic. Forecast reports come from stochastic/probabilistic modeling (SRA/ICSRA) and are risk- and uncertainty-informed.
- **Report catalog (by type).** Status: Timeline, Milestone Status, Summary Schedule (a.k.a. "Master Schedule" when it summarizes the complete IMS), IMS Report, Health Check, Critical Milestones, Total Float, Margin Status, Critical Path, Resource Allocation. Progress: Milestone Progress, Milestone Variance Report/Log, Milestone Trend, Summary Schedule Variance, Float/Slack Trend (with Float Erosion Tables), Margin Trend/Log, EVM Reports (BEI, CEI, HMI, SPI, SPIt), Risk-Based Completion Trend, Risk-Based Tracking against MA/ABC, Schedule Baseline Change Log. Forecast: Probability of On-Time Delivery ("BandAid" chart), Stochastic Critical Path, Sensitivity (tornado) Report, Confidence Level (CDF) Report, SRA Reports, JCL Scatterplot Report.
- **"Critical" vs. "critical path."** A milestone with zero total float may be "critical" only because of an imposed constraint, not because it drives the P/p finish or end-item delivery. Reports should distinguish the two, and using the longest-path method rather than relying on total-float alone helps separate genuine critical-path drivers from merely constrained activities.
- **Contractor and external-partner reporting.** Government and contractor must share a clear understanding of schedule content, level of detail, format, frequency, tools, thresholds, responsibilities, and controls before integrating contractor data into the P/p IMS. Requirements live in the SOW, CDRL, and IMS DRD for contracts; in an MOU, Space Act Agreement, LOA, SOW, or SMP for partners; and must account for FAR and ITAR where applicable.
- **Records retention.** Storage must comply with NPR 1441.1 and NRRS 1441 with appropriate security safeguards. The Configuration Manager owns collection through a single authoritative source; the P/S helps identify required data and authoritative sources and is informed when authorized parties are granted access.
- **Informal backups vs. formal archives.** Informal backups (IMS and BoEs backed up at least monthly or before major changes; performance/analysis data, models, and results) protect against loss between status periods. Formal archives retain the original baseline IMS, significant replans, all rebaselines, and the as-built IMS, plus versions at each major LCR milestone — together with the historical narrative, schedule/cost data packages, analysis input/models/results, and lessons learned.
- **Knowledge capture and lessons learned.** Lessons learned are developed and archived throughout the life cycle, at minimum at the end of each LCR milestone, and answer whether the objective was met, whether work was on time/within budget/to spec, whether stakeholders were satisfied, and what to improve. Both effective and ineffective practices are recorded.

## Mental Models

- **Documentation is the foundation; communication is the structure built on it.** Capture first so the message can be reproduced, traced, and defended; only then disseminate. A briefing without documented backing is an assertion, not a record.
- **One database, many lenses.** Picture every report — a one-page Summary Schedule for senior management, a dense management tracker for the PM, a JCL scatterplot for a confidence decision — as a different filter over the same integrated Schedule Database. If two reports disagree, the fault is in the lenses, not the data, because there is only one source.
- **Match the lens to the decision.** Status answers "where are we now?", Progress answers "how are we doing against the plan?", and Forecast answers "where are we likely to end up, given risk?" Pick the report type from the question being asked, and tune the level of detail to the audience: more for the PM, less for senior reviewers and external stakeholders.
- **File heredity should be self-evident.** Good CM/DM naming encodes identifying attributes and recent changes, not just a version stamp, so an analyst can "time travel" back to a prior point and re-depart from it. Regardless of convention, the lineage of any file must be apparent at a glance.
- **The schedule is both a communication tool and a catalyst for communication.** The IMS is not just something you report on; presenting it surfaces conversations, surfaces risks, and drives decisions.
- **Archive the iterations that matter, not every iteration.** The intent of the formal archive is the key life-cycle events and final products — baseline, replans, rebaselines, as-built, LCR versions — plus enough supporting data (BoEs, models, assumptions, metrics) to reconstruct and re-run, not a copy of every status run.
- **Keep a "wish list."** Throughout the life cycle, record the schedule statistics that personnel wish they had on hand; preparing a summary at closeout is far cheaper than mining archives later, and feeds future P/ps' BoEs through SCoPe.

## Anti-patterns

- **Reporting from separate schedule sources instead of one integrated Schedule Database.** The handbook states reporting must come from a single integrated database, not separate sources — implying that pulling figures from disconnected schedules breaks traceability and consistency.
- **Assuming every WBS element must report to the same depth.** Explicitly called out as "not a valid assumption"; forcing uniform detail wastes effort on low-risk tasks and can starve high-risk ones of granularity.
- **Communication that is too wordy or too lengthy.** The handbook warns that overlong communication may be misunderstood or ignored; clear, short, familiar, consistent words and phrases are prescribed instead.
- **Confusing "critical" milestones with "critical-path" milestones.** Treating any zero-float milestone as a true schedule driver — when its float is zero only because of an imposed constraint — misleads stakeholders about what actually threatens the finish date.
- **Treating a Summary Schedule as an analysis tool or an IMS.** A Summary Schedule does not carry enough detail for thorough analysis and is not an IMS; using it as one produces false confidence, since a Gantt view typically hides dependencies and cannot show how one slip cascades.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Documentation and Communication are pillars, not afterthoughts.** They begin at the onset of Schedule Management and run throughout the life cycle, linking every other sub-function. Their tools, protocols, and formats must be defined early, in lock-step with the SMP and Schedule Database.
2. **CM/DM is the backbone.** A systematic, structured, electronic file-management system controls schedule information across the life cycle. Naming conventions, data structure, consistent coding, and change control make data minable, recoverable, and trustworthy — and they apply across planning, development, assessment/analysis, and maintenance/control.
3. **Everything traces to one IMS and one database.** All schedule reports trace to the IMS; all reporting comes from a single integrated Schedule Database. Reporting depth varies by element importance and audience, but the source never does.
4. **Reports come in three flavors — status, progress, forecast.** Choose the type from the decision being supported: actual snapshot, deterministic plan-vs-actual comparison, or probabilistic projection from SRA/ICSRA. The chapter supplies a concrete report format for nearly every need within each type.
5. **Reporting is governed and phased.** Internal reviews (monthly status, PSRs/PPRs for R&T, integrated discipline/phase reviews for space flight) feed routine control; LCR/KDP reporting follows NPR 7120.5 or 7120.8 maturity requirements and the SRB data-drop schedule, culminating in a Decision Memo that records the decision, its basis, and the MA/ABC parameters.
6. **Contractor and partner reporting must be specified up front.** SOW/CDRL/DRD for contracts and MOU/LOA/Space Act/SMP for partners must pin down content, detail, format, frequency, tools, thresholds, responsibilities, and controls — leveraging existing partner systems while still meeting NASA best practice, and respecting FAR/ITAR.
7. **Back up informally, archive formally, and capture knowledge.** Back up the IMS and BoEs at least monthly or before major changes; formally archive the baseline, replans, rebaselines, as-built, and LCR-milestone versions with narrative, data packages, and analysis files in P/p- and Agency-level repositories per the records-retention rules; and develop lessons learned at least each LCR milestone to improve future P/ps.

## Connects To

- **Chapter 7 (Schedule Maintenance and Control)** — baselining, replanning, rebaselining, change control, EVM metrics (BEI, CEI, HMI, SPI, SPIt), margin trend, and risk-based tracking originate there; this chapter governs how those products are documented and communicated.
- **Schedule Database and Schedule Management Planning** — the single integrated database and the SMP define the protocols, lexicon, formats, and report templates this chapter applies.
- **SRA / ICSRA and JCL** — forecast reports (confidence levels, stochastic critical path, sensitivity/tornado, BandAid charts, JCL scatterplot at the 70% NASA requirement) are the communication layer over the probabilistic analysis methods.
- **NPR 7120.5 / NPR 7120.7 / NPR 7120.8** — set the LCR/KDP cadence, required products, and maturity that drive reporting; R&T reviews (PSRs, PPRs, PARs, CAs) come from 7120.8.
- **NPR 7123.1** — internal reviews for space-flight P/ps are part of the systems-engineering work processes defined here.
- **NPR 1441.1 and NRRS 1441** — records-management requirements and retention schedules that constrain how schedule data is stored and accessed.
- **Standing Review Board Handbook, SOPI 6.0, and the Terms of Reference (ToR)** — govern independent-assessment data drops and the maturity/timing of products delivered to the SRB.
- **NASA Cost Estimating Handbook (Version 4.0)** — defines the BoE minimum standard (independent reproducibility) that underpins schedule documentation.
- **SCoPe (Schedule Community of Practice), Agency Schedule Repository, CADRe, and EVM Repository** — the Agency-level homes for archived IMSs, narratives, data packages, lessons learned, and the tool/DRD references this chapter relies on.
