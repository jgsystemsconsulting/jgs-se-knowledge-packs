# Chapter — Schedule Risk Analysis (SRA & Integrated Cost-Schedule Risk Analysis)

> Source basis: NASA Schedule Management Handbook, 2024 edition (Revision 2, effective 2024-03-15), Section 6.3 "Analyze the Schedule." This chapter is grounded in the 2024 edition only; do **not** rely on the superseded 2010 SP-2010-3403 for any of the practices below.

## Core Idea

In this handbook, "analyzing the schedule" means performing a Schedule Risk Analysis (SRA): a statistical, simulation-based examination of how duration uncertainties and discrete risks — captured in the Integrated Master Schedule (IMS) — propagate through the network to affect the probability that the program or project (P/p) finishes by its planned dates. The SRA shifts the question from "what date does the deterministic schedule say?" to "what is the *likelihood* the plan is achievable, and where should we invest to improve it?"

The SRA answers five management questions: whether the product will deliver on cost and schedule at acceptable risk; how best to reduce, mitigate, avoid, or accept schedule risk; how to prioritize mitigation effort; whether margin set aside is sufficient for expected and unexpected risk; and which tools are available to assess that risk.

An **Integrated Cost-Schedule Risk Analysis (ICSRA)** extends the SRA by adding a cost model plus cost uncertainties and cost risks. Where the SRA produces risk-informed *schedule* outputs (confidence levels, completion ranges, stochastic critical paths), the ICSRA additionally produces risk-informed *cost* outputs and, crucially, the **Joint Confidence Level (JCL)** — the probability that a P/p will come in at or under both its targeted cost and its targeted schedule date simultaneously. The two analyses are deliberately staged: build and verify the SRA first, then append the cost model to transform it into an ICSRA.

The underlying engine is Monte Carlo simulation. The simulation tool is described in the source as essentially a mathematical machine: it operates faithfully on whatever inputs it is given, so the *judgment* — and therefore the value — lives entirely in the thoughtful construction of inputs and the disciplined reading of outputs.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Schedule Risk Analysis (SRA)** — the core method. Uses the IMS (or an Analysis Schedule) as its foundation, attaches duration uncertainties and discrete risks to activities, and computes probability distributions for selected completion dates. It is triggered either by a pre-planned event — a major life-cycle review, or one of the routine monthly and quarterly reviews — or by a special request raised once a P/p starts to look at risk; it can also be run on a standing cadence as a proactive management technique.

- **Integrated Cost-Schedule Risk Analysis (ICSRA)** — the SRA plus a cost model and cost risks/uncertainties. Produces cost CDFs (S-curves), cost-schedule scatterplots, and the JCL. Required for spaceflight and IT P/ps under NPD 1000.5 to establish or rebaseline a plan.

- **Joint Confidence Level (JCL)** — the probability that cost will be at or below target *and* schedule at or below target. Required by NPR 7120.5 / NPD 1000.5; the source notes a 2019 NASA Associate Administrator memo extending JCL requirements to projects and single-project programs with a life-cycle cost of $1 billion or more. The 70% JCL is the requirement for single-project programs and projects above a $250M life-cycle cost.

- **Analysis Schedule** — a summarized, simplified surrogate for the IMS, built specifically to facilitate loading of cost/resource, uncertainty, and risk data. Used when the full IMS is impractical for simulation. Must be demonstrated to be an accurate emulator of the IMS — same major-milestone dates, same critical paths.

- **Risk Driver method** — an alternative to per-activity three-point impacts (attributed in the source to Hulett). Risks and uncertainties are modeled as multiplicative "risk factors" (Min/M-L/Max percentages of remaining duration) held in the tool's uncertainty/risk register and assigned to many activities at once. Best suited to higher-level strategic risks; auto-correlates every activity sharing a factor.

- **Duration Ratio method** — a performance-based way to derive uncertainty bounds from the P/p's own history, computed from actual versus baseline durations pulled directly from the IMS. Excludes milestones, summary activities, and LOE; sharpened by data segmentation and outlier removal.

- **Schedule Estimating Relationships (SERs)** — schedule analogues of cost estimating relationships (CERs); a mathematical relationship expressing duration as a function of one or more technical or cost parameters. Found in NASA tools such as SMART and NICM.

- **Three macro methods for uncertainty distributions**: data-driven (analogous/parametric, most defensible early in the life cycle), performance-based (an offshoot of data-driven, e.g., EVM-derived), and SME-based (expert elicitation of Min/M-L/Max, used where no data exist).

- **NASA-licensed SRA/ICSRA tools** — JACS and Polaris, both under Agency license and available on the ONCE database; the source recommends performing all SRAs/ICSRAs on one of these NASA-provided platforms. Tool capabilities are catalogued in the NASA Schedule Management Tools Matrix.

- **External references the source leans on**: the Joint Cost, Schedule, Risk, and Uncertainty Handbook (CSRUH) for correlation and merge-bias guidance; the NASA Cost Estimating Handbook for cost-loading, CERs, and the resource-loading policy clarification; the GAO Schedule Assessment Guide and NDIA PASEG for supporting practice; and NPR 8000.4 for the formal definitions of risk and uncertainty.

## Key Concepts

**Uncertainty vs. risk.** Uncertainty is the inherent indefiniteness in a baseline plan — the impossibility of predicting a future outcome exactly — characterized by a probability distribution drawn from assessor experience and historical data. It has *no likelihood of occurrence*: some value is always drawn and applied in every simulation iteration. Risk, by contrast, combines a likelihood and a consequence of a future undesired event. The practical division of labor: the Risk Management System (RMS) captures the discrete risks actively being watched and managed, while uncertainty absorbs both the unidentified risks and the ordinary estimating error in the plan.

**Prerequisites and data collection.** An SRA can begin once the Schedule Assessment and Analysis Plan, ground rules and assumptions, an IMS or Analysis Schedule, task duration uncertainties, and a current/complete discrete risk list are available; an ICSRA adds a cost estimate, cost uncertainties, and cost risks (liens, encumbrances, threats, sunk costs). The two cardinal data properties are **consistency** (the IMS, risk list, and cost data must share a WBS and be traceable; EVM data used for uncertainty must be reconciled to the current performance measurement baseline, especially after a rebaseline) and **configuration control** (the analyst is responsible for keeping data sets and SRA Model versions in lockstep so outputs are traceable to inputs).

**IMS vs. Analysis Schedule trade.** Best practice is to use the IMS itself as the SRA/ICSRA framework when feasible — it is the highest-fidelity, configuration-controlled, routinely maintained representation of the work, with full traceability of risk drivers down to individual float values. The Analysis Schedule is a fallback when the IMS is too large, structurally unsuitable, or split across a server environment. The Analysis Schedule is easier to visualize, simulate, and tailor, and can be truncated to current time — but it trades away fidelity, can lose logic ties, can mask or introduce false critical paths, and shifts the burden onto the analyst to prove it faithfully emulates the IMS. Practical guidance: keep it to roughly 100–3000 working tasks, preserve the IMS calendars and natural float (don't fake float with lag), match level of detail to where the risks are, and you need not model completed work as long as it is captured as sunk cost (fiscal-year cutoffs work well).

**The six SRA build procedures.** (1) Set up the model from the IMS or Analysis Schedule; (2) develop and load schedule duration uncertainty parameters from the Basis of Estimate (BoE); (3) apply correlation; (4) develop and load discrete schedule-risk parameters from the RMS; (5) map those risks to the activities they actually affect; (6) test and verify the risk inputs respond correctly before simulating.

**Modeling a discrete risk.** A risk parameter has two halves: a *likelihood* (commonly a Bernoulli draw — returns 1 X% of the time, 0 otherwise) multiplied by an *impact* distribution (commonly triangular Min/M-L/Max, or uniform when little is known). The most common mapping technique is **Activity Duration Impact**: a zero-duration "delay" milestone is inserted between the at-risk activity and its successor; when the Bernoulli draw fires, a sample from the impact distribution becomes that milestone's duration. Other strategies include Delayed Start, Delayed Completion, Probabilistic Branching (a random draw selects an alternate path), and Conditional Branching (an IF-condition on a predecessor selects the path). Risks are further classed as parallel (independent, resolved separately, the longest drives) or serial (cumulative, one must resolve before the next is exposed).

**Correlation.** Per the CSRUH, uncertainty analysis is incomplete until correlation is assessed. Correlation measures the degree to which variables move together (0.0 weak to 1.0 strong) and does not establish cause and effect. Where data-driven values aren't available, industry guidance suggests values between 0.25 and 0.75; the handbook's recommended default is **0.3**. Correlation alters both mean and standard deviation of outputs, and behaves differently for serial vs. parallel tasks. Ignoring correlation (treating it as zero) understates total-level variance — often dramatically — and overstates both schedule risk and the apparent effect of merge bias.

**Merge bias.** Where many parallel paths feed one milestone, the convergence point has a higher probability of being late because every feeding path must finish on time for it to start on time. More parallel paths shift the mean right and reduce variance; lower correlation between the merging paths increases the merge-bias impact. Rolling parallel paths up into a single summary activity in an Analysis Schedule can hide or underestimate this effect — a key reason to keep risk-exposed convergence points detailed.

**Building the ICSRA: cost hammocks.** Because traditional resource-loaded schedules are rare at NASA, the handbook builds the ICSRA by **cost-loading** the schedule via *hammock* tasks. A hammock is anchored start-to-start to the first activity and finish-to-finish to the last activity of a sequence (the "hammock between two trees" image); as those activities stretch or shrink under risk and uncertainty, the hammock expands or contracts and accrues or sheds cost accordingly. Costs are separated into **time-dependent (TD)** (e.g., labor, facility time — these scale with duration) and **time-independent (TI)** (e.g., travel, parts, firm-fixed-price subcontracts). A critical modeling rule: the TD cost impact of a schedule-delay risk must *not* be entered as a separate cost-risk impact, because duration uncertainty already drives it automatically; only the TI portion (e.g., replacement parts) belongs in the risk's cost entry. The ICSRA build is an eight-procedure appendage to the completed, tested SRA.

**Reading the outputs.** The simulation produces, per selected output: a **PDF** (normalized frequency histogram, area 1.0) giving the probability of any value, and a **CDF / S-curve** (the integral of the PDF) giving the probability of achieving a value *or less*. S-curve shape carries meaning: slope reflects the Coefficient of Variation (flat = high uncertainty, typical early; steep = lower uncertainty, typical post-PDR as risks retire); a left-side "dog-leg" indicates a backstopped earliest-possible date (e.g., a shared test facility slot); long right tails flag high-impact/low-probability risks (often de-integration scenarios in I&T); a bimodal PDF signals two clusters of risk impact magnitudes.

**Sensitivity, criticality, cruciality.** Three complementary measures: **Sensitivity** ("how big") correlates a task's or risk's variation with overall P/p duration or cost, displayed in tornado charts. **Criticality** ("how often") is the percentage of iterations an activity landed on the critical path — the basis for finding *stochastic* (probabilistic) critical paths. **Cruciality** ("how big and how often") multiplies duration sensitivity by criticality; a key-milestone driver that is not on the overall network critical path can show 0% cruciality. The **Schedule Sensitivity Index (SSI)** combines criticality with activity duration standard deviation, favoring activities that are both often-critical and highly variable — but SSI can miss a low-variance, high-impact risk. A standing caveat: these are correlation measures (−1.0 to 1.0), not direct cost/schedule impact measures, and the ranking re-sorts as risks are added or removed.

**The JCL scatterplot.** Each Monte Carlo iteration yields one cost-schedule pair; plotting all pairs (date on x, cost on y) for, say, 5000 iterations gives 5000 equally likely points. The **Frontier Curve** is the locus of points sharing a given JCL value (e.g., 70%); points up-and-right exceed it, points down-and-left fall short. The single reported JCL is taken at the "knee in the curve" where cost and schedule have equal probability. The Frontier Curve asymptotically approaches the individual cost and schedule percentile lines. Vertical voids in the scatter are weekends and holidays (no possible finish). The four quadrants around the individual percentile lines partition outcomes into under/over cost × under/over schedule, each quadrant's share giving that joint probability. A handbook-noted relationship: the equal-percentile knee of a 70% JCL frontier typically corresponds to roughly the 78–84th percentile on an individual S-curve — which is why selecting the 80th percentile from an SRA S-curve traces cleanly back to Agency JCL guidance.

**The seven analysis applications.** Once verified, the SRA/ICSRA supports: (1) **Analysis of Alternatives** — comparing option S-curves, where a later deterministic date with a steeper curve (more certainty) can beat an earlier but flatter option; (2) **Stochastic Critical Path Analysis** — surfacing probabilistic critical paths that lurk behind the deterministic ones; (3) **Risk Sensitivity / Prioritization** — removing the top tornado risk and re-running iteratively to find true drivers (the source warns that ranking by correlation coefficient alone is a poor sensitivity measure for schedule); (4) **Confidence Level / Completion Range** — bounding the finish date, typically at 30%/70% (or 20%/80% for conservatism); (5) **JCL Analysis**; (6) **Margin Allocation and Sufficiency** — Case A tests whether existing margin suffices for a required date, Case B renegotiates the date to obtain adequate margin (e.g., plan to the 50th percentile, hold margin to the 70th); and (7) **Funding/Reserves Analysis** plus **Risk-Based Trend Analysis** — tracking margin and reserve adequacy across the life cycle.

## Mental Models

- **The simulation is a faithful idiot.** The Monte Carlo tool computes exactly what you feed it. It adds no wisdom and forgives no garbage. Every insight comes from how you shape the inputs and interpret the outputs — never from the tool "knowing" the answer.

- **Uncertainty is the floor; risk is the bump.** Uncertainty applies in every single iteration (no likelihood gate), so it sets the baseline spread of the schedule. Run *uncertainty-only* first to establish that floor, then layer discrete risks on top so you can see what each risk actually adds.

- **The hammock stretches like a real budget.** Picture the cost line strung between the first and last activity of a work band. When risk pushes those activities apart, the hammock sags wider and accrues more cost; when they pull in, it tightens. That is how the ICSRA turns schedule slip into dollars automatically — which is also exactly why you must not double-enter the time-dependent cost as a separate risk.

- **Deterministic gives you a date; the S-curve gives you a bet.** A single point estimate hides the odds. The S-curve replaces "we finish on 9 July" with "we have a 70% chance of finishing by 16 July," turning a claim into a probability you can manage against.

- **Steeper beats earlier.** When comparing alternatives, a steep S-curve means certainty. An option whose deterministic date is later but whose curve is steep can be the better choice than an earlier option with a shallow, long-tailed curve — because you can actually count on it.

- **Find the path that hides.** The deterministic top-three critical paths are what the scheduling tool shows you. The stochastic critical path is the one that becomes critical 32% of the time and ambushes you. The SRA exists to drag that hidden path into the light before it surprises management.

- **The knee is the honest point.** On the JCL scatterplot, the single defensible number is where cost and schedule are equally likely — the knee of the frontier. Anywhere else you are implicitly favoring one dimension over the other.

## Anti-patterns

The source explicitly names these failure modes:

- **Double counting.** Counting the same risk/uncertainty impact more than once: applying uncertainty to activities whose estimates (from parametric models, CERs/SERs, or past-P/p data) *already* embed similar risk; applying both a wide uncertainty distribution *and* a separate discrete risk for the same event; or entering a schedule-delay risk's time-dependent cost impact separately when duration uncertainty already drives it. Remedies named: walk the risk register with SMEs to disambiguate inherent risk; perform an explicit **"in/out" determination** per risk (retain "out" risks *deactivated but not deleted*, since the call is somewhat arbitrary); and document every removal or reduction in the BoE.

- **Double counting by over-mapping.** Linking one risk to every activity along a logic string, which multiplies its effect. The fix: map a discrete risk to the *first* activity in the affected string and let the impact ripple through the logic; use separate, individually identified risks for genuinely separate impacts on the same path.

- **Underestimating uncertainty (framing bias).** SMEs reliably capture only part of the true range; analysts overestimate best cases and underestimate worst cases. Compensate by deliberately *widening* the uncertainty distribution rather than accepting the elicited bounds.

- **Forgetting to zero out margin tasks.** Leaving margin-activity durations in the schedule during simulation effectively double-counts the risk — once in the margin task and once in the probabilistic impacts. Set margin tasks to zero (modeled as a "0" uncertainty distribution for traceability) so the simulated slip can be compared cleanly against allocated margin.

- **Leaving hard constraints in the model.** "Must Finish On," "Finish No Later Than," and similar constraints prevent activities from moving freely and corrupt the simulation; they must be found and replaced (e.g., with ASAP / Start No Earlier Than). The verification test for this: extend an at-risk activity's duration past its float and confirm the finish date moves by the right number of days — if negative float appears, a downstream hard constraint is present.

- **Including only the top / red-yellow risks.** Loading just the headline risks (or only those on the deterministic critical path) assumes you already know the critical path with certainty and discards the very insight probabilistic analysis exists to provide. Load *all* identified risks, including green ones, so clusters and alternate driving paths can surface.

- **Treating a milestone or a test as a mitigation.** Holding a Life-Cycle Review, running a test, "watching," or assigning a tiger team does not by itself lower a risk's likelihood or consequence — a test may merely *confirm* the risk. If a mitigation activity is irrational in this way, use pre-mitigated values. More generally, ignore claimed mitigation unless it is funded and traceable in the baseline plan, and re-assess after each mitigation step rather than blindly accepting the planned post-mitigation LxC.

- **Ranking risks by correlation coefficient alone.** Cited research shows this is a poor sensitivity measure, especially for schedule; prefer the iterative remove-the-top-risk-and-re-run approach, recognizing that the ranking re-sorts each time.

## Key Takeaways

- The SRA converts a deterministic IMS into a probability statement about achievability; the ICSRA adds cost to produce the JCL — the joint probability of meeting both cost and schedule targets.
- Build and verify the SRA *first*, then append the cost model to form the ICSRA. Use the IMS as the framework when feasible; drop to an Analysis Schedule only when the IMS is impractical, and prove the surrogate emulates the IMS (same milestone dates, same critical paths).
- Distinguish uncertainty (always applied, no likelihood) from discrete risk (likelihood × impact). Run uncertainty-only to set a baseline, then add risks.
- Model a risk as Bernoulli likelihood × an impact distribution (triangular or uniform), most often via a zero-duration delay milestone (Activity Duration Impact).
- Assess correlation always — ignoring it understates variance and overstates risk; default to 0.3 absent data, within an industry range of 0.25–0.75.
- Cost-load via TD/TI hammocks; never re-enter a schedule-delay risk's time-dependent cost separately.
- Read the S-curve's shape (slope/CV, dog-legs, tails, bimodality) and use sensitivity/criticality/cruciality — but treat them as correlations, not impacts.
- Take the JCL at the equal-probability knee of the frontier curve; an 80th-percentile pick on an individual S-curve traces back to the 70% JCL guidance.
- Guard relentlessly against double counting, hard constraints, non-zeroed margin, partial risk lists, and fictional mitigations — the source names each as a failure mode.
- Stage the analysis through trial → preliminary → final → update versions, archive inputs and outputs together for traceability, and document all assumptions in the BoE.

## Connects To

- **Schedule Assessment (Section 6.2 of the handbook).** Whether using the IMS or an Analysis Schedule, the schedule must first pass the Assessment checks (Health Check, Critical Path and Structural Check, Resource Integration Check) before it is fit to simulate; the SRA feeds the third "reliability" tier of Schedule Assessment, addressing margin sufficiency and affordability dimensions.
- **Schedule Development (Chapters 4–5).** The SRA consumes the BoE-documented duration and cost uncertainties, the Activity Attributes table's reserved text fields for risk parameters, and the cost/resource/budget-loading definitions; margin-setting practice (e.g., GSFC Gold Rules, JPL Design Principles) is what the margin-sufficiency analysis tests.
- **Risk Management (NPR 8000.4 and the P/p RMS).** The SRA draws its discrete risks, likelihoods, and consequences straight from the RMS, and best practice quantifies risk parameters *inside* the official RMS for traceability and configuration control; the analysis returns prioritized, risk-informed mitigation guidance.
- **Cost estimating (NASA Cost Estimating Handbook).** Supplies CERs, cost uncertainties, correlation guidance, and the policy clarification that "resource-loaded schedule" may be satisfied by cost loading; the ICSRA's cost model replicates (not recreates) the formal cost estimate.
- **Earned Value Management.** EVM indices (SPI, BEI, CEI, HMI; CPI for cost) are a source for adjusting uncertainty parameters, and the SRA's on-time-finish likelihood supplements standard cost-based EVM metrics with a risk-informed schedule indicator.
- **Agency commitment process (NPR 7120.5 / NPD 1000.5).** SRA/ICSRA outputs — schedule completion ranges, confidence levels, and the JCL — feed the establishment of the Management Agreement (MA) and Agency Baseline Commitment (ABC), and inform Confirmation Review / Authority-to-Proceed decisions including the unallocated future expense (UFE) needed to reach the required confidence level.
- **Tools and data infrastructure.** Execution relies on the NASA-licensed JACS and Polaris tools (and the Schedule Management Tools Matrix), the ONCE database for historical/normalized cost-schedule data and SER tools (SMART, NICM), and CADRe for historical risk lists — with results communicated and archived per the handbook's reporting and records-management guidance.
