# Chapter — Sensitivity, Risk, and Uncertainty Analysis (Steps 8-9)

## Core Idea
A point estimate, standing alone, tells decision-makers nothing about how confident they should be in it. Steps 8 and 9 of the GAO 12-step process exist to surround that single number with information about how it could move. **Sensitivity analysis (Step 8)** isolates the effect of changing one input at a time, revealing which assumptions and parameters the estimate is most responsive to. **Risk and uncertainty analysis (Step 9)** then lets many inputs vary together — using statistical simulation — to produce a *probability distribution* of total cost and a defensible **confidence level**. The two steps are complementary: sensitivity tells you which factors matter; risk and uncertainty tells you the realistic range and how much **contingency** to carry. Together they convert a deterministic estimate into a risk-adjusted one that supports credible budgeting.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)
- **Sensitivity analysis (Step 8 of the 12-step process)** — recalculates the estimate by substituting different quantitative values for selected inputs, then compares against the original. Applied as a best practice to *all* estimates, and especially to high-cost, high-risk elements. Used to find cost-sensitive design elements ("what-if analysis"), to optimize parameters, and to test how sensitive an analysis-of-alternatives ranking is to changing factors.
- **The five-step sensitivity procedure** — a named sequence the Guide prescribes: (1) identify the assumptions and parameters (including key cost drivers) to test; (2) re-estimate total cost by varying one factor between two set bounds (e.g., min/max or performance thresholds); (3) document the result; (4) repeat for every factor identified, one at a time; (5) evaluate which factors move the estimate most and document. Use whenever performing Step 8.
- **Tornado chart** — a bar-chart artifact (the Guide's figure 12) that ranks parameters by their positive or negative effect on the point estimate. Used as one method to *identify* factors for sensitivity testing, often borrowed from an existing risk/uncertainty analysis.
- **Risk and uncertainty analysis (Step 9 of the 12-step process)** — quantitative, statistically based analysis that predicts the probability of executing a program within budget by capturing the cumulative effect of program risks and uncertainty. Use after the point estimate exists, drawing on data collected in Steps 6-7.
- **Three-point estimate methodology** — assigns a minimum, most-likely, and maximum to each WBS element (the Guide's table 12 illustrates this for a UAV air vehicle), feeding a simulation. Use when capturing combined risk/uncertainty from historical data or SME inputs, accepting that you cannot trace results to individual risk events.
- **Risk driver approach** — quantifies the probability that each risk register entry occurs and its multiplicative effect on assigned WBS element costs; if a risk does not fire in an iteration, that element's cost is unchanged (the Guide's tables 13 and 14 show driver and uncertainty inputs for the UAV). Use to tie contingency to identifiable, mitigable risk events. The two methods can be combined — three-point for bias/uncertainty, risk drivers for discrete events — provided risks are not double-counted.
- **Monte Carlo simulation** — the engine for both methods: it repeatedly draws random values from each element's probability distribution, sums them per iteration, and runs thousands of iterations to produce an output distribution. Named by the Guide as the community best-practice approach (with the DoD/NASA Joint Agency Cost Schedule Risk and Uncertainty Handbook cited for alternatives such as scenario-based, method-of-moments, and risk-scoring methods).
- **Cumulative distribution function (S curve)** — converts the total-cost distribution into a curve mapping each cost value to its confidence percentile (the Guide's figures 14-16). Use to select a defensible confidence level and read off the corresponding budget and contingency.
- **Prediction interval statistical analysis** — the best-practice, objective method for bounding the probability distribution of a cost estimating relationship (CER); it captures regression error and yields a wider variance than naive bounds.
- **Risk register** — the program document listing identified risks (with handling actions, occurrence probability, and cost/schedule consequence). It is the starting point for risk interviews and the source of risk-driver inputs.

## Key Concepts

### Step 8 — Sensitivity
- **One factor at a time.** The defining mechanic of sensitivity analysis is varying a single parameter while holding everything else fixed, so the analyst can attribute the resulting change to that one factor. A factor is "sensitive" when a small change in its value produces a large change in total cost.
- **Sensitivity is graded for an analysis of alternatives.** When testing how robust an AoA ranking is, the Guide grades a parameter as *sensitive* if a 10-50 percent change flips the recommended alternative, and *very sensitive* if a change under 10 percent does so.
- **Identify factors with traceability, not guesswork.** Sources of variation must be documented and traceable; merely applying a subjective plus-or-minus percentage with no valid basis does not qualify as a sensitivity analysis. Factors commonly varied include life-cycle length; workload volume/mix/pattern; requirements and configuration changes; assumptions about operations, fielding, inflation, technology heritage, and development time; learning curves; performance characteristics; testing requirements; acquisition strategy; labor rates; and software size/reuse. Always include the factors most likely to change — especially assumptions made for lack of knowledge or outside the program office's control.
- **Find the key cost drivers.** One way is to compute each cost element's share of total cost and vary the inputs to the largest contributors. Analysts should also test parameters behind high-*risk* elements even when those elements are not large cost drivers, because they may become schedule drivers. There must be a clear link from technical-baseline parameters and assumptions to the cost-model inputs being varied.
- **Output is a range with reasons.** A sensitivity analysis spans a best-/worst-case range around the point estimate, accompanied by discrete explanations for what drives the high and low ends. In the Guide's worked example (figure 11), raising material quality in an aircraft adds roughly $1.668 million while shifting the learning curve from 91 to 88 percent removes about $60 million — illustrating which assumptions dominate. - **Known limitation.** Sensitivity analysis does not give an overall probable range, because it changes only one factor at a time. The real risk — many factors moving simultaneously — is what Step 9 addresses.

### Step 9 — Risk and Uncertainty
- **Risk vs. uncertainty are distinct.** A *risk* is a potential event that may or may not occur and whose outcome can be quantified; it can be a threat (harmful) or an opportunity (beneficial), and listable risks belong in the risk register. *Uncertainty* is always present and reflects situations where little is known about the outcome — from inherent variability, historical-data inconsistencies, estimating-equation error, and bias. Both must be accounted for.
- **Two sources of estimate-to-actual difference.** Beyond lack of knowledge about the future, there is inherent uncertainty from estimating error and estimating bias. Bias can be *cognitive* (estimator inexperience) or *motivational* (management deliberately lowering the estimate or shortening the schedule to make a program attractive). A complete, objective analysis is the counter to over-optimism.
- **Ranges narrow but levels can rise as a program matures.** Early on, the range of potential cost is wide because requirements are immature; as requirements firm up the range narrows — yet the estimate level itself can climb because clearer requirements expose more cost (the Guide's figure 13). Redraw this as a converging-band trend, not a copy of the figure.
- **The simulation mechanic.** Assign each WBS element (or risk driver) a probability distribution; statistical software draws randomly from each, sums per iteration, and repeats thousands of times. Because inputs are distributions, the output is a distribution — typically lognormal, since underlying element distributions tend to be right-skewed (large overruns are more probable than large underruns).
- **Reading the S curve for contingency.** The cumulative distribution maps cost values to confidence percentiles. In the Guide's small example, about a 40 percent chance the program lands at or below $825,000; the 50th percentile (median) is $908,000 with $83,000 contingency; the 70th percentile is $1,096,000, about $271,000 over the point estimate. Note the *mean* usually exceeds the 50th percentile because of right skew — so funding at the median still leaves a greater chance of a large overrun than a large underrun.
- **Three-point worked example.** For the UAV (32 air vehicles), summing most-likely WBS costs gives a $2.47 billion point estimate. After modeling risk (triangular distributions are typical), the 50th-percentile cost rises to $2.61 billion — $140 million above the point estimate — and there is only about a 15 percent chance of coming in at or below the point estimate. An 80 percent confidence target implies $2.79 billion. The drawback: a three-point range cannot attribute the distribution to specific risk events, so it yields contingency without telling you which risks drive it.
- **Risk-driver worked example.** Modeling the same program with discrete risk drivers (each a multiplicative factor applied only when the risk fires) widens the spread: roughly a 1 percent chance of landing at or below $2.47 billion, and an 80th-percentile cost of $3.08 billion including $615 million contingency. Drivers also let one risk touch multiple WBS elements and let one element be hit by multiple risks — and they automatically induce correlation among the elements they share.
- **Distribution shape matters.** Choosing the right distribution per element is a modeling decision; the shape should match the characteristics of the risk it represents (the Guide's appendix X catalogs shapes). For CERs, use prediction intervals to set objective bounds.
- **Correlation is mandatory.** Positively correlated elements (e.g., shared contractor productivity) move together; if the simulation is allowed to draw a low value for one and a high value for another, risk cancels out and the distribution is artificially narrow. Specifying correlation keeps each iteration internally consistent and widens the tails. In the Guide's example, adding 90 percent correlation barely moves the 50th percentile (~$2.611 to $2.612 billion) but raises the 80th percentile by about $24 million. Correlation matters most at upper percentiles (e.g., the 80th) and little near the 50th. The cited Joint Agency handbook notes default correlation suggestions of 0.25-0.63, recommending 0.3. The risk-driver method largely sidesteps manual pairwise correlation because shared drivers correlate elements automatically.
- **Collecting risk data.** Gather it alongside the point-estimate data when possible. Source categories include economic (labor/inflation), cost estimating (learning curves, CER error, optimistic process savings), programmatic (resource shortfalls), requirements (spec/quantity/architecture changes), schedule (test failures, optimistic tasks, procurement delays), and technology (unproven tech, optimistic reuse, design changes). Prefer historical data; otherwise use in-depth interviews or risk workshops with SMEs directly responsible for the work, across all organizational levels.
- **Interview integrity.** Start from the existing risk register, invite new risks, and guarantee anonymity and non-attribution so SMEs will state honest worst/best cases. Interview SMEs without an authority figure present to avoid motivational bias; a companion in the room means responses cannot be assured unbiased.
- **Adjusting optimistic experts.** Studies show SMEs at best capture about 70 percent of the true uncertainty range, so the distribution tails should be skewed wider — for example, by treating the experts' stated min and max as the 15th and 85th percentiles and adjusting. Organizations should publish default distribution bounds for when objective data are absent, and some experts recommend vetting the whole analysis through a core expert group.
- **Choosing a confidence level and contingency.** Contingency is the difference between the point estimate and the estimate at the chosen percentile. There is no universal "correct" confidence level, though budgeting to the mean of the S curve is common. For high-risk programs, a higher level (70-80 percent) increases the chance of staying within budget, provides for currently unknown risks, and reduces the chance of re-baselining. But budgeting *every* portfolio program to its 80th percentile can push the whole portfolio above the 95th percentile and make it unaffordable (the Guide cites Anderson's "The Trouble with Budgeting to the 80th Percentile").
- **Allocating, phasing, converting.** Selecting a contingency turns the estimate into a *risk-adjusted* estimate. Because percentiles do not sum (child elements at the 80th percentile exceed the parent's 80th percentile), contingency cannot be split equally or proportionally to cost; it must reflect each element's distribution magnitude and variance. Two named allocation methods: **allocation by standard deviation** (distribute the parent-minus-children gap in proportion to each child's standard deviation) and **allocation by need** (distribute in proportion to each element's probability of overrunning its point estimate, adjusted for correlation). Allocation is done before phasing. Phasing spreads contingency across future years — proportionally, by SME-judged risk-realization timing, or by analogy to historical programs (and software can auto-phase, but assumptions must be checked). Finally, convert to budget-year dollars with inflation indexes, exactly as for a point estimate (Step 7).
- **Update periodically.** The S curve only quantifies the imperfectly understood risks known at analysis time; unidentified threats and opportunities are not captured. Re-run the analysis at key decision points, for budget submissions, and especially before re-baselining or major risk-register changes.

## Mental Models
- **Sensitivity isolates; risk and uncertainty combine.** Step 8 turns one knob at a time to find what the estimate listens to. Step 9 turns all the knobs at once, randomly, thousands of times, to find where the estimate actually lands. Run Step 8 first to learn which knobs deserve careful distributions in Step 9.
- **The point estimate is a forecast, not a fact.** Because risk and uncertainty always exist, actuals will differ from the estimate. The deliverable that decision-makers can trust is a *range with a confidence reading*, not a single number.
- **The S curve is a price list for confidence.** Pick the percentile you can defend, read the cost, and the gap above the point estimate is the price (contingency) of that confidence. Higher confidence costs more contingency — and across a portfolio, buying high confidence everywhere bankrupts the portfolio.
- **Correlation is the anti-cancellation rule.** Without it, the simulation lets element risks cancel and lies to you with a tight, comfortable distribution. With it, elements that share a cause rise and fall together, and the tails tell the truth.
- **Two paths to the same distribution, different receipts.** Three-point gives you the *amount* of contingency but no itemization; risk drivers give you the same number *plus* an itemized list of which risks bought it — and therefore which ones you can mitigate to lower it.
- **Bias is a modeled quantity, not a character flaw.** Cognitive and motivational bias, and the 70-percent expert-coverage gap, are predictable enough to correct for by skewing the tails — treat optimism as a measurable input to be widened, not a judgment to be trusted.

## Anti-patterns
- **Subjective plus-or-minus with no basis.** The Guide explicitly rejects varying factors by an arbitrary percentage without a documented, traceable basis — it is not a valid sensitivity analysis, and it breaks the required link from technical baseline to cost-model inputs.
- **Skipping sensitivity analysis entirely.** Without it, stakeholders cannot tell which variable most affects the estimate, and decisions get made without understanding how assumptions hit cost (the JIAC/RAF Croughton case, where omitting sensitivity analysis dropped the estimate's credibility to "minimally met").
- **Scoping the risk analysis to only part of the cost.** Analyzing risk on a fraction of the life cycle prevents any defensible confidence level for the whole (the Coast Guard polar icebreaker case modeled risk only on detail design and construction, leaving ~75 percent of life-cycle cost unquantified; the 2020 Census case covered only ~37 percent of life-cycle cost and could not justify its 10 percent management-discretion contingency).
- **Risk data from arbitrary percentages.** Risk inputs must come from a quantitative risk assessment, not invented factors; otherwise the confidence level is meaningless.
- **Ignoring correlation.** Doing so understates the distribution's spread and manufactures false confidence — high-risk programs in particular should show wide ranges.
- **Trusting a poorly-executed analysis.** Low-quality data or a narrow focus on only the risks that worry the program office (rather than a broad set) gives management a false sense that all risks are covered, and decisions then rest on bad information.
- **Motivational bias in elicitation.** Interviewing SMEs with an authority figure present, or penalizing/ostracizing those whose worst case exceeds management's view, corrupts the inputs; anonymity and non-attribution are required.
- **Treating allocated contingency as a spending commitment.** Allocation is a modeling exercise; contingency stays with management until a specific need is realized, and will not be spent element-by-element as forecast.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Step 8 (Sensitivity)** varies one factor at a time to find which assumptions and parameters the estimate is most responsive to; its output is a documented best-/worst-case range with discrete reasons — not a subjective percentage swing.
2. The **five-step sensitivity procedure** (identify factors → re-estimate by varying one → document → repeat for all → evaluate) must trace each varied input back to the technical baseline.
3. **Step 9 (Risk and Uncertainty)** lets many factors vary together via **Monte Carlo simulation** to produce a probability distribution of total cost and an **S curve** of confidence levels.
4. **Risk** (a may-or-may-not event, quantifiable, threat or opportunity) and **uncertainty** (always present, from variability/error/bias) are distinct and both must be modeled.
5. Two modeling methods: **three-point estimates** (min/most-likely/max per element; gives contingency but not its drivers) and **risk drivers** (per-risk probability × multiplicative effect; ties contingency to mitigable events). They can be combined without double-counting.
6. **Correlation must be specified** or risks cancel and the distribution is falsely narrow; it matters most at upper percentiles.
7. **Contingency** = (estimate at chosen confidence percentile − point estimate); the percentile is a management choice, with the S-curve mean a common target and 70-80 percent used for high-risk programs — but not blanket across a portfolio.
8. The **risk-adjusted estimate** must then be **allocated** (by standard deviation or by need), **phased** across years, and **converted** to budget-year dollars — and the whole analysis re-run periodically, especially before re-baselining.
