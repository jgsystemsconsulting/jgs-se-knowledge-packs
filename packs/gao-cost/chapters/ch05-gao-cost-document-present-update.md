# Chapter — Documenting, Presenting, and Updating the Estimate (Steps 10-12)

## Core Idea

The final three steps of the GAO twelve-step process convert a finished set of numbers into a durable, defensible, and living management tool. Step 10 (Document the Estimate) captures every parameter, assumption, method, and calculation in enough detail that an analyst who knows nothing about the program could reconstruct the same result. Step 11 (Present the Estimate to Management) communicates that documented estimate clearly enough that approvers can judge its completeness and quality and formally accept it — an estimate is not considered finished until management approves it. Step 12 (Update the Estimate) keeps the estimate current by reconciling it against actual costs and program changes over the life cycle, so it remains a credible predictor and a benchmark for tracking progress. Together these steps protect the credibility of the entire estimating effort: without them, the analytical work done in Steps 1-9 cannot be validated, defended, or reused.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Step 10: Document the Estimate** (Chapter 13) — the documentation step. Used at the end of estimate development (and ideally in parallel with it) to produce a written justification showing how the estimate was built and how to update it.
- **Step 11: Present the Estimate to Management** (Chapter 14) — the approval step. Used to brief the documented life cycle cost estimate (LCCE) to the approving authority and obtain formal acceptance.
- **Step 12: Update the Estimate** (Chapter 15) — the maintenance step. Used continually as the program matures to refresh the estimate with actuals, variances, and program changes.
- **Cost Estimate Documentation Elements** (the source's Table 15) — a sectioned template for what a complete documentation package contains. The source lists these document sections: Cover page and table of contents; Executive summary; Introduction; System description; Program inputs; Estimating method and data by WBS cost element; Sensitivity analysis; Risk and uncertainty analysis; Management approval; and Updates reflecting actual costs and changes. The "estimating method and data by WBS cost element" section is described as the bulk of the documentation. (Embedded source table — redraw if needed; do not reproduce.)
- **Survey of each step** — each chapter closes with a "Survey of Step" block organized into Process Tasks, Best Practices, and Likely Effects if Criteria Are Not Fully Met. This recurring structure is how the Guide makes each step auditable.
- **The four reasons documentation is a best practice** (Chapter 13) — a named rationale set: documentation (1) validates and defends the estimate, (2) lets an unfamiliar analyst recreate or update it step by step, (3) supports analysis of cost changes and feeds a reusable cost/technical data history, and (4) enables an effective independent review and reconciliation with an independent cost estimate (ICE).

External authorities the source cites in these chapters: **OMB's Capital Programming Guide** (Supplement to Circular A-11, Part 7), cited in Step 12 as making regular updating not just sound practice but a requirement. The chapters also reference an **independent cost estimate (ICE)**, **work breakdown structure (WBS)**, **cost estimating relationships (CERs)**, **earned value management (EVM)** and **estimates at completion (EACs)** as inputs to documentation and updating.

## Key Concepts

- **Documentation provides total recall.** The core function of Step 10 documentation is to capture the estimate's detail so completely that someone other than the original preparers can replicate it. It doubles as a reference for future estimates and as a written justification of how the estimate was developed.
- **Narrative plus cost tables.** The recommended documentation format pairs a written narrative with cost tables, focused on the methods and calculations used to derive each number. The narrative must also explain *why* particular methods and data sets were chosen, why those choices are reasonable, and the pros and cons of each method.
- **Audit trail.** Good documentation contains enough backup data, methods, and results to serve as an audit trail, so a program's costs can be tracked cleanly across life cycle phases. A reviewer should be able to follow any number — its data, the calculation behind it, the modeling assumptions, and the rationale — all the way to an originating source document and so verify and validate it.
- **Two quality criteria.** The source names two criteria for high-quality documentation: it must describe the process, data sources, and methods in enough detail to let analysts easily reconstruct the estimate; and it must present results in a format that makes briefings and reports to management straightforward.
- **Documentation need not be a single written report.** Some organizations use cost models that generate documentation automatically; others use detailed spreadsheets with notes and hyperlinks. What matters is traceability back to source documents, plus a stored electronic copy of the supporting cost model so updates can be done efficiently and authorized personnel can find it later.
- **Consistency between baseline and estimate.** The technical baseline description and the technical data carried in the documentation must be consistent with the cost estimate itself.
- **Presentation must mirror documentation.** What is briefed to management must match the documented estimate, succinctly illustrating the main cost drivers and final cost, broken out across each program phase — development, production, operations and maintenance, and disposal. The brief should also surface concerns, challenges, and whether adequate time and resources were available to build the estimate.
- **Acceptance is a recorded event.** Cost estimators should explicitly ask whether management accepts the estimate and at what desired confidence level. Acceptance and any feedback are acted on and recorded back into the documentation package, which becomes the evidence that the estimate was reviewed and approved. The source notes "management" varies by organization but typically means the requester of funds — program manager, program executive officer, or acquisition authority.
- **Updating is recurrent, not one-time.** Programs (especially developmental ones) evolve, so the estimate must be revised at milestones, when requirements change, and as actual costs arrive. Proposed program changes should first be examined for their advantages and cost effects; if accepted, they are managed and controlled so the baseline keeps representing current reality.
- **Variance discipline.** Updating means recording reasons for variances and reconciling current-versus-old estimates against the prior baseline, including incorporating EVM EACs and independent EACs where applicable. That documented comparison is what lets estimators gauge both the quality of their own estimating and the way the program is shifting over time.
- **Archiving for reuse.** Actual cost, technical, and historical schedule data (including items like source-lines-of-code sizing, effort, and risk items) should be archived in a database so future programs can draw on them.

## Mental Models

- **Documentation as a recipe, not a result.** The test is whether a stranger to the program could follow the documentation and arrive at the same answer. If they can't recreate it step by step, it isn't documented — it's just a number with a story attached.
- **Write as you go.** The source is explicit that documentation should not be a last-minute effort. Left to the end, the rationale and judgments behind the estimate are hard to recapture and easy to lose, which creates credibility gaps. Documentation done in parallel with development keeps the data, methods, and rationale defensible — and "more is preferred over too little."
- **Approval is a confidence transfer.** Presenting the estimate clearly and consistently is what moves confidence from the estimator to the decision-maker. Communicating ground rules, methods, and results simply lets people unfamiliar with the estimate gauge its competence; muddled communication denies management the basis to trust it.
- **A static estimate decays.** An estimate that is never updated loses its value as a predictor and as a yardstick for tracking progress. The SLS case (below) makes the point concrete: an estimate frozen while the program absorbed years of new performance data was judged less reliable as a management and oversight tool.
- **The three-part survey lens.** For each of these steps, ask the same three questions the Guide asks: What process tasks were performed? Were the best-practice criteria met? And what happens if they were not? This frames documentation, presentation, and updating as auditable, not optional.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes through its "Likely Effects if Criteria Are Not Fully Met" sections and supporting case studies:

- **Undocumented or thinly documented estimates** are not useful for updates or information sharing and hinder understanding and proper use. Without good documentation, management and oversight cannot be convinced the estimate is credible, supporting data won't seed a historical database, questions about approach or data can't be answered, lessons learned and the history of cost changes go unrecorded, and the scope of the analysis stays undefined. Such an estimate may simply not be defensible. (Case Study 19, VA Construction / GAO-18-479: VHA guidance required supporting documents only after a project was approved and did not require all detail — parameters, assumptions, descriptions, methods, calculations — raising the prospect of unreliable estimates affecting funding decisions.)
- **Leaving documentation to the end** increases the chance of overlooking important information and losing the rationale, causing credibility issues.
- **Under-informing management at the brief.** If management isn't given enough about technical characteristics, assumptions, data, methodologies, sensitivity, and risk and uncertainty, it cannot have confidence the estimate is complete and high quality.
- **Failing to update.** Without regular updates, it becomes difficult to analyze cost changes, harder to collect data for future estimates, and impossible to give decision-makers accurate information for assessing alternatives. Without a documented current-versus-old comparison, estimators cannot quantify the variance between successive estimates — leaving them blind both to the quality of their estimating and to how the program is shifting. (Case Study 20, Space Launch System / GAO-15-596: NASA had not refreshed its cost and schedule estimates; the estimate met five of the six relevant best practices substantially but still fell short of a fully reliable rating, the estimate omitted realized risks and roughly two years of additional contractor performance data. GAO recommended at least annual updates reflecting actuals and recorded variance reasons before each budget request.)

## Key Takeaways

- An estimate isn't done when the math is done — it's done when it is documented to the point of total recall, briefed to and accepted by management, and placed under a regime of regular updates.
- Document in parallel with development, pairing narrative and cost tables, and justify why each method and data set was chosen; the standard is reconstructability by an unfamiliar analyst.
- Use a sectioned documentation package (cover/TOC, executive summary, introduction, system description, program inputs, estimating method and data by WBS element, sensitivity analysis, risk and uncertainty analysis, management approval, and updates), keeping the technical baseline consistent throughout and storing the supporting cost model electronically.
- Presentation content must mirror the documentation and span every program phase; obtaining and recording management's acceptance (and desired confidence level) is the formal close of the estimating effort.
- Updating is a recurrent discipline tied to milestones, requirement changes, and actuals — record variance reasons, reconcile against the old baseline, fold in EVM/EAC data where applicable, and archive cost and technical data for future estimates.
- Regular updating is reinforced by OMB's Capital Programming Guide as a requirement, not merely a good habit.
