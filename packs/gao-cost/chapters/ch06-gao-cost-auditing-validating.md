# Chapter — Auditing and Validating the Cost Estimate

## Core Idea
The four characteristics of a high-quality estimate and the 18 best practices are not just authoring guidance — they double as **audit criteria**. By treating each best practice as a measurable expectation, an auditor or independent evaluator can establish two things: (1) whether a particular life cycle cost estimate is *reliable*, and (2) whether an organization's cost-estimating process, guidance, and regulations are thorough enough to reliably produce such estimates. The chapter inverts the rest of the Guide: where earlier chapters told estimators what to do, this one tells reviewers what to look for and how to grade what they find.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The four characteristics as gradable criteria** — comprehensive, well documented, accurate, and credible. The chapter restates each characteristic as a yardstick a reviewer applies, then maps the 18 best practices underneath the characteristic they support.
  - When/how: an auditor scores the estimate against each best practice, rolls the per-practice judgments up to the characteristic level, and rolls those up to an overall reliability verdict. An estimate that fully reflects all four characteristics is deemed *reliable*.
- **Best-practice-to-characteristic mapping (Figures 18–21)** — four figures pair the relevant estimating steps and best practices with each characteristic: comprehensive (Fig. 18), well documented (Fig. 19), accurate (Fig. 20), and credible (Fig. 21).
  - When/how: used to know which of the 12 steps and which best practices a reviewer must examine to defend a judgment about a given characteristic. *(These embedded figures are GAO-authored mappings; redraw them rather than reproduce them.)*
- **The 12-step process and per-step task lists as a guidance-assessment tool (Table 17)** — the same 12 steps and the task lists that close each chapter let an organization (or its auditor) check whether internal cost-estimating guidance, policy, and directives cover the full process.
  - When/how: an evaluator compares an agency's written guidance against the 12 steps and their tasks to find gaps; a step the guidance fails to require is a gap in the process, not just in one estimate.
- **Government Auditing Standards definition of "criteria"** — the chapter borrows the formal audit meaning of criteria: the laws, regulations, contracts, standards, requirements, measures, and benchmarks against which performance is judged (GAO-18-568G).
  - When/how: criteria give context for evaluating evidence and framing findings; auditors must pick criteria relevant to the audit objective and confirm the criteria are still valid for current conditions.
- **Select laws and regulations as criteria sources (Table 16)** — a catalog of statutes and rules that supply cost/EVM criteria for federal agencies (e.g., the Weapon Systems Acquisition Reform Act of 2009, Nunn-McCurdy unit-cost reporting under 10 U.S.C. § 2433, the Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act, Clinger-Cohen, the FAR/DFARS EVM provisions, GPRA Modernization Act, and the American Innovation and Competitiveness Act).
  - When/how: a starting point for identifying engagement-specific criteria; the Guide warns auditors to check for newer legislation each engagement. *(Table 16 is a GAO compilation; redraw as a paraphrased table, do not copy verbatim.)*
- **The sample data collection instrument (Appendix IV)** — a questionnaire-style instrument, paired with the reasons each item is needed, for gathering the evidence an audit requires.
  - When/how: auditors collect estimate and EVM data via questionnaires, structured interviews, direct observation, or independent computation, then evaluate that data for integrity and quality.

## Key Concepts
- **Reliability, defined for audit purposes.** From the auditing perspective, data are reliable when they are reasonably complete and accurate, fit the intended purpose, and have not been improperly altered. This is a narrower, evidence-centric meaning than the everyday sense of the word.
- **What each characteristic demands of a reviewer.**
  - *Comprehensive*: confirm the estimate captures the full technical scope via a product-oriented WBS (at least three levels of indenture, with a WBS dictionary, lower levels summing to higher), covers the entire life cycle for both government and contractor across all funding sources, ties to a documented technical baseline, and documents every cost-influencing ground rule and assumption with its rationale and associated risk.
  - *Well documented*: confirm the documentation names the source data, its reliability, and the estimating method for each element; describes how data were normalized and which inflation indexes converted constant-year to budget-year dollars; lets an unfamiliar analyst replicate the estimate (narrative plus cost tables, executive summary, sensitivity and risk results, and an electronic copy of the cost model); stays consistent with the technical baseline; and records management's review and acceptance.
  - *Accurate*: confirm the math is sound (elements sum correctly, figures agree across documents), the right inflation rates were applied and costs converted to budget-year dollars, the estimating technique chosen for each element fits its method (analogy adjustments justified; build-up backed by a parts list and auditable labor rates; parametric/CER models calibrated and validated on a homogeneous, sufficiently sized dataset with inputs inside the valid range; learning curves applied only to repetitive labor with production breaks accounted for; expert opinion used sparingly with bias acknowledged), the estimate is grounded in applicable historical data from comparable programs, and it is updated regularly with variances between planned and actual costs documented, explained, and reviewed.
  - *Credible*: confirm a sensitivity analysis varied the major cost drivers (not an arbitrary plus/minus with no basis), a risk and uncertainty analysis modeled per-element probability distributions and correlation — typically via Monte Carlo — to produce an S-curve and tie the point estimate to a cumulative probability and a defensible contingency, high-value elements were cross-checked with alternative methods, and the estimate was reconciled against an independent cost estimate.
- **Roll-up logic.** Best-practice judgments aggregate to the characteristic level; the four characteristic verdicts aggregate to the overall reliability verdict. A "partial" finding on any characteristic prevents an unqualified reliability claim (the Ford-Class case shows this in action).
- **Three distinct audit products.** Using the task lists and best practices, evaluators can (1) assess one estimate's reliability, (2) evaluate how well an organization's processes address best practices, and (3) independently validate an estimate.
- **The independent cost estimate (ICE) as the strongest validation lever.** Performed outside the program office's influence, built on the *same* technical baseline and ground rules, deep enough to reconcile against the program estimate, with documented and justified differences. ICEs carry less organizational bias, tend to fold in more risk, and so often forecast higher costs — making an un-reconciled estimate a candidate for proceeding underfunded.
- **EVM-specific evidence.** Reviewers should obtain the EVM system compliance/certification letter showing the contractor satisfies the 32 EVM guidelines (the guidelines act as test points for system quality), examine contract performance reports for reasonableness and consistency, request integrated baseline review (IBR) results to see identified risks, look for internal use of EVM data (not just external reporting), and confirm trained staff perform surveillance regularly. EVM data should derive from a system validated against EIA-748.
- **Data normalization and the "do not over-scrub" rule.** Auditors should probe how historical data were normalized for program redesigns, schedule slips, and budget cuts — but the cost community's view is that genuine program *inefficiencies* should be left in, because complex developments normally hit problems and scrubbing them out understates future risk.

## Mental Models
- **The estimate is the witness; the best practices are the cross-examination.** Each best practice is a question with an expected answer. The estimate either produces the evidence or it does not — judgment hangs on what is documented and traceable, not on the estimator's reassurance.
- **Two audit lenses, never conflate them.** One lens grades a single *estimate* (is this number reliable?); the other grades the *guidance* that produces estimates (will this organization reliably make good numbers at all?). The first uses the four characteristics; the second uses the 12 steps and task lists. Case Study 21 (Ford-Class CVN-79) is the first lens; Case Study 22 (DOE/NNSA guidance) is the second.
- **Converging evidence beats a single source.** Confidence comes from multiple independent data sources pointing at the same number; a lone source is a single point of failure for the whole estimate.
- **A characteristic is only as strong as its weakest practice.** "Substantially comprehensive but only partially documented, accurate, and credible" still yields *unreliable* — partial credit on the practices does not buy a passing characteristic.
- **Criteria can expire.** Borrowed laws, regulations, and standards may no longer reflect sound management or current conditions; a diligent auditor revalidates criteria each engagement rather than assuming last year's list still holds.

## Anti-patterns
- **A subjective plus/minus passed off as sensitivity analysis.** Varying factors by an arbitrary percentage with no valid basis is explicitly *not* a sensitivity analysis; the variation must trace to the technical baseline parameters and supporting data.
- **Guidance that mandates only part of the process.** The DOE example (Case Study 22) required just one of the 12 best-practice steps — an ICE at two decision points for projects of $100M or more — while omitting risk-and-uncertainty analysis, ground rules and assumptions, documentation, the point estimate, and the estimating structure. Suggesting good practice in an optional companion guide while *requiring* almost none of it makes reliable estimates unlikely.
- **Over-normalizing historical data.** Stripping out the effects of past program problems removes realism and injects unnecessary risk into the new estimate.

## Key Takeaways
1. The 18 best practices and four characteristics serve as **audit criteria**: grade each practice, roll up to the characteristic, roll up to a single reliability verdict.
2. An estimate is **reliable** only when it fully reflects all four characteristics — comprehensive, well documented, accurate, and credible; any "partial" blocks the claim.
3. The same toolkit supports **three jobs**: assessing one estimate, evaluating an organization's process/guidance, and independently validating an estimate.
4. Use the **12 steps and per-step task lists (Table 17)** to audit *guidance*; use the **characteristics and Figures 18–21 mappings** to audit a *specific estimate*.
5. **Criteria** carry their formal Government Auditing Standards meaning; auditors must pick relevant criteria, confirm they are still valid, and check for new statutes each engagement (Table 16 is the starting catalog).
6. The **ICE** is among the most reliable validation methods — outside influence, same baseline and ground rules, reconciled with documented differences.
7. For **EVM**, demand the 32-guideline compliance letter, EIA-748-validated data, CPRs, IBR results, evidence of internal use, and trained surveillance staff.
8. Gather evidence by **questionnaire, interview, observation, or independent computation**, then test it for integrity — and look for **converging sources** rather than trusting a single one.
