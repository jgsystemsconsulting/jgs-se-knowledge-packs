# Chapter — Earned Value Management

## Core Idea

Earned value management (EVM) is the discipline of measuring the budgeted value of work actually accomplished in a period, then comparing that value against the budgeted value of work that was scheduled and against the actual cost of the work performed. The Guide's central argument is that spending data alone misleads. Its railroad example makes this concrete: a contract for four miles of track in four weeks for $4 million has spent only $2 million after three weeks, which looks like an underrun — but only one mile has been laid, so the work is just 25 percent complete, the projected cost is $8 million, and the schedule will roughly triple. Earned value supplies the missing third number that turns a spending report into an objective read on program health. By deriving cost and schedule metrics from these three quantities, EVM can warn managers of trouble far earlier than expenditures can, while the window to recover is still open. The Guide frames EVM as the management-execution counterpart to the cost estimate: the estimate says what a program could cost, and EVM measures whether the program is delivering against a time-phased baseline built from that estimate. Linking the two — so cost estimators and EVM analysts share data rather than working in isolation — is itself a named best practice.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **EIA-748** (the EVM systems national standard, jointly established by ANSI and the Electronic Industries Alliance in 1998; the Guide cites the most recent revision, **EIA-748-D**, of January 2019). It defines the **32 guidelines** an EVM system must satisfy. Used as the compliance benchmark for validating, accepting, and surveilling any EVM system.
- **The 13 fundamental EVM activities** (Chapter 18). The Guide's decomposition of the EVM process: (1) bound the scope through a WBS; (2) assign each piece of work to a performer; (3) lay the work out against a timeline; (4) cost out the resources, then authorize the budgets; (5) fix objective earned-value measures up front; (6) stand up the performance measurement baseline; (7) run the plan while capturing actual costs; (8) read the performance data and log the variances; (9) project EACs from the EVM data; (10) run an integrated cost-and-schedule risk analysis; (11) set the EVM EAC against the EAC the risk analysis yields; (12) act on the risk through management decisions; and (13) revise the baseline whenever changes land.
- **Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB)** — the time-phased, cumulative budget plan (typically an S-curve) against which performance is measured. It excludes management reserve and fee, so it does not equal the contract value. PMB plus management reserve forms the **contract budget base (CBB)**; adding fee yields the total contract price.
- **Integrated Baseline Review (IBR)** — a joint government/contractor review conducted before or soon after award (and at major modifications) to verify the PMB is realistic and that risks are understood. The Guide calls the IBR the crucial link between cost estimating and EVM, because it confirms the estimate was converted into an executable plan.
- **Contract Performance Report (CPR)** — the standardized report that is the primary source of cost and schedule status. It has five formats (Format 1: cost/schedule by product-oriented WBS; Format 2: same data by organizational breakdown structure, sometimes optional; Format 3: budget baseline and forecasts; Format 4: forecast staffing; Format 5: narrative variance explanations). The Guide notes that within DoD this standard CPR has been superseded by the **Integrated Program Management Report (IPMR)** (data item DI-MGMT-81861A), which carries two further formats, 6 and 7.
- **The core EVM measures**: budgeted cost for work scheduled (**BCWS**, planned value), budgeted cost for work performed (**BCWP**, earned value), and actual cost of work performed (**ACWP**). The Guide notes some texts instead use PV, EV, and AC. Derived terms include budget at completion (**BAC**), estimate at completion (**EAC**), budgeted cost of work remaining (**BCWR** = BAC − BCWP), and variance at completion (BAC − EAC).
- **The three performance indexes** (the source's Table 23): **CPI** = BCWP/ACWP, **SPI** = BCWP/BCWS, and **TCPI** = BCWR/(EAC − ACWP). Values above 1.0 are favorable; the TCPI states the efficiency required on remaining work to hit a target.
- **EAC index-based formula and the "best predictor by completion stage" table** (Table 24, attributed to DOD). EAC = ACWP (cumulative) + (BAC − BCWP (cumulative)) / efficiency index. The efficiency index varies — cumulative CPI tends toward the lowest/best-case EAC; the schedule cost index (a form of CPI × SPI) compounds cost and schedule problems and is the worst-case predictor. The table maps which factor predicts best early, middle, and late in a program; it also names the **Rayleigh model** (nonlinear regression of ACWP against time) and regression-based EACs.
- **The methods for measuring earned value** (Table 21, attributed to PMI's *Practice Standard for Earned Value Management*, 2nd ed.): fixed formula (0/100, 50/50, etc.), percent complete, weighted milestone, and physical measurement; plus the two subjective methods, **level of effort (LOE)** and **apportioned effort**, to be used sparingly.
- **EVM system acceptance process** (Chapter 20, four phases): system design/implementation, then the **Self-Assessment Review (SAR)**, the optional **Progress Assessment Review (PAR)**, and the independent **Compliance Evaluation Review (CER)**, which relies on the NDIA EVMS Intent Guide to judge EIA-748 compliance.
- **Over-target baseline (OTB) and over-target schedule (OTS)** — a formal reprogramming that adds budget above the contract's negotiated cost (and/or extends schedule past contract completion) to restore a meaningful basis for measurement. The sum of PMB, undistributed budget, and management reserve is the **total allocated budget (TAB)**; TAB minus CBB is the over-target baseline. The **single point adjustment** is the technique that eliminates cumulative variances and replans remaining work to start measurement afresh.
- **Three high-level characteristics of a reliable EVM system** (the Guide's closing framework, Table 30): **Comprehensive**, **Accurate**, and **Informative**, each backed by named best practices.

The Guide also lists external implementation guides in its Table 20 (DOD, NDIA/IPMD, DOE, FAA) and cites OMB's **Capital Programming Guide** (Supplement to Circular A-11, Part 7), the **Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act of 1994**, the FAR (48 C.F.R. subpart 34.2), and GAO's own **Schedule Assessment Guide** (GAO-16-89G). The Defense Contract Management Agency (DCMA) is named as DOD's surveillance and validation body, with no civilian equivalent. ## Key Concepts

- **The three quantities and two variances.** All of EVM rests on BCWS (what should have been done), BCWP (what was done, valued at budget), and ACWP (what it cost). **Cost variance** = BCWP − ACWP; **schedule variance** = BCWP − BCWS. Positive variances mean underrunning cost or working ahead; negative means overrunning or falling behind. Crucially, schedule variance in EVM is expressed in dollars, not time, and should always be checked against the critical path to learn whether a delay actually matters.
- **Program-level EVM beats contract-level EVM.** Implementing EVM across the whole program — not just the contractor's piece — is a best practice and supports federal policy (FASA 1994 set the goal that agencies achieve, on average, 90 percent of cost, performance, and schedule goals). Program-level EVM holds government functional staff accountable, forces a risk-adjusted budget, and surfaces key decision points early.
- **Management reserve is for unknowns, held outside the PMB.** It covers risks unknown at start, can only be applied to in-scope work, and cannot be used to erase existing variances. Programs typically set aside 5-10 percent, ideally derived from cost and schedule risk analysis. When a risk is realized, reserve is distributed into the appropriate control account.
- **Control accounts are the focal point.** Where the WBS and the organizational breakdown structure (OBS) intersect, control accounts are created, each with a control account manager who owns the work. Actual costs are collected and variances reported at this level. Control accounts break into near-term **work packages** (typically 4-6 weeks) and far-term **planning packages**, with budgets assigned to both so total cost is known up front.
- **Rolling-wave planning.** Detailed planning proceeds in waves — near-term work in detailed work packages, far-term work held in planning packages and converted as it approaches. The Guide warns that planning to arbitrary calendar increments (e.g., six months) can leave insufficient detail; a best practice is to plan the wave to a design review, test, or other major milestone instead.
- **Objective measurement matters; LOE is a red flag.** Earned value must be taken with objective, pre-defined measures. Level of effort (time-based, no tangible deliverable) and apportioned effort (proportional to a discrete activity) are subjective and should be used only when nothing better fits. If more than roughly 15 percent of budget is LOE, it warrants scrutiny — though no percentage is a hard threshold.
- **The CPR is the workhorse, and Formats 1 and 5 do the heavy lifting.** Format 1 exposes lower-level control-account variances; Format 5 explains their root causes, corrective actions, and impacts. The Guide is emphatic that a Format 5 lacking real root-cause detail strips the EVM data of management value.
- **The 15-20 percent rule.** Studies of more than 700 defense programs found limited opportunity to recover a program once it is 15-20 percent complete; performance indicators at the 15 percent mark can predict the final outcome, and the cumulative CPI stabilizes (varying less than 10 percent) after about 20 percent complete and usually worsens thereafter.
- **EAC discipline.** Develop a *range* of EACs, at the program level and at lower WBS levels (so poor performers are not masked), at least monthly. Validate the EVM data before projecting. Compare the index-based EAC range against the contractor's bottom-up EAC and against an integrated cost-schedule risk analysis EAC; agreement between independent methods builds confidence, divergence triggers review. Use TCPI-versus-CPI as a reasonableness test — a rule of thumb is that a TCPI more than five points above CPI signals an over-optimistic EAC.
- **Validation, surveillance, and a clean audit trail.** A system must first be validated/accepted against EIA-748 (SAR → optional PAR → independent CER), then kept honest by periodic surveillance — document reviews, data traces, spot checks, and control-account-manager interviews — selecting programs for review by risk. DCMA performs this for DOD; civilian agencies must arrange an independent function (e.g., an inspector general or interagency agreement).
- **Over-target baselines are a last resort.** When the remaining baseline is so unrealistic that variances obscure new, still-mitigable problems, an OTB/OTS formally adds budget (and possibly schedule) and usually zeroes historical variances via a single point adjustment. ACWP is never altered and must reconcile to accounting records. Sensible windows: the contract should be 20-85 percent complete, with projected growth over ~15 percent and more than a year of work remaining, and the benefits must outweigh the cost.

## Mental Models

- **Cost without earned value is half a story.** Comparing planned spending to actual spending tells you nothing about whether the money bought the work it was supposed to. The railroad example is the canonical proof: the "underrun" was actually a program heading for double its budget and triple its schedule. Always carry all three numbers — BCWS, BCWP, ACWP — together.
- **The baseline is a recipe with a clock.** The PMB is not just a budget; it is the resource-consumption plan spread over the schedule into an S-curve. Watch its shape for pathologies: **front-loading** (too much budget in early tasks, hiding later shortfalls) and the **rubber baseline** (continually shifting budget to chase actuals so variances never appear). Both defeat the early-warning purpose.
- **Budget is the plan, not the funding.** A control account's budget is fully earned at 100 percent complete regardless of what it actually cost. Past budget, earned value, and actual cost must stay together to preserve reporting integrity. An underrunning control account can signal that future packages are over-budgeted, and that surplus can be recalled into management reserve for critical-path work.
- **Read variances against the network, not in the abstract.** A negative schedule variance off the critical path may not move the finish date; one on (or about to reach) the critical path will. And schedule variance self-corrects at completion (when work done equals work planned), whereas cost variance generally does not — overruns rarely reverse. Because schedule slips usually drag cost behind them, the schedule cost index (CPI × SPI) is treated as the pessimistic forecast.
- **Performance early predicts performance late.** Because near-term budgets are better understood than far-term ones, all contracts are front-loaded to some degree and early indicators are credible. The corollary: a contractor predicting no overrun while sitting on a large cumulative negative cost variance (the airborne-laser case) is almost certainly being optimistic, and an independent EAC range will expose it.
- **The right EAC factor changes with the program's age.** No single efficiency factor is best throughout. SPI carries predictive weight early but loses it later (returning to 1.0 at completion); short-period averages (e.g., three months) beat longer ones in the middle when burn is high; cumulative CPI yields a best-case EAC. Pick the factor to the stage, and bracket the answer with several.
- **A baseline that lies is worse than no baseline.** The Guide's recurring "Likely Effects" warnings frame the whole chapter: undetected anomalies skew metrics; un-updated baselines stop reflecting reality; LOE overuse hollows out objectivity. The reliable-system test reduces to three questions — is it comprehensive (validated, IBR'd, scheduled, surveilled), accurate (anomaly-free, consistent across formats, realistic EACs), and informative (actually used by management to drive corrective action)?

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes, chiefly through its "Likely Effects if Criteria Are Not Fully Met" section, its eleven common concerns about EVM, and its case studies:

- **Front-loaded baseline** — a disproportionate share of budget allocated to early tasks, leaving too little for far-term work; it hides problems until too late to correct, risking severe overrun in later phases.
- **Rubber baseline** — continually shifting the baseline budget to match actual expenditures so cost variances are masked; it produces deceptive baselines and delays insight until problems are nearly impossible to mitigate.
- **Outdated baseline** — failing to incorporate changes quickly, so variances stop reflecting reality and the EVM data lose meaning.
- **Excessive level of effort** — reporting a high share of budget as LOE produces non-objective data and the system will not perform as intended.
- **Weak Format 5 / no root-cause analysis** — variance narratives that describe problems only at a high level without root causes or mitigation plans make the data useless for management. (Case Study 24, Navy Shipbuilding / GAO-05-183: several ship programs reported only high-level reasons for variances, hampering corrective action; GAO recommended requiring variance reports that identify root causes, mitigation, and future cost impacts.)
- **Unreconciled data anomalies** — negative work-performed values, ACWP with no BCWP, BCWP with no BCWS, and similar inconsistencies distort EACs. (Case Study 25, James Webb Space Telescope / GAO-16-112: contractor EVM data contained recurring undocumented anomalies and optimistic EACs over a 17-month span, undermining reliability; GAO recommended requiring contractors to identify, explain, and document every anomaly.)
- **Baseline divorced from external commitments** — measuring only against aggressive internal targets while the committed baseline goes untracked. (Case Study 23, Space Launch System / GAO-15-596: NASA's EVM tracked an internal December 2017 launch date rather than the committed November 2018 date, so growth against the external commitment was neither reported nor apparent, leaving decision-makers without early insight.)
- **Using an uncertified EVM system** — relying on data from a system never validated against EIA-748 gives no assurance the data are reliable. (Case Study 26, NASA / GAO-13-22: more than half of ten major spaceflight projects lacked a fully certified system.)
- **EVM measures as award-fee criteria** — tying award fee to variances or indexes pushes contractors to hit a number rather than achieve outcomes, encouraging overstated performance, baseline manipulation, front-loading, or miscategorizing discrete work as LOE to hide variances until the last moment; tying award fee to dates for management milestones like the IBR can force a premature review.
- **Recurrent over-target baselines / frequent single point adjustments** — repeated rebaselining suggests poorly understood scope or weak EVM discipline and can look like a program "getting well" by hiding real performance; frequent single point adjustments distort metrics, produce unreliable index-based EACs, and hide true variances. (Case Study 27, Airborne Laser / GAO-04-643R: repeated restructurings and rebaselines roughly doubled program cost amid unforeseen technology complexity, eroding decision-makers' confidence.)
- **The eleven common concerns** — the Guide catalogs and rebuts eleven recurring objections (e.g., "EVM is too expensive," "not useful for small projects," "merely a government reporting requirement," "data are backward-looking," "variances are always bad," "we use Agile so we don't need it"), treating each as a misconception rather than a valid reason to skip EVM. Notably, it frames variances themselves as neither good nor bad — just a measure of deviation worth inspecting once they cross a threshold.

## Key Takeaways

- EVM works by carrying three numbers together — planned value (BCWS), earned value (BCWP), and actual cost (ACWP) — and reading cost variance (BCWP − ACWP) and schedule variance (BCWP − BCWS) from them; spending data alone hides whether work is actually getting done.
- The process is a 13-activity arc from "define scope with a WBS" through "update the baseline as changes occur," anchored by a realistic, time-phased performance measurement baseline that excludes management reserve and fee.
- Build the baseline on objective earned-value measures and validate it with an Integrated Baseline Review — the crucial link that confirms the cost estimate became an executable plan — then manage from the CPR, doing the real analysis in Formats 1 and 5.
- Recovery opportunity collapses after a program is 15-20 percent complete and the cumulative CPI stabilizes after ~20 percent, so early, honest indicators are decisive; develop a *range* of EACs monthly, at program and lower WBS levels, and reasonableness-check them with TCPI-versus-CPI and an independent risk-analysis EAC.
- A reliable EVM system is comprehensive (EIA-748-certified, IBR-verified, schedule-grounded, surveilled), accurate (anomaly-free, format-consistent, realistic EACs), and informative (actually used to drive corrective action); ACWP is sacrosanct and must always reconcile to accounting records.
- Watch for the named pathologies — front-loading, rubber baselines, outdated baselines, excessive LOE, hollow Format 5 narratives, and award-fee schemes that reward gaming the numbers — and treat over-target baselines and single point adjustments as rare, last-resort recoveries, not routine fixes.
- Link cost estimating and EVM as one practice: estimators and EVM analysts should share data so risks in the estimate are tracked to specific WBS elements, and EVM EACs feed back into the updated cost estimate.

## Connects To

- **The GAO twelve-step estimating process** — the cost estimate (and its WBS, ground rules and assumptions, and risk-and-uncertainty work) is the source of the time-phased baseline EVM measures against; Step 12 (Update the Estimate) pulls EVM EACs and independent EACs back into the estimate, closing the loop.
- **Work breakdown structure (WBS) and organizational breakdown structure (OBS)** — the single program WBS defines the work; its intersection with the OBS creates the control accounts where earned value is planned, collected, and reported.
- **GAO Schedule Assessment Guide (GAO-16-89G)** — the network schedule, critical path, total float, and schedule risk analysis that EVM depends on (Activities 3 and 10) are detailed there; schedule variances must be probed against the critical path defined by that guide.
- **Risk management** — EVM and risk management are meant to be integrated: management reserve sizing comes from cost/schedule risk analysis, realized risks are distributed from reserve into control accounts, and the integrated cost-schedule risk analysis produces a probabilistic EAC to compare against the EVM extrapolation.
- **EIA-748 and the external guides (NDIA/IPMD Intent, Surveillance, Acceptance, and Application Guides; DOD, DOE, FAA guides; the IBR Process Guide)** — the standard and its companion guides govern system validation, acceptance (SAR/PAR/CER), and surveillance.
- **OMB Capital Programming Guide (Circular A-11, Part 7), FASA 1994, and the FAR (48 C.F.R. subpart 34.2)** — the policy stack mandating EVM on major acquisitions with development effort and reinforcing program-level, EIA-748-compliant systems.
- **DCMA and agency surveillance functions** — DOD's institutional mechanism for validation, predictive analysis, and surveillance, with the explicit caveat that civilian agencies must stand up an independent equivalent (e.g., inspector general or interagency agreement) to use EVM as intended.
