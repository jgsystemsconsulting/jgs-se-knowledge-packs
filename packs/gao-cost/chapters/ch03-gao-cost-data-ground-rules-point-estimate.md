# Chapter — Ground Rules, Data, and Developing the Point Estimate (Steps 5-7)

## Core Idea

Steps 5 through 7 are where a cost estimate stops being a scope statement and becomes an actual number. Step 5 (Identify Ground Rules and Assumptions) pins down the suppositions that bound the estimate — the conditions everyone agrees to estimate against, plus the judgment calls the analyst must make where no condition was directed. Step 6 (Obtain the Data) gathers, validates, and normalizes the historical record that every estimating method ultimately rests on; the Guide treats data quality as the single biggest lever on whether an estimate is a defensible position or a guess. Step 7 (Develop the Point Estimate) selects an estimating method for each work-breakdown-structure (WBS) element, builds the cost model, time-phases and sums the elements into one point estimate, and then tests that number against cross-checks and an independent cost estimate. The three steps are sequential by design: you cannot collect the right data until you know what you are estimating and under what assumptions, and you cannot build a credible model until the data are validated and normalized. A weakness in any one of them — an optimistic assumption, a misapplied data set, or a method used outside its valid range — propagates straight into the final cost and the budget built on it.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Step 5: Identify Ground Rules and Assumptions** (Chapter 8) — the bounding step. Used early, right after the technical baseline and WBS are set, to define the estimate's scope and baseline conditions and to surface misunderstandings with customers before the modeling begins.
- **Step 6: Obtain the Data** (Chapter 9) — the data step. Used after scope, baseline, WBS, and GR&As are settled, to build and execute a data collection plan and to validate and normalize what is gathered. The Guide calls this often the most difficult, time-consuming, and costly activity in estimating.
- **Step 7: Develop the Point Estimate** (Chapter 10) — the modeling step. Used once data are collected, analyzed, and validated, to select methods, build the cost model, and roll the WBS elements up into a single point estimate that sits between best- and worst-case extremes.

## Key Concepts

- **Ground rules are agreed; assumptions are postulated.** Ground rules are a common set of agreed-upon estimating standards that minimize definitional conflict — when conditions are directed, they become ground rules. Assumptions are judgments about past, present, or future conditions taken as true without firm proof, and are needed only where no ground rule was provided. Both must rest on expert judgment from experienced program and technical staff.
- **Assumptions carry outsized leverage.** Many assumptions profoundly drive cost, and management's later rejection of even a single assumption can unravel large parts of the estimate. That is why every cost-influencing GR&A must be briefed to management and documented, with its rationale and supporting historical data.
- **Element-specific GR&As carry the most uncertainty.** Because they are tailored and detailed, the source flags them as the riskiest GR&As and insists they be checked for realism and well documented for the estimate to be credible.
- **Solid assumptions are measurable, specific, and sourced.** The Guide contrasts a quantified, time-bounded assumption (e.g., a stated volume and growth rate) with a vague one; only the measurable form supports later risk and sensitivity analysis. Assumptions grow more reliable as the program matures, and collecting them in a single location lets risk and sensitivity analysis run efficiently.
- **Certain ground rules should always be risk-tested.** Schedule slip in particular should always be modeled for its cost and schedule effects, especially when the schedule is aggressive or was never assessed for realism. Optimistic technology forecasts and technology-maturity assumptions are called out as recurring optimism traps that demand an uncertainty analysis and direct conversations with the program's engineers.
- **Data are the foundation; quality sets the ceiling.** An estimate is only as defensible as its data, and reliable estimates are rooted in historical data — typically data from existing programs, adjusted for differences. Convergence of independent data sets toward one value gives confidence; a poorly defined baseline or WBS makes collection harder.
- **Three data types, mutually contextualizing.** Cost data (labor dollars/hours, overhead, material, facilities capital cost of money, profit), schedule/program data (lead times, durations, delivery and IOC dates, contract type, sourcing), and technical data (physical and performance attributes). Program and technical data give context to cost data, which alone can be meaningless — the Guide's hospital-utility and aircraft-fuel examples show numbers that mean nothing without their parameters.
- **Prefer primary over secondary, actuals over estimates.** Primary data come straight from the original, traceable, audited source and are highest quality; secondary data are derived and lower quality, sometimes "sanitized" actuals whose changes the analyst can't see. Actual costs beat estimates because they are the most accurate data available, though secondary data may be all that exists and can still serve for cross-checks.
- **Applicability and limitations must be scrutinized.** Before use, data are tested against questions about normalization needs, work-content consistency, performance variation over time, whether they are actuals vs. proposals vs. negotiated prices, level of detail, recurring/nonrecurring separability, and dispersion among multiple values. Historical data carry two predominant limitations: they reflect contractor marketplace circumstances that must be recorded, and they eventually become dated.
- **Visualize, then quantify.** After collection, the analyst builds a scatterplot (cost on the y-axis as the dependent variable against technical or operational independent variables) to spot outliers, relationships, and likely cost drivers — tighter scatter means a likelier driver. Descriptive statistics (sample size, mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation = standard deviation ÷ mean) and visual displays (bar charts for means, histograms for distributions and outliers) characterize the data.
- **Outliers and bias are handled with discipline.** An outlier sits roughly beyond three standard deviations (statistically about 0.3 percent of cases); it is removed only with documentation showing it is a true anomaly, never simply because it looks too high or low, because historical data must capture natural program variation. Bias sources are named explicitly — over-optimism, group think, dominating personalities, inexperience, management pressure, motivational bias (fear of punishment), confidence bias (over-optimism about success), recency weighting, and false-pattern detection.
- **Normalization removes external influences.** Cost-unit normalization adjusts mainly for inflation (an item costing the same nominal amount in two different years differs in real terms) and converts to equivalent dollar units; sizing-unit normalization requires defining exactly what a unit means (e.g., whether a software line of code counts comments); key groupings keep like with like; technology normalization is a subjective forecast of how technological change shifts cost over time.
- **Inflation handling is a named subdiscipline.** Estimates are prepared in base-year (constant) dollars to remove price-level distortion, then converted to budget-year dollars for budgeting. The analyst selects rather than constructs an index — often one directed by higher authority such as OMB so all programs share economic assumptions — choosing the market basket that best matches the program (the consumer price index is named as a poor fit for a fighter aircraft). Weighted indexes convert between constant and budget-year dollars; raw indexes change the base year of constant dollars. Schedule slip changes how long dollars are inflated, and contingency should be phased and inflated like any other funding.
- **The three primary methods have distinct profiles.** Analogy uses one similar program's actual cost, adjusted for differences via scaling parameters, and must pass a "reasonable person" test; it relies on a single data point and risks subjective adjustment. Engineering build-up rolls up detailed lower-level labor and material estimates and is most appropriate in production when configuration is stable; it is accurate but slow, inflexible to what-if questions, and prone to summation errors and omissions. Parametric develops a statistically valid CER relating cost to cost drivers and is well suited to early phases with few known characteristics; it requires good historical data, must be used inside its input range, and can become a "black box" if the analyst can't see how the data were normalized.
- **CER statistics decide validity.** The source names R-squared (strength of association, 0 to 1, higher is better), the F statistic (whether the CER as a whole is significant), and the t statistic (whether an individual coefficient is significant), plus the role of the significance level. CERs must be presented with their statistics, used inside the data range, validated against a separate data set, checked for common sense, calibrated, and kept current.
- **Method choice tracks the life cycle.** Definition is thin early on and actuals accrue later, so a program typically uses several methods over its life — parametric and analogy early, engineering build-up and extrapolation once stable configuration and actuals exist; expert opinion is used sparingly to fill gaps and account for possible bias.
- **Learning curves apply only to recurring effort.** Learning curve theory holds that as cumulative quantity doubles, effort drops by a fixed percentage (modeled by Y = AX^b with slope b). It requires manual, complex, repetitive labor; continuous production (or adjustment for breaks); minimal technological change between lots; and known program-office culture. Recurring and nonrecurring costs must be separated first, and production-rate and production-break effects considered.
- **The point estimate is built, validated, and benchmarked.** Each WBS element is estimated, costs expressed in constant-year dollars, results time-phased to the schedule, and the elements summed. The estimator then runs quality control (catching bad formulas, double-counting, omitted or mismatched costs), cross-checks cost drivers, and compares against an ICE.
- **The ICE is a top-tier validation.** An ICE is built by a team outside the acquisition chain, from the same baseline and ground rules, ideally with different methods and data; it tends to be higher and closer to actual cost because it is more objective and less burdened by organizational bias. Greater organizational distance from the program office correlates with higher, more realistic estimates. The two estimates must be reconciled, and the differences documented, justified, and presented to management.

## Mental Models

- **Ground rules vs. assumptions = "given" vs. "I had to decide."** If a condition was handed down and agreed, it is a ground rule and the analyst estimates against it. If nothing was directed, the analyst must make a defensible, expert-backed assumption — and own the risk that flipping it later could reshape the estimate.
- **The single-assumption fault line.** Treat every cost-driving assumption as a switch that management can flip. Brief and document each one so a later "no" from a decision-maker does not silently invalidate the estimate; the Columbia class case shows optimistic labor-hour assumptions doing exactly that kind of damage.
- **Data collection is detective work, not retrieval.** Knowing the history behind a number — was it driven by extra shifts, a new manufacturing process, a strike — determines whether you can use it directly, adjust it, or reject it. Numbers without their backstory are inert; the hospital-utilities and aircraft-fuel examples are the canonical reminder.
- **Normalize before you compare.** Two costs are not comparable until they share a base year, equivalent units, like work content, and matched technology vintage. Skipping normalization smuggles external influences into the model and quietly invalidates every downstream comparison.
- **Scatterplot first, regression second.** Visual inspection is the cheap, fast way to find outliers and candidate cost drivers before committing to statistics. Let the picture nominate the drivers; let the F, t, and R-squared statistics confirm them.
- **Stay inside the fence.** A CER predicts well only within the input range it was built on. Estimating a million-line program from a CER built on tens-of-thousands-of-line programs, or 200 workstations from a 7-to-47 workstation CER, breaks the relationship — the example even produces a positive cost for zero workstations, which is nonsense.
- **Match the method to where you are in the life cycle.** Early, low-definition programs favor parametric and analogy; stable, production-phase programs with real actuals favor engineering build-up and extrapolation. There is no single best method — only the best method for the data and phase you actually have.
- **Distance buys objectivity.** The further an independent estimator sits from the program office (no common superior, nothing at stake in the program's success), the less optimism leaks in. An "independent" estimate paid for by the program office can drift toward success-oriented bias and become optimistic — independence is structural, not nominal.
- **The point estimate is a midpoint, not a verdict.** It deliberately sits between best- and worst-case extremes, awaiting the risk and uncertainty analysis (Step 9) that turns it into a range. Validating it — QC, cross-checks, ICE reconciliation — is what earns the right to defend that midpoint.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes through its case studies and "Likely Effects if Criteria Are Not Fully Met" sections:

- **Overly optimistic assumptions.** Unsubstantiated or unprecedented efficiency assumptions distort the estimate and lead to overruns and inaccurate budgets. (Case Study 10, Columbia Class Submarine / GAO-19-497: the Navy's procurement estimate rested on optimistic touch-labor-hour assumptions — claiming construction efficiencies not supported by Virginia class and other shipbuilding history — so the estimate did not reflect likely program costs and risks; the Congressional Budget Office judged a far smaller labor reduction was realistic.)
- **Underestimating technology maturity.** Program advocates tend to understate technical challenge, and programs typically assume required technology will arrive on schedule; proceeding into detail design and construction with immature technologies invites design instability, cost growth, and schedule delay. (Case Study 11, Columbia Class Submarine / GAO-18-158: the Navy's 2015 technology readiness assessment did not flag several performance-critical technologies as critical, so Congress lacked the full risk picture as financial commitments grew, and a detail-design contract was awarded while critical technologies were still unproven.)
- **Ignoring historical data.** Declaring a program "unprecedented" to avoid using analogous historical cost data weakens credibility and accuracy, because actuals from the program and comparable programs are exactly what challenge optimistic assumptions. (Case Study 12, F-35 Sustainment / GAO-16-439: DOD's ALIS estimate used contractor-provided material-cost data rather than actual ALIS costs or analogous F-22 data, even though multiple ALIS versions had been fielded — additional analysis and historical data would have improved credibility and accuracy.)
- **Undocumented inflation handling.** Applying an inflation rate without documenting why it was chosen or how it was applied makes the estimate inaccurate, and a wrong index can overstate or understate program cost. (Case Study 13, Coast Guard / GAO-12-741: officials applied a flat inflation rate but could not document its basis or application, so the maintenance cost-estimating process did not fully reflect best practices.)
- **Overly optimistic technical-parameter assumptions in an analogy.** Claiming more capability at lower weight/cost than the technology allows produces an indefensible analogy and predictable cost growth. (Case Study 14, Space Acquisitions / GAO-07-96: the AEHF program office assumed far more capacity than the predecessor at half the cost and weight; the independent Cost Analysis Improvement Group, using weight as the cost predictor, judged that more capacity and lower weight were not simultaneously achievable and predicted a multi-billion-dollar overrun.)
- **Unreconciled independent estimates.** An ICE built from a different baseline, or never reconciled with the program estimate, undermines the credibility the ICE was meant to provide. (Case Study 15, Coast Guard Acquisitions / GAO-18-600: the heavy-polar-icebreaker estimate only partially met the credible criterion because it was not fully reconciled with a comparable ICE — the independent estimate used a different methodology and an earlier ship design/baseline, and no reconciliation occurred after the design was finalized.)
- **Unjustified outlier removal.** Dropping an extreme data point without documented justification makes it look like data manipulation and strips out natural program variation; extreme cases should rarely be removed.
- **Unsupported expert opinion.** Because it lacks objectivity and documentation, expert opinion is weak as a primary method and risks a dominant voice swaying the group; it should be used sparingly and probed for whether it rests on real data.
- **Black-box parametrics.** Plugging numbers into a CER without understanding how its data were normalized forces the analyst to accept the model on faith and increases estimate risk; using a CER outside its input range destroys its predictive ability.

## Key Takeaways

- Settle ground rules and assumptions first: document every cost-influencing GR&A with its rationale and historical support, get technical-community input, separate global from element-specific GR&As, and tie each assumption's risk to a specific WBS element — because a single rejected assumption can invalidate much of the estimate.
- Always risk-test the assumptions that move cost most, schedule slip and technology maturity foremost, and write assumptions in measurable, specific, sourced form so sensitivity and uncertainty analysis can actually use them.
- Treat data collection as the credibility bottleneck: plan it, prefer primary sources and actuals, understand each number's history and limitations, and watch for bias from interviews and proposals.
- Validate and normalize before modeling — scatterplot and descriptive statistics to find drivers and outliers; normalize by cost units, sizing units, key groupings, and technology maturity; separate recurring from nonrecurring and fixed from variable; and convert everything to a constant base year.
- Choose the estimating method by data availability and life-cycle phase — analogy and parametric early, engineering build-up and extrapolation when stable and actuals exist — and present CERs with their statistics, used only inside their valid range.
- Apply learning curves only to manual, complex, repetitive recurring effort with continuous (or break-adjusted) production, after separating recurring from nonrecurring costs.
- Build the point estimate by estimating each WBS element, expressing costs in constant-year dollars, time-phasing to the schedule, and summing — then validate with quality control and cross-checks, and reconcile against an independent cost estimate built by a team structurally removed from the program office.
