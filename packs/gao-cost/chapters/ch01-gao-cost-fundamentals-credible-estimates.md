# Chapter — Cost Estimating Fundamentals and the Four Characteristics of a Credible Estimate

## Core Idea

A cost estimate, in the framing of the GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (GAO-20-195G), is the sum of individual cost elements built from established methods and valid data to project what a program will cost in the future, given what is known at the present moment. That estimate is not a one-time deliverable: managing it means revising it as actual data arrive, adjusting it when the program changes, and analyzing the gaps between what was estimated and what was actually spent. The Guide's central claim is that reliability is not a matter of luck or seniority — it is the predictable output of a disciplined, repeatable process. When that process is followed, the resulting estimate exhibits four distinct, named characteristics: it is **comprehensive, well documented, accurate, and credible**. Management reduces the odds of cost overruns and missed performance targets precisely by ensuring an estimate carries all four.

The reason this matters for government is structural. Reliable cost information is what makes oversight of public funds possible, and the federal fiscal path is described as unsustainable, driven largely by health care programs and net interest on the debt. When resources are scarce, every dollar committed to one program is a dollar denied to another — so an unrealistic estimate does not just harm its own program, it can force funding cuts that ripple across an entire portfolio. The Guide positions credible estimating as a discipline that protects the whole budget, not just the line item.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (the "Cost Guide," GAO-20-195G)** — the document itself, offered as a consistent, best-practice methodology that any federal agency can apply to build, manage, and assess the cost estimates of its programs. It revises and updates the original 2009 GAO Cost Guide.
- **The Four Characteristics of a Reliable Cost Estimate** — introduced in Chapter 3 as the criteria GAO's research identifies for reliability: comprehensive, well documented, accurate, credible. These are the assessment yardstick auditors apply.
- **The 18 Best Practices** — introduced in Chapter 3, organized so that each best practice maps to one of the four characteristics. Implementing the practices systematically is what produces an estimate that exhibits the characteristics.
- **The 12-Step Cost Estimating Process** — introduced in Chapter 3 as the procedural backbone (initiating, researching, assessing, analyzing, and presenting an estimate). Chapters 4 through 15 of the Guide each address one step; Chapter 16 recaps the steps, practices, and characteristics for the auditor.
- **Earned Value Management (EVM)** — defined as a project management tool that integrates technical scope with schedule and cost for planning and control, comparing the value of work performed against both its planned value and its actual cost, surfacing cost and schedule variances. OMB requires EVM for major acquisitions involving development effort. The Guide deliberately links cost estimating to EVM because both are needed to set realistic baselines and manage risk.
- **Life Cycle Cost Estimate (LCCE)** — introduced in Chapter 2 as a structured accounting of all labor, material, and effort to develop, produce, operate, maintain, and dispose of a program, spanning past (sunk), present, and future costs regardless of funding source. The LCCE is meant to become the program's budget baseline.
- **OMB's Capital Programming Guide** (Supplement to Circular A-11, Part 7) — cited as the external authority requiring agencies to run a disciplined capital programming process, perform risk management, and build a baseline assessment with a full life-cycle accounting for each major acquisition. It splits capital programming into three phases: a planning-and-budgeting phase first, an acquisition phase next, and finally a management-in-use phase covering operations and maintenance.
- **External standards bodies relied upon** — ICEAA and AACEI (cost estimating standards) and PMI (EVM standards), named as sources the Guide drew on.
- **Companion GAO guides** — the Schedule Assessment Guide and the Technology Readiness Assessment Guide, with which this Cost Guide was aligned in format and which absorb related material (e.g., schedule risk analysis lives in the Schedule Assessment Guide).
- **Analysis of Alternatives (AOA)** — named as an analytical study comparing operational effectiveness, cost, and risk across candidate solutions; GAO identifies 22 best practices for the AOA process (detailed in the Guide's appendix XI).

## Key Concepts

**Cost analysis versus cost estimating.** The Guide treats these as related but distinct. Cost analysis is the broader discipline used to gain knowledge about costs — it can evaluate trade-offs, compare alternatives, assess an existing estimate, or study past efforts. Cost estimating is the narrower activity of predicting a specific program's cost, drawing on historical data adjusted for new materials, technology, software, and teams. Cost estimating is a subset of cost analysis. Successful analysts blend accounting, budgeting, computer science, economics, engineering, mathematics, operations research, and statistics — so the analyst must either build broad knowledge or have ready access to experts.

**The four characteristics, defined.**
- *Comprehensive*: completely defines the program and reflects the current schedule and technical baseline; structured in enough detail that cost elements are neither omitted nor double-counted; and any assumptions or exclusions are reasonable, identified, explained, and documented.
- *Well documented*: can be repeated or updated and traced back to original sources during an audit; documentation captures the methods, calculations, results, rationale, assumptions, and data sources behind every cost element.
- *Accurate*: each cost element is estimated with the best methodology from the collected data; properly adjusted for inflation; underlying formulas, databases, and inputs are validated; contains few minor mathematical errors; grounded in a historical record from comparable programs; and regularly updated, with variances between estimated and actual costs documented, explained, and reviewed.
- *Credible*: discusses and documents the analysis's limitations, including uncertainty or bias in source data and assumptions; varies major assumptions to test sensitivity; includes a risk and uncertainty analysis that establishes a confidence level; cross-checks high-value elements with alternative methodologies; and is compared against an independent cost estimate from outside the acquiring organization.

**The 12 steps.** In sequence: (1) define the estimate's purpose; (2) develop the estimating plan; (3) define the program via a technical baseline description; (4) determine the estimating structure (a product-oriented WBS); (5) identify ground rules and assumptions; (6) obtain the data; (7) develop the point estimate and compare it to an independent estimate; (8) conduct sensitivity testing; (9) conduct a risk and uncertainty analysis to set a confidence level; (10) document the estimate so an outsider could recreate it; (11) present the estimate to management for approval; (12) update the estimate to reflect actual costs and changes.

**The process is not strictly linear.** Although drawn as successive steps, the Guide stresses they need not run in order. Documentation (step 10) should happen continuously rather than as one end-of-effort push. Sensitivity analysis (step 8) and risk/uncertainty analysis (step 9) often run together from the same risk output. Steps 3 through 7 — defining the program, structuring the estimate, setting ground rules, obtaining data, and developing the point estimate — are the most fluid; they may run concurrently or cyclically depending on program phase, estimate maturity, data availability, and which risks have been realized. When updating, the team can re-enter the process at any step.

**Step 2 has no associated best practice — and that is deliberate.** Of the 12 steps, step 2 (develop the estimating plan) is the lone step with no best practice mapped to it, because it does not yield a definitive attribute of the finished estimate. Instead, failing to do step 2 well is treated as a *cause* — a root reason the other best practices end up unmet. That is why the count is 12 steps but 18 best practices, not a one-to-one mapping.

**Types of cost estimates.** Auditors encounter LCCEs, which include the **program office estimate (POE)** — owned by the program manager, covering the full life cycle phased by fiscal year — and the **independent cost estimate (ICE)**, conducted by an organization outside the acquisition chain of command using the same technical and programmatic baseline, primarily to validate and reconcile against the POE and to provide an unbiased reasonableness test. Other estimate types include the **budget estimate** (built from the POE/LCCE, covering only the upcoming years and translated into budget-year dollars), the **rough order of magnitude (ROM)** estimate (quick, low-data, for what-if and high-level alternative comparison — never to be treated as budget-quality), the **independent cost assessment (ICA)** (a non-advocate's evaluation of an estimate's quality, less formal than an ICE), the **independent government cost estimate (IGCE)** (for setting budgets for contracting actions and checking a contractor's proposal), and the **estimate at completion (EAC)** (the projected final cost of contracted work based on EVM performance).

**Budget-year versus base-year dollars.** The Guide flags a terminology trap: "budget year" dollars include inflation (estimators often call these "then-year" dollars), while "base year" dollars are expressed in a fixed year's value without inflation (often called "constant year" dollars). Confusing the two corrupts comparisons.

**Contingency versus management reserve.** A precise distinction: contingency funding covers "unknown unknowns" outside a contractor's control, is held at or above the government program office, and is funding-related; management reserve covers "known unknowns" tied to the contract's scope, is managed at the contractor level, and is budget-related. The Guide notes other organizations may use these terms differently.

**Affordability.** Affordability is the degree to which a program's funding needs fit inside the agency's overall projected budget. It is not the cost estimator's job alone — it pulls in agency leadership and the planning, requirements, and acquisition communities — but it depends heavily on estimate quality. The Guide treats it as a best practice to run affordability assessment at the **agency level** rather than program by program, so leadership can make portfolio-wide trade-offs, and recommends extending the assessment years beyond the budget window (DOD policy nominally covers a 30-to-40-year horizon).

## Mental Models

**The cone of uncertainty.** Estimates start wide and narrow over time. Early in a program, requirements are least understood and the room for change — and cost growth — is greatest. As knowledge accumulates and risks are either mitigated or realized, actual costs begin replacing estimates and the estimate tightens. This convergence is the cone of uncertainty. The practical implication: an early estimate is not "wrong" for being uncertain; it is correctly reflecting how little is known yet, which is why estimates must be continually refreshed.

**The estimate as a living document.** The Guide repeatedly frames a credible estimate as something continually updated as actuals replace originals, with step 12 linking the estimate back to data collected by an EVM system. Examining why estimates and actuals diverged feeds lessons learned and strengthens the credibility of future estimates — a continuous-improvement loop, not a closed file.

**The honest broker / independent view.** Over-optimism is treated as the deep root of unreliable estimating. The countermeasure is an independent perspective — performed by inside or outside analysts — that brings program risks into the open. If an organization will not confront the risks its program faces, it has little hope of managing them. This is the conceptual basis for the ICE and for the credible-characteristic requirement to compare against an outside estimate.

**The bow wave.** When the cost profiles of several programs in a portfolio are plotted against time, funding demand can look satisfied in the near term but spike later — an impending surge in required funds is a bow wave. Spotting it early lets leadership decide which programs continue, get cancelled, or get restructured before the wall is hit.

**Better data, better estimate.** Much of an analyst's time goes to collecting and normalizing data, and estimate quality depends on the quality of historical databases. In most cases better data yields a better estimate — which is why experienced, well-trained analysts (not whoever is available under deadline) are essential.

## Anti-patterns

The source explicitly names recurring failure modes:

- **Over-optimism as a culture.** The Guide cites a 2012 NASA Inspector General finding that an optimistic culture, while helpful for pursuing cutting-edge technology, also leads management to overestimate their ability to deliver within cost and schedule and to underestimate risk — yielding unrealistic baselines. Program managers often believe they have built risk into the plan when they have not.
- **Assuming immature technology is ready.** Postulating that a new technology will be available when needed, only to find it is not, drives cost up. Case Study 1 (Space Acquisitions, GAO-07-96) documents NPOESS, where funds were committed before maturity and only 1 of 14 critical technologies was mature at initiation.
- **Unrealistic assumptions about new processes.** Treating first-time integration efforts as simpler than they are.
- **Optimistic baselines, illustrated.** If a software baseline assumes 100,000 source lines of code but the real total is 200,000, or assumes 80,000 reused lines but only 10,000 prove reusable, cost is underestimated. Case Study 4 (Ford-Class Aircraft Carrier, CVN 79, GAO-17-575) shows the Navy assuming an 18-percent labor-hour reduction unprecedented in 50 years of carrier construction, ignoring the lead ship's lessons.
- **Program instability.** Budget-driven changes to schedules and quantities: cutting development funding stretches schedules; cutting production quantities raises average unit cost; production breaks can drive specialized vendors away or force price restructuring. Case Study 2 (Federal Real Property / St. Elizabeth's, GAO-14-648) shows inconsistent funding escalating an estimate by over $1 billion and delaying completion by more than a decade.
- **Inadequate risk treatment and meaningless confidence levels.** Failing to address risk — especially risks outside the estimator's control — produces point estimates that tell decision-makers nothing about likelihood of success. GAO has repeatedly found confidence levels that were meaningless because analysts did not understand the underlying mathematics or tools.
- **Eliminating contingency funding.** Budget cuts often target contingency, which removes program managers' ability to respond to inevitable problems.
- **Unreliable EVM that yields false signals.** An incorrectly implemented EVM system produces unreliable results. Case Study 3 (Nuclear Waste Cleanup / DOE, GAO-19-223) found unexplained anomalies, negative values (which should be rare because they imply undoing completed work), and estimates at completion that were implausibly low — sometimes zero against original budgets in the hundreds of millions.
- **Rebaselining to reset the scoreboard.** Described as perhaps the biggest EVM pitfall: a new baseline serves a legitimate purpose when goals are no longer achievable, but it shortens the reported performance period and resets measured cost growth to zero, giving an incomplete view of true performance.
- **Treating a ROM as budget-quality.** A rough order of magnitude analysis, built from limited data in a short time, should never be relied on as a budget-quality estimate.
- **Decentralized, inconsistent estimating.** Case Study 6 (HUD IT, GAO-17-281) ties unreliable estimates to a lack of agency guidance, producing decentralized and inconsistent practices; estimates that omitted either government or contractor life-cycle costs lacked a sound basis for investment and budget decisions.

## Key Takeaways

- Reliability is engineered, not assumed: an estimate is reliable only when it is comprehensive, well documented, accurate, and credible — and those characteristics are produced by systematically implementing the 18 best practices through the 12-step process.
- The four characteristics are the auditor's criteria; the 12 steps are the producer's procedure. Chapter 16 turns both into the basis for assessing an estimate's reliability and an agency's estimating guidance.
- The 12-to-18 mismatch is intentional. Step 2 (the estimating plan) maps to no best practice because skipping it is a *cause* of failures elsewhere, not an attribute of the product.
- The process is fluid, not a waterfall. Document continuously, run sensitivity and risk analysis together, and treat steps 3–7 as a concurrent/cyclical cluster.
- An estimate is a living document. Step 12 closes the loop to EVM actuals; analyzing variances strengthens future estimates.
- Over-optimism is the common denominator behind unreliable estimates; an independent view (the "honest broker," operationalized as the ICE) is the principal defense.
- Build the budget from the LCCE, assess affordability at the agency/portfolio level over a long horizon, and watch for the bow wave.
- Mind the vocabulary traps: budget-year vs base-year dollars, and contingency (unknown unknowns, funding-related, government-held) vs management reserve (known unknowns, budget-related, contractor-held).
