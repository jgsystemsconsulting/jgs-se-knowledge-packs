# Chapter — The Twelve-Step Process: Planning and Program Definition (Steps 1-4)

## Core Idea
The first four of the twelve steps in GAO's cost estimating process are about getting set up before any numbers are calculated: deciding *why* the estimate exists and how deep it must go (Step 1), staffing and scheduling the effort (Step 2), pinning down *what* is being costed in a cost-free technical description (Step 3), and choosing the *structure* that will organize every later calculation (Step 4). These steps front-load the decisions that determine whether the eventual estimate can be comprehensive, well-documented, accurate, and credible. Skip or shortchange them and the downstream math, however careful, rests on an undefined scope, an undefined program, or an inconsistent structure.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Step 1 — Define the Estimate's Purpose** (`GAO-20-195G`, Chapter 4). The purpose flows from the intended use, which in turn fixes the estimate's scope and level of detail. Use it first, before anything else, to settle whether the estimate supports affordability/management decisions or the budget process, whether it is required by law or policy, and how much detail the program's maturity actually supports.
- **Step 2 — Develop the Estimating Plan** (`GAO-20-195G`, Chapter 5). Establishes the team composition, a written study plan, and a realistic schedule. Use it once the purpose and scope are known, to translate them into who does the work, by when, and how.
- **Step 3 — Define the Program / Technical Baseline Description** (`GAO-20-195G`, Chapter 6). A common, cost-free description of the system — technical, programmatic, and schedule detail — from which all life cycle cost estimates (LCCEs) are derived. Use it to bound the estimate with real program parameters and to give independently developed estimates a shared starting point.
- **Step 4 — Determine the Estimating Structure / Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** (`GAO-20-195G`, Chapter 7). A product-oriented, hierarchical decomposition of the end product into manageable elements. Use it as the cornerstone framework that ties together cost, schedule, EVM, and risk for the rest of the program.
- **Life cycle cost estimate (LCCE)** — referenced throughout Steps 1 and 3 as the structured accounting of all labor, material, and effort to develop, produce, operate, maintain, and dispose of a program, spanning sunk, present, and future costs regardless of funding source.
- **Parametric cost estimating** — named in Step 1 as the higher-level technique to reach for when technical definition and cost data are thin, linking cost and schedule to size, functionality, performance, or complexity ahead of detailed design.
- **External certification programs** cited in Step 2 as evidence of estimator competence: ICEAA's professional certification, AACEI's cost-professional credential, and the Defense Acquisition University's three-level cost-estimating certification.
- **Centralized cost estimating organization** — the Step 2 best-practice organizational model. The Guide's example is DoD's Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE) function, which sits within the Office of the Secretary of Defense.

## Key Concepts

### Step 1 — Purpose, scope, and right-sizing the detail
- **Two general purposes.** Estimates either help managers judge affordability, track performance against plans, and compare alternative solutions; or they feed the budget process by sizing the funding a program needs to execute. More specific uses include trade studies, independent reviews, and baseline changes.
- **Scope is driven by customer need and data availability.** Analysts must first establish whether the estimate is requested or mandated. A statutory example is the independent cost estimate required before a major defense acquisition program advances into system development and demonstration or production and deployment — and that estimate must cover the full life cycle (development, procurement, military construction, operations and support) irrespective of funding source or management control.
- **Detail should match maturity.** An early-concept program with no firm definition of work cannot support an extensive estimate; detail grows as the program is better defined. Crucially, more detail is not automatically more accurate — chasing fine detail too early can hurt quality. When technical description is weak, estimating at a higher WBS level (e.g., the system level) better ensures that lower-level elements are captured.
- **Include all life cycle costs.** A proper LCCE spans government and contractor costs from inception through design, development, production, operations and maintenance, and disposal. Anything excluded must be documented and justified. Omitting costs typically breaks the comprehensive characteristic and can undermine the well-documented, accurate, and credible characteristics — for example, a missing element both understates total cost and leaves its risk and uncertainty out of the quantitative risk analysis.

### Step 2 — The team and the plan
- **Why the team carries so much risk.** Estimating is inherently judgment-laden: it depends on a web of assumptions and an interpretation of what historical data represent. That subjectivity concentrates risk in the estimating team, especially for complex or cutting-edge programs. A well-trained, centralized, multidisciplinary team with adequate time materially improves an agency's ability to produce reliable estimates.
- **Multidisciplinary composition.** Program-office estimates are normally built by teams blending financial management, engineering, acquisition, logistics, scheduling, mathematics, and communications skills, plus reviewers from affected centers, facilities, and laboratories. A scheduler integrated on the team is treated as critical, because schedules underpin the performance plan; GAO's Schedule Assessment Guide (`GAO-16-89G`) is cited as the companion reference.
- **The written study plan.** An analytic approach is captured in a study plan listing tasks, responsible parties, and due dates. Because the plan is effectively the agreement between the customer and the estimating team, it should reflect the approved approach and be distributed formally to everyone involved. For complex programs the team may be a formal integrated product team; for independent estimates it may be smaller and less formal.
- **Effort scales with the estimate type.** A rough-order-of-magnitude estimate (quick, high-level) takes far less time than a budget-quality estimate. A first-time budget-quality estimate can take many months; an update to a prior estimate can go faster. More required detail means more time and staff.
- **Schedule realism and the data-collection bottleneck.** The team should build a schedule with realistic milestones and margin for foreseeable delays, and avoid optimism. Obtaining the data (Step 6) is one of the most time-consuming steps and benefits from contractor site visits that reveal the data's strengths and limits. If time is short, the constraint belongs in the ground rules and assumptions so management understands the effect on confidence. Timeliness matters as much as quality — an estimate that arrives too late is less useful — but compressing the schedule that produces it erodes the depth of analysis.
- **Centralized vs. decentralized.** Centralizing estimators standardizes processes, concentrates expertise, shares resources, improves independence, and offers a career path — and notably helps the team resist pressure to lower an estimate that exceeds the budget. Decentralization tends toward ad hoc processes, thinner government resources, and reduced independence (program offices fund the work and rate the analysts), though it does give better access to technical experts. Heavy reliance on support contractors raises oversight, trackability, and institutional-knowledge concerns; government customers of contractor-produced estimates need enough experience to judge whether those estimates conform to the Guide's best practices.
- **Certification and continuous learning.** Because experience and skill matter, the Guide points to ICEAA, AACEI, and DAU certifications and stresses ongoing training and workshop participation for estimators, EVM analysts, and auditors alike.

### Step 3 — The technical baseline description
- **What it is and why it dominates accuracy.** The technical baseline documents the underlying technical, programmatic, and schedule assumptions needed to estimate cost, and gives a *common* description of the system from which all LCCEs derive. The amount of information it contains directly affects the estimate's quality and flexibility — less information means more assumptions and more uncertainty. The Guide is explicit that the final accuracy of the estimate depends on how well the program is defined.
- **It must contain no cost data.** Keeping cost out lets the same baseline serve as the shared starting point for independently developed estimates, preserving their independence.
- **Ownership and approval.** Program offices, holding the deepest program knowledge, develop and maintain the baseline across the life cycle; an integrated team builds it early — drawing in systems and design engineers, schedulers, financial managers, cost estimators, and test-and-evaluation specialists — and the program manager approves it.
- **A living document that must be maintained.** Early baselines are expected to be imprecise and to evolve; what matters is that the baseline always reflects the best available information, that unknowns are handled by management-agreed assumptions with documented justifications, and that the risk level of those assumptions is captured so credibility can be judged. Updating it for reviews, milestone decisions, and major changes is essential — an unmaintained baseline erodes the estimate's credibility and makes it hard to trace a new cost baseline as assumptions shift.
- **Typical content (paraphrased from Table 4).** The baseline addresses system purpose and mission fit; detailed technical and performance characteristics; software description and sizing; the WBS; legacy/predecessor systems; acquisition plan or strategy; development/test/production quantities and program schedule (including a Gantt chart and critical path); a system test and evaluation plan; deployment details; safety, training, personnel, logistics, and environmental plans; an operational concept; and a tracked record of changes from the prior baseline. Content varies by program and even by alternative — when multiple alternatives are in play, each generally gets its own baseline.
- **System characteristics vary by type (paraphrased from Table 5).** Different system classes are characterized by different parameters — aircraft by weights, dimensions, speed, and wing geometry; information systems by architecture, COTS use, memory, processor, and language; construction by site, materials, permits, and stories; ships, missiles, space systems, and ground vehicles by their own physical and performance attributes. The Guide presents this list as illustrative, not exhaustive. Once defined, requirements must be tracked continually, with both the baseline and the estimate updated when they change.
- **Development approach shapes the baseline.** Incremental programs need a baseline that states whole-program characteristics and the content of each increment; spiral programs have baselines that evolve, where each early version must state plainly which requirements are in and which are out — undefined requirements drive cost increases and delivery delays (illustrated by Case Study 7, the DOD Guam realignment, where a $1.3 billion water/wastewater estimate failed the credible characteristic largely for lack of a defining technical baseline).

### Step 4 — The work breakdown structure (WBS)
- **The cornerstone.** A WBS deconstructs the end product into smaller elements suitable for management control, defining in detail the work needed to meet program objectives. It is established when the program begins and grows more detailed as knowledge accrues, providing the framework for estimating cost, building schedules, identifying resources, and locating risk.
- **Product-oriented, not functional.** A product-oriented WBS — organized by deliverables such as hardware or software components — is the best practice because it ties cost, schedule, and technical performance to specific parts of the program. A functional WBS (organized by activities like manufacturing, engineering, or quality control, or by trades like carpenters) cannot point management to the root cause of an overrun on a specific item. Elements at the second and third levels should therefore be products or deliverables, with functional efforts embedded inside them.
- **What does *not* belong as a standalone element.** The Guide lists items that should be embedded rather than broken out separately: functional engineering efforts (design engineering, requirements analysis, logistics, risk, quality assurance, test engineering), material resources, accounting classifications like direct cost, funding types (e.g., RDT&E), rework/retest/refurbish (treated as activities), nonrecurring/recurring splits (handled through reporting structure), cost-saving initiatives, the organizational structure, the schedule itself (the WBS drives the schedule, not vice versa), meetings/travel/computer support, generic terms, and tooling (folded in with the equipment it produces).
- **Minimum structure and the summation rule.** Every WBS should have at least three levels: level 1 is the single program element, level 2 the major segments, level 3 the subsystems. The number of levels grows with complexity and risk. A foundational rule: the sum of a parent's children must equal the parent, so each element maps to exactly one work effort and nothing is omitted or double-counted (illustrated with a simple construction WBS in Figure 7).
- **Common elements.** Beyond product-oriented elements, every WBS carries program management as a level-2 element plus common supporting elements — integration/assembly/test/checkout, systems engineering, training, data, system test and evaluation, peculiar and common support equipment, operational/site activation, facilities, and initial spares (paraphrased from Table 6). Including them ensures all effort is identified up front. The program WBS spans the whole program; a contractor may build a contract WBS that extends lower-level components to reflect its responsibilities, and a subcontractor's level-1 effort may map to a deeper level in the prime/program WBS (Figures 8 and 9).
- **A living document with a dictionary.** Like the technical baseline, the WBS matures as the program does; each WBS is paired with a **WBS dictionary** that describes, in brief narrative form, the work for each element and its place in the hierarchy. The dictionary can be converted, with minor additions, into a statement of work, and is updated under a formal process once the program is baselined.
- **Standardization as best practice.** Standardizing the WBS lets an organization collect and share data across programs, yields more consistent estimates, supports cost estimating relationship development, and enables common cost measures across contractors and programs. Standardization is best applied at a high level (e.g., level 3) to preserve flexibility in the lower levels, while high-risk or costly elements may be standardized deeper for insight. The *same* standardized WBS should drive the cost estimate, the schedule, and the EVM performance measurement baseline, and supports better portfolio decisions about contingency and systemic problem areas.
- **The integrating role.** The WBS is the common thread among cost, schedule, EVM, and risk. Its hierarchy lets lower-level elements roll up to measure cost, schedule, and technical performance in an EVM system, pinpoints which components drive overruns, anchors the resource-loaded schedule behind the EVM baseline, flags risk-prone elements for contingency and schedule risk analysis, and gives government and contractors a common reporting language. Without it, root-cause analysis of cost, schedule, and technical problems is difficult (illustrated by Case Study 8, the DOE/NNSA Plutonium Disposition program, which lacked a program-level WBS, and Case Study 9, the 2020 Census Internet response option, whose estimate was not built on a defined-element WBS).

## Mental Models
- **Purpose sets the dial for everything downstream.** Intended use → scope → level of detail. Decide the use first; the rest of the estimate's shape follows from it, and detail should track program maturity rather than the analyst's appetite for precision.
- **More detail is not more accuracy.** Premature granularity over a poorly defined system manufactures false precision; estimating at a higher WBS level can be the more honest and more complete choice.
- **The baseline is the contract about *what* is being costed; the WBS is the contract about *how* it is organized.** One is deliberately cost-free so independent estimates share a neutral starting point; the other is the structural skeleton every later number hangs from.
- **Independence is structural, not just attitudinal.** A centralized organization and a cost-free technical baseline are mechanisms that let a team resist pressure to lower an estimate — independence is engineered into the setup, not merely asserted by the estimator.
- **The parent-equals-sum-of-children rule is a completeness check.** If children do not sum to their parent, either something is missing or something is double-counted — the rule turns the WBS into a self-auditing structure.
- **Living documents, not artifacts.** Both the technical baseline and the WBS start imprecise and are expected to evolve; their value comes from being maintained through reviews and milestones, with assumptions progressively replaced by known information so uncertainty declines over time.

## Anti-patterns
- **Pursuing too much detail too early.** The Guide explicitly warns that chasing fine-grained detail before the system is well defined can be detrimental to estimate quality.
- **A functionally organized WBS.** Organizing the WBS by function or trade (the source's carpenters-vs-framing example) deprives management of the information needed to trace an overrun to its root cause; product orientation is the best practice.
- **Missing or stale technical baseline.** Estimating without an adequate program description leaves the analyst unable to identify the bounding technical and program parameters (Case Study 7's Guam estimate), and failing to maintain the baseline through reviews and milestone decisions undermines the estimate's credibility and traceability.
- **No program-level WBS / no WBS dictionary.** Building project-level structures without a formalized program-level WBS (Case Study 8) — or estimating against undefined high-level elements with no WBS dictionary (Case Study 9) — means there is no assurance that all relevant costs are captured.
- **Excluding costs without documentation.** Leaving life cycle costs out of the estimate without documenting and justifying the exclusion breaks the comprehensive characteristic and can take well-documented, accurate, and credible down with it.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Step 1 — Purpose** fixes scope and detail from the estimate's intended use; size the detail to program maturity, include all life cycle costs, and document any exclusions, because more detail is not the same as more accuracy.
2. **Step 2 — Estimating Plan** stands up a multidisciplinary, ideally centralized team, captures the approach in a formally distributed written study plan, and builds a realistic schedule that protects the time-consuming data-collection step.
3. **Step 3 — Technical Baseline** gives a single, cost-free, management-approved program description from which every LCCE derives; it is a living document whose completeness and maintenance directly set the estimate's accuracy and credibility.
4. **Step 4 — WBS** provides a product-oriented, at-least-three-level structure where children sum to their parent, paired with a WBS dictionary, standardized at a high level for data sharing while flexible below.
5. **Consistency is the payoff of Step 4**: the same standardized WBS drives the cost estimate, the schedule, and the EVM performance measurement baseline, making the WBS the integrating thread across cost, schedule, EVM, and risk.
6. The recurring failure mode across the case studies is **definition before calculation** — undefined requirements, missing baselines, and absent WBSs all surface as estimates that fail the four characteristics of a reliable estimate.
