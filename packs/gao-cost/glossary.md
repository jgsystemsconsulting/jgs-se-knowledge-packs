# GAO Cost Estimating Guide Glossary

Key terms from the GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (GAO-20-195G), alphabetical, with chapter references. Definitions are synthesized in original words, not reproduced from the source.

**Accurate (characteristic)** — one of the four characteristics of a reliable estimate: the estimate is unbiased, neither overly conservative nor optimistic, based on an assessment of most-likely costs, and adjusted for inflation. (Ch 1, Ch 6)

**Actual cost of work performed (ACWP)** — in EVM, the cost actually incurred for the work that has been completed in a period; also called actual cost (AC). (Ch 7)

**Analogy method** — an estimating method that bases a new item's cost on the known cost of a similar existing item, adjusted for differences. Fast and useful early, but weak when no close analog exists. (Ch 3)

**Analysis of Alternatives (AOA)** — a structured study comparing the operational effectiveness, cost, and risk of candidate solutions against a mission need; GAO defines 22 best practices in five phases and four characteristics (well-documented, comprehensive, unbiased, credible). (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Anderlohr method** — a learning-curve technique that quantifies learning lost during a production break across five weighted categories (personnel, supervisory, continuity, methods, tooling). (Ch 8)

**Budget at completion (BAC)** — the total budgeted cost for the authorized work; the final value of the performance measurement baseline. (Ch 7)

**Budgeted cost for work performed (BCWP)** — earned value: the budgeted value of the work actually accomplished. (Ch 7)

**Budgeted cost for work scheduled (BCWS)** — planned value: the budgeted value of the work that was scheduled to be done. (Ch 7)

**Comprehensive (characteristic)** — one of the four characteristics: the estimate covers the full life cycle and complete scope (a complete WBS), and documents all assumptions and ground rules with nothing omitted or double-counted. (Ch 1, Ch 6)

**Confidence level** — the probability, read off the cumulative distribution (S curve), that the program can be executed at or below a given cost; used to select a defensible budget point. (Ch 4)

**Contingency / management reserve** — funds set aside to cover the difference between the point estimate and a higher, risk-adjusted confidence level (contingency), or held by the contractor for in-scope unknowns (management reserve, excluded from the PMB). (Ch 4, Ch 7)

**Cost estimating relationship (CER)** — a mathematical relationship (often a regression) that predicts cost from a physical, performance, or programmatic driver; the basis of the parametric method. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Cost performance index (CPI)** — BCWP / ACWP; a cost-efficiency ratio where values above 1.0 are favorable. (Ch 7)

**Credible (characteristic)** — one of the four characteristics: the estimate's limitations are discussed via sensitivity and risk/uncertainty analysis, major cost drivers are cross-checked, and it is reconciled against an independent cost estimate. (Ch 1, Ch 6)

**Cumulative average theory** — a learning-curve formulation where each doubling of cumulative quantity reduces the cumulative *average* unit cost by the slope; associated with T. P. Wright (1936). (Ch 8)

**Cumulative distribution function (S curve)** — the curve mapping each possible total cost to its confidence percentile, produced from the risk simulation output. (Ch 4)

**Earned value management (EVM)** — a management discipline that integrates technical scope, schedule, and cost to measure the budgeted value of work performed against planned value and actual cost, surfacing cost and schedule variances early. (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**EIA-748** — the national EVM systems standard (current revision EIA-748-D, 2019) defining the 32 guidelines an EVM system must satisfy. (Ch 7)

**Engineering build-up method** — a "bottom-up" estimating method that sums detailed labor and material costs for each lowest-level WBS element; most accurate but data- and time-intensive. (Ch 3)

**Estimate at completion (EAC)** — a forecast of total cost at program completion, computed as ACWP plus remaining work divided by an efficiency index (e.g., cumulative CPI). (Ch 7)

**Four characteristics of a reliable cost estimate** — comprehensive, well documented, accurate, and credible; the criteria GAO research identifies for reliability and the yardstick auditors apply. (Ch 1, Ch 6)

**Ground rules and assumptions (GR&A)** — the agreed conditions the estimate is built against (global GR&As) plus the analyst's judgment calls where none was directed (element-specific GR&As). (Ch 3)

**Green Book (GAO-14-704G)** — GAO's *Standards for Internal Control in the Federal Government*: five components and seventeen principles against which a cost-estimating process is judged as an internal control. (Ch 8)

**Independent cost estimate (ICE)** — an estimate built by a group outside and above the program office from the same baseline, used to test the point estimate for reasonableness and reconciled against it. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Integrated baseline review (IBR)** — a joint government/contractor review verifying the performance measurement baseline is realistic and risks are understood; the crucial link between cost estimating and EVM. (Ch 7, Ch 8)

**Learning curve** — the empirical finding that recurring production cost falls by a constant percentage each time cumulative quantity doubles; modeled as Y = AX^b. (Ch 3, Ch 8)

**Life cycle cost estimate (LCCE)** — a structured accounting of all labor, material, and effort to develop, produce, operate, maintain, and dispose of a program across past (sunk), present, and future costs; meant to become the budget baseline. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Monte Carlo simulation** — the simulation engine that repeatedly draws random values from each cost element's probability distribution and sums them over thousands of iterations to produce a total-cost distribution. (Ch 4)

**Parametric method** — an estimating method that uses CERs to predict cost from drivers (size, performance, complexity); useful when technical definition is thin. (Ch 3)

**Performance measurement baseline (PMB)** — the time-phased, cumulative budget plan (an S-curve) against which EVM performance is measured; excludes management reserve and fee. (Ch 7)

**Point estimate** — the single best-estimate total cost produced in Step 7 by selecting methods, building the model, time-phasing, and summing the WBS elements; sits between best and worst case. (Ch 3)

**Risk and uncertainty analysis (Step 9)** — quantitative, statistically based analysis (via simulation) that produces a probability distribution of total cost and a defensible confidence level. (Ch 4)

**Risk driver approach** — a simulation input method that quantifies the probability each risk-register entry occurs and its multiplicative effect on assigned WBS element costs. (Ch 4)

**Risk register** — the program document listing identified risks with handling actions, occurrence probability, and cost/schedule consequence; the source of risk-driver inputs. (Ch 4)

**Schedule performance index (SPI)** — BCWP / BCWS; a schedule-efficiency ratio where values above 1.0 are favorable. (Ch 7)

**Sensitivity analysis (Step 8)** — recalculating the estimate by varying one input at a time between bounds to reveal which assumptions and parameters the estimate is most responsive to. (Ch 4)

**Source lines of code (SLOC)** — the predominant software sizing metric (count of code lines), favored because many organizations hold historical SLOC databases. (Ch 8)

**Technical baseline description** — a common, cost-free technical/programmatic/schedule description of the system from which all LCCEs are derived (Step 3). (Ch 2)

**Technology readiness level (TRL)** — a 1-9 maturity scale used to communicate how proven a technology is, from basic research (1) to a proven system in its operational environment (9). (Ch 8)

**Three-point estimate** — assigning a minimum, most-likely, and maximum cost to each WBS element to feed the risk simulation. (Ch 4)

**To-complete performance index (TCPI)** — BCWR / (EAC − ACWP); the cost efficiency required on remaining work to hit a target cost. (Ch 7)

**Tornado chart** — a bar chart ranking parameters by their positive or negative effect on the point estimate; used to pick factors for sensitivity testing. (Ch 4)

**12-step cost estimating process** — GAO's procedural backbone for initiating, researching, assessing, analyzing, and presenting an estimate; each step maps to best practices and characteristics. (Ch 2-Ch 5)

**18 best practices** — the actionable practices that, implemented systematically, produce an estimate exhibiting the four characteristics; each maps to one characteristic. (Ch 1, Ch 6)

**Well documented (characteristic)** — one of the four characteristics: the estimate is thoroughly documented (sources, methods, calculations) so an unfamiliar analyst could reconstruct it, and is traced to source data. (Ch 1, Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Work breakdown structure (WBS)** — a product-oriented, hierarchical decomposition of the end product into manageable elements; the cornerstone that ties cost, schedule, EVM, and risk together (Step 4). (Ch 2, Ch 8)
