# Chapter — RMA Fundamentals: Figures of Merit, Statistics, and Probability Distributions

*Source: FAA-HDBK-006D, "System Reliability, Maintainability, and Availability (RMA)," October 8, 2020 (Section 4). This is the current D revision; use it rather than earlier 006-series revisions. The handbook is explicit that it is guidance only and is not to be cited as a requirement.*

## Core Idea

Reliability, maintainability, and availability are not vague engineering virtues — they are quantitative measures, which FAA-HDBK-006D calls *figures of merit*, that can be attached to any "system of interest." The handbook deliberately keeps that term elastic: a figure of merit can target a component, a system, a system-of-systems, a program, a service, or a facility. The chapter's argument runs in three moves. First, define the figures of merit (reliability, maintainability, availability and their many derived metrics) so that performance can be expressed as numbers. Second, supply the statistical scaffolding — the bathtub-curve view of failure over a lifecycle, confidence intervals, and disciplined data collection — that tells you whether those numbers mean anything. Third, when the real data needed to compute the figures of merit does not exist (the system is too immature, or the data is too costly to gather), reach for probability distributions as mathematical stand-ins for the underlying failure and restoration behavior. The throughline is that RMA is a measurement discipline: pick the right metric, collect enough data to make it statistically significant, and where data is missing, model it with the distribution whose shape actually fits the phenomenon.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Figures of Merit (Section 4.1)** — the handbook's umbrella name for RMA quantitative metrics. Introduced as the bridge between qualitative RMA concepts and computable numbers, applied to any "system of interest."
- **Reliability via the exponential formula (Section 4.1.1)** — reliability stated either as a probability of satisfactory operation over a specified period, or as Mean Time Between Failure (MTBF). For systems that fail exponentially during normal operation, R is expressed through the exponential relation R = e^(−t/m), where t is mission time and m is MTBF (or uMTBO for services and facilities). The handbook ties this to FAA Order JO 6040.15H, under which t is fixed at 24 hours for facilities and services.
- **Maintainability metrics — MTTR, sMTTR, uMTTR (Section 4.1.2)** — Mean Time To Restore as the arithmetic average outage duration, decomposed into scheduled (sMTTR) and unscheduled (uMTTR) variants, where the restore duration spans detection, fault isolation, access, repair or replacement, and return to operation.
- **Availability metrics — Operational, Equipment-and-Services, MTBO, uMTBO, sMTBO, MTBF, Inherent Availability (Section 4.1.3)** — a family of ratios and mean-time metrics. Operational Availability (Availability_OP) counts all downtime; Availability_ES strips out scheduled downtime; Inherent Availability (Availability_i) is computed as MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR), omitting scheduled downtime because planned outages are announced in advance.
- **Series availability allocation (Section 4.1.3)** — for independent subsystems, system availability is the product of subsystem availabilities; equal allocation assigns each of n subsystems the nth root of the target.
- **Bathtub Curve Concept (Section 4.2.1)** — the three-phase failure-rate model (early/decreasing, random/constant, wear-out/increasing) used for maintenance planning when historical data is absent.
- **Confidence Intervals (Section 4.2.2)** — a range-with-a-percentage view of an unknown parameter, presented as preferable to a bare point estimate.
- **Data Collection plan (Section 4.2.3)** — a required plan naming the RMA metrics to capture and how data is gathered and stored, with statistical significance as the gating criterion.
- **Probability Distributions (Section 4.3)** — seven named models offered as approximations when real data is unavailable: Normal, Exponential, Poisson, Lognormal, Binomial, Weibull, and Empirical.

## Key Concepts

**Reliability has two faces.** It is either a probability (best suited to systems with a finite mission time) or a mean-time figure (MTBF), preferred for repairable systems that must run continuously. When normal-operation failures follow the exponential distribution, reliability collapses to R = e^(−t/m). The handbook is careful about vocabulary: MTBF is used for system components, but FAA substitutes uMTBO (unscheduled Mean Time Between Outages) for services and facilities, with the 24-hour mission time mandated by Order JO 6040.15H.

**Maintainability is about restoration, not operation.** It is the ability to keep a system in — or return it to — a specified condition through maintenance work. The handbook splits maintenance into *unscheduled* (reactive, triggered by an outage or failure) and *scheduled* (planned/preventative, retaining performance through inspection, detection, servicing, and periodic component replacement), and states a clear preference for the scheduled kind. MTTR is the average outage duration and includes everything from detecting the outage through disassembly, repair or replacement, and final restoration. It further separates into sMTTR (planned events) and uMTTR (the residual unscheduled events).

**Availability is a ratio, computed several ways depending on what downtime you count.** Operational Availability includes every source of downtime, scheduled and unscheduled, for deployed systems. Equipment-and-services availability (Availability_ES) excludes scheduled downtime. Both are expressed as percentages of maximum available hours. Around these sit the between-outage metrics: MTBO counts both outage types; uMTBO counts only unscheduled outages (the target of Reliability Centered Maintenance, which tries to drive them down but cannot eliminate the possibility); sMTBO counts only scheduled outages such as periodic maintenance, improvements, non-FAA circuits, administrative actions, flight inspection, and software events. MTBF is computed separately for systems and subsystems because a constituent can fail without taking down the whole service.

**Inherent availability is the clean, schedule-free view.** Expressed mathematically, availability is a function of operating time (reliability) and downtime (maintainability). Inherent Availability deliberately omits scheduled downtime — the logic being that a planned outage is announced ahead of time — and is computed as MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR). Any practical MTBF assumes a roughly constant failure rate, i.e., the flat middle of the bathtub curve where failures are random.

**Availability composes multiplicatively across a series.** Because subsystem availabilities are assumed independent, the system availability of a series is the product of the subsystem values. The simplest allocation, when a target is known, is to assign each subsystem the nth root of the system target; unequal allocation requires heavier mathematics, but however it is done, the product of subsystem availabilities must equal the system availability.

**RMA, but for software, is hard.** The handbook flags that in modern information systems the real causes of unscheduled interruptions are often latent software defects and delays in restoring or replacing failed spares — not hardware failure — and that restoration can hinge more on restart time than on physical replacement. Hardware availability yields to straightforward combinatorial probability models, but for software-intensive fault-tolerant systems the dominant factor is the effectiveness of fault-detection-and-recovery software, which depends on undiscovered defects and therefore often cannot be credibly predicted before the software exists.

**Confidence intervals beat point estimates.** A mean life of 10 years is a single number; stating a 95% chance the component lasts 9 to 11 years carries more information. The confidence interval is the plausible range for the unknown parameter, and the confidence level is the long-run frequency with which such intervals capture the true value — for 95%, the proportion of intervals from an infinite set of independent samples that would contain the true parameter.

**Statistical significance gates everything.** A higher availability target needs more data to confirm: demonstrating 0.99999 takes more evidence than 0.9999. The handbook names three drivers of required sample size — the variable's distribution (normally distributed variables generally need less data than exponentially distributed ones), the data's variability (higher variance demands more data), and the desired confidence interval (tightening the interval at a fixed confidence level requires a larger sample). Too small a sample can be dominated by anomalies and yield misleading figures of merit, so determining a minimum statistically significant sample size is itself a core RMA task — balanced against the time and cost of collecting it.

**Distributions are models, not truth.** When the data does not exist, distributions stand in for it — but they are only estimates of true failure and restoration behavior, and choosing a distribution that does not match the data renders the results useless or misleading. A distribution is expressed as a probability density function (continuous variables) or a probability mass function (discrete variables); the handbook notes the practitioner never computes a probability by hand because software, including Microsoft Excel, does it.

**The seven distributions and where each fits:**
- *Normal (Gaussian):* the bell curve, parameters mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ); applies mainly to mechanical/electromechanical hardware where repair tasks vary little around the mean. The handbook warns against using it when the RMA variable is time, since it can produce negative times, and points to the exponential instead.
- *Exponential:* single rate parameter λ (failures per unit time), memoryless because events are independent; mean equals 1/λ, so MTBF = 1/λ when times-between-failure are exponential. Doubles as the reliability/survival function — the probability a failure has not yet occurred by a given time.
- *Poisson:* discrete; the probability of a given count of events in a fixed interval at a known constant rate, independent of time since the last event (a Poisson Process). Its single parameter λ equals both the mean and the variance; used to model a small average number of system changes such as failures in an interval.
- *Lognormal (Galton's):* fits failure data where repair durations are unequal, such as fatigue-induced mechanical failures; the log of time-to-failure is normal, and unlike the Normal it takes only positive values, making it well-suited to reliability work. The handbook names Lognormal and Weibull as the two most-used distributions in reliability analysis.
- *Binomial:* discrete, two-outcome (success/fail) with a constant per-trial probability; the failure probability is simply the complement of the success probability.
- *Weibull:* two parameters, shape (k) and scale, letting it be fitted to many RMA datasets. Shape > 1 means an increasing failure rate; shape = 1 reduces it to the exponential (constant rate, no reliability growth or decay); shape < 1 means a decreasing failure rate, i.e., reliability growth. Scale behaves like the Normal's standard deviation — larger scale, more spread. Weibull can serve as the survival function S(x) = 1 − F(x).
- *Empirical:* when no parametric distribution fits, estimate the density or cumulative distribution directly from the sample data.

## Mental Models

**Figures of merit are a measurement contract.** RMA's first job is to turn "is it reliable enough?" into a number with a definition, so two parties can agree on what was promised and whether it was met. Treat each metric as a contract clause: it names exactly what downtime is counted (or excluded) and over what period.

**The bathtub curve is the map of *when* failures happen.** Three regimes: a decreasing rate of early "infant mortality" failures, a long flat plateau of random failures at constant rate, and a rising tail of wear-out failures past the design lifetime. The curve is conceptual — its value is for maintenance planning when you have no historical data. Two mental sub-models attach to it: the early and wear-out phases trace exponential decay and growth respectively (a new system's bugs decay exponentially; an aging system's failures grow exponentially), while the dominant middle stretch is treated as constant-yet-random. Practical MTBF lives in that flat middle.

**Schedule-aware vs. schedule-blind availability.** Keep two lenses. The schedule-blind lens (Operational Availability) counts every minute the system was down, planned or not — what the user actually experiences. The schedule-aware lens (Inherent Availability, Availability_ES) discounts planned outages because they were announced. Knowing which lens a stated availability number uses is the difference between a meaningful and a misleading comparison.

**Series multiplication is unforgiving.** Because availabilities multiply across independent subsystems, adding subsystems erodes the product — a chain of "good enough" subsystems can fall short of a demanding system target. The nth-root rule is the quick way to back out per-subsystem budgets from a system goal.

**Reliability and maintainability can stand in for availability.** From a mathematical standpoint, specifying reliability and maintainability may suffice to pin down availability, since availability is a function of operating time and downtime. But to avoid forcing readers through advanced conversions, the handbook's convention is to provide both reliability and availability metrics directly, with the caution that the two must not conflict mathematically.

**Distributions are shape-matching tools.** Each distribution is a shape; the skill is matching the shape to the phenomenon — symmetric repair variation around a mean → Normal; memoryless constant-rate failures over time → Exponential; counts of rare events in an interval → Poisson; positive-only, skewed repair times → Lognormal; yes/no trials → Binomial; tunable failure-rate trend (growing, constant, or shrinking) → Weibull; nothing parametric fits → Empirical. Weibull is the shape-shifter, collapsing to the exponential at shape = 1.

## Anti-patterns

The handbook frames these as cautions rather than labeled "anti-patterns," but it explicitly warns against:

- **Using the Normal distribution when the RMA variable is time** — because it can produce negative times. The exponential is preferred for time-based reliability calculations.
- **Computing figures of merit from too small a sample** — a small sample can carry a disproportionate share of anomalies, producing less reliable and potentially misleading values.
- **Choosing a distribution that does not match the data** — if the statistical distribution of the data is misjudged, the derived results are useless and potentially misleading.
- **Over-trusting predicted availability for software-intensive systems** — for many software systems availability cannot be predicted with sufficient accuracy before the software is developed, because it hinges on the effects of undiscovered defects in the recovery software.

## Key Takeaways

- RMA concepts become engineering-usable only as *figures of merit* — quantitative metrics defined against an explicitly scoped "system of interest."
- Reliability is expressible as a probability (finite-mission systems) or as MTBF (continuously operating repairable systems); under exponential failure, R = e^(−t/m), with FAA using uMTBO and a 24-hour mission time for services and facilities per Order JO 6040.15H.
- Maintainability splits scheduled from unscheduled work; MTTR (and its sMTTR/uMTTR variants) captures the full restore cycle from detection to return-to-service.
- Availability comes in several flavors that differ only in which downtime they count: Operational (all of it), Equipment-and-Services (no scheduled), and Inherent = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR); series systems multiply, with the nth-root rule for equal allocation.
- Software-driven outages — latent defects, restore delays, restart-bound recovery — make availability hard to predict before the software exists, unlike combinatorially predictable hardware.
- The bathtub curve organizes failure behavior across a lifecycle (early, random, wear-out) and underwrites maintenance planning when historical data is missing.
- Confidence intervals and a deliberate data-collection plan are what make figures of merit trustworthy; sample size is driven by the variable's distribution, its variability, and the target interval, and an undersized sample is worse than useless.
- When data is missing, distributions approximate it — but they are models: match the shape (Normal, Exponential, Poisson, Lognormal, Binomial, Weibull, Empirical) to the phenomenon, with Lognormal and Weibull cited as the reliability workhorses and Weibull reducing to the exponential at shape = 1.

## Connects To

- **Reliability Centered Maintenance (RCM):** named here as the discipline aimed at minimizing unscheduled outages (uMTBO), connecting the availability metrics directly to maintenance strategy.
- **FAA Order JO 6040.15H:** the cited authority fixing mission time t at 24 hours for facilities and services, anchoring the reliability formula to FAA operational practice.
- **Systems Engineering Body of Knowledge (SEBoK):** the handbook points readers to SEBoK as an external source for the probability and statistics background underlying RMA, and as the basis for cautions such as avoiding the Normal distribution for time variables.
- **The bathtub curve (Section 4.2.1) ↔ practical MTBF:** the constant-failure-rate plateau is the precondition under which a practical MTBF value is meaningful, linking the statistical-concepts material back to the figures of merit.
- **Probability distributions ↔ figures of merit:** the distributions in Section 4.3 exist to approximate the same quantities (MTBF, MTTR, failure rate) that Section 4.1 defines, closing the loop when measured data is unavailable.
