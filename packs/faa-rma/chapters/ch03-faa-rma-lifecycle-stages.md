# Chapter — Stages in the RMA Lifecycle

> Source: FAA-HDBK-006D (2020), Section 5, "Stages in the RMA Lifecycle." All claims in this chapter are grounded in that section. Use 006D (2020), not 006B.

## Core Idea

An FAA reliability, maintainability, and availability (RMA) program is not a one-time analysis bolted onto a design — it runs across the whole life of a system, organized into four named stages that track the systems engineering lifecycle. On the descending (left) side of the engineering Vee, the work is about *determining and specifying* the correct RMA requirements; on the ascending (right) side, it shifts to *verifying and validating* that those requirements were actually met. Once the system is fielded, the program transitions to monitoring, where maintenance (maintainability) becomes the dominant concern even as reliability and availability statistics keep getting reported.

The four stages — Concept/Operational Assessment, Requirements Development, Testing and Implementation, and Monitoring and Post-Implementation — map onto the FAA's Acquisition Management System (AMS) phases, so an RMA practitioner can locate exactly where in an acquisition a given activity belongs. Reliability and availability are derived in large part through mathematical analysis, whereas maintainability requirements come from FAA orders and standards. A central premise of the section is that throughout these stages, "system" is read in its broadest sense — it may be a service, a program, a system of systems, a single system, or a facility — and the scope depends on the role of whoever is looking at it (a program manager's "system" can contain many systems that an individual systems engineer treats as just one assigned system).

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **The four RMA Stages** — named up front as the organizing spine of the entire section: Concept/Operational Assessment, Requirements Development, Testing and Implementation, and Monitoring and Post-Implementation. Each subsequent subsection (5.3 through 5.6) is one stage, and the work is decomposed into eleven numbered steps that run across them.
- **Systems Engineering Vee model** — used in §5.1 ("RMA in Context") to position RMA across development. The left side carries requirements-determination stages; the right side carries verification and validation. The handbook stresses the model is *iterative*, not waterfall — its stages can be repeated as needed, and right-side stages are verified and later validated against the artifacts produced on the left side.
- **Acquisition Management System (AMS)** — the FAA's acquisition lifecycle, mapped to the RMA stages in Table 5-1. Service Analysis and Strategic Planning (SA&SP) and Concept and Requirements Definition (CRD) align to Concept/Operational Assessment; CRD and Investment Analysis (IA) align to Requirements Development; Solution Implementation aligns to Testing and Implementation; and In-Service Management (ISM) aligns to Monitoring and Post-Implementation.
- **RMA Stages Use Case Model (Figure 5-2)** — a SysML use case diagram used to *scope* the RMA effort: it shows what is inside the RMA effort and, by exclusion, declares anything absent from it to be out of scope. The Government owns all RMA activities, though the developer plays a key role in testing.
- **Service Risk Assessment** — introduced in §5.3.2 as the process for determining service criticality. It convenes all project stakeholders (domain and program representatives, project manager, systems engineers, requirements developers and managers, operations personnel, logisticians, a safety practitioner, human factors and acquisition/contracting personnel, test and evaluation engineers, second-level engineering, the NAS Resiliency Team, and other SMEs as needed) and draws on sources such as NAS-RD service availability requirements, the CONOPS, a Technology Readiness Assessment, facility and environmental factors, and relevant ICAO/RTCA standards.
- **Service Thread Loss Severity Categories** — the four-level criticality taxonomy that the Service Risk Assessment produces: Safety-Critical, Efficiency-Critical, Essential, and Routine. The NAS Requirements Document (NAS-RD-2013) attaches availability floors and restore-time limits to each.
- **Reliability Block Diagram (RBD)** — introduced as Step 6. It is built from the functional architecture, assigns quantitative reliability (and availability) measures to each block, and models success as an uninterrupted path from input to output. The handbook calls it a deductive, top-down symbolic-logic model that is often nested by level (subsystem, system, system of systems) and that feeds related analyses such as FMEA, FMECA, and Fault Tree Analysis.
- **Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** and **Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)** — introduced together under §5.4.2.3 (Graphical Analysis) as the complementary pair that finds design faults the pure math cannot. FTA is top-down from an observable top-level failure event, optionally using an Ishikawa (Fishbone) cause-and-effect diagram to trace causes to a root; FMEA is bottom-up and inductive, cataloguing every part/function failure mode and its effect.
- **Reliability Growth / Reliability Growth Testing** — the FAA's program (Step 9, §5.5.2) for driving reliability upward over time through design and development changes, with growth-modeling to plan improvements before fielding.
- **Duane model and Crow model** — the two reliability-growth tracking models the handbook singles out (§5.8.2): the Duane model, which fits future reliability to a mathematical curve and rose to prominence in the 1960s–70s, and Larry Crow's extension, which generalizes Duane to make it more flexible.
- **Reliability-Centered Maintenance (RCM)** — the FAA's adopted maintainability approach (§5.7, §5.8.1), a structured framework for choosing the optimum mix of maintenance activities, grounded in SAE JA1011 §1.1 (adopted by the FAA). Its three phases are functional analysis, failure analysis, and a decision process.
- **DCACAS (Data Collection, Analysis, and Corrective Action System)** — the closed-loop post-implementation tool (Step 10) for capturing, tracking, and resolving fielded RMA issues, fed by FAA repositories such as NASPAS and NAPRS.

## Key Concepts

- **The eleven steps across four stages.** Concept/Operational Assessment covers Step 1 (identify the service thread via the NAS Enterprise Architecture), Step 2 (determine service criticality through the Service Risk Assessment), and Step 3 (allocate parameters). Requirements Development covers Step 4 (examine the architecture), Step 5 (perform analysis), Step 6 (develop the RBD), and Step 7 (allocate RMA requirements). Testing and Implementation covers Step 8 (develop test artifacts) and Step 9 (test reliability growth). Monitoring and Post-Implementation covers Step 10 (collect and report data) and Step 11 (monitor and take corrective action).
- **Service thread and service criticality come first.** Before any number is allocated, the practitioner locates the system within the NAS by its service thread and establishes how severe its loss would be. NAS services are made of one or more service threads delivered to pilots and controllers.
- **The four severity categories carry hard numbers.** Per NAS-RD-2013: Safety-Critical services require two or more service threads and availability of at least 0.99999; Efficiency-Critical at least 0.9999; Essential at least 0.999; Routine at least 0.99. Restore-time limits also apply — for example, 6 seconds for efficiency-critical, 10 minutes for essential, and 72 hours for routine service restoration, with a 0.5-hour MTTR cap on non-routine service-thread components.
- **Availability is a joint function of reliability and maintainability.** Availability is the probability the system can perform when called upon, given it is neither failed nor under repair — so it depends on both how often the system fails (reliability) and how quickly it is restored (maintainability). It is expressed between 0 and 1, computed from MTBF and MTTR, and no real system reaches a guaranteed 1.0.
- **Series vs. parallel composition.** In series, any one subsystem failure brings down the system, so system reliability is the *product* of subsystem reliabilities and each subsystem must be *more* reliable than the whole. In parallel (e.g., a primary plus a backup), all redundant elements must fail before the service is lost, which raises overall availability. Combining systems always lowers availability below that of any single constituent.
- **Allocation flows top-down through the functional architecture.** A functional analysis (per the Systems Engineering Manual) yields the functional architecture and artifacts like the functional flow block diagram (FFBD), from which the RBD is built and RMA requirements are allocated to subsystems exactly as other requirements are.
- **Software is not hardware.** Nearly every NAS system has a software component, and software does not follow the classic bathtub curve. Software engineers should be consulted regularly, because RMA cannot be achieved by treating hardware or software alone.
- **Stringent availability forces testing-by-analysis.** Some NAS parts fail so rarely that they cannot be verified empirically — analysis is the only practical test method, and the Government should specify the required RMA analysis in its statement of work. For automation software, the sheer number of latent defects and the stringency of requirements mean there is no statistically valid way to prove compliance before fielding; multiplying operating hours across many fielded installations is what surfaces and corrects defects.
- **Fielded RMA can degrade.** After the In-Service Decision, usage changes and enhancements can introduce unintended consequences (e.g., more software failures from added code complexity, heavier legacy-function load from traffic growth), and platforms can become unsupportable or reach end-of-life — which is why monitoring through NAPRS and a closed-loop corrective process matter.
- **Maintainability is planned early but realized late.** It must be planned during development even though its requirements may not fully materialize until operation. RCM bakes maintenance into the design and plans for it ahead of failure, and several FAA orders (JO 7210.3AA, 6000.15H, JO 6040.15H) govern how it is approached.

## Mental Models

- **The Vee as a mirror.** Picture the left arm as "promises" (requirements determined and allocated) and the right arm as "proof" (verification then validation). Every right-side activity is checked against an artifact created on the left side. Because the Vee is iterative, you can loop back and re-derive when reality disagrees.
- **Severity dictates the budget.** Don't start from a desired availability number — start from *how badly loss hurts the NAS*. The Service Risk Assessment converts hazard and efficiency impact into one of four severity categories, and only then does the category hand you the availability floor and restore-time ceiling to allocate downward.
- **Series is a chain, parallel is a net.** A series path is only as strong as its weakest link (multiply reliabilities; each link must over-perform). A parallel arrangement is a safety net — every strand has to break before you fall — which is exactly why safety-critical services demand two or more independent service threads.
- **Math sizes the system; FTA/FMEA finds the flaws.** The reliability/availability equations tell you whether the *numbers* close, but they cannot expose a bad design. Run Fault Tree Analysis top-down and FMEA bottom-up to catch hidden faults early enough to fix them before they are built.
- **Field-then-grow for software.** Accept that complex automation will be fielded short of its reliability target, then let the accumulated operating hours of many installations drive the defect count down and the MTBF up — a managed reliability-growth process, not a pre-deployment guarantee.
- **Close the loop after fielding.** Treat post-implementation as a control system: NAPRS/NASPAS data in, RMA issues identified and tracked, corrective action out — by design change, requirement change, waiver, or new investment (tech refresh or sustainment).

## Anti-patterns

The source does not present a named anti-pattern catalogue, so none are asserted here. (It does, however, frame several recurring failure conditions descriptively — e.g., specifying reliability and availability requirements that do not correlate, attempting to verify stringent software reliability by statistical methods before deployment, and post-fielding RMA degradation from enhancements or end-of-life platforms — without labeling them as anti-patterns.)

## Key Takeaways

- RMA runs through four lifecycle stages mapped to AMS phases and decomposed into eleven concrete steps; left-side stages determine requirements, right-side stages verify and validate them.
- Severity comes before numbers: the Service Risk Assessment yields one of four Service Thread Loss Severity Categories (Safety-Critical, Efficiency-Critical, Essential, Routine), each carrying NAS-RD-2013 availability floors (down to 0.99999 for safety-critical) and restore-time limits.
- Availability depends on both reliability and maintainability and is computed from MTBF and MTTR; combining systems always lowers availability relative to a single element.
- Series composition multiplies reliabilities (weakest link dominates); parallel composition requires all elements to fail (redundancy raises availability) — and safety-critical services need two or more independent service threads.
- The RBD is the central allocation artifact, built from the functional architecture; FTA (top-down) and FMEA (bottom-up) complement the math by surfacing design faults early.
- Stringent NAS availability forces testing-by-analysis, and complex automation software is fielded under a managed reliability-growth program rather than a pre-deployment statistical guarantee.
- After fielding, maintainability dominates: RCM plans maintenance into the design, while DCACAS and NAPRS provide the closed-loop monitoring and corrective-action machinery, with reporting governed by FAA Order JO 6040.15H.
