# Chapter 8: RMA Requirements Guidelines

> Source: FAA-HDBK-006D (2020), Appendix D, "RMA Requirements Guidelines." This pack uses the D-revision; do not substitute 006B.

## Core Idea
Appendix D turns the handbook's RMA theory into a library of example, fill-in-the-blank requirement statements an author can paste into a Program Requirements Document (PRD) and then tailor. The guidance is explicit that not every example applies to every system, and that many lines need adjustment for the specific system at hand. The example requirements are written to FAA Order 1000.36, the agency's writing-standards order, which directs authors to phrase requirements with "must" rather than "shall." The overall logic is that RMA requirements are either allocated down a service's criticality thread or derived from the criticality thread of a parent system or service — so requirement authoring starts from criticality, not from an arbitrary reliability target.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Program Requirements Document (PRD) authoring guidance** — Appendix D's purpose: supply example RMA requirement statements for the PRD, each carrying blank slots (the `___` placeholders) the author fills with system name and target values. Used when drafting or reviewing the RMA section of a PRD; every example is a starting point to be tailored, not a mandate.
- **Criticality-thread allocation** — RMA requirements are allocated by a service's criticality thread, or derived from a parent system or service's thread. Used as the entry point: you classify the service's criticality first, then the appropriate RMA targets follow. The note attached to the general requirement points out that segregating routine and essential services from critical ones lets the less stringent (cheaper) requirements apply to the less critical services.
- **The four-level severity / criticality scheme** — Safety-Critical, Efficiency-Critical, Essential, and Routine, each defined by what the loss of the service thread would do to NAS safety and efficiency. Used to select the row of RMA targets that applies to a service.
- **The NAS-RD severity-to-RMA target table** — for each severity level the NAS Requirements Document (referenced here as NAS-RD / NAS RD-2013) fixes an availability/reliability figure and an MTTR. These apply to the *overall* system or service; subsystem values must be derived through analysis (the handbook points to its Section 7 for that derivation). Used to set the top-level numeric targets before allocation.
- **The three RMA measure sections (D.2 / D.3 / D.4)** — Reliability, Availability, and Maintainability each get their own definition, formula, and a numbered list of example requirements. Used as the per-discipline source of requirement language and the math behind each target.

## Key Concepts
- **The general RMA requirement.** The headline example states that the named system must meet the RMA requirements for critical, essential, and routine services per the NAS Requirements Document (cited as NAS RD-2013). The stated intent is simply that each service is delivered with RMA matching its importance.
- **Severity-level definitions (paraphrased from D.1).**
  - *Safety-Critical* — losing the service thread would create an unacceptable safety hazard while the system falls back to reduced-capacity operation.
  - *Efficiency-Critical* — the loss can be absorbed by cutting capacity without harming safety, but the economic effect on NAS efficiency may be local or NAS-wide.
  - *Essential* — the loss can be absorbed by cutting capacity without harming safety, with only a local efficiency impact.
  - *Routine* — the loss has only a minor effect on the safety and efficiency of NAS operations.
- **The target table (per NAS-RD, paraphrased).** Safety-Critical and Efficiency-Critical carry the highest availability/reliability figures (.99999 and .9999); Essential is at .999; Routine at .99. The MTTR column pairs short restoration targets with the higher levels (on the order of seconds) and longer ones with the lower levels (minutes up to 72 hours), and the Safety-Critical row is satisfied through a minimum of two Efficiency-Critical threads rather than a single number. These are overall-system values; subsystem values are derived analytically.
- **Suggested RMA design requirements (D.1 list).** A bullet set of design-level "must" statements, each to be detailed per program. Highlights: no single point of failure except for Routine-level systems; restart without manual data re-entry; automatic switchover from a failed redundant element to its backup and continuous health monitoring of all redundant elements; scheduled hardware and software maintenance with no added specialist workload; replacement of any Maintenance Significant Item (MSI) while operation continues uninterrupted on redundant equipment; maintenance on one data path not affecting the alternate path; capturing system state at a point in time; automated scheduled restarts to a known state; isolating failed resources for maintenance; identifying software and hardware problems; collecting support data and displaying problem descriptions; and accepting new operational, maintenance, test, and training software.
- **Reliability (D.2).** Defined two ways: the probability that a system operates satisfactorily for a stated period under stated conditions, or, for continuously operating repairable systems, the unscheduled Mean Time Between Outages (uMTBO). The probability form suits systems with a finite mission time; the uMTBO form suits systems that must run continuously. The reliability formula uses mission time `t` (set to 24 hours for most FAA purposes) and `m` (the uMTBO), where uMTBO is the mean operating service time between unscheduled outages. Example requirements name a uMTBO in hours per failure, require critical and essential services to keep running through a single hardware/software component failure, require no single point of failure, and (where redundancy is used) require recovery from a single redundant-component failure plus automatic switchover and health monitoring.
- **Availability (D.3).** Measured as the percentage of maximum available time during which the system can perform its mission. Three measures appear: *operational availability (Aop)*, the ratio of total operating service hours to maximum service hours; *equipment and service availability (Aes)*, which takes maximum service hours, subtracts the total unscheduled hours, and divides by that same maximum; and *system availability as the product of subsystem availabilities*. MTBF (or Mean Time Between Outages) lets a component of availability be expressed in time, and MTBF can feed both availability and reliability. Example requirements set Aop for critical services to 0.99999, service availability of equipment to 0.999, Aop of routine services to 0.99, a minimum service-availability percentage, a per-subsystem Aop chosen so the product reaches the needed system figure, and a subsystem MTBF target.
- **Maintainability (D.4).** Defined as the ability to retain a component in, or restore it to, a specified condition when maintenance is done by appropriately skilled personnel using prescribed procedures and resources at each level of maintenance and repair. It splits into *corrective* (unscheduled, triggered by a failure, restores performance — the requirements must permit it) and *preventative* (scheduled, keeps performance up via inspection, detection, servicing, and periodic component replacement — the requirements should specify it). MTTR is the maintainability measure: the average time to restore a failed system, spanning failure detection, fault isolation, disassembly for access, repair or replacement, and the remaining restoration steps. The note clarifies MTTR applies to Lowest/Maintenance items and that diagnostic design is what makes the required MTTR achievable. Example requirements cover LRU accessibility and removability at the operational location, offline diagnosis/fault isolation without operational equipment, continued critical/essential operation during scheduled maintenance, an MTTR target in minutes, scheduled hardware and software maintenance with no added specialist workload, access openings where periodic maintenance is expected, parts replaced within their wear-out period, MSI replacement with uninterrupted redundant operation, support-data collection, and Maintenance & Control (M&C) displays presented as movable resizable windows with visual alarms for errors, out-of-tolerance conditions, recovery actions, overloads, or other operation-affecting conditions.

## Mental Models
- **Criticality first, numbers second.** You never pick an availability figure in isolation — you classify the service's severity level, read the matching row of the NAS-RD table, then allocate or derive from there. Authoring an RMA requirement is a thread-tracing exercise.
- **Spend protection through segregation.** Because the less critical services can carry the less stringent targets, physically or logically separating routine/essential threads from critical ones is the lever that keeps cost down — the note frames this as a money-saving design choice, not just an architectural one.
- **Templates with blanks, not finished requirements.** Every D.x example is a skeleton with `___` slots; the handbook keeps warning that the line may not apply or may need tailoring. The author owns the judgment of which examples survive and what values fill them.
- **The three measures are linked, not siloed.** uMTBO/MTBF threads through both reliability and availability; MTTR threads through both maintainability and (via downtime) availability. Availability is the product across subsystems, so a weak subsystem drags the whole figure down — which is why subsystem targets are chosen so their product reaches the system goal.
- **Diagnostics enable the maintainability number.** MTTR is not just a target to assert; the note makes clear the diagnostic design has to support fault detection and isolation, or the required restoration time is unreachable.

## Anti-patterns
*The source does not name explicit anti-patterns in this slice. It does, however, attach two cautions worth honoring as guardrails:*
- **Applying every example requirement verbatim.** The appendix opens by cautioning that the examples may not fit every system and often need tailoring — pasting the full list unedited contradicts the guidance.
- **Asserting an MTTR without designing the diagnostics to meet it.** The D.4 note flags that achieving the required MTTR depends on diagnostic capability; stating the number without the supporting design is called out as a gap.

## Key Takeaways
1. Appendix D is a tailorable example-requirement library for the RMA section of a PRD — every statement is a starting point with blanks to fill, not a fixed mandate.
2. RMA requirements are allocated by, or derived from, a service's (or its parent's) criticality thread — classify severity before setting any number.
3. The four severity levels — Safety-Critical, Efficiency-Critical, Essential, Routine — map to NAS-RD availability/reliability figures (.99999 / .9999 / .999 / .99) and matching MTTR targets, applied at system level with subsystem values derived by analysis.
4. Segregating less critical services lets the cheaper, less stringent requirements apply to them — an intentional cost lever.
5. Reliability is a probability (finite mission) or a uMTBO (continuous operation), with mission time `t` defaulting to 24 hours for most FAA use.
6. Availability has three forms — operational (Aop), equipment/service (Aes), and the product of subsystem availabilities — and shares MTBF with reliability.
7. Maintainability covers corrective and preventative maintenance, is measured by MTTR (detection through repair-verification), and is only achievable if diagnostics are designed to support it.
8. The example requirements use "must" per FAA Order 1000.36, the FAA Writing Standards order.
