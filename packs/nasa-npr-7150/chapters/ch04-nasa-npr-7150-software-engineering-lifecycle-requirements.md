# Chapter — Software Engineering (Life Cycle) Requirements

> Source caveat: This chapter synthesizes Chapter 4 of NPR 7150.2D, a NASA Procedural Requirement issued through the NASA Online Directives Information System (NODIS) and rendered here from the NODIS HTML. These are software-engineering requirements that complement the broader software-for-systems area; they are written as binding "shall" statements directed at the project manager, each carrying a unique SWE identifier. The directive itself notes it does not bind the public except as authorized by law or incorporated into a contract, and that printed copies are uncontrolled.

## Core Idea

NPR 7150.2D Chapter 4 lays out the engineering activities that span the software life cycle, sequenced from requirements through architecture, design, implementation, testing, and finally operations, maintenance, and retirement. Each activity is expressed as a mandatory obligation on the project manager, tagged with a requirement number (the SWE-### identifiers). The throughline is that quality is built in early and verified continuously: requirements anchor everything downstream, architecture locks in the costliest-to-reverse decisions, and verification is woven through coding and testing rather than bolted on at the end. The chapter treats the project manager as the single accountable owner for establishing, recording, and maintaining the artifacts and evidence at every phase.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **SWE-### requirement identifiers** — The organizing scheme for the whole chapter. Every "shall" statement carries a unique tag (for example SWE-050 for establishing software requirements, SWE-057 for transforming requirements into a recorded architecture, SWE-066 for testing software against its requirements). These IDs let projects trace compliance and tailor obligations by software classification.
- **NASA-HDBK-2203** — Cited in the requirements note as the place to find recommended content for a software specification.
- **NASA-STD-7009 / NASA-HDBK-7009 (Standard and Handbook for Models and Simulations)** — Named as sources for verification-and-validation techniques and for analyzing models and simulations, invoked where flight software qualification relies on validated and accredited models, simulations, and analysis tools (SWE-070).
- **NPR 7120.5** — Referenced to define Category 1 and Category 2 projects, which determine when a software architecture review is mandatory (SWE-143).
- **NPR 8705.4** — Referenced for the Class A / Class B payload risk classification that, combined with project category, triggers an architecture review.
- **NPR 8705.2** — Cited as the source of additional test and integration requirements specific to Class A software.
- **NPR 2800.2** — Cited as defining the accessibility obligations under Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act that purchased or developed software must meet.
- **Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act** — Named as an external statutory constraint on software design.
- **NASA Software Architecture Review Board (SARB)** — Its NTRS-archived paper on quality attributes for mission flight software (NTRS ID 20160005787) is referenced in the architecture note as a guide for architects on quality attributes.
- **NASA Space Asset and Enterprise Protection Program** — Named as the source of guidelines a software architecture follows to protect mission architectures.
- **COTS / GOTS / MOTS / OSS / reused software components** — A recurring taxonomy of non-custom software sources that the chapter repeatedly pulls into scope (requirements capture, configuration management before test, and acceptance testing).

## Key Concepts

- **Requirements as the life-cycle foundation (4.1).** The chapter opens by attributing many industry failures to weak elicitation, poor specification, and unmanaged change. Requirements feed not just the product but also planning, estimating, and monitoring. They originate from customer, user, and stakeholder needs together with design and development constraints, and developing them spans elicitation, analysis, documentation, verification, and validation. Ongoing customer validation — through rapid prototyping and customer-involved reviews of iterative and final requirements — is treated as integral rather than a one-time checkpoint.
- **Well-formed requirements (note to SWE-050).** The technical-requirements process turns baselined stakeholder expectations into unique, quantitative, measurable statements. The validation criteria named are: well-formed (clear and unambiguous), complete (consistent with stakeholder needs), consistent (free of conflicts), and individually verifiable and traceable to a higher-level requirement.
- **Requirements analysis and safety flow-down (SWE-051, SWE-184).** Analysis draws from top-level systems-engineering requirements, safety and reliability analyses, and hardware specifications and design. Safety constraints, controls, mitigations, and assumptions distributed across hardware, operator, and software must be captured in the requirements documentation.
- **Change and inconsistency control (SWE-053, SWE-054, SWE-055).** Requirement changes are tracked and managed; inconsistencies among requirements, plans, and products are identified, corrected, and tracked to closure; and validation confirms the software will behave as intended in the actual customer environment.
- **Architecture as the highest-leverage decision (4.2).** The text asserts that a software-reliant system's quality and longevity are mostly set by its architecture, which embodies the earliest and most expensive-to-change decisions. An architecture formalizes subsystem decomposition and inter-product dependencies, serves as the basis for change-impact assessment, sets rules for downstream engineers, and provides a stable, documented structure (views, patterns, rules). It also documents valid and invalid operating modes or states. SWE-057 requires transforming requirements into a recorded architecture; SWE-143 mandates an architecture review for Category 1 projects and for Category 2 projects carrying Class A/B payload risk.
- **Design refinement to code-ready units (4.3).** Design defines architecture, components, modules, interfaces, and data to satisfy requirements. Architectural views typically include subsystem decomposition into design entities, configuration items, internal/external interface definitions, dependency relationships, and finite state machines. Lower-level entities are documented by attributes — among them an identifier and type; the entity's purpose and function; its constraints; the subordinate entities and dependencies it has; its interface; and the resources, processing, and data it involves. SWE-058 requires a maintained design, derived from the architecture, detailed enough that units can be coded, compiled, and tested.
- **Implementation discipline (4.4).** Implementation turns requirements and design into code, data, and records under defined coding methods and standards (SWE-060, SWE-061). Static analysis tools are required during development and test to detect, at minimum, defects, security issues, code coverage, and complexity (SWE-135). Unit testing (SWE-062) must produce repeatable results (SWE-186); each release needs a version description (SWE-063); and critical development/maintenance tools must themselves be validated and accredited so they don't inject errors (SWE-136).
- **Testing as verification and validation (4.5).** Testing verifies code against requirements and design, finds and closes defects before delivery, and validates behavior in the intended environment. Required artifacts are test plans, procedures, the tests themselves (including test-only code), and reports (SWE-065). Software is tested against its requirements (SWE-066) and placed under configuration management before testing (SWE-187) — and that CM net includes the test harness, models, simulations, ground support, and any COTS/GOTS/MOTS/OSS/reused components. Results are evaluated and recorded (SWE-068). Validation occurs on the target platform or a high-fidelity simulation matching processor, timing, memory, and interfaces (SWE-073).
- **Code coverage as a diagnostic (SWE-189, SWE-190).** Coverage is selected, implemented, tracked, recorded, and reported, and is verified by analyzing test-execution results. Where 100 percent structural coverage is not reached, the gap is classified into one of four causes, each demanding project-manager action.
- **Regression, hazard, and uplink testing (SWE-191, SWE-192, SWE-193, SWE-211).** Regression testing demonstrates no defects or security vulnerabilities were introduced into previously tested software. Requirements tracing to a hazardous event, cause, or mitigation are verified by test. Acceptance tests cover loaded or uplinked data, rules, and code for nominal and off-nominal scenarios. Embedded non-custom components are tested to the same level as custom-developed ones for their intended use.
- **Operations, maintenance, and retirement (4.6).** These are planned across the whole life cycle, not deferred. Operational concepts derive from customer requirements and are validated in operational or simulated environments. Obligations include planning and implementing these activities (SWE-075), delivering the product with as-built records (SWE-077), completing pre-delivery verification that all requirements are met or dispositioned and all designated defects resolved (SWE-194), maintaining per the applicable software classification (SWE-195), and identifying archived records and tools plus access procedures for retirement or disposal (SWE-196).

## Mental Models

- **Cost-of-change curve, front-loaded.** The chapter's logic — requirements first, architecture as the costliest-to-reverse decision, design techniques chosen to "prevent and eliminate errors as early as possible," static analysis during development — all expresses the principle that defects get cheaper to fix the earlier they are caught.
- **The four-cause coverage triage.** Uncovered structural code is not simply "missing tests." It is one of four distinct diagnoses — a missing requirement, a missing test, extraneous/dead code, or deactivated code — and each points to a different corrective action. This converts a coverage number into a structured investigation.
- **Traceability spine.** Requirements trace up to higher-level requirements and down to design entities, tests, and (for safety) hazards. A break anywhere in the spine — a requirement with no test, code with no requirement — is a defect to be reconciled.
- **Single accountable owner.** Every obligation is phrased as "the project manager shall," concentrating life-cycle accountability rather than diffusing it across roles.
- **Tools are suspect until accredited.** The directive treats development and analysis tools as themselves capable of injecting defects; validation and accreditation of critical tools is the control that lets their output be trusted.

## Anti-patterns

The source does not enumerate a formal anti-pattern list, but it explicitly frames certain conditions as problems requiring action:

- **Poor requirements practice** — the chapter attributes top industry problems specifically to inadequate requirements elicitation, specification, and change management.
- **Extraneous / dead code** — code not traceable to any requirement and not needed by the software, surfaced as one of the four coverage-gap causes that obliges the project manager to act.
- **Unmanaged inconsistencies** — discrepancies among requirements, plans, and products that are left unresolved, which SWE-054 exists to drive to closure.

## Key Takeaways

- Chapter 4 sequences the software life cycle into six engineering phases — requirements, architecture, design, implementation, testing, and operations/maintenance/retirement — each carried by binding SWE-### requirements on the project manager.
- Requirements are the foundation for product, plan, and estimate alike; they must be well-formed, complete, consistent, verifiable, and traceable, with customer validation ongoing throughout.
- Architecture is positioned as the dominant determinant of system quality and the hardest thing to change later, which is why architecture reviews are mandated for the highest-category and highest-risk projects.
- Verification is continuous: static analysis and unit testing during implementation, then plans/procedures/tests/reports, configuration management before test, target-platform validation, coverage analysis with four-cause triage, regression, hazard, and uplink testing.
- Non-custom software (COTS/GOTS/MOTS/OSS/reused) is held to the same engineering and test rigor as custom code, and critical tools must be validated and accredited before their output is trusted.
- Operations, maintenance, and retirement are planned from the start and close with as-built records, pre-delivery verification, classification-appropriate maintenance, and a defined archival/disposal procedure.
