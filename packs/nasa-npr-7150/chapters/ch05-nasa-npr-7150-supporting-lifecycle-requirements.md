# Chapter — Supporting Software Life Cycle Requirements

> Source caveat: This chapter synthesizes Chapter 5 of NASA Procedural Requirement (NPR) 7150.2D, a NASA directive published in the NASA Online Directives Information System (NODIS) and rendered here from its NODIS HTML. NPR 7150.2D states software-engineering requirements that complement NASA's broader software-for-systems area; the directive binds NASA projects rather than the public, and the authoritative version is the one verified in the NODIS Library before use. Requirement identifiers (the bracketed `SWE-###` tags) are reproduced exactly because they are the directive's own traceability keys, not prose.

## Core Idea

Chapter 5 collects the cross-cutting practices that hold a software effort together across its entire life cycle, rather than belonging to any single phase such as design or coding. Where earlier chapters track work that moves forward through the life cycle, these five areas — configuration management, risk management, peer reviews and inspections, measurement, and non-conformance handling — run continuously alongside that forward motion. Each area is framed the same way: a short explanation of why the practice matters, followed by a numbered set of "the project manager shall" obligations, each carrying its own `SWE` identifier so the requirement can be traced, tailored, and audited. The recurring actor is the project manager, who is accountable for establishing the procedures, performing the activities, and reporting the results upward to NASA's technical and assurance authorities.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Software Configuration Management (SCM)** — Defined in 5.1 as applying configuration management throughout the software life cycle to keep configuration items complete and correct. The directive describes SCM as exercising both technical and administrative direction and surveillance: identifying and recording the functional and physical characteristics of items, controlling changes, recording and reporting change status, and verifying compliance. It establishes and preserves the integrity of a project's products over the full life cycle.
- **IEEE 828-2012** (the IEEE configuration-management standard for systems and software engineering) — Cited in the note to 5.1.5 as describing what CM processes must exist, how they are carried out, who is responsible, when activities occur, and what resources are needed across a product's life cycle. The note positions CM in systems and software engineering as a specialty within the broader discipline of configuration management and calls it essential to both systems and software engineering.
- **Software peer reviews and inspections** — Introduced in 5.3 as in-process technical examinations of work products performed by peers to find and remove defects early in the life cycle, conducted under defined procedures that cover preparation, the review itself, recording and reporting results, and certifying completion criteria.
- **NASA-STD-8739.9, Software Formal Inspection Standard** — Named in the note to 5.3.2 as the source of recommended best practices and guidelines for software formal inspections.
- **Software measurement** — Framed in 5.4 as a primary tool for managing software processes and judging product quality, with analysis of measures giving insight into organizational capability and surfacing improvement opportunities. The directive scopes measurement programs to three levels: the project, the Center software organization, and the NASA Chief Engineer.
- **NASA-HDBK-2203** — Referenced in the note to 5.4.2 as holding a set of candidate management indicators usable on a software development project.
- **Software non-conformance / defect management** — Established in 5.5 as the tracking and handling of software non-conformances, explicitly including defects in tools and applicable ground software, with defined severity levels and mandatory assessments for acquired and reused components.

## Key Concepts

- **Software configuration items are broad.** 5.1.4 (SWE-081) requires the project manager to identify the items and versions under control, and the examples reach past source code to records, data, tools, models, and scripts. The note widens this further: the controlled set includes the tools and settings used to build the software — compiler or assembler versions, makefiles, batch files, and specific environment settings — because they can affect the software itself.
- **Layered control and clear change authority.** 5.1.5 (SWE-082) calls for procedures that designate the control levels each item must pass through, name who may authorize changes, and name who may make changes at each level. This separates the authority to approve a change from the act of making it.
- **Status accounting and audits.** The project manager prepares and maintains configuration-status records (5.1.6, SWE-083) and performs configuration audits to confirm the correct version of each item and that items conform to their defining records (5.1.7, SWE-084). Joint NASA/developer audits are also in scope (5.1.9, SWE-045).
- **Residual risk awareness.** Risk management (5.2, SWE-086) requires recording, analyzing, planning, tracking, controlling, and communicating software risks and their mitigations. The note reminds managers to stay aware of risk that remains after a mitigation is complete or after a risk has been accepted.
- **Defects caught early, by the right people.** Peer reviews and inspections (5.3) target early defect removal. 5.3.2 (SWE-087) enumerates what must be reviewed and reported — requirements, plans (cybersecurity included), designated design items, code as the plans define it, and test procedures. When forming a review team, the directive suggests including testing, system testing, software assurance, software safety, software cybersecurity, and IV&V personnel.
- **Reviews run on technique, criteria, and follow-through.** 5.3.3 (SWE-088) requires a checklist or a formal reading technique (perspective-based reading is given as the example), established readiness and completion criteria, tracking of identified actions to closure, and named required participants. 5.3.4 (SWE-089) requires recording the necessary measurements from each review.
- **Measurement serves explicit goals.** 5.4 lists what a measurement program is meant to achieve, including improving future planning and cost estimation, recording product information across the life cycle, monitoring and controlling life-cycle processes, preserving history and lessons learned, providing evidence that processes were followed, and giving indicators of software quality.
- **Measurement obligations and flow-up.** The project manager establishes, records, maintains, reports, and uses management and technical measures (5.4.2, SWE-090); analyzes the data with documented procedures (5.4.3, SWE-093); and, on request, opens the data and analyses to the sponsoring Mission Directorate, to the Chief Engineer for the Agency, to the Center-level Technical Authorities, to SMA at headquarters, and to any other organization entitled to them (5.4.4, SWE-094).
- **Margins and volatility are tracked deliberately.** 5.4.5 (SWE-199) requires monitoring measures so the software will meet or exceed performance and functionality requirements and satisfy constraints; the note ties this to computer-resource usage such as processor, memory, I/O, storage, and network capacity tracked against margins over time. 5.4.6 (SWE-200) adds collecting and reporting requirements-volatility metrics.
- **Non-conformances span acquired software too.** Tracking covers defects in tools and ground software (5.5.1, SWE-201). Severity levels must be defined and applied across tools, COTS, GOTS, MOTS, OSS, reused components, and applicable ground systems (5.5.2, SWE-202), with a minimum set of classes ranging from loss of life or vehicle, through mission success and user-visible issues with workarounds, to an "other" catch-all. Mandatory assessments of reported non-conformances apply to acquired and reused components (5.5.3, SWE-203), and process assessments apply to all high-severity non-conformances as a closed-loop process (5.5.4, SWE-204).

## Mental Models

- **The horizontal layer.** Picture the phase-by-phase life cycle running left to right; Chapter 5 is the band that runs underneath all of it. Configuration management, risk, reviews, measurement, and non-conformance handling are not stations the work passes through once — they are continuous services applied to every station.
- **Configuration management as a ledger with custody rules.** SCM behaves like a controlled ledger: every item and version is registered, every change is authorized by a named role and made by a (possibly different) named role, the status is recorded, and audits reconcile the actual artifacts against the ledger. The build environment is part of the ledger, not outside it.
- **The review as an instrumented process, not a meeting.** A peer review or inspection in this model has entry criteria, a reading technique, exit criteria, tracked actions, and recorded measurements. It produces both a cleaner work product and data — measurements that feed the measurement program.
- **Measurement as a multi-level feedback loop.** Data collected at the project level flows up to the Center software organization and the NASA Chief Engineer, where it informs planning, assurance, and process improvement, then loops back into how future projects are estimated and run.
- **Closed-loop defect handling.** A high-severity non-conformance is not finished when fixed; it triggers a process assessment that asks why the process allowed it, closing the loop back to the process rather than only the product.

## Anti-patterns

The source does not name anti-patterns as such. It does, however, set a minimum severity-classification scheme in the note to 5.5.2 (loss of life or vehicle; mission success; user-visible with operational workarounds; and an "other" class for cases not meeting the prior criteria), implying that leaving high-consequence non-conformances unclassified or untriaged is to be avoided. It likewise notes that assessments of acquired and reused components should be done pre-flight with mandatory code audits for critical defects, implying that deferring such scrutiny past flight readiness is undesirable.

## Key Takeaways

- Chapter 5 governs five life-cycle-spanning disciplines — SCM, risk management, peer reviews and inspections, measurement, and non-conformance management — each expressed as project-manager obligations tagged with traceable `SWE` identifiers.
- Configuration control extends beyond code to the tools, environments, and settings that shape the software, with layered control levels, separated change authority, status records, and reconciling audits.
- Peer reviews and inspections are defined, instrumented activities aimed at early defect removal, with prescribed scope (requirements, plans, designated designs, code, and test procedures), reading techniques, criteria, action tracking, and recorded measures.
- Measurement is a deliberate, multi-level program with explicit goals and mandatory flow-up of data and analyses to NASA's technical and assurance authorities, including resource-margin and requirements-volatility tracking.
- Non-conformance management applies across acquired and reused software (COTS, GOTS, MOTS, OSS, reused components) with defined severity levels, mandatory assessments, and a closed-loop process for high-severity issues.
