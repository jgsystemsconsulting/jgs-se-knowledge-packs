# Chapter 8: Launch Abort Modeling and Worked PRA Examples (Appendices A–E)

## Core Idea
Crewed launch safety lives or dies on the abort system, because no realistic booster reaches the reliability that human-spaceflight goals demand on its own — Shuttle ascent was projected near one loss in 100,000 flights yet suffered two losses inside its first 113. This chapter does two jobs the broader NASA risk material only points to. First, it lays out the specialized, simulation-coupled modeling needed to estimate how effectively a vehicle can escape its own failures, tracing a failure from a lost-function initiator, through propagation and detection, into a near-field hazard environment, and finally through the descent risks the crew faces given survival. Second, it provides the quantitative back-end the rest of the pack assumes you already know: the probability and reliability mathematics (Appendix A), the hardware failure distributions (Appendix B), the Bayesian parameter-estimation recipes (Appendix C), and two fully worked PRA case studies (Appendix D logic-based, Appendix E simulation-based). It is the chapter that turns the qualitative risk framework into numbers.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Abort assessment process (initiator → propagation/detection → environment → vulnerability → integration)**: The end-to-end method for estimating abort risk. It takes the failure "initiators" that the general PRA (the earlier chapters) produces as inputs, then evaluates how each one propagates to a crew-threatening near-field environment, the loss-of-crew probability given that environment, and the remaining descent and landing risks given survival. Used whenever abort capability is a cornerstone of crew safety; it explicitly extends binary fault-tree/event-tree PRA by adding phenomenological and simulation-based risk models so the result is an integrated, quantitative abort-effectiveness estimate plus insight into the dominant risk drivers.
- **Phase-evolving abort risk assessment (Pre-Phase A through Phase E)**: A tailoring framework that ties the depth and questions of the abort assessment to NASA's vehicle development phases. Pre-Phase A compares gross architecture attributes (propulsion choice, stage arrangement); Phase A focuses on sensitivity and trade studies around the physics of the most critical failure modes; Phase B accounts for component-level initiators and propagation; Phase C tightens uncertainties with design and test data and examines trigger selection and logic; Phase D drives uncertainties down before first crewed flight; Phase E folds in flight data. Used to follow a cycle of initial conservatism then successive refinement, concentrating effort where it most improves crew safety per unit cost.
- **Three-stage failure propagation framework (initiator, manifestation, environment)**: A model that inserts an intermediate "failure manifestation" stage between initiators and final hazard environments. A manifestation is the critical point past which vehicle or mission integrity is irrevocably lost. Propagation paths branch from initiator and manifestation stages and converge at the environment stage, so a small set of common states captures a large number of unique failure paths. Used for mature crew-risk models; earlier concept-phase models collapse this to a two-stage map (broad initiator class directly to worst-case environment with zero propagation time).
- **Abort trigger model**: A conceptual unit with three parts — a monitored physical state (a vehicle health parameter that goes anomalous in certain failures), a sensor to detect the anomaly, and verification-plus-initiation logic embedded in avionics software. Used to induce automatic aborts that either buy warning time through early detection or directly mitigate the failure; necessary for fast-acting, high-consequence failures that humans cannot react to in time, where warning time is the propagation interval between abort initiation and the onset of an immediate crew threat.
- **Blast overpressure quantification procedure (the 13-step worked recipe)**: A concrete, ordered procedure from the Ares I Ascent Abort Risk Assessment at PDR-level fidelity for turning a blast into a failure probability. It specifies blast energy and initial flame Mach (>1 detonation/CBM, <1 deflagration), interpolates within the Baker–Strehlow–Tang blast families, dimensionalizes via Sachs scaling, integrates a blast trajectory, layers in vehicle velocity and shock-reflection (heat-shield interaction) effects, then intersects the blast with the abort-vehicle trajectory and places the resulting threat in pressure–impulse (P-I) space relative to the crew-module vulnerability curve to read off a failure probability. Used to produce failure-probability tables as functions of mission elapsed time (MET) and warning time.
- **Pressure–Impulse (P-I) vulnerability curve**: A two-dimensional failure criterion that improves on a single peak-overpressure limit by adding wave duration (impulse) as a second failure mode. A blast threat above/right of the curve fails the module (probability 1), below/left it survives (probability 0), and continuous probabilities in between come from representing uncertainty in the curve's location. The curve must be expressed in post-interaction (reflected) terms to stay consistent with the computed environment.
- **Integrated abort model (spreadsheet → Monte Carlo hybrid)**: The single stochastic model that links all abort elements and computes loss-of-mission (LOM), loss-of-crew (LOC), and other figures of merit. Early phases may need only a spreadsheet (total LOM is the sum of per-initiator LOM probabilities; LOC is the sum of each LOM probability times its abort effectiveness). As detail grows — phase- and time-dependent failure probabilities, detection mechanisms, hazard environments, triggers — it migrates to a Monte Carlo hybrid simulator. Used to support sensitivity analyses, uncertainty quantification, and the trade studies that set requirements; the guide instantiates it as the Ares I GoldSim model.
- **Ares I GoldSim integrated risk model**: A worked instance of the integrated model built in the GoldSim hybrid (continuous + discrete-event) simulation environment, organized as five sections — inputs, failure initiators, Ares-generated failure environments, abort-only environments, and end-states/results. It samples initiators per mission phase, then branch probabilities, then warning time, maps manifestation to environment, samples hazard tables for LOC, and finally samples abort-only survival. Used to estimate overall ascent abort effectiveness at PDR with all components incorporated; all data are imported from accompanying Excel spreadsheets.
- **Boolean structure-function / minimal-cut-set formalism (Appendix A)**: The logic-of-certainty foundation. Events get binary indicator variables; series and parallel systems get their failure/success indicator expressions; the top event is a structure (switching) function of the inputs. A cut set is a component set whose joint failure guarantees system failure, and a minimal cut set (MCS) contains no smaller cut set. The structure function in disjunctive (sum-of-products) form over the MCSs, combined with the rare-event approximation, is what fault-tree quantification rests on. Used as the mathematical basis for every fault tree in the pack; coherent (negation-free) functions use cut sets, non-coherent ones require prime implicants.
- **Common aleatory models with conjugate Bayesian updating (Appendix C)**: The three workhorse single-parameter inference setups — binomial (failure on demand, conjugate beta prior), Poisson (initiating events / failures in time, conjugate gamma prior), and exponential (random durations, conjugate gamma prior). Conjugacy lets the posterior be written by arithmetic: beta updates as α_post = α_prior + x, β_post = β_prior + (n − x); gamma updates as α_post = α_prior + x, β_post = β_prior + t (or + t_total). Used for parameter estimation throughout PRA, with credible intervals read straight from spreadsheet BETAINV/GAMMAINV functions, or computed via WinBUGS/OpenBUGS (MCMC) for nonconjugate priors such as lognormal or logistic-normal.
- **Small-ET/large-FT logic-based PRA (Appendix D, Example 1 — Lunar Base)**: The classical PRA technique for steady-state, maintained, crewed facilities, where maintenance drives components to a steady-state availability. Small event trees depict perturbations and most system detail lives in large fault trees. Demonstrated end to end across fifteen steps (objectives/scope, success criteria, end states, system familiarization, IE development, MLDs, alternative IE methods, screening/grouping, scenario development, ESD analysis, success criteria, ET analysis, FT analysis, data analysis, integration/quantification), quantified in SAPHIRE.
- **Large linked-ET PRA (Appendix D, Example 2 — Planet X science mission)**: The technique for uncrewed, maintenance-precluded missions, where component reliability monotonically decreases with time and availability is therefore time-dependent and phase-conditional. Mission phases are modeled as separate event trees coupled by transition states that carry forward system status (fully functional vs. degraded/loss-of-redundancy). Used because conditional, time-dependent availability is awkward for small-ET/large-FT software but natural for linked ETs.
- **Master Logic Diagram (MLD) with pinch points (Appendix D)**: A hierarchical, FT-like graphic (but without explicit logic gates, and whose initiators need not be failures) for systematically generating a comprehensive candidate initiating-event list. Its top event is an end state; lower levels descend phase → function → system → component → failure mode. A pinch point is the branch-termination criterion: when every lower level shares the same system-response consequence as the level above, further development yields no new IEs. Used to organize IE brainstorming into a complete, eventually mutually-exclusive set.
- **Discrete-event simulation PRA (Appendix E — Lunar Base atmospheric leak)**: A scenario-based alternative to logic models that decomposes an ESD into simulation objects (Compartment, Leak Detection, Maintenance, Escape Craft), each a block diagram of states and transition events, advanced along a mission timeline (7,300 days / 20 years) over many iterations to produce cumulative LOM/LOC-versus-time curves. Used when behavior is highly time-dependent or unique — the expanded second example adds crew-outside-the-habitat missions, repairman availability, and abort-in-transit logic that traditional fault/event trees would handle only through awkward conditional and convolution calculations.

## Key Concepts
- **Abort effectiveness**: The conditional probability of crew survival given that a loss-of-mission-type failure has occurred. LOC risk falls as abort effectiveness rises, and for a fixed booster reliability the abort system is what separates an acceptable crew-risk goal from an unreachable one — which is why the assessment exists.
- **Failure initiator**: The starting point of abort analysis: the loss of a critical ascent function, described in enough detail to identify the component, the physical manifestation, the severity, the timing/ascent phase, and the occurrence probability (taken with its uncertainty distribution from the general PRA). The worked example — a load-bearing upper-stage member losing structural integrity while thrust is applied — names component, failure mode, and vehicle state at once.
- **Failure manifestation**: The intermediate critical stage between initiator and environment — e.g., for a fuel-supply anomaly, a "contained" failure (engine shutdown / performance loss) versus an "uncontained" failure that breaches the engine boundary. Refined manifestations such as External Fire or Turbomachinery Burst replace a generic "uncontained engine failure" as analysis matures.
- **Launch-vehicle-generated failure environment**: The near-field hazard at the end of propagation — overpressure from blast, high-speed fragments, a fireball, and loss of control. The first three usually coexist after a confined-by-missile (CBM) explosion of a liquid booster; their intensity and decay with distance, and their variation with flight condition, must be quantified for the crew to be shown to survive.
- **Confined-by-missile (CBM) explosion**: The worst-case liquid-booster detonation-class environment, producing blast overpressure waves and high-speed fragment fields whose magnitude depends on fuel remaining at failure and whose reach depends on vehicle/capsule altitude and relative velocity.
- **Blast yield**: The explosion energy, expressed as equivalent TNT mass, driven by how much propellant vaporizes and mixes before ignition — inherently scenario-specific and to be treated as highly uncertain; early work may use a fixed TNT-to-propellant ratio, later work computes it from mixing volumes and pressures.
- **Detonation vs. deflagration**: Supersonic flame fronts (detonation) make far more intense overpressure than subsonic fronts (deflagration). Internal propellant mixing should be assumed potentially detonable; unconfined release usually is not, except on the pad where ground and structures supply confinement.
- **Debris catalog**: The initial distribution of fragment mass and imparted velocity that drives fragment-field risk. Vehicle-specific catalogs are rarely available early, so surrogate catalogs from existing vehicles stand in; fragments are propagated with simplified (3-DOF) trajectory dynamics plus randomization, and Monte Carlo strike-probability estimation is practical when the piece count is small.
- **Loss-of-control environment**: Unlike explosions, this hazard does not extend spatially from the booster; it acts through the attitude and rate conditions handed to the abort system at separation, which can exceed structural limits or defy stabilization — strongly dependent on dynamic pressure and Mach number.
- **Warning time and detection**: Detection is a necessary condition for any abort; warning time is the interval an abort buys before an immediate threat arrives. Even a detected failure is fatal if the hazard environment develops faster than the abort, so propagation speed (fast- vs. slow-acting) is a first-order safety variable.
- **Crew-module capability vs. vulnerability**: Capability (abort-motor performance → separation distance, aerodynamics → descent trajectory, descent/landing system → land-vs-water risk) shapes the environments the crew faces; vulnerability (compartment rupture strength, seat acceleration limits, skin penetration thresholds) sets the failure criteria. A low-fidelity first pass on even a mature design is endorsed to spotlight the highest-risk subsystem (e.g., landing, parachutes, seats) before refining.
- **Indicator variable and structure function**: An event's truth maps to a 1/0 indicator; since indicators are binary, X^k = X. The top-event indicator is a structure (switching) function mapping the input vector of 0s and 1s to 0 or 1 — the algebra under series, parallel, and k-out-of-n systems.
- **Minimal cut set (MCS) and rare-event approximation**: An MCS is an irreducible joint-failure set; the structure function in sum-of-products form over MCSs, with intersections simplified first (X² = X), gives the system probability, and because PRA deals with rare events the union is approximated by the sum of MCS probabilities.
- **Aleatory model, prior, posterior, credible interval (Bayesian terms)**: The aleatory model (e.g., binomial) describes the random variability in the data (number of failures); the prior encodes pre-data knowledge of the parameter; the posterior reflects how data shifted that knowledge; a 90% credible interval runs from the 5th to the 95th percentile of the resulting distribution. The worked relief-valve example shrinks a prior spread of nearly two orders of magnitude after observing 2 failures in 285 demands.
- **Conjugate / noninformative / nonconjugate priors**: Conjugate priors keep prior and posterior in the same family and avoid integration (beta–binomial, gamma–Poisson/exponential). The Jeffreys prior is the standard noninformative choice (beta(0.5,0.5) for binomial → posterior mean (x+0.5)/(n+1); gamma(0.5,0) for Poisson → (x+0.5)/t). Lognormal and logistic-normal priors are common nonconjugate cases requiring numerical integration or MCMC; the logistic-normal is used when the failure probability could approach unity, since a lognormal could illegally exceed one.
- **Initiating event (IE), screening, grouping/binning**: An IE is the perturbation that starts a scenario. A broad candidate spectrum (benign to catastrophic, likely to extremely unlikely) is generated, then screened (drop the negligible, too-benign, or out-of-scope) and grouped — combining IEs with similar system response, summing their frequencies and conservatively assigning the group the most severe member's impact.
- **End states and transition states**: End states terminate a sequence (LOC, LOM, OK; the Planet X mission adds LOV-before/after-land-clear, LOV, MIN). Transition states differ — they carry critical-system status (functional vs. loss-of-redundancy) across linked event-tree phases rather than terminating anything.
- **ESD vs. ET equivalence**: An event sequence diagram and its event tree are equivalent in IEs, pivotal events, end states, and sequences; only the graphical presentation differs. ESDs are built first to rigorously link IE to end states, then converted to ETs for quantification.
- **Common-cause failure and the beta factor**: Redundant components share a common-cause failure mode given the same basic-event name in the FT. The beta-factor model splits a total failure rate into independent and common-cause parts — with a total rate of 1E-6/hr and β = 0.1, the independent rate is 9E-7/hr and the CCF rate 1E-7/hr.
- **Quantification and importance measures in SAPHIRE**: Linked ET/FT logic reduces to MCSs; event-sequence and end-state probabilities follow by Boolean algebra and probability theory (point estimate first, then uncertainty by Monte Carlo — the Lunar Base needed 50,000 iterations). Fussell-Vesely, risk reduction ratio, and risk increase ratio rank contributors; in the Lunar Base, four sequences cover over 99.6% of LOC frequency.
- **Hazard function and the bathtub curve**: The failure rate h(t) — the conditional failure density given survival to t — traces infant mortality (decreasing), useful life (roughly constant), and wear-out (increasing). The exponential distribution is the unique constant-rate case; the Weibull's shape parameter b<1, =1, >1 reproduces the three bathtub regimes.
- **Unreliability vs. unavailability**: Unreliability F(t) refers to a time interval; unavailability q(t) is the probability of being down at a specific instant. For continuously monitored, repairable items the steady-state unavailability is roughly λ·MTTR (λτ), a result the Lunar Base example uses to drop a 2-out-of-3 system to ~1.7E-3.

## Mental Models
- **A booster you can't make reliable enough, you make survivable instead.** The abort system exists precisely because state-of-the-art launchers cannot meet crew-safety goals alone; abort effectiveness is the multiplier that converts an unacceptable booster into an acceptable crewed vehicle, so modeling it well is not optional polish — it is where most of the crew-safety margin comes from.
- **Follow the failure, don't just count it.** Binary PRA gives you the probability a function is lost; abort modeling traces that lost function forward through manifestation, environment, detection, and escape. The probability of the initiator is only the first of several conditional gates before it becomes a loss of crew.
- **Branch-and-converge keeps the state space small.** Letting paths branch out of initiators and manifestations but converge back into a shared set of environments is the trick that captures many distinct failure paths with few common states — refine by adding states, adding branches, or sharpening probabilities and propagation times, in that order of payoff.
- **Speed beats severity for survivability.** A detected, severe-but-slow failure can be survived; an undetected or fast failure cannot. Warning time is the currency of abort, which is why triggers and propagation rates dominate the model as much as the hazard intensity does.
- **Conservative first, refined where it pays.** Start by mapping every uncertain or fast-acting manifestation to the worst-case environment with zero propagation time, then spend physics-based fidelity only where re-mapping to a less severe environment or recovering propagation time visibly improves abort effectiveness. The mapping refinement (e.g., bounding CBM probability by the chance of shrapnel actually penetrating the tank) is where conservatism gets bought back.
- **Two failure modes need two-dimensional criteria.** A peak-overpressure limit sees only one way to break the capsule; the P-I curve adds duration. The same lesson recurs — debris vulnerability gains a mass-velocity penetration criterion, structural response gains full impulse-plus-overpressure analysis — each refinement removes a piece of conservatism by representing a real physical mechanism.
- **Build the model only as detailed as the question.** Add triggers explicitly only if trigger effectiveness is the metric in question; otherwise fold them into the detection mechanism for a simpler model. Detail should be added because it sharpens the figure of merit, not merely because the data exist.
- **Maintained vs. unmaintained decides your modeling technique.** A crewed, maintained base reaches steady-state availability and suits small-ET/large-FT logic; an uncrewed mission's reliability decays monotonically, making availability phase-conditional and time-dependent — which pushes you to linked ETs with transition states. The physics of repair, not analyst taste, selects the method.
- **Conjugacy is the analyst's shortcut; MCMC is the escape hatch.** When the prior is conjugate, the posterior is arithmetic and the credible interval is a spreadsheet call. When a generic database hands you a lognormal, you lose the closed form and reach for WinBUGS/OpenBUGS — accepting a numerically-defined posterior you may refit or histogram.
- **Simulation trades easy uncertainty for easy complexity.** Discrete-event simulation makes time-dependent, conditional, "almost-failed" scenarios cheap to add — but uncertainty quantification and importance measures get expensive (rerun the whole thing many more times), rare events are hard to capture with naïve sampling, and the tools are not yet widely accepted. Pick it when complexity, not uncertainty, is the bottleneck.
- **Sanity-check the symmetry.** If tank-A-plus-valve-B fails the oxygen system, then tank-B-plus-valve-A must appear with equal probability; mismatched "identical" probabilities flag a data-entry error. Quantification output is checked against expected symmetries and reasonable IE frequencies before it is trusted.

## Anti-patterns
- **Expecting high launch-vehicle reliability without sufficient analysis.** The guide names the Shuttle experience directly: early ascent estimates of roughly one loss per 100,000 flights were not justifiable, and reality delivered two losses in the first 113 flights. Treating an unanalyzed reliability claim as a safety basis is the error abort modeling exists to correct.
- **Adding model detail merely because data are available.** The integration model should be only as detailed as needed to meaningfully assess risk with the available information; detail is justified by better estimation of the phase's figure of merit, not by data availability.
- **Applying conventional risk-importance measures to frequency-type initiating events.** Importance measures rely on driving a probability to unity or zero, but an IE frequency has no upper bound, so forcing it to unity actually lowers it — the guide warns that frequency-characterized IEs (like the smoldering wire) should be excluded from conventional importance ranking even though SAPHIRE lists them.
- **Using the rare-event approximation outside its validity.** The exponential approximation F(t) ≈ λt holds only for λt ≲ 0.1; the 2-out-of-3 example explicitly notes the approximation is inappropriate when λt exceeds that bound.
- **Defining the vulnerability curve against incident rather than reflected blast values.** To stay consistent, the crew-module P-I failure curve must be expressed in post-interaction (reflected) terms; mixing incident environment values with a reflected vulnerability criterion corrupts the failure-probability assignment.
- **Letting initiating events overlap.** The final IE set must be mutually exclusive; because many PRA codes cannot Boolean-reduce IEs with common contributors, overlapping IEs introduce logical inconsistencies internal to the software. A battery short retained as an IE must therefore be removed from the power fault tree to avoid double-counting.
- **Using a noninformative prior when you actually know something.** Complete ignorance is almost never the real situation; defaulting to a noninformative prior where informative knowledge exists can yield excessively conservative results — Jeffreys is reserved for data-dominated or deliberate-reference cases.

## Key Takeaways
1. Abort modeling extends binary PRA with phenomenological and simulation models, taking the general PRA's failure initiators and carrying each through manifestation, environment, detection/warning-time, crew-module vulnerability, and descent risk to an integrated, quantitative LOC and abort-effectiveness estimate — because no realistic booster meets crew-safety goals without an effective abort.
2. The assessment is tailored to the development phase, following initial conservatism then successive refinement: worst-case environments and zero propagation time early, physics-based re-mapping and recovered warning time later, with effort concentrated on the highest-probability, highest-consequence, or highest-uncertainty initiators.
3. The three-stage initiator→manifestation→environment framework, with branching-then-converging paths and abort triggers (state + sensor + avionics logic) that buy warning time, is the structural heart of a mature crew-risk model; propagation speed and detectability matter as much as hazard severity.
4. Environments are quantified with concrete, ordered procedures — the 13-step Ares I blast-overpressure recipe (energy, flame Mach, Baker–Strehlow–Tang families, Sachs scaling, trajectory intersection, P-I placement against the reflected vulnerability curve) produces failure-probability tables in MET and warning time — and integration migrates from a spreadsheet to a Monte Carlo hybrid such as the five-section Ares I GoldSim model.
5. Appendix A supplies the Boolean structure-function / minimal-cut-set machinery and Kolmogorov/Bayes probability rules; Appendix B supplies the hardware models (binomial demand failures, exponential/Weibull/Poisson time behavior, the bathtub hazard curve, unreliability vs. unavailability); Appendix C supplies conjugate Bayesian updating (beta–binomial, gamma–Poisson/exponential) with Jeffreys noninformative and lognormal/logistic-normal nonconjugate priors handled by spreadsheet functions or WinBUGS/OpenBUGS MCMC.
6. Appendix D demonstrates two complementary logic-based PRAs — the maintained, steady-state Lunar Base via small-ET/large-FT with MLDs, pinch points, beta-factor CCF, and SAPHIRE quantification/importance ranking; and the unmaintained, time-dependent Planet X mission via large linked ETs with transition states carrying loss-of-redundancy status across phases.
7. Appendix E shows discrete-event simulation as the method of choice for highly time-dependent or conditional scenarios (the Lunar Base atmospheric leak, then an expanded crew-outside-the-habitat variant), trading away cheap uncertainty quantification and importance measures for the ability to model complexity that would otherwise require convolution and conditional calculations.
