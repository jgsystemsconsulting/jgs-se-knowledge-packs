# Chapter 6 — Physical, Structural, and Phenomenological Models

## Core Idea
A PRA is built on logic models — fault trees (FTs), event trees (ETs), and event sequence diagrams (ESDs) — whose branch points need numerical probabilities. Many of those branch points are not governed by random part failures but by **physics**: whether a structure breaks under load, whether an operator can act before impact, whether debris reaches a populated area, whether a fire propagates between zones. This chapter is about the quantitative, physics-based models that supply those numbers. Phenomenological modeling is a physics-driven method for systems whose event progression is governed by physical processes; it complements reliability analysis by quantifying the logic-tree nodes that are driven by physics rather than by statistics. Where the `nasa-risk` material establishes the qualitative scenario logic and the risk-informed decision framing, this chapter supplies the deterministic-plus-probabilistic machinery that turns that logic into defensible failure probabilities — directly tying an existing design definition to the physical failure mechanism, which gives design teams actionable engineering information.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)
- **Phenomenological (physics-based) modeling** (Sec. 10.1) — used to quantify dynamic failure modes driven by physical conditions, interactions, and design limits, and to characterize unintended interactions between a system and its (uncontrollable, possibly deviating) environment. Use it when behavior is dynamic and unsteady, so small off-nominal deviations can cascade into serious failures — situations where statistical means and steady-state reliability methods do not apply. The models are deterministic engineering equations evaluated through time-dependent algorithms.
- **Stress-strength formulation** (Sec. 10.3) — first devised in civil and mechanical engineering to predict whether a structural member would hold, then generalized: a "strength" parameter (component capability in some dimension) is probabilistically compared against a "stress" parameter (the demand applied). Use it to quantify binary PRA branch points (FTs, ESDs) by computing the probability that capability exceeds demand. The probability of success / reliability is given by an integral of the stress and strength pdfs (Eq. 10-1). Worked example: the launch-vehicle attitude-control-malfunction ESD at lift-off, where the "enough time for the Flight Control Officer to activate the Flight Termination System" branch is quantified by comparing time-to-ground-impact (demand) against FCO/FTS response time (capability).
- **Range safety phenomenological models** (Sec. 10.4) — back-end consequence models that estimate casualty or health effects of launch-vehicle accidents. Named sub-types: **inert debris** models, **blast impact** models, **ray-focusing** models (local overpressure concentration / window breakage), and **plume dispersion** models (toxic propellant plumes). Use them in the consequence-evaluation portion of a space-system PRA. All are partly deterministic (known physics) and partly probabilistic (aleatory and epistemic uncertainty on key parameters).
- **Casualty Expectation (E_C)** (Sec. 10.4.1) — a measure of collective public risk: for one launch, how many casualties are expected across a given geographic area. Its baseline acceptance criterion comes from the range-safety requirement **EWR 127-1**, which (without a waiver) caps E_C summed over all areas for a single launch at 30 in a million.
- **LARA (Launch Risk Analysis)** (Sec. 10.4.1) — a software-implemented inert-debris approach used to demonstrate EWR 127-1 compliance at the Western and Eastern Ranges (used for all Space Shuttle launches). Five-step recipe: pick a flight-time interval and assume failure, pick a failure/breakup mode, build the fragment impact-point distribution, estimate E_C for the fragment, then weight by probability of failure (POF) in the interval and sum to a total risk profile. Monte Carlo is layered on top to fold in trajectory, wind, and aerodynamic variability.
- **Blast impact and re-entry risk models** (Secs. 10.4.2–10.4.3) — extend debris modeling to account for explosive yield of solid-propellant fragments (yield histogram fed to a blast model with breakage/overpressure tables and real-time weather through ray-focusing). Re-entry models handle high-altitude solid-propellant accidents over much larger ground areas, hinging on trajectory simulation and the vacuum **Instantaneous Impact Point (IIP)** trace compared against population density.
- **MMOD risk framework** (Sec. 10.5) — Micro-Meteoroid and Orbital Debris risk expressed as a conditional-probability chain (Eq. 10-2): Probability of Mission Loss = P_I · P_(C/I) · P_(D/C) — impact, conditional impact on a critical component, conditional fatal damage. Flux inputs come from **ORDEM** (NASA JSC) and **MASTER** (ESA); detailed damage from **hydrocode** modeling or empirical **Ballistic Limit Equations**; approximate damage from a stress-strength model using MMOD kinetic energy vs. shielding energy-absorption capacity. For deeper treatment the source points to **NASA-HDBK 8719.14, Handbook for Limiting Orbital Debris**.
- **Ground-based fire PRA** (Sec. 10.6) — location-dependent fire-risk formulation (Eqs. 10-7/10-8): partition the facility into mutually exclusive **fire zones**, estimate per-zone ignition frequency, propagation probability, and consequence, then sum. Bayesian updating (Bayes' Theorem) combines generic ignition databases with facility-specific fire records.
- **Probabilistic structural analysis** (Ch. 11) — propagation of input uncertainties through a deterministic structural model (from closed-form equations up to nonlinear finite element / fracture mechanics), with failure defined by a **limit state** g that separates safe (g > 0) and failed (g ≤ 0) regions. Output is a reliability function plus probabilistic sensitivity factors. Use it instead of factors of safety when uncertainties are large, members and failure modes are many, or in-service conditions diverge from design values.
- **Limit-state / g-function** (Sec. 11.2) — g = Z(X) − z or g = z − Z(X), written so failure is always g ≤ 0; probability of failure is the volume of the joint pdf in the failure region.
- **Stress-versus-strength analytical solutions** (Sec. 11.3) — closed-form p_f for the R−S problem when variables are independent **normal** (linear g) or **lognormal** (multiplicative g), via the standard normal CDF Φ; Monte Carlo for the general case.
- **MPP-based reliability methods** (Sec. 11.4) — **Most Probable Point** methods: **FORM** (first-order, linearizes the limit state at the MPP), **SORM** (second-order, parabolic fit using curvatures), **AMV** (advanced mean value, fast low-order polynomial surrogate to locate the MPP). The **reliability index** (β) counts how many standard deviations separate the mean from the MPP; p_f ≈ Φ(−β).
- **Sampling methods** (Sec. 11.4) — **Monte Carlo** (benchmark; works for any model but needs many samples for small p_f), **Latin Hypercube Simulation (LHS)**, and **Adaptive Importance Sampling (AIS)** to push samples into the failure region. Trade-offs are tabulated in source Table 11-1.
- **Probabilistic finite element analysis (PFEA)** (Sec. 11.5) — drive a deterministic FEA code from a probabilistic code; distinguishes **scalar** random variables (single FE input quantity, e.g., a point load) from **field** random variables (many quantities, e.g., a hole radius driving many node coordinates via a scaling vector).
- **NASGRO® and probabilistic fracture mechanics (PFM)** (Sec. 11.6) — NASGRO is NASA's standard FM tool for metallic structures; the **NASGRO equation** (Eq. 11-38) gives crack-growth rate da/dN, integrated between initial and final crack sizes for fatigue-crack-growth (FCG) life. PFM treats inputs as random variables. Per the most recent NASA fracture-control requirement, probabilistic damage-tolerance analysis is allowed only with the approval of the responsible fracture-control authority.

## Key Concepts
- **Stress vs. strength, in one sentence**: the component survives while applied demand stays below capability and fails when demand exceeds it; p_f = P(S > R). What makes it *probabilistic* is that R and S are random variables (each a distribution with a mean, a standard deviation, and a type), not single numbers.
- **Demand and capability generalize**: in the launch example, "stress" is time-to-impact and "strength" is operator/system response time — the formulation is not restricted to mechanical stress in psi.
- **Deterministic core, probabilistic wrapper**: every model here rests on first-principles physics evaluated deterministically, with Monte Carlo (or other propagation) superimposed to carry parameter variability. LARA, the blast model, and re-entry E_C all integrate deterministic and probabilistic pieces this way.
- **Aleatory vs. epistemic uncertainty**: inherent variability (aleatory) is irreducible — geometry, material properties, loads, assembly — and is modeled with pdfs (e.g., Weibull, lognormal) or random fields. Model error (epistemic) comes from incomplete information and *is* reducible as data accrue. Probabilistic structural analysis carries both.
- **Why factors of safety fall short**: a safety-factor design hides how much conservatism actually lives in the relationships among loads and member strengths, especially when uncertainties are large or when in-service loads, configuration, or usage drift from the design basis. The probabilistic approach gives a quantitative risk number usable for both new and existing hardware.
- **Conditional-probability chaining (MMOD)**: mission-loss probability factors cleanly into impact → critical-component hit → fatal damage, each estimable on its own (flux × area for impact, geometric area ratio for the conditional hit, energy stress-strength for damage).
- **Location dependence of fire risk**: because ignition frequency, propagation probability, and the exposed equipment/personnel all vary by location, fire PRA *must* be spatial — hence the fire-zone partition. Fire matters because it can take out components (like cables and wires) that are otherwise so reliable they would never appear in the logic model.
- **Monte Carlo sample economics**: roughly ten failures are needed to estimate a probability with confidence, so a 10⁻³ probability needs on the order of 10,000 samples — and aviation gas-turbine components may require p_f at or below 10⁻⁹ per flight, which is why efficient methods (LHS, AIS, MPP) were developed.
- **The MPP picture**: in transformed (u-) space the joint pdf is rotationally symmetric about the origin (the mean response), most of the probability concentrates near the MPP, and the failure probability is governed by the shortest distance β from the origin to the limit state.
- **PFM input classes**: the four families to characterize are the **initial crack** (often from an NDE probability-of-detection curve, or an equivalent-initial-flaw-size distribution), **in-service inspections** (which reshape the surviving crack-size distribution), **loads** (constant vs. variable amplitude, uncertain cycle counts, remote-to-local transfer-function error), and **life scatter** (material variability plus life-model error, often pooled into one actual-to-predicted scatter variable).

## Mental Models
- **The logic tree gets its numbers from physics.** Reliability methods fill in the random-failure initiators; phenomenological models fill in the *consequent* branch points whose outcome depends on how the system physically interacts with its surroundings. A PRA without the physics layer simply cannot quantify those nodes.
- **Statistics for steady, physics for unsteady.** If a process is steady-state, statistical reliability methods are appropriate. Once interactions are dynamic and unsteady, the outcome depends on a constantly evolving system state — mean-time-to-failure from a handbook is meaningless because there is no fixed state to average over.
- **Fidelity should track design maturity.** Start with high-level, top-down parametric models when data are sparse; add fidelity only when existing models become insufficient. A strong analysis is not the one with the most detailed physics everywhere — it is the one that best illuminates the interactions that drive risk at the current phase. Detailed analysis on placeholder data is less meaningful than a coarse analysis with honest uncertainty ranges.
- **Uncertainty is a roadmap, not just an error bar.** Early-phase uncertainties expose the next most pertinent design weaknesses, defining a risk-based analysis path; worst-case simplifying assumptions naturally point to the areas needing refined analysis and better data.
- **Limit state = the dividing surface.** Picture the joint pdf of the random variables; the g = 0 surface cuts it into a safe volume and a failure volume. Reliability is the safe volume; p_f is the failure volume. Everything else (FORM, SORM, sampling) is just a way to compute that volume.
- **Approximate methods are calibrated against Monte Carlo.** Monte Carlo is the benchmark to verify the faster MPP methods; FORM/SORM trade some accuracy (over- or under-prediction depending on limit-state curvature) for large efficiency gains, and AIS gives an efficient error check on them.

## Anti-patterns
- **Using statistical/steady-state reliability methods on dynamic, unsteady processes** — the source is explicit that traditional reliability methods based on statistical means and steady-state behavior are often inappropriate here; interactive unsteady processes do not follow statistical behavior and must be tracked with time-dependent algorithms.
- **Leaning on handbooks/expert data where physics governs** — physics-based models depend on laws of nature, so mean-time-to-failure cannot be read from a handbook when the system state is continuously evolving; they also do not, by themselves, capture human factors or human error.
- **Over-modeling early** — building detailed analyses on insufficient or placeholder data is called out as much less meaningful than general analyses carrying honest uncertainty ranges; fidelity should be added only when the existing models become insufficient.
- **Over-conservative deterministic fracture control** — conservatively bounding several independent, highly variable inputs at once can yield an unsustainably conservative "safe life" (the joint probability of multiple variables simultaneously hitting extremes is often negligible), driving needless expensive replacement/inspection while the true fracture risk is very low — and deterministic fracture control yields no quantitative risk number to feed the system risk summation.

## Key Takeaways
1. Phenomenological/physical models exist to **quantify the logic-tree branch points that physics governs**, complementing — not replacing — reliability methods, and connecting the design definition directly to the failure mechanism.
2. **Stress-strength** is the unifying device: compute the probability that capability exceeds demand (Eq. 10-1 / Eq. 11-6), and you can quantify binary PRA nodes ranging from FCO/FTS timing to MMOD shielding to beam yield.
3. The standard pattern is **deterministic physics + Monte Carlo wrapper** (LARA, blast, re-entry, MMOD, structural), integrating known physics with parameter variability and uncertainty.
4. Back-end **range-safety models** (inert debris, blast, ray-focusing, plume) translate accidents into public-consequence terms, with **Casualty Expectation** judged against the **EWR 127-1** 30-in-a-million criterion.
5. **Probabilistic structural analysis** replaces opaque factors of safety with a quantitative p_f via a **limit-state function**, solved analytically for normal/lognormal R−S, or by **Monte Carlo / LHS / AIS / FORM / SORM / AMV** otherwise.
6. **PFM with NASGRO** handles fatigue-crack-growth life under uncertainty; its hardest task is characterizing the four input classes — initial crack, in-service inspection, loads, and life scatter — and it is permitted in NASA work only with fracture-control-authority approval.
7. **Match model fidelity to design maturity** and treat uncertainties as a guide to the next risk-driving question, not merely as error bars.
