# Chapter 7: Uncertainty Propagation, Results Presentation, and Importance Ranking

## Core Idea
A PRA does not finish when scenarios are quantified; it finishes when the uncertainty in those numbers has been carried through the model, the results have been displayed in a form a decision-maker can read, and the individual events and parameters have been ranked by how much they actually drive the risk. This chapter supplies the quantitative machinery the broader NASA risk pack only sketches: how epistemic uncertainty in input parameters is propagated to the risk metric by sampling, how the propagated distribution and the underlying risk curves are presented, and how importance measures convert a single risk number into a ranked list of what matters.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Bayesian two-step uncertainty propagation**: The standard framework for computing output uncertainty. Step one builds a probability density function (pdf) for each uncertain input parameter, where that pdf encodes the analyst's state of knowledge about the parameter's value. Step two combines those input distributions through an appropriate mapping technique to produce a distribution for the output (risk) function. Used whenever the model output's uncertainty must be quantified from uncertain inputs; the resulting output distribution reflects only the parameter-value portion of uncertainty and is conditional on the modeling assumptions and the assumed input distributions still holding.
- **Three propagation techniques (Sampling, Moment propagation, Discrete Probability Distribution)**: The guide names three families. Sampling maps input distributions via crude Monte Carlo or Latin Hypercube Sampling to get an empirical output distribution. Moment propagation maps the first and second moments of inputs to the output's mean and variance using variance/covariance propagation. The Discrete Probability Distribution method discretizes input distributions before mapping. Of these the guide develops only sampling, because it has become the industry standard, is supported by PRA codes, and is now accessible through spreadsheet-based sampling tools.
- **Crude Monte Carlo Sampling**: The traditional, fully random draw from each input distribution. Used as the baseline sampling method; its weakness is that draws cluster where probability density is high, so the low-probability tails are under-represented and a large iteration count is needed for skewed distributions — a problem that motivated LHS.
- **Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)**: Sampling without replacement. The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of each input is partitioned into intervals (strata), and one value is drawn at random from within each interval per its distribution. Used to get more uniform coverage of the input domain with fewer samples than crude Monte Carlo, which is why the guide treats it as the preferred sampling method.
- **Convergence procedure for sampling runs**: A stepwise recipe when no automatic convergence criterion is set — start at no fewer than 5,000 iterations, record the mean, standard deviation, 5th percentile, median, and 95th percentile, then add iterations in increments of at least 1,000 and stop when the average change in each tracked statistic between consecutive runs falls below a target (typically a 1–5% range). Used to establish statistical regularity so the output statistics are trustworthy.
- **Three-category epistemic-uncertainty context (completeness, model, parameter uncertainty)**: Drawn from the regulatory-guide reference, this framework decomposes uncertainty about whether a probabilistic requirement is met into completeness uncertainty (events the model left out), model uncertainty (the simplifications and approximations inside the model), and parameter uncertainty (uncertainty in the input distributions themselves). Used when integrating physics-based phenomenological models into PRA, where full Monte Carlo quantification of epistemic uncertainty is usually too resource-intensive and sensitivity studies must stand in for it.
- **Importance measures (Fussell-Vesely, Risk Reduction Worth, Birnbaum, Risk Achievement Worth, Differential)**: A suite of quantitative rankings of how the risk metric responds to changes in an event probability or parameter. F-V and RRW measure the risk benefit of improving an element; Birnbaum (BM) and RAW measure sensitivity and worst-case impact; DIM extends ranking to parameters and adds an additive property. Used after quantification to rank events/parameters and support risk-informed resource allocation and mitigation decisions.
- **Exceedance curve construction**: A method for displaying risk when the consequence is a continuous, observable quantity (e.g., number of fatalities). Scenarios are ordered by increasing consequence severity, and the exceedance probability at each consequence level is the sum of the frequencies of all scenarios at or above that level. Used to build the aleatory risk curve; layering multiple curves at different confidence levels then communicates the epistemic uncertainty in frequency, consequence, or both.

## Key Concepts
- **Aleatory vs. epistemic uncertainty**: Aleatory uncertainty is the natural variability of the physical processes; epistemic uncertainty is the limit of our knowledge about those processes and about parameter values. Both must be accounted for, and this chapter focuses on propagating the epistemic, parameter-value portion.
- **Output function and input parameters**: The risk metric of interest is written as an output function h of uncertain inputs, R = h(x1,…,xn). The propagation question is simply how R varies when the uncertain inputs vary according to their assumed distributions — and what confidence bounds, median, and other statistics result.
- **Uncertain input event vs. uncertain parameter**: A basic event's probability (e.g., a failure-to-perform probability computed from a failure rate and mission time) becomes an uncertain *input event* precisely because the fundamental *parameter* feeding it — the failure rate — carries epistemic uncertainty described by its own pdf (the worked example uses a lognormal with a mean failure rate of 1E-3 per hour and an error factor of 3, the error factor being the ratio of the 95th percentile to the median).
- **Iteration**: One quantification of the output function from a single set of random input draws. Repeating the draw-and-quantify cycle n times yields n independent output values that together approximate the output distribution; with enough iterations the sampled input values reproduce their assumed distributions.
- **Statistical regularity**: The state in which additional iterations barely move the output statistics. The mean and standard deviation are the primary quantities to watch for convergence.
- **Epistemic dependency**: When nominally identical components are assigned the same failure rate, or when shared organizational factors (same operating/test/maintenance staff) couple parameters like failure rate, maintenance frequency, downtime, and human error probability, their uncertainties are correlated. Ignoring this coupling underestimates both the mean of the result and its spread. The worked example shows the mean dropping by roughly 5% when two identical-device failure rates are wrongly treated as independent rather than coupled.
- **Correlation coefficient**: The mechanism for specifying dependency, ranging from -1 to 1. A positive value means high draws move together; a negative value means one runs high when the other runs low. For lack of data, PRA models often assume full correlation (coefficient 1.0) between dependent variables, implemented either by declaring pairs fully correlated or by replacing all dependent variables with a single shared variable.
- **Point estimate vs. propagated distribution**: Substituting the expected unavailabilities directly into the Boolean expression gives a single point estimate of the risk metric; sampling the parametric form of that same expression gives the full distribution with its percentiles. The worked accident-scenario example yields a point estimate near 3E-7 per mission and a sampled distribution whose mean, variance, and 5th/50th/95th percentiles are reported.
- **Completeness uncertainty**: The residual doubt that the model has captured a broad enough spectrum of events; limits the ability to verify requirement compliance unless a wide candidate-event search has been done.
- **Model uncertainty**: The inexactness from the simplifications and approximations every model contains; only sensitivity studies showing insensitivity to those approximations can bound its effect on a compliance claim.
- **Scenario ranking vs. event ranking**: Ordering scenarios by frequency tells you which *groups* of failures dominate, but it can completely miss a single component that appears across many low-frequency scenarios. Importance measures fill this gap by ranking individual events and parameters.
- **Fussell-Vesely (F-V)**: For the sum-of-products risk form, the fractional reduction in baseline risk when the event's probability is set to zero — interpretable as the conditional probability that a minimal cut set containing the event occurs, given system failure. High F-V flags elements where reliability investment buys the most risk benefit.
- **Risk Reduction Worth (RRW)**: The ratio of baseline risk to the risk computed with the event set to zero (a "perfect" component). F-V and RRW are algebraically linked — F-V equals one minus the reciprocal of RRW — and both serve the same "where does improvement pay off" question.
- **Birnbaum Measure (BM)**: The partial derivative of risk with respect to the event probability — pure sensitivity, independent of the event's own probability. Highly reliable but structurally critical elements (e.g., passive components) score high on BM.
- **Risk Achievement Worth (RAW)**: The ratio of risk with the event probability set to one (assumed failed) to the baseline risk. RAW identifies the elements whose unexpected failure would most increase system risk, and it is normally computed by re-quantifying the reduced model with that event forced to unity.
- **Differential Importance Measure (DIM)**: An importance measure that overcomes three limits of the classical measures — they only test extreme (0/1) values, they cannot rank parameters (only basic events), and they are not additive. DIM expresses the fraction of the total change in R attributable to a change in a given basic event or fundamental parameter, and it sums across a group of elements. It is computed under two criteria: H1 (equal absolute change for all parameters, valid only when parameters share dimensions, i.e., probabilities only) and H2 (equal percentage change for all parameters, valid in all conditions).
- **Exceedance probability**: For an ordered set of scenarios, the probability (or frequency) of all accidents whose consequence meets or exceeds a given level; plotted on a log-log scale the risk curve is concave-downward, with asymptotes read as the maximum possible consequence and the probability of any consequence at all.
- **Dependence-suspect minimal cut sets (DSMCS)**: Minimal cut sets containing two or more components sharing a property (common environment, common test procedure, common design) that makes them prone to dependent failure; flagged and re-quantified in sensitivity studies to test the impact of failure coupling.

## Mental Models
- **Sampling is a simulated mass-production experiment.** Imagine thousands of nominally identical components whose failure rates are scattered according to the parameter's distribution; for a fixed mission time their failure probabilities form a spread. You cannot run that experiment in reality, so you simulate it by drawing a consistent set of rate values — that draw *is* the sampling.
- **The output distribution is conditional, not absolute.** It captures only the parameter-value uncertainty. Whatever the percentiles say, they still assume your modeling assumptions and your chosen input distributions are correct — assumption uncertainty is a separate problem handled by sensitivity studies, not by propagation.
- **A converged Monte Carlo run can still be wrong.** Even if the simulation is converged to several significant figures, the answer is only as good as the inputs, the model form, and the phenomena the model chose to ignore. Convergence buys precision, not validity.
- **The universe-to-launch-pad picture frames what the model leaves out.** Concentric scope — World, the modeled portion, the model — makes visible that the model deliberately excludes almost everything, and that the excluded near-field is where completeness uncertainty hides.
- **Couple identical components or you will under-report risk twice.** Treating two identical-device failure rates as independent understates both the mean result and its spread; the example's ~5% mean drop is a concrete reminder.
- **No single importance measure is "the" ranking.** F-V/RRW answer "where does improvement pay off," BM answers "where is the model most sensitive," RAW answers "what hurts most if it fails." A passive, ultra-reliable part can rank near the bottom on F-V yet top on BM. Read the measure that matches the decision.
- **Pick the display to match the consequence type.** A single occur-or-not end state calls for a pdf, CDF, or box-plot of the frequency; a continuous observable consequence calls for exceedance (risk) curves, layered to show confidence levels.
- **Numbers need only be accurate enough to separate the significant from the trivial.** The decision-maker has to tell risk-significant elements from minor ones; many insights survive the uncertainty, so precision beyond that threshold is wasted.
- **Importance ranking, not scenario ranking, finds the cross-cutting culprit.** A component buried in many low-frequency scenarios can dominate aggregate risk while never appearing in a dominant scenario — only event-level importance measures surface it.
- **For phenomenological models, sensitivity studies substitute for full propagation.** Monte Carlo over a thousand-line physics script is usually too expensive, so structured sensitivity studies on the statistical data and modeling assumptions become the practical stand-in for rigorous quantification.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating epistemically dependent variables as independent.** The guide is explicit that failing to couple the epistemic uncertainty of identical components or basic events underestimates the mean result and its uncertainty — the worked example quantifies the bias at about a 5% mean reduction.
- **Under-iterating with skewed distributions under crude Monte Carlo.** Because random draws under-populate the tails, risk/reliability models with skewed distributions need a high iteration count; too few iterations leave the low-probability tail poorly represented and the output statistics unreliable.
- **Claiming requirement compliance from a converged simulation alone.** Without addressing completeness, model, and parameter uncertainty, a probabilistic requirement cannot be verified no matter how tightly the Monte Carlo has converged.
- **Reading too much into the propagated distribution.** The output distribution represents only the parameter-induced uncertainty and is predicated on the validity of the modeling assumptions and the assumed input distributions; interpreting it as the total uncertainty overstates confidence.

## Key Takeaways
1. Epistemic uncertainty in input parameters is propagated to the risk metric by the Bayesian two-step process — build an input pdf, then map the inputs to an output distribution — and the guide standardizes on sampling, with LHS preferred over crude Monte Carlo for its more uniform domain coverage and lower sample count.
2. Convergence is not optional: track mean, standard deviation, and key percentiles, start at 5,000+ iterations, add at least 1,000 at a time, and stop when consecutive-run changes fall to the low-single-digit-percent range.
3. Epistemic dependency among identical or organizationally coupled parameters must be modeled (typically as full correlation); ignoring it understates both the mean and the spread of the result.
4. The propagated distribution captures only parameter uncertainty — completeness and model uncertainty remain, and for physics-based phenomenological models, sensitivity studies stand in for the otherwise prohibitive full quantification.
5. Results presentation is a deliberate communication task: occur-or-not metrics use pdf/CDF/box-plot displays (optionally conditioned on a fixed parameter), and continuous observable consequences use layered exceedance curves that carry the epistemic uncertainty alongside the aleatory risk.
6. Scenario frequency ranking is insufficient; importance measures (F-V, RRW, BM, RAW, DIM) rank individual events and parameters, each answering a different decision question, and DIM uniquely ranks parameters and is additive across element groups.
