# Chapter 1: Foundations — Risk Management and the PRA Framework

## Core Idea
Probabilistic Risk Assessment (PRA) is a structured, logical analysis method that identifies and quantifies the risks of complex technological systems so that safety and performance can be improved cost-effectively. Where NASA's risk-management doctrine (RIDM and CRM) sets the *decision* context, PRA supplies the *quantitative engine* underneath it: it answers what can go wrong, how likely it is, and what the consequences are — each with its uncertainty made explicit — and turns those answers into a risk profile that supports resource allocation. This chapter is where the qualitative risk-management framework of the nasa-risk pack acquires its numbers, its scenario logic, and its uncertainty discipline.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **PRA Procedures Guide (NASA/SP-2011-3421, 2nd ed.)** — A guide of recommended procedures (not a textbook or exhaustive sourcebook) covering different levels and types of PRA for aerospace applications. *Used to:* complement NASA's PRA practitioner course and to help analysts select an analysis approach suited to their problem. It is organised into a management introduction (Chs. 1–3), detailed methods (Chs. 4–12), results presentation (Ch. 13), launch abort modelling (Ch. 14), and supplementary appendices.
- **Triplet definition of risk (Kaplan & Garrick, 1981)** — Risk represented as a set of triplets — scenarios, likelihoods, and consequences — answering "What can go wrong? How likely is it? What are the consequences?" *Used as:* the conceptual backbone of PRA; preferred over collapsing risk into a single expected-value summary because prevention requires understanding the *elements* of adverse and favourable scenarios.
- **Risk-Informed Decision Making (RIDM)** — A three-part process (identify alternatives in the context of objectives; analyse the risk of each alternative; make a risk-informed selection) drawn from NPR 8000.4A and the Risk Management Handbook (NASA/SP-2011-3422). *Used to:* inform systems-engineering decisions and set baseline performance requirements by feeding PRA-derived uncertainty into the choice among alternatives.
- **Continuous Risk Management (CRM)** — A cyclical process of Identify, Analyze, Plan, Track, and Control, supported by Communicate-and-Document. *Used to:* manage performance risk against explicitly-stated requirements throughout development and implementation, after RIDM has selected an alternative.
- **Risk-Informed Safety Case (RISC)** — A body of evidence (tests, analysis, operating experience) plus commitments to specific manufacturing and operating practices. *Used because:* it is generally impractical, or even impossible, to "prove" a complex system's risk is below a threshold, so the RISC assembles the best available proxy that the PRA's credited assumptions and parameters will actually hold.
- **Scenario / Initiating-Event logic chain (IE → pivotal events → end state)** — The structural model of an accident scenario. *Used to:* decompose risk systematically using Master Logic Diagrams (MLDs) for IE identification, Event Sequence Diagrams (ESDs) and Event Trees (ETs) for scenario structure, and Fault Trees (FTs) for pivotal-event detail — the WASH-1400 lineage of methods.
- **Graded approach** — A philosophy (developed further in the System Safety Handbook) that the depth and resources of a PRA should match the stakes and complexity of the decision. *Used to:* start parametric, engineering, and logic modelling at low detail in a given life-cycle phase and refine iteratively as the project matures.

## Key Concepts
- **Why NASA came late to PRA**: Early Apollo-era probability estimates for a lunar round trip came out discouragingly (and suspiciously) low, so NASA stepped back from quantitative risk analysis for roughly two decades, relying instead on Hazard Analysis (HA) and Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA). PRA matured meanwhile in the nuclear, petrochemical, offshore, and defence sectors. The Challenger accident (1986), the House Committee's investigation, and the Slay Committee's 1988 report ("apply PRA at the earliest possible date") pushed NASA back toward quantitative methods.
- **The aggregate-of-benign-events insight**: PRA repeatedly exposed weaknesses that strong deterministic experts had missed. A central lesson is that examining single low-probability, high-consequence events is not enough — high-consequence scenarios can also emerge from chains of high-probability, near-benign events, and in aggregate those are often *more* damaging than the rare catastrophic single event.
- **Design-basis evaluation vs. risk evaluation**: A design-basis approach postulates a functional challenge and checks whether the system can cope, often presuming functional redundancy is adequate. Because it is not built on a quantitative risk view, it does not yield resource allocation that is optimal from a risk standpoint, and it cannot reliably ensure the right set of initiating events was chosen. Risk evaluation, by contrast, requires an *integrated* assessment across a comprehensive scenario set.
- **Performance risk vs. individual risk**: Performance risk is the probability of a shortfall against a performance requirement in any mission-execution domain (safety, technical, cost, schedule). An individual risk is a specific departure from plan assumptions (e.g., unexpectedly high attrition of design engineers) that *contributes to* performance risk without being identical to it. PRA's job is to quantify each performance risk while absorbing the individual risks that surface during the project.
- **The scenario logic chain**: A scenario begins with an Initiating Event (a perturbation requiring a response), possibly needs enabling conditions, proceeds through pivotal events (success/failure of responses, or occurrence/non-occurrence of phenomena), and terminates in an end state classified by consequence type — Loss of Crew (LOC), Loss of Vehicle (LOV), Loss of Mission (LOM), and others identified in NPR 8715.3C.
- **Minimal cut sets (MCSs)**: A cut set is a combination of conditions (e.g., specific component failures) whose joint satisfaction causes the undesired top event; a *minimal* cut set is one in which dropping any single member stops it from forcing that top event. MCSs are a major PRA output — the basis for quantifying top-event likelihood and for qualitative insight into how a scenario is prevented (prevent every MCS; prevent each MCS by preventing any one of its elements).
- **From ESD to ET to FT**: The ESD is a flowchart whose paths are scenarios — its advantage is communication with designers and crews, and it can mirror operating procedures. The ET (which first appeared in WASH-1400) distils the same pivotal-event logic into a tree where each path is a mutually-exclusive scenario; the conventional "down" branch denotes failure. Complex pivotal events are then expanded with Fault Trees, whose AND/OR gates resolve a top event into combinations of basic events. Shared basic events appearing in multiple pivotal-event FTs are how conditional dependencies between pivotal events are correctly captured.
- **Basic-event quantification**: A basic event must be directly quantifiable from data. Component failures are often modelled with the constant-failure-rate exponential distribution; basic-event probabilities in the guide's worked example are lognormally distributed, characterised by a mean and an error factor (the ratio of the 95th percentile to the median).
- **Aleatory vs. epistemic uncertainty**: Aleatory (stochastic) uncertainty is the natural variability of physical processes; epistemic (state-of-knowledge) uncertainty reflects limits in what we know — sparse failure data, or data of doubtful applicability to the case at hand. Both must be carried through the analysis. Epistemic uncertainty in basic-event probabilities propagates into uncertainty in the risk metric, typically via Monte Carlo sampling across the basic-event distributions.
- **Bayesian updating**: Bayes' Theorem maps a prior distribution over a parameter into a posterior that reconciles with new evidence. A diffuse prior (little supporting data) is dominated by new evidence; a tight prior (much supporting data) resists it. The guide's example shifts a failure-rate prior toward an observation of two failures in 1,000 hours, suppressing high-rate bins that the evidence renders implausible.
- **Risk metrics and importance measures**: A risk metric is a probabilistic performance measure in the decision model — for NASA, probabilities such as P(LOC), P(LOV), P(LOM), or large capital loss. Because full MCS detail is unsurveyable in real problems, results are usually viewed through importance measures, a middle level of detail between the raw MCS list and the single top-level number.

## Mental Models
- **Failure space, not just success space**: Certain thought processes must be carried out in failure space to surface risk-significant failure modes; if you only postulate the design-basis challenge, off-normal events are never tested against the system.
- **Completeness is the whole game**: If the dominant scenario is not in the set, the overall frequency is wrong *and* the prevention measures it would have suggested are invisible. The hydrazine-leak example turns on exactly this — a leak *upstream* of the isolation valves cannot be isolated, and missing that possibility would have hidden the dominant contributor and overstated the system's safety. Build the model hierarchically (scenario level, then cut-set level) so completeness can actually be assessed.
- **Risk analysis is self-focusing**: Scenarios can be subdivided endlessly, but resources are finite. Discard contributors by "risk significance" defined against the decision objectives — and expect that priorities set this way will differ sharply from design-basis-driven priorities. Phased analysis (a rough first pass to decide what deserves careful modelling) is a legitimate way to spend effort well.
- **You can't prove safety, so build a case**: PRA results cannot be "proven," so the RISC marshals evidence and operational commitments — including ongoing analysis of near-misses — as the best obtainable proxy for safety. The trade process itself, not just the chosen features, belongs in the RISC so that risk-takers (e.g., astronauts) can see the trade was done honestly.
- **Multiplicative independent prevention**: When two prevention measures act on independent elements of the dominant scenario, their probabilities multiply — in the example, jointly reducing leak-upstream fraction and avionics susceptibility yields a factor-of-20 reduction. But evaluate any fix in the full model: re-routing wiring to dodge one hazard can create another, and narrow-context risk reduction can distort conclusions.
- **Iterate, don't single-pass**: Scenario modelling is not done in one pass; the "data analysis" block in the PRA task flow spans the whole diagram and feeds back from quantification, uncertainty analysis, sensitivity analysis, and interpretation into logic modelling.

## Anti-patterns
- **Trusting design-basis adequacy as a risk verdict**: A traditional system-level evaluation might declare an engineered isolation function "adequate," yet the risk analysis can show the un-isolatable case dominates. Concluding "adequate, therefore safe enough" without an integrated risk model lets unimportant issues absorb attention while important ones go unidentified.
- **Examining risk-reduction measures in too narrow a context**: Optimising one scenario in isolation can mislead — a change may shift probability into other scenarios or alter the logic model, so its true impact only shows up in the whole model.
- **Shortchanging mission-success-criteria / phenomenology evaluation**: Because analysing mission success criteria can be expensive, a logic-model-driven effort is tempted to skimp on it — but that evaluation is part of structuring scenarios and underpins valid success/failure assignment.
- **Citing only the expected accident frequency**: It is rare for a useful PRA to stop at the mean top-event frequency; decisions often depend on percentiles (e.g., whether a threshold is met at the 95th percentile) and on importance measures that flag specific vulnerabilities worth fixing even when the overall number looks acceptable.

## Key Takeaways
1. PRA operationalises the triplet definition of risk (scenarios, likelihoods, consequences) and makes uncertainty an integral part of *every* step, not a calculation bolted on at the end.
2. NASA's modern risk management is RM = RIDM + CRM: RIDM sets baseline requirements by analysing alternatives under uncertainty, and CRM manages performance risk against those requirements through the life cycle — PRA is the principal quantitative tool for both.
3. The dominant value of PRA over classical reliability and design-basis methods is *completeness and integration*: it evaluates a comprehensive scenario set, including events more severe than the design basis, to support risk-optimal resource allocation.
4. The scenario logic stack — MLD → ESD → ET → FT → basic events → MCSs — is what lets a messy physical reality be mapped into computer-tractable logic while preserving the dependencies (shared basic events) that matter.
5. Both aleatory and epistemic uncertainty must be modelled; epistemic uncertainty is propagated (typically by Monte Carlo) into the risk metric, and Bayes' Theorem is the workhorse for building parameter distributions from limited or partially-applicable data.
6. Because PRA results cannot be proven, credibility rests on completeness, scrutability, peer review, and a Risk-Informed Safety Case backed by evidence and operating commitments.
7. Apply a graded approach: match modelling depth to the stakes and the life-cycle phase, refining iteratively rather than building maximal detail up front.
