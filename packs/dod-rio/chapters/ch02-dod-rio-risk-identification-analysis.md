# Chapter 2 — Risk Management: Planning, Identification, and Analysis

> **Scope caveat.** This chapter is the DoD-acquisition-side complement to the `nasa-risk` and `dau-se-guidebook` packs. It captures how defense programs plan, identify, and analyze *risks* (probabilistic future events) — the first three steps of the RIO five-step process. Use `nasa-risk` for the civil-agency continuous-risk-management treatment and `dau-se-guidebook` for where risk management sits inside the broader 16-process SE model. Where they overlap (likelihood/consequence scoring, the risk matrix), this chapter gives the DoD acquisition criteria and vocabulary specifically.

## Core Idea
A defense program manages each risk through a repeatable, documented process: **plan** how risks will be handled, **identify** what can go wrong and why, then **analyze** how likely it is and how badly it would hurt cost, schedule, and performance. Risk is characterized by *likelihood and consequence*; the program spends resources to lower the probability of a bad future event or to soften its impact if it occurs. This per-risk loop runs continuously across the life cycle and feeds prioritization and mitigation. (An **issue** — a near-certain event characterized by consequence alone — uses a parallel process and is treated separately.)

## Frameworks Introduced
Exact names as they appear in the source slice, with when/how each is used:

- **Risk and Issue Management Process (five-step)** — the overview process: Risk Management Process Planning → Identification → Analysis → Mitigation/Correction → Monitoring, wrapped by continuous Communication and Feedback (Figure 2-1). Applied to a *discrete* risk or issue; broadly valid across life-cycle phases, with the detail of each action varying by phase. This chapter covers the first three steps.
- **Program Risk Management Process (PRMP) document** — the detailed process-description artifact. Records risk management expectations, organization (e.g., the **Risk Management Board (RMB)** with its membership and meeting cadence), ground rules and assumptions, candidate risk categories, tool use, and personnel training; it also states its own review/update cycle. The **Systems Engineering Plan (SEP)** summarizes the process and may simply refer out to the PRMP for detail.
- **Risk categories: Technical / Programmatic / Business (External)** — the three-way grouping of acquisition risks (Section 2.2.3 in the guide). Used at identification time to classify a candidate risk and decide who owns it; Technical further splits into Technology, Engineering, and Integration sub-types.
- **Risk statement (two- or three-element; "if–then" format)** — a structured way to write each risk: the potential *event* plus its *consequence(s)*, and where known a third element, the existing contributing *circumstance (cause)*. The "if–then" form is given as one example; the statement must exclude any mitigation or solution.
- **Sample Consequence Criteria for the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) pathway (Table 2-1)** — a 1-to-5 ordinal scale of cost, schedule, and performance impact, anchored to acquisition baselines. Used during analysis to rate the impact of a fully realized risk; programs tailor simplified versions for other AAF pathways.
- **Typical Likelihood Criteria (Table 2-2)** — a 1-to-5 ordinal scale mapping probability-of-occurrence bands (e.g., Level 5 "Near Certainty" at the high end down to Level 1 "Not Likely") to likelihood levels. Used to rate how probable the event is under existing conditions.
- **Risk Reporting Matrix (Figure 2-5) and prioritized Risk Matrix (Figure 2-6)** — the 5×5 likelihood-by-consequence grid that converts the pair into a low (green) / moderate (yellow) / high (red) risk level for reporting, then displays the prioritized risk set with register roll-ups (e.g., counts of red/yellow/green).
- **Risk Register (Table 2-4)** — the central repository tracking each risk and the actions the RMB approves.
- **Risk Identification: Integration and Ilities (RI3) (2008)** — cited as one example methodology that pairs a top-level set of key processes/topics (such as design maturity and stability) with lower-level trigger questions.
- **Expected Monetary Value (EMV) / Weighted Consequence method (Table 2-3)** — a quantitative prioritization aid: EMV = likelihood × cost consequence if realized; subtracting mitigation cost yields an expected return on investment used to rank competing risks.

## Key Concepts

### Risk management process planning (Section 2.1)
Planning is the set of activities to develop, implement, and document how the program will manage individual risks. When establishing the process, programs: assign roles, responsibilities, and authorities; select and document the overall approach (process/procedures, likelihood and consequence analysis criteria, mitigation procedures, and cost/schedule thresholds that quantify impacts); establish traceability of each risk back to technical requirements and program objectives; align government and contractor roles, tools, and information exchange; size the resources (budget, facilities, personnel, schedule); and set the risk-management "battle rhythm." A trained **risk manager**, appointed by the PM, oversees the process in a supportive role — compiling identified risks in the register and summarizing them at a manageable level of detail.

### Risk identification (Section 2.2)
Identification asks *what can go wrong*, *what is particularly difficult*, and *what information is lacking* — examining the program for risk events and their underlying causes that could hit cost, schedule, or performance. The program drills down to **root cause** deep enough to support later analysis and mitigation design. Anyone on the government or contractor team may raise a candidate risk at any point in the life cycle; government personnel specifically carry a duty to independently assess technical risk. A frequently cited source of risk is **insufficient margin against failure** in the intended (combat or use) environment.

Identification starts by understanding the product/service and the requirements shaping it, then structures the **Acquisition Strategy** to mitigate the *key* risks (highest likelihood and impact). Methods named in the slice include: reviewing source documents and a reference design / mission profile; standard inquiry (brainstorming, interviews, lessons learned); mining JCIDS documents, government technical requirements/specs, SSRA, Analysis of Alternatives (AoA) products, Technology Readiness Assessments (TRAs), and test results; examining RFPs and proposals during source selection; systems-engineering activities across the life cycle (trade studies, dependency/interoperability analysis, SETRs during TMRR and EMD, critical-technology maturity assessment, FMEA / fault-tree / reliability analyses, specialty engineering); using leading indicators; commissioning independent assessments (Red Teams, Non-Advocate Reviews, ITRAs, Nunn-McCurdy Reviews, Critical Change Reviews, Independent Cost Estimates); analyzing metric trends (KPPs, KSAs, TPMs, schedule, budget, EVM, Class I/II change rates); watching external influences (requirements creep, funding changes, synchronization with critical external programs, statutory/policy shifts, industrial-base availability); and production concerns (make-buy, supplier changes, obsolescence, manufacturing readiness). Planning documents (AS, SEP, TEMP, IMS) are themselves a risk source — poor planning can miss intrinsic risks or introduce new ones (the worked example: failing to plan test-range time causing a later schedule hit).

### Risk categories (Section 2.2.3)
- **Technical** — risks that may keep the end item from performing as intended; from requirements, technology, engineering, integration, test, manufacturing, quality, logistics, system security, training. The slice stresses three commonly confused sub-types: **Technology** (moving advances out of the lab through prototyping into engineering), **Engineering** (applying engineering principles to turn stakeholder requirements into affordable systems — technical processes, technical management, products), and **Integration** (interfacing system elements internally and with other systems, functional and physical, component up to system-of-systems).
- **Programmatic** — non-technical risks generally within the PM's or PEO's control or influence: estimating, planning, execution, communications, contract structure.
- **Business (External)** — non-technical risks originating outside the program office or beyond the PM's control: dependencies, resources, priorities, regulations, stakeholders, market factors, weather. These are escalated up the chain as appropriate, and the program holds contingency plans for those it cannot directly control.

A single risk often spans categories (a technical root cause producing cost and schedule risk). The PM focuses controllable risks and works the chain of command on the rest.

### The risk statement (Section 2.2.3)
A good statement pairs the potential **event** with its **consequence(s)**, adding the contributing **cause** when known. A structured, single, consistent format prevents vague statements; the "if–then" form is one example. The statement must not embed a mitigation strategy or other extraneous content. Candidate risks are evaluated by working-level staff and SMEs, then presented to the **RMB** for a disposition: approved, rejected, deferred (need more information), management action, or engineering process/practice item. Items handled by management action or engineering process do not enter the formal risk process — unless a limiting constraint appears, which returns them as a candidate.

### Risk analysis (Section 2.3)
Analysis answers *what are the likelihood and consequence* and *how high is the risk*. The program estimates likelihood, estimates cost/schedule/performance consequences, derives a risk level for each risk, then — once all risks have levels — prioritizes for mitigation. Government and contractor should use a **common framework**; consistent predefined criteria let decision-makers compare risks objectively. Analysis is partly subjective and qualitative, but programs underpin it with **quantitative data where practical** (dollars, schedule time, mission loss can replace qualitative scores). Working from consequence first is offered as an efficiency tactic.

- **Consequence (Section 2.3.1)** — the deviation of a fully realized risk against cost, schedule, and performance baselines, scored 1-5 against tailored criteria. Breaching a KPP or APB threshold should trigger a Level 5 performance rating. The score captures the *greatest* anticipated impact across the three areas with no further mitigation assumed (a cost-2 / schedule-3 / performance-2 risk is characterized as a **3**). Programs should use fully burdened costs — including a reasonable share of program burn-rate overhead for schedule extensions.
- **Likelihood (Section 2.3.2)** — the evaluated probability the event occurs under existing conditions, tied to a specific risk statement and scored 1-5 against probability bands. Likelihood is judged together with consequence and projected mitigation effectiveness; a high enough likelihood-and-consequence pair can justify changing course absent assured mitigation.
- **Aggregate risk** — beyond individual risks, programs assess overall exposure: multiple risks can jeopardize a program more than any one, through complexity, stretched resources, risk interactions, or compounded likelihood. **Monte Carlo** schedule risk analysis (SRA) and cost risk analysis (CRA) simulate the cumulative effect on total duration or cost.
- **Reporting and prioritization (Section 2.3.3)** — the 5×5 matrix maps each risk's likelihood against the *maximum* of its three consequence scores to a low/moderate/high level. Risk level alone does not set priority; programs also weigh mitigation cost-effectiveness, frequency, time frame, and inter-risk relationships, and may use EMV/ROI (Table 2-3) to rank where scarce resources go. A KPP-threatening risk is normally prioritized above a higher-ROI risk. Safety and system-hazard / ESOH risks ride the same prioritized matrix at key decision points.
- **Risk register (Section 2.3.4)** — built as early as possible; records category, risk statement, likelihood, consequence, planned mitigation, owner, WBS/IMS linkage, closure dates, and change history (often the if–then event plus root cause). It supplies traceability and lessons learned; the risk, issue, and opportunity registers may be combined.

## Mental Models
- **Likelihood × consequence, then maximum-of-three.** A risk's reported level comes from its likelihood paired with the *single worst* of its cost, schedule, and performance consequences — not an average. Worst-case in any one dimension drives the rating.
- **Score the unmitigated risk.** Consequence is assessed as if the risk were *fully realized with no further mitigation*. Mitigation lowers the *target* rating later; it must not soften the initial score.
- **Common framework over agreement.** Government and contractor will disagree on individual risks and priorities; the point is a shared scoring framework so the disagreements are visible and comparable, not a forced consensus.
- **Margin is the lens for technical risk.** Reframing technical risk as *design margin against failure in environments* turns mitigation into a concrete move: restore margin through more robust design, or accept defined losses as part of the system solution.
- **Plan the planning documents are a risk surface.** The AS, SEP, TEMP, and IMS are not just outputs of risk management — gaps or sequencing errors inside them are themselves a source of risk to hunt for.
- **Manage the portfolio, not just the items.** Individual-risk hygiene is necessary but insufficient; aggregate/compounding exposure (modeled via SRA/CRA Monte Carlo) is a distinct thing leaders must watch.

## Anti-patterns
Patterns the source explicitly warns against:
- **Fractional scoring.** Consequence and likelihood values are ordinal (1-5). A score like 2.4 falsely implies precision and invalidates comparisons — programs should avoid it.
- **Putting mitigation inside the risk statement.** The risk statement must hold only event, consequence, and (if known) cause — never a mitigation strategy, alternate solution, or other extraneous information.
- **Vague or inconsistent risk statements.** Without a single structured format (e.g., if–then) statements drift; the structured approach exists specifically to prevent this.
- **Over-conservative requirements/specs.** Programs should check that requirements allocated to specifications are not excessively conservative and that each delivers value commensurate with its cost and schedule.
- **Confusing technology, engineering, and integration risk.** The guide calls out that programs sometimes conflate these three technical sub-types, and defines each to keep them distinct.

## Key Takeaways
1. The per-risk loop is **plan → identify → analyze → mitigate → monitor**, with continuous communication and feedback; this chapter covers the first three steps. Detail varies by life-cycle phase.
2. The **PRMP document** holds the detailed process (RMB, ground rules, categories, tools, training); the **SEP** summarizes it. Document cost/schedule thresholds in both.
3. Identify risks early and continuously, from many sources, and drill to **root cause**; structure the Acquisition Strategy around the highest likelihood-and-impact key risks.
4. Classify each risk as **Technical, Programmatic, or Business (External)**; technical splits into Technology, Engineering, and Integration.
5. Write a **structured risk statement** — event + consequence (+ cause), no mitigation, ideally "if–then."
6. Analyze with **1-5 ordinal** likelihood and consequence scales; consequence is the *maximum* across cost/schedule/performance, scored unmitigated, using fully burdened costs.
7. Use a **common government/contractor framework**, quantitative data where practical, and Monte Carlo SRA/CRA for **aggregate** exposure.
8. The **risk matrix** converts likelihood × max-consequence into low/moderate/high; prioritize with extra factors and **EMV/ROI**, but always rank KPP-threatening risks high.
9. Maintain a **risk register** from the start as the single source of truth and lessons-learned trail; consider merging risk/issue/opportunity registers.
