# Chapter — Managing Risk by AAF Pathway: UCA, MTA, and Major Capability Acquisition

## Core Idea
The DoD does not run one risk process for all acquisitions. Under the Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF), the program manager selects an acquisition pathway whose tempo, oversight, and decision structure already encode a particular risk posture — then tailors the risk effort to match. A two-year urgent fielding accepts unresolved deficiencies that a multi-year major program would never carry past a milestone; a five-year prototyping effort deliberately fails tests early so it can go fast; a major capability program front-loads its hardest risk decisions into the earliest phases, before money is committed. This chapter shows how RIO management changes shape across three of the six pathways — Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA), Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA), and Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) — with emphasis on the hardware-centric MCA life cycle where systems-engineering technical reviews carry the risk-burndown load.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)** — described in DoDI 5000.02; the umbrella of six pathways (UCA, MTA, MCA, Software Acquisition, Defense Business Systems, and Acquisition of Services) that the PM chooses among and tailors. Policy authority sits in DoDD 5000.01. Use it as the top-level decision: pick the pathway whose timeline and structure fit the capability, then tailor within it. One of the six tenets named in DoDD 5000.01 is to actively manage risk.
- **Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA) pathway** — governed by DoDD 5000.71 and DoDI 5000.81; for fielding capabilities against urgent or emerging operational needs in under two years, with aggressively streamlined process. Use it when the operational clock, not the engineering clock, sets the schedule. Its phases are Pre-Development, Development, Production and Deployment, and Operations and Support.
- **Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) pathway** — governed by DoDI 5000.80; delivers capability in roughly two to five years via two paths: Rapid Prototyping (fieldable prototype of new capability within five years) and Rapid Fielding (production quantities of proven-technology systems with minimal development). Notably, MTA is not subject to the JCIDS requirements process by statute, except where the pathway explicitly defines it.
- **Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) pathway** — derived from DoDI 5000.85; the traditional milestone-driven life cycle (MDD, then Milestones A, B, C through IOC/FOC) for complex hardware-intensive systems. DoDI 5000.85 requires the PM to document a comprehensive risk-management approach and the top program risks with mitigation plans in the Acquisition Strategy. DoDI 5000.88 requires the technical approach, key technical risks, and mitigation execution to appear in the Systems Engineering Plan (SEP).
- **Framing Assumptions** — assumptions made (sometimes unconsciously) when building the Acquisition Strategy and program framework; they must be identified and validated because an incorrect one can place the whole program at risk. Use them as a named risk category. Examples the guide gives include requirement priority or achievability, schedule dependencies, procurement quantities, threats, availability of specialty metals or technologies, and model/simulation accuracy.
- **Government and Contractor Risk-Management Integration** — the principle that program-office, prime, and subcontractor risk processes should be compatible so risk registers align and data transfers cleanly; the RFP is the lever, requiring offerors to state the tasks, processes, and tools for joint risk management. Appendix B of the source lists typical Government and contractor risk responsibilities.
- **Disposition Analysis (UCA)** — the structured end-of-life decision for an urgently fielded capability, conducted no later than one year after entering O&S, recommending Termination (demilitarization/disposal), Sustainment for the current contingency, or Transition to a Program of Record.

## Key Concepts
- **Pathway choice is a risk decision.** The PM tailors the Acquisition Strategy to the chosen pathway, and any pathway may be tailored to the specific capability. The selection itself sets how much risk the program will carry and how fast it must move.
- **MCA entry point is set by risk, not by default.** The guide ties the entry milestone directly to where the risk lives: if technology maturity or requirements stability is doubtful, enter at Milestone A and run a risk-reduction phase with competitive prototypes; if technologies are mature, integration risk is acceptable, and requirements are stable and achievable, enter at Milestone B (EMD); if a materiel solution already exists and needs only militarization, enter at Milestone C with a small R&D effort.
- **Earliest decisions dominate risk.** For MCA, the guide states the most important risk-control decisions are made early in the life cycle, while financial commitment is still low — which is why the PM prioritizes burning down the highest reducible risks in TMRR.
- **SETRs as risk gates.** The MCA life cycle threads systems-engineering technical reviews (SETRs) and audits through every phase as risk instruments: ASR, SRR, SFR, PDR in the early/TMRR phases; CDR, TRR, SVR, FCA, PRR in EMD; OTRR, IOT&E, PCA in P&D. The PCA verifies the built product matches the design and the Capability Production Document, and surfaces fielding and sustainment risks. Mandatory technical reviews trace to DoDI 5000.88; test reviews to DoDI 5000.89.
- **Readiness assessments quantify risk before commitment.** The guide calls for a Government Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA) early in TMRR and again before Milestone B to assess critical technology elements, a Manufacturing Readiness Assessment (MRA) for manufacturing maturity (and again before LRIP and FRP), and Schedule Risk Assessments (SRAs) on a regular cadence.
- **Accepted deficiencies are a feature of UCA, not a defect.** Assessing a UCA capability does not require resolving every identified deficiency — including safety deficiencies — before production or deployment. The Decision Authority, with the user and requirements validation authority, decides which deficiencies must be fixed and which risks to accept; accepted risks let the user community build tactics, techniques, and procedures to reduce the operational risk.
- **Contract type follows the risk profile.** The program's risk profile is the dominant factor in choosing contract type. Cost-type contracts suit higher inherent technical risk (typical of development) and keep the Government in control of mitigation decisions; fixed-price suits stable requirements and well-understood, minimal technical risk. Crucially, even on fixed price the Government still needs the product and bears the consequence of non-delivery, so risk is never fully transferred to industry.
- **Operational risk is a first-class category in UCA.** The Pre-Development analysis weighs not just acquisition risk (cost, schedule, performance) of each course of action but the operational risk to the requesting commander if no effective solution is delivered inside the commander's stated timeline.
- **Supply-chain risk is treated as a life-cycle thread.** Across UCA Pre-Development, MTA Rapid Prototyping, and the MCA RFP, the guide repeats a risk-based life-cycle management approach: identify susceptibilities, vulnerabilities, and threats across the supply chain — supplier, product and subcomponents, and the chain itself — and develop mitigations spanning the entire life cycle.
- **Sustainment Reviews close the MCA loop.** In O&S, programs plan Sustainment Reviews (SRs) — multidisciplinary assessments of the deployed system's in-service health and its enabling elements (training, manuals, documentation) — covering operational-hazard risk, product-baseline integrity, and supply-chain status, per DoDI 5000.91.

## Mental Models
- **The pathway is a pre-set risk dial.** Rather than calibrating risk tolerance from scratch each time, treat the chosen pathway as a dial already turned to a position: UCA accepts more, moves fastest, and tolerates open deficiencies; MCA accepts least, moves slowest, and gates each phase with SETRs. Tailoring is fine adjustment within that pre-set position.
- **Spend risk where money is cheap.** In MCA, picture the cost curve rising sharply across the life cycle. The guide's logic is to attack the highest reducible technical, engineering, and integration risks during TMRR — the "period of lower financial commitment" — using prototyping, so that an EMD commitment is made against understood, manageable risk.
- **Go-fast means fail early on purpose.** For MTA, knowledge bought late is paid for in schedule. The model is to test the hard things first, accept the risk of early test failures, and use developmental testing to pull risk out before the operational demonstration — keeping that demonstration at least a year before MTA completion to preserve schedule margin.
- **Descope before you slip.** MTA frames the prototype as negotiable: be willing to de-prioritize traditional KPP requirements, set complex or high-risk objectives aside for follow-on or spin-off programs, and descope the fielded capability — as long as the residual capability still helps the warfighter.
- **Event-driven, not schedule-driven.** A recurring MCA discipline: proceed to the next phase only when the program has demonstrated it has mitigated the entry risks, building schedules from relevant historical data rather than an externally imposed date, and not letting schedule urgency override sound engineering and management judgment.
- **Validate what you assumed without noticing.** Framing assumptions are dangerous precisely because they are invisible. The model is to surface them deliberately, test their continued validity at the AoA and at each phase, and treat each as a risk with an impact if it proves wrong.

## Anti-patterns
The source explicitly names practices to avoid; these are stated as cautions, not invented.
- **Big-bang technology development in MTA.** Avoid structuring a Rapid Prototyping program as significant technology development followed by integration and test of multiple developmental components — that is the long-cycle pattern MTA exists to escape.
- **Over-constraining the design trade space.** In MSA, avoid pinning down the design too tightly; minimize the number of KPPs and KSAs (per the JCIDS Manual) so the trade space stays open. MTA similarly warns against complex requirements.
- **Letting schedule urgency outweigh engineering judgment.** In TMRR, the guide explicitly cautions against allowing schedule urgency to override good engineering and management.
- **Requirements creep through derived requirements.** When assessing the preliminary design and allocated baseline, ensure derived requirements do not become a vector for requirements creep.
- **Relying solely on an externally imposed timeline.** Develop a realistic, appropriately phased schedule grounded in historical analogues rather than accepting an imposed date as the plan.

## Key Takeaways
1. There is no single DoD risk process — the AAF pathway you select (UCA, MTA, MCA, or one of the other three) sets the baseline risk posture, and tailoring adjusts it. DoDD 5000.01 makes "actively manage risk" a tenet of the whole system.
2. UCA buys speed by accepting risk: deficiencies, including safety ones, can be carried past production/deployment by an explicit Decision Authority call, and the pathway ends in a formal disposition analysis choosing termination, contingency sustainment, or transition to a program of record.
3. MTA buys speed by failing early and descoping: test the hard things first, hold the operational demonstration a year before completion for margin, and trade off or defer complex requirements — while still managing SWAP-C, R&M, and software/supply-chain security on the Rapid Fielding side.
4. MCA front-loads the hard risk decisions: choose the entry milestone by where risk lives, burn down technology/engineering/integration risk in TMRR while money is cheap, and gate every phase with SETRs (ASR/SRR/SFR/PDR → CDR/TRR/SVR/FCA/PRR → OTRR/IOT&E/PCA) plus TRA, MRA, and SRA.
5. The Acquisition Strategy (DoDI 5000.85) and the SEP (DoDI 5000.88) are the two documents where MCA risk must be written down: top risks with mitigations in the Strategy, technical risks and mitigation execution in the SEP.
6. Contract type is a risk instrument — the program's risk profile dominates the cost-type vs. fixed-price choice, and risk is never fully transferred to industry because the Government still needs the delivered product.
7. Framing assumptions are a named risk category: surface them, validate them at the AoA and each phase, and treat an incorrect assumption as a program-level risk.
