# Chapter 7: Additional Methods — RMF, Cyber, Software/Agile, Digital Engineering, and ITRA

## Core Idea
The general RIO management approach is not the only risk method a DoD program runs. At specific points in the acquisition life cycle, programs layer in **specialized methods** that surface kinds of risk the general process does not naturally generate — cybersecurity authorization risk, mission-level cyber-attack effects, software/Agile delivery risk, modeling-induced risk in a digital ecosystem, and independent technical-risk findings. The unifying instruction of this material is that each specialized method produces its own risk and issue data, and the program must **map that data back into its enterprise RIO process** rather than letting it live in a parallel silo. Programs are also told to weigh the cost, schedule, and performance risk of *implementing* spectrum-supportability, electromagnetic-protection (EP), and cybersecurity requirements and their testing — i.e., the controls themselves carry program risk.

> **Scope caveat:** This is the DoD-side complement to the `nasa-risk` and `dau-se-guidebook` packs. Where NASA frames risk through RIDM/CRM and DAU frames the eight technical-management processes, this chapter covers the DoD-specific *specialized* methods and the directives (DoDI 8510.01, DoDI 5000.88, DoDI 5000.89, etc.) that mandate them. Use it alongside, not instead of, those packs.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **Risk Management Framework (RMF) for DoD Systems** — defined by *DoDI 8510.01*. Brings a risk-based approach to cybersecurity, drives cybersecurity into the life cycle early and throughout, maximizes reciprocity, and emphasizes continuous monitoring. It replaced the DoD Information Assurance Certification and Accreditation Process and swapped the term "information assurance" for "cybersecurity." Paired with *DoDI 8500.01, "Cybersecurity,"* which stands up the DoD program to protect and defend systems.
  - **When/how:** Applies to all DoD systems (and partnered systems that agreed to follow DoD standards). Programs start RMF activities at system inception — e.g., during capability identification or at a major modification. Systems need a valid authorization before operating; sub-system-level technologies that do not need an Authority to Operate still run the **"Assess Only"** procedures.
- **The seven RMF steps** — *Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor*. Run in parallel with the system life cycle, and integrate test and evaluation as a continuum.
- **NIST SP 800-39 three-level governance** — the DoD governance structure implements this tiered model: **Level 1 (Organization / OSD strategic)**, **Level 2 (Mission or Business Processes)**, **Level 3 (Systems)**. Each level names its own governance elements (e.g., DoD CISO, Risk Executive Function, RMF TAG/KS at Level 1; Component CIO/CISO at Level 2; Authorizing Officials and the System Cybersecurity Program at Level 3).
  - **When/how:** Use it to place a given cyber risk at the right decision tier rather than treating all cyber risk as program-level.
- **Mission-Based Cyber Risk Assessment (MBCRA)** — required of acquisition programs by *DoDI 5000.89, "Test and Evaluation."*
  - **When/how:** Run to identify and prioritize cyber risk in terms of mission effect, feeding T&E.
- **Cyber Table Top (CTT)** — a *type* of MBCRA, described in the *DoD Cyber Table Top Guide (2021)*. A wargame-style, analytically demanding exercise with two opposing teams (a mission-execution force vs. a cyber force trying to defeat it) that explores how cyber-offensive actions degrade a U.S. system's ability to complete its mission.
  - **When/how:** Use early and repeatedly to surface vulnerabilities, demonstrated exploitation paths, and resulting mission impacts *before* operational testing, so resources go where mission risk is highest.
- **DoD Enterprise DevSecOps Fundamentals (2021)** — the reference for DevSecOps concepts and normalized definitions, including the security-throughout pipeline that extends Agile to deployment.
- **Software Engineering for Continuous Delivery of Warfighting Capability (2023)** — named source whose metrics chapter (with case studies) underpins the software-metrics guidance below.
- **Digital Engineering (DE)** — defined per the *DoD Systems Engineering Guidebook (2022)* and the *DoD Digital Engineering Strategy (2018)*. In essence, an integrated, digital way of working that treats authoritative system data and models as one continuous thread running the length of the system's life.
- **FMECA (Failure Modes, Effects, and Criticality Analysis)** — named as a risk process; *DoDI 5000.88* requires it for all defense acquisition programs, based on the digital representation of the system and digital artifacts.
- **DE Body of Knowledge (DEBoK)** — shared knowledge base (de-bok.org) of authoritative sources and DE lessons learned.
- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA)** — congressionally required (since 2017) on Major Defense Acquisition Programs ahead of milestone and production decisions; governed by *DoDI 5000.88* and *DoD ITRA Execution Guidance (2020)*. Gives senior leaders an independent view of technical risk, including critical-technology and manufacturing-process maturity.
  - **When/how:** USD(R&E) conducts ITRAs on ACAT ID programs; Services/Agencies do ACAT IB/IC with USD(R&E)-determined approval authority.
- **Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM)** — the methodology behind ITRA, organized as **eight technical risk areas** assessed against **seven factors** (enumerated below).
- **S&T Protection Guide / Program Protection guidance** — named OUSD(R&E) documents for managing unwanted-technology-transfer risk under *DoDD 5000.01, DoDI 5000.02, and DoDI 5000.83*.

## Key Concepts
- **Specialized risk feeds enterprise risk.** Spectrum supportability, EP, and cybersecurity risks are generated by specialized methods (RMF, MBCRA) but must be mapped into the program's general risk process; the *implementation and testing* of those requirements is itself a cost/schedule/performance risk to evaluate.
- **Authorization as a gate.** No DoD system operates without a valid authorization; below the system level, the lighter **Assess Only** path still applies. Cyber risk therefore has a hard, life-cycle-anchored decision point.
- **Cyber risk is mission-framed.** The CTT/MBCRA value is translating a technical vulnerability into a *mission impact* a leader can act on, well before operational test exposes it.
- **Modern software changes the cadence of RIO.** Agile/DevSecOps (continuous integration, delivery, deployment; build-integrate-test as a continuum) lets programs reassess RIO far more frequently — retiring risks and finding new ones each sprint. The recurring challenge is wiring day-to-day Agile signals into the *enterprise* risk process.
- **Micro-risk vs. macro-risk aggregation.** Two integration directions are both recommended: roll **micro-risks** (day-to-day, low-fidelity high/moderate/low ratings) *up* into program risks, and break **macro-risks** (identified at program start) *down* into sprint-sized mitigation tasks. Micro-level RIO usually rides a different, lower-fidelity consequence scale, so aggregating it into the program register needs deliberate mapping to the program's tailored consequence definitions.
- **Technical debt is risk made visible.** The Agile vignette ties accumulated technical debt (a monolithic, tightly coupled inherited module; safety-partitioning shortcuts) to named program risks — safety-release approval, software maintainability, and performance — and tracks them on the product/sprint backlog.
- **Four core software output measures: Size, Time, Effort, Defects.**
  - *Size* — story points, user stories, features, epics, initiatives (or SLOC, function points, requirements). Consistency matters more than the unit; gaming the count introduces uncertainty about team performance.
  - *Time (duration)* — rarely an explicit metric, but invaluable as the denominator for velocity, burn-down, etc.; lead time from backlog to delivery predicts how fast a fix or new capability reaches the warfighter.
  - *Effort* — usually hours; an input to development and a major cost driver; metrics like hours-per-story-point flag budget and low-value-work risk.
  - *Defects* — logged by severity; metrics like defects-per-sprint or blocker counts gauge quality against customer expectation.
- **Three DE risk views.** When implementing DE / model-based acquisition: (1) risk *introduced* by modeling deficiencies, (2) risk *reduced* by better modeling, and (3) modeling used *to predict or assess* risk (uncertainty quantification).
- **DE risk-reduction considerations.** Programs weigh prior-stage DE investment, organizational DE lessons learned, remaining life-cycle activities, authoritative data management, secure/interoperable ecosystems, cybersecurity of data and hosting, model V&V/accreditation status for intended use, government insight at WBS level, IP/data rights, workforce training, and whether required models arrive as CDRL items for sustainment.
- **ITRA characterizes risk with the program's own criteria.** Each ITRA risk is described by consequence (to the program and interdependent programs if fully realized) and likelihood, using the *same* likelihood/consequence criteria established in the main guide; deviations must be noted with rationale. Consequence captures the greatest cost/schedule/performance impact *as if fully realized, before further mitigation*; fully burdened costs are used where possible; independent likelihood judgments weigh mitigation strength and the track record of similar programs at burning down risk.
- **DTRAM eight risk areas** (the technical domains it scores): Mission Capability; Technology; the area of System Development and Integration; MOSA (Modular Open Systems Approach); Software; Security/Cybersecurity; Manufacturing; and RAM-and-Sustainment (Reliability, Availability, Maintainability).
- **DTRAM seven factors** (the lenses applied to each area): a Scope/Requirements factor; an Architecture-and-Design factor; one for Decision-and-Control; then Schedule; the program's Resources; how it handles Evaluation; and finally Performance/Quality.
- **Unwanted technology transfer is an acute, distinct risk.** Most technologies are unclassified at inception, so adversaries use public funding/program information to find U.S. and allied entities (companies, DoD-funded universities) and transfer technology via many mechanisms. S&T protection is distinct from program protection: it guards Essential Technology Elements (ETEs) and enabling technologies, is not always required, and — when required — is built from an upfront risk review and a periodically refreshed S&T Protection Plan tailored to the pathway (grants, contracts, in-house).

## Mental Models
- **The funnel and the silo.** Each specialized method (RMF, MBCRA/CTT, Agile metrics, DE/FMECA, ITRA, S&T protection) is a *sensor* tuned to one risk class. Left alone, each is a silo with its own scale; the program's job is to **funnel** each sensor's output into one enterprise register with a common consequence language.
- **Same-risk, two clocks.** The vignette's comparison table is the model: a single safety/maintainability/performance risk can be run on the **traditional clock** (reviewed at a monthly board, long burn-down, artifacts finalized at the end, risk may become a late issue) or the **Agile clock** (reviewed each sprint and at daily standup, burn-down in a couple of sprints, safety treated as a continuously-monitored quality attribute, team-owned decisions). Choose the clock per risk and per program maturity.
- **Tier the cyber decision.** Map a cyber risk to NIST SP 800-39 Level 1/2/3 before reacting — strategic/enterprise, mission/business, or system — so it lands with the right authority (CISO/Risk Executive vs. Component CIO vs. Authorizing Officer).
- **Pivot the metric, not just the product.** Agile pivots apply to measurement too: if a metric isn't informing the decisions that mitigate risk or prevent issues, change the metric. A retrospective that shows stories-completed-vs-planned slipping is an early risk signal, not just a status line.
- **Three lenses on a model.** For any model in a DE ecosystem, ask in turn: could the *model itself* be wrong (introduced risk)? does it *retire* risk others can't see? and can it *quantify* the uncertainty in a decision (modeling for risk)? FMECA built on the digital system representation is the canonical example, letting risk flow design → manufacturing → product support.
- **Independence as a consequence-discipline.** ITRA's force is that an outside team rates likelihood/consequence on the program's own criteria but without the program's optimism — pricing risk "as if fully realized, before mitigation," with fully burdened costs.

## Anti-patterns
*(Named or directly implied by the source.)*
- **Reducing story points as the team matures, or inflating them when defects appear** — the guide calls this out as introducing risk by muddying a fundamental measure of team output.
- **Not measuring duration** — claiming "1,000 story points" with no time base is meaningless; failing to measure time is a stated risk to interpreting any flow metric.
- **Not measuring effort** — flagged as risking budget overrun or over-investing in low-value stories/features.
- **Deferring technical debt until fielding** — the traditional column treats maintainability as "possibly ignored until product fielded," leaving unknown post-fielding risk with no in-development measurement — the negative case the Agile approach is set against.
- **Keeping micro-level Agile RIO in a separate, low-fidelity scale and never aggregating it** — leaving day-to-day risk disconnected from the enterprise register is the integration failure the guide repeatedly warns against.
- **Letting specialized (spectrum/EP/cyber) risks bypass the management process** — the appendix's opening instruction is precisely to map them in.

## Key Takeaways
1. **RMF (DoDI 8510.01)** runs cybersecurity as risk across seven steps (Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor); systems need authorization to operate, with an **Assess Only** path below system level; governance is tiered per **NIST SP 800-39** Levels 1–3.
2. **MBCRA (DoDI 5000.89)**, with **Cyber Table Top** as a key type, translates cyber vulnerabilities into *mission* impact before operational test.
3. **Agile/DevSecOps** lets RIO be reassessed every sprint; the hard part is aggregating **micro-risks** up and decomposing **macro-risks** down into the enterprise process with a consistent consequence scale.
4. Track software with the four core measures — **Size, Time, Effort, Defects** — and pivot the metric when it stops informing risk decisions.
5. **Digital Engineering** carries three risk views (introduced / reduced / predictive); **FMECA on the digital model is required by DoDI 5000.88** and lets risk flow from design to sustainment.
6. **ITRA (DoDI 5000.88 / DTRAM)** gives leaders an independent technical-risk view across **eight risk areas** and **seven factors**, scored on the program's own likelihood/consequence criteria, priced as if fully realized before mitigation.
7. **S&T / technology-transfer protection** is a distinct, acute risk class guarding Essential Technology Elements via a tailored, periodically refreshed S&T Protection Plan.
