# Chapter 2: Prohibited AI Practices (Article 5)

## Core Idea
Chapter II contains a hard ban on eight categories of AI practices deemed to cause unacceptable harm to fundamental rights, autonomy, and human dignity. These prohibitions apply from 2 February 2025 — the earliest application date in the Act. There is no conformity-assessment route around them; they are absolute.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Article 5 eight-category prohibition list**: An exhaustive enumeration of AI practices that are unconditionally or conditionally banned. Unlike high-risk requirements (which can be met), prohibited practices cannot be made compliant — only avoided.
  - When to use: As a first filter before any other analysis. If an AI system falls here, development, placement, and use must stop.
- **Real-time RBIS conditional prohibition** (Art 5(1)(h) + Art 5(2-7)): Use of real-time remote biometric identification systems (RBIS) in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement is banned except for three strictly limited objectives, requires prior judicial/administrative authorisation, and is subject to annual reporting.
  - When to use: When law enforcement agencies or their suppliers are considering deploying biometric identification in public spaces.

## Key Concepts
- **Subliminal manipulation** (Art 5(1)(a)) — AI systems deploying techniques beyond a person's consciousness or purposefully manipulative/deceptive techniques that materially distort behaviour, impairing informed decision-making, causing significant harm.
- **Vulnerability exploitation** (Art 5(1)(b)) — AI that targets persons due to age, disability, or social/economic situation to distort their behaviour causing significant harm.
- **Social scoring** (Art 5(1)(c)) — evaluation or classification of natural persons over time based on social behaviour or personality characteristics where the score leads to detrimental treatment in unrelated social contexts or disproportionate treatment.
- **Criminal risk prediction by profiling** (Art 5(1)(d)) — AI that assesses or predicts a person's risk of committing a criminal offence based solely on profiling or personality/character traits (distinguished from AI supporting human assessment of involvement based on objective facts).
- **Facial recognition database scraping** (Art 5(1)(e)) — AI systems creating or expanding facial recognition databases through untargeted scraping of facial images from internet or CCTV.
- **Emotion inference at work/education** (Art 5(1)(f)) — AI used to infer natural persons' emotions in workplace or educational settings, except for medical or safety reasons.
- **Biometric categorisation by sensitive attributes** (Art 5(1)(g)) — AI that categorises individuals based on biometric data to deduce race, political opinion, trade union membership, religious/philosophical beliefs, or sexual orientation. (Does not cover labelling of lawfully acquired biometric datasets or law enforcement.)
- **Real-time remote biometric identification (RBIS) in public spaces** (Art 5(1)(h)) — the use of real-time RBIS in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement, except for the three enumerated objectives.

## Mental Models
- **"Harm threshold pair"**: Prohibitions in (a) and (b) require both the technique AND significant harm (or reasonable likelihood of significant harm). The technique alone without the harm element is insufficient for the ban to apply.
- **"Three-gate test for real-time RBIS"**: Allowed only if (1) the objective is one of the three listed in Art 5(1)(h), (2) prior judicial/administrative authorisation is obtained (or sought within 24 hours in genuine urgency), and (3) use is strictly necessary and proportionate (temporal, geographic, personal scope).

## Anti-patterns
- Misreading (d) as prohibiting all recidivism-risk tools: the ban covers AI that assesses risk based solely on profiling or personality traits; AI that supports human assessment of involvement based on objective verifiable facts linked to criminal activity is excluded.
- Conflating real-time and post-remote RBIS: Art 5 bans real-time RBIS in public spaces (with narrow exceptions); post-remote RBIS for law enforcement is regulated (Art 26(10)) but not banned.
- Assuming emotion recognition is entirely banned: the ban is limited to workplace and educational institution contexts; medical and safety uses are explicitly excluded.

## Key Takeaways
1. Eight practices are prohibited, applying from 2 February 2025 — no compliance pathway exists.
2. Social scoring prohibition is broad: it covers AI-driven scoring regardless of the deploying entity, not just government use.
3. The real-time RBIS prohibition is the most procedurally complex: it has limited permitted objectives, mandatory prior authorisation (urgency exception: apply within 24 hours), and annual Commission reporting requirements.
4. Biometric categorisation for sensitive attributes is prohibited even when using lawfully acquired data, unless it is purely labelling/filtering of existing datasets or law enforcement use.
5. Subliminal and manipulation bans require proven or reasonably likely significant harm — the technique alone is not enough.
6. Art 5(8) preserves other Union-law prohibitions: the list is a floor, not a ceiling.

## Connects To
- **Chapter I (Art 3)**: Definitions of RBIS, real-time RBIS, biometric categorisation, and emotion recognition systems underpin the prohibitions.
- **Chapter VII (Art 66)**: The AI Board may recommend revision of Art 5 under its annual review function.
- **Annex II**: Lists criminal offences relevant to the real-time RBIS law-enforcement exception in Art 5(1)(h)(iii).
- **Chapter XII (Art 99)**: Violations of Art 5 carry the highest tier of fines: up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.
