# Chapter 4 — Integrated Technical Management, Interface Management, and Risk Management

> Civil-agency SE process companion to NASA/DAU. The FAA SEM names seven technical management disciplines and threads them through the Acquisition Management System (AMS) life cycle. This chapter covers the first three; it parallels the NASA and DAU technical-management treatments but is tailored to FAA documents (SEMP, ISPD), reviews, and the National Airspace System (NAS).

## Core Idea
The FAA groups its continuous, cross-cutting governance work into **seven technical management disciplines** — Integrated Technical Management, Interface Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, SE Information Management, Decision Analysis, and Verification and Validation. They are applied throughout solution development with as much rigour and formality as the project warrants, and they sit on top of a **Quality Management System (QMS)** that must be present for the whole life cycle. This chapter treats the first three: **Integrated Technical Management** (planning the technical effort and then monitoring and controlling it), **Interface Management** (defining and controlling the boundaries where system elements meet), and **Risk Management** (the FAA's unified treatment of Risks, Issues, and Opportunities). Together they form the "control loop" that keeps an FAA program delivering products and services that satisfy stakeholder needs across the AMS phases.

## Frameworks Introduced (source names)
- **The seven Technical Management Disciplines** — the SEM's named set: Integrated Technical Management, Interface Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, SE Information Management, Decision Analysis, Verification and Validation. Applied continuously and consistently throughout solution development; this chapter covers items 1–3.
- **Quality Management System (QMS)** — a system that steers, gauges, governs, and continually betters the products and services through defined policies, processes, and procedures. Required across the life cycle. The government may require that a contractor have a QMS but generally cannot dictate which one. Process-improvement standards the FAA draws on include CMMI, iCMM, ITIL, ITIM, DO-178/DO-278, the ISO/IEC 15504 and ISO/IEC 33001-99 process-assessment series, ISO 9001/14001/20000, and Enterprise SPICE.
- **Integrated Multidisciplinary Team (IMT)** — the larger team of stakeholders, decision makers, SMEs, and domain engineers within which the systems engineer acts as the *catalyst*, concurrently balancing product/process development, budget, specialty inputs, and stakeholder preferences. May be formal (e.g., a Capture Team) or informal.
- **Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP)** — the master document that integrates all SE activities and ties them to cost, performance, and schedule objectives. Two variants exist: the **Program SEMP** (program-management view, initiated during Research for Service Analysis) and the **Contractor's SEMP / CSEMP** (delivered early in Solution Implementation, after contract-award kickoff).
- **Implementation Strategy and Planning Document (ISPD)** — the primary AMS planning document for executing within cost, schedule, benefit, and performance baselines; carries the **Integrated Master Schedule (IMS)** and is directly traceable to the WBS in the SEMP. The Program SEMP is summarized in the ISPD; the ISPD is a program-management responsibility.
- **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** — defines the total project scope; the SEM's rule of thumb is a depth of three or four tiers, each tier holding roughly five to nine elements.
- **Technical Performance Measurement (TPM)** and **Critical Performance Requirements (CPR)** — TPM is the quantitative technique for tracking design adequacy against requirements over time, producing periodic trend and variance reports; CPRs are the critical parameters whose maturation toward a target value is projected to completion.
- **Program Performance Measurement (PPM)** and **Earned Value Management (EVM)** — the program-level analog to TPM; EVM integrates cost, schedule, technical performance, and risk by comparing earned value against the planned value defined in the WBS.
- **Control gates, technical reviews, and audits** — the "mechanisms" used to control progress, with predefined entry and exit criteria. The FAA's named milestone set: **TRA, SRR, SDR, SSR, PDR, CDR, TRR, ISR, FCA, PCA, PRR**, plus the nine-level **Technology Readiness / Levels of Maturity** scale used in the TRA.
- **Interface Requirements Document (IRD)** and **Interface Control Document (ICD)** — the two primary outputs of Interface Management; the IRD controls interface *requirements*, the ICD controls interface *design* ("as-built"). Governed by **FAA-STD-025f**. For service-oriented work, the **Web Service Requirements Document (WSRD)** and **Web Service Description Document (WSDD)** augment/replace ICDs, governed by **FAA-STD-070** and **FAA-STD-065**.
- **Interface Working Group (IWG)** and **Interface Change Request (ICR)** — the body and the change mechanism that keep interfaces controlled as the design evolves.
- **N2 diagram** — a bidirectional matrix that places functional or physical entities on the diagonal and uses the off-diagonal cells to capture interface inputs and outputs; feeds the Functional and Physical Interface Lists.
- **Risk, Issue, and Opportunity (RIO) management** — the FAA's unified process treating three distinct objects: **Risk** (future, negative, probabilistic), **Issue** (already occurred or certain, negative), **Opportunity** (future, positive, probabilistic). Five process steps: **Identify → Analyze & Assess → Develop Plan → Execute Plan → Track & Monitor**.
- **Risk Management Board (RMB)** and the **status lifecycle** — the governance body plus the defined status set (Watch Item, Draft, Proposed, Pending, Approved/Not Approved, Disposition Ready, Closed, Retired, Realized) whose order should not change.

## Key Concepts

### Integrated Technical Management (ITM)
- **Two halves.** ITM is "develop the technical plans" plus "technically monitor and control." Planning answers *what tasks, what schedule, what resources, who is responsible*; monitoring/control measures progress against the plan, finds variances, and feeds corrective-action decisions.
- **Strategy first.** Each project has a tailored ITM strategy that identifies stakeholders, gathers inputs, assigns roles, fixes plan formats and schedules, sets re-planning criteria, and establishes a stakeholder communication plan.
- **The plan family.** ITM produces the SEMP (and its subordinate plans), the ISPD, and a **Life Cycle Plan (LCP)**. The LCP spans five planning sections: Integrated Logistics Support; Real Property Management; Deployment and Transition; Disposal; and Sustainment and Technology Evolution.
- **Technical plans table.** The SEM enumerates per-process plans: Requirements Management, Decision Analysis, Interface Management, SE Information Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, Verification, and Validation plans — each defining inputs, activities, outputs, responsibilities, deliverables, and schedule.
- **Monitoring techniques vs. control mechanisms.** *Techniques* measure (TPM, CPR tracking, EVM); *mechanisms* are the control gates — technical reviews and audits with entry/exit criteria — that decide whether the project advances a phase.
- **Reviews and audits.** Reviews assess maturity (SRR establishes the allocated baseline; PDR is often the last viable point for technology insertion before detailed design; CDR judges readiness to begin manufacturing). Audits verify against baseline: the **FCA** confirms functions/performance by testing at the configuration-item level; the **PCA** confirms corrective actions and that documentation matches the as-built system.
- **Process improvement.** ITM closes the loop through tailored standard processes and a mechanism to capture and report issues/improvements to process owners — even when the reporting project itself cannot benefit.

### Interface Management
- **What an interface is.** The set of performance, functional, and physical attributes that has to hold at a shared boundary; the SEM types it as external, internal, functional, or physical. FAA systems interoperate with many other systems, platforms, humans, and elements, so interfaces must be identified and controlled as early as possible.
- **Process steps.** First bound the system and name its interfacing systems, then identify interfaces, develop the IRD, create the ICD, and keep the documentation updated. Internal interfaces lie within the boundary; external interfaces lie outside it.
- **Functional vs. physical.** Functional interfaces clarify responsibilities — every interface ties to a minimum of two functions, and since performance requirements trace to functions, it yields at least two verifiable interface requirements. Physical interfaces govern features, dimensions, tolerances, and material properties of mating elements.
- **IRD → ICD chain of compliance.** The IRD specifies requirements in verifiable terms, has to stay aligned with the final Program Requirements Document (fPRD), and is placed under Configuration Management. The ICD (usually vendor-produced) gives the as-built design solution and must comply with the IRD. Compliance flows: ICD complies with IRD, IRD complies with fPRD.
- **Change control.** Interface changes run through the IWG via an ICR that describes the problem, the proposed fix, impact analyses (performance, life-cycle cost, no new problems introduced), resources/cost, and system impact. In-scope ICRs return to the originator and document custodian; out-of-scope ones escalate to the program manager; approved changes flow back into Configuration Management.
- **SOA/NextGen tailoring.** The transition toward NextGen and a net-centric, Service-Oriented Architecture environment shifts interfaces from point-to-point toward services, introducing the WSRD and WSDD as service-tailored documents that can augment or replace ICDs.

### Risk Management (RIO)
- **Three objects, one rating concept.** Every RIO item carries a rating that combines likelihood and consequence. The triage question is temporal and directional: a future negative is a **Risk**, a realized/certain negative is an **Issue**, a future positive is an **Opportunity**.
- **Four lower-level objectives.** Timely identification, consistent assessment of the rating, communication of actions across the organization, and review of action performance.
- **Identify.** Identification runs at every stage and whenever plans or status change materially. The three guiding questions: *What can go wrong? (risk)*, *What has gone wrong? (issue)*, *Where can something be improved? (opportunity)*. Each risk gets a **risk realization date** so only active risks consume attention. Program risk originates in three areas — **technical, schedule, cost** — classified by root cause, not symptom. Safety and security add gates: safety needs an identified hazard before a risk exists; security needs a viable threat coupled with an exploitable vulnerability.
- **Statement constructs.** Risk: *if [cause], then [impact]*. Issue: *due to [cause], [impact] was experienced*. Opportunity: *[impact] may be achieved if [cause] can be accomplished*. A precise statement improves analysis, assessment, and communication.
- **Analyze & Assess.** Likelihood and impact are scored separately on scorecards, then combined into a single rating expressed as a Level (High/Medium/Low) plus an Alpha (likelihood A–E) and numeric (impact 1–5). Likelihood bands run from "Not Likely" (0–10%) to "Near Certainty" (90–100%); impact is rated against the affected baseline across technical, schedule, and cost columns. Issues skip likelihood (they have occurred), so only impact is scored. Items are continuously re-analyzed; programs may augment the FAA scorecards but must document any augmentation in the RMP.
- **Develop Plan.** Plans require a **Plan Strategy** — Avoid, Transfer, Control, Accept, or Research and Knowledge (Control is the most common; Research and Knowledge reduces uncertainty by enlarging the knowledge pool) — plus a description and discrete steps. Medium/high risks typically require mitigation plans; low risks may be monitored. Plans should not be stated in terms of their own mitigation, contingency plans back up the first-choice plan, and a **burn-down ("waterfall") chart** projects how each mitigation step lowers the rating.
- **Execute and Track.** Mitigation steps are folded into the program's planning, scheduling, budgeting, and cost-accounting systems and inserted into the IMS; lowest-level tasks are flagged with their risk level and higher WBS levels inherit the maximum present below, enabling roll-up visibility and drill-down. Tracking is periodic, event-driven, or continuous.
- **Escalation, transfer, disposition.** Items unmanageable at one board escalate to a higher one (both organizations must agree), or transfer across Lines of Business. The RMB dispositions items per the status definitions; disposition follows successful plan completion, a passed critical milestone, loss of relevance, or unsuccessful management.
- **Reporting and SoS.** Aggregate status is shown on a **Probability Impact Diagram (PID)**. Major FAA programs feed risk data into the OMB **Exhibit 300** per Circular A-11. In the FAA's System-of-Systems reality, risks link in parent-child relationships across program, portfolio, and enterprise levels; a common tool (e.g., Active Risk Manager) is preferred so interfacing organizations can interchange data.

## Mental Models
- **Plan, then govern.** Integrated Technical Management is two motions: *develop the plans* (SEMP → ISPD → LCP) and then *monitor and control* against them. Techniques measure; mechanisms (gates) decide.
- **The systems engineer is the catalyst of the IMT.** The role is not to own every decision but to keep the multidisciplinary team concurrently balancing product, process, budget, specialty inputs, and stakeholder preferences across the life cycle.
- **TPM is a trend, not a snapshot.** Tracking a critical parameter's *maturation toward its target* is what turns a measurement into early warning; a value with no projected profile is a lagging gauge.
- **Compliance flows downhill, change flows uphill.** ICD complies with IRD complies with fPRD; meanwhile a change starts as an ICR and is filtered by the IWG before it can alter any of them.
- **RIO triage by one temporal question.** *Has it happened yet?* Not yet and bad → risk; already (or certainly) → issue; not yet and good → opportunity. Each rides its own scorecard with its own playbook, and an issue has no likelihood left to assess.
- **Roll-up the maximum.** A WBS summary task is only as healthy as its worst child — risk level inherits upward, so the schedule itself becomes a risk dashboard.

## Anti-patterns (named by the source)
- **Assuming a waterfall WBS.** The SEM states the WBS rests on a "waterfall" development assumption that is almost never true, which is precisely why it becomes the chief *evolving* component of the SEMP rather than a fixed artifact.
- **Stating a risk in terms of its mitigation plan.** The source explicitly warns against this; a risk should be captured by a good root-cause summary statement, with mitigation kept separate.
- **Tailoring by addition.** For the ISPD, tailoring is done only by *deleting* planning requirements (each deletion needs a documented rationale); the sole additions permitted are ones that are unique to the program.
- **Targeting symptoms instead of root causes.** Risk identification must consider sources or root causes, not symptoms, and classify a risk by its root cause.
- **Treating funding loss as a program risk.** The SEM notes that a possible cut in funding is usually *not* a program risk, since that call is made at the Agency level; it distinguishes cost, budget, and funding accordingly.

## Key Takeaways
1. The FAA names **seven technical management disciplines** over a mandatory **QMS**; this chapter covers Integrated Technical Management, Interface Management, and Risk Management.
2. **ITM = plan + control**: develop the SEMP (Program and Contractor variants), summarize it in the ISPD, span the life cycle with the LCP, then monitor with TPM/CPR/EVM and control through reviews and audits with entry/exit criteria.
3. The **WBS** anchors scope (3–4 levels deep, 5–9 wide) but is treated as the evolving core of the SEMP, not a frozen waterfall.
4. **Interface Management** produces the **IRD** (requirements) and **ICD** (design) under a strict compliance chain (ICD→IRD→fPRD), governed by FAA-STD-025f and, for services, the WSRD/WSDD under FAA-STD-070/065, with the **IWG** controlling change via the **ICR**.
5. **RIO** treats risk, issue, and opportunity as three objects with shared likelihood×impact rating logic and a five-step process (Identify → Analyze & Assess → Develop Plan → Execute → Track & Monitor); issues skip likelihood.
6. Plan strategies are **Avoid, Transfer, Control, Accept, Research and Knowledge** (Control is most common); medium/high risks require mitigation plans, tracked via burn-down charts and rolled up through the IMS/WBS.
7. RIO governance runs through the **RMB**, a defined status lifecycle, escalation/transfer across program-portfolio-enterprise levels, a **PID** for aggregate reporting, and OMB **Exhibit 300** / Circular A-11 obligations.
