# Chapter — Requirements Analysis, Architectural Design Synthesis, and Cross-cutting Technical Methods

> Civil-agency SE process companion to NASA/DAU. This chapter renders the FAA Systems Engineering Manual (SEM) Sections 3.3–3.5 as the FAA practices them inside the Acquisition Management System (AMS) and the National Airspace System (NAS). The underlying logic — requirements decomposed and traced, architecture allocated and evaluated, models used to buy down risk — is the same canon NASA and DAU teach; what is FAA-specific is the *document machinery* (pPRD/iPRD/fPRD, Type A/B/C specs, ISEF views, DOORS, the 80%-stable gate). Treat the FAA names as a localization of the shared SE vocabulary, not a different discipline.

## Core Idea
Three tightly coupled processes carry a need from words to a buildable, verifiable design. **Requirements Analysis** (3.3) elicits and disciplines requirements — developing them and then managing them under change. **Architectural Design Synthesis** (3.4) turns those requirements, framed by the functional architecture, into a physical architecture, then selects the most balanced alternative on cost, schedule, performance, and risk. **Cross-cutting Technical Methods** (3.5) — modeling, simulation, and prototyping — supply evidence to feed both, finding and fixing problems before anything is fielded. The three iterate in a loop with Functional Analysis: a "design loop" back to functions and a "requirements verification loop" back to requirements, so that requirements, functional architecture, and physical architecture stay mutually consistent.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Requirements Analysis = Requirements Development + Requirements Management** (SEM 3.3). Development creates functional and performance requirements from the functional architecture and stakeholder analysis; Management controls them and their documentation across the lifecycle. *When/how:* used at both NAS and system level; a program should not commit to full investment while its requirements set is still in flux.
- **The four Requirements Development sub-processes** (SEM 3.3.1.2): (1) identify and capture, (2) analyze and decompose, (3) derive, (4) establish verification methods. *When/how:* iterative and often parallel, driven by the criteria in the Requirements Management Plan.
- **PRS → MRS pipeline** (SEM 3.3.1.2). A **Primitive Requirements Statement** is captured in a punctuation-free `Name + Relation + Value` form; it is synthesized into a **Mature Requirements Statement**, a complete specification sentence. *When/how:* PRS are first assigned to a function in the functional architecture, then matured by adding paragraph number/title, subject, directive verb, sentence ending, and any clarifying note.
- **Standard Requirements Constructs / directive verbs** (SEM 3.3.1.2): *shall* (mandatory), *will* (a government declaration of purpose), *may* (option). *Should* is barred from requirements documents; *and/or* is barred; vague "minimum/maximum" is replaced by explicit relational operators. *When/how:* applied when writing every MRS.
- **Characteristics of Individual and Aggregate Requirements** (SEM 3.3.1.2): the per-requirement quality set — necessary, concise, implementation-free, attainable, complete, consistent, traceable, unambiguous, verifiable, allocatable — plus an aggregate-set test for completeness and consistency across the whole set. *When/how:* used both to write requirements and to score them in reviews and audits.
- **Verification methods + VRTM** (SEM 3.3.1.2 / cross-ref 4.7): the four methods — inspection, analysis, demonstration, test — rolled into a **Verification Requirements Traceability Matrix** that is part of every FAA requirements and specification document. *When/how:* each requirement gets a verification method; an unverifiable requirement is not a valid requirement.
- **Requirements Management Plan** (SEM 3.3.2.1): the basis for disciplined requirements practice, usually carried inside the SEMP but sometimes standalone. *When/how:* defined in Technical Management (Section 4).
- **Horizontal and Vertical Integration** (SEM 3.3.2.2): horizontal = consistency among SE products at the same level (functional ↔ requirements ↔ physical architecture); vertical = traceability from program-level up to enterprise-level requirements. *When/how:* governed by the **Integrated Systems Engineering Framework (ISEF)**.
- **The PRD evolution: pPRD → iPRD → fPRD** (SEM 3.3.3): preliminary, initial, and final Program Requirements Documents that mature across CRD, Investment Analysis, and toward the Final Investment Decision; the enterprise level sits in the **NAS-RD**. *When/how:* an approved fPRD is required to obtain a full investment decision.
- **Specification types A / B / C** (SEM 3.3.3.3): **Type A** System Specification (the FAA-E spec per FAA-STD-067, baselined at SRR), **Type B** Development Specification (baselined at PDR), **Type C** Product Specification (baselined at CDR). *When/how:* used in Solution Implementation to govern what a prime contractor must provide.
- **Critical Performance Requirements (CPR)** (SEM 3.3.4.1): the minimum set of make-or-break performance attributes, written as MRSs, summarized in a CPR table, and copied verbatim into the Acquisition Program Baseline. *When/how:* identified in the iPRD, finalized in the fPRD; their V&V status gates milestone approvals.
- **Architectural Design Synthesis = Develop alternatives + Allocate requirements + Evaluate alternatives** (SEM 3.4.2). *When/how:* three iterative, often-parallel activities that produce solution architectures, requirements allocation, and evaluated alternatives.
- **Requirements Allocation Matrix (RAM)** (SEM 3.4.2.2): two-way traceability from physical architecture ↔ requirements ↔ functional architecture, minimally carrying Function ID, Function Name, the derived requirement, and the implementing physical component. *When/how:* expanded later in V&V to add verification methodology.
- **ISEF / FEAF / DoDAF architecture views** (SEM 3.4.2.1): NAS and NextGen architectures are documented in **ISEF** views (which are based on **DoDAF**); IT investments use **FEAF**. Common products include AV-1, AV-2, OV-1, OV-2, OV-3, OV-5, OV-6c, OV-7, SV-1, SV-2, SV-4, SV-5, TV-1/2. *When/how:* the framework dictates which products are required at each point in the acquisition lifecycle; views may be tailored.
- **Architecture description tools** (SEM 3.4.5): Concept Description Sheets, Architecture Block Diagrams (internal + external), Interface Drawings, and Schematic Block Diagrams (with N² for interfaces). *When/how:* used to describe and compare alternatives at each design level.
- **Modeling vs. Simulation taxonomy** (SEM 3.5.1): models split into **representations** (input→output mapping, no structural mimicry) and **simulation models** (mimic structure); model *types* are physical, graphical, mathematical/deterministic, statistical. *When/how:* selected by which system characteristics are of interest. The chapter explicitly cites the **INCOSE SE Handbook** definition of a model.
- **Simulation types in order of fidelity** (SEM 3.5.1.2): discrete-event, continuous (e.g., Monte Carlo), agent-based, artificial-intelligence (Bayesian networks, fuzzy logic), game/virtual-world, and optimizing simulations. *When/how:* chosen by the behavior to be predicted and the fidelity required.
- **Rapid vs. Traditional prototyping** (SEM 3.5.2): rapid prototypes are quickly assembled from a tool's menu of existing elements (laser sintering, 3D printing, CNC, simulation environments) and usually are *not* prototypes of the planned system; traditional prototypes faithfully represent the planned system, partially or fully. *When/how:* used to surface user-need understanding, evaluate concepts, and reduce risk; intended to be set aside once their learning purpose is met.

## Key Concepts
- **Inputs that start Requirements Development**: stakeholder needs, the **ConOps**, the **functional architecture**, and **policy/standards** (FAA policy, ICAO SARPs, statutes, Executive Orders). The ConOps functions are the first input to the "capture requirements" step.
- **Functional Architecture → Requirements traceability ladder** (SEM Table 9): each level of functional analysis yields a derived product — ConOps → preliminary architecture, FA-1 → preliminary program requirements, FA-2 → initial, FA-3 → final, FA-4 → system-level specification, and on to the Nth level. Requirements are developed at *every* level of the functional architecture.
- **The capture sub-process is broad**: it defines stakeholder needs, constraints, operational scenarios, **MOEs/MOPs**, interfaces (functional from Functional Analysis; physical from SV-1 and IRDs), the utilization environment, life-cycle process concepts, modes of operation, and **TPMs**. MOEs measure *outcomes*; MOPs measure *outputs*; TPMs derive from MOPs and are kept few — limited to measures that put cost/schedule/performance at risk — and are tracked against a planned value profile in the SE Master Schedule.
- **Deriving requirements** pushes statements to the lowest practical level before allocation to the physical architecture or WBS, propagating impact analysis in all directions (parent, child, peer) until no further impact spreads; safety requirements are traced into the hardware/software baseline.
- **Requirements stability gate**: a set is "stable" when at least **80%** of requirements are unchanged; the recommendation is no change for the last quarter before requesting FID. Stability is measured against TBD counts and change-prone requirements.
- **Vertical traceability example** (SEM Table 12): an enterprise "the NAS shall determine demand" decomposes downward to a component-level spec that quantifies the prediction (per resource, user-selectable interval, hours into the future).
- **SoS requirements distribution** (SEM 3.3.4.2 / 3.4.6): a program's success can depend on functionality provided by *other* programs; enterprise requirements flow down and are distributed across investments, so the same SoS requirement must appear — with its source/allocation — in the documentation of every involved program. Difficulty comes from programs sitting at different AMS maturity levels; an automated tool helps.
- **Requirements Management tooling**: the FAA standard is **DOORS** (IBM Rational). Any alternative tool should provide documentation, traceability, allocation, verification, traceability-impact assessment, and compatibility with other tools.
- **Architecture inputs**: requirements (varying by lifecycle phase) and architectures — the functional architecture plus the approved enterprise architecture (trace vertically to enterprise OV/SV; TV provides standards/constraints; AV-2 seeds reusable entities). EA products come from the NAS EA Portal or the Chief Enterprise Architect.
- **Develop-alternatives drivers**: technology requirements (benefit must outweigh cost/schedule/performance risk), specialty-engineering attributes, reuse of existing EA components, **COTS** opportunities, and **make-or-buy** analyses.
- **Evaluate-alternatives logic** (SEM 3.4.2.3): assess constraint compliance and any *adverse emergent properties* from element interaction; run effectiveness, trade-off, and risk analyses. The branch is explicit — if **all** alternatives fail the **same** requirements, run the **design loop** (revisit the functional architecture); if compliance **varies** across alternatives, run the **requirements feedback loop** per design. The preferred (often "best value") architecture is then baselined under formal CM and recommended to the Joint Resource Council.
- **Hardware/software partitioning** (SEM 3.4.5): a function's assignment to hardware vs. software is decided here; define the top-level HWCI/CSCI before fixing which Computer Software Elements perform the function, and track each function to its hardware/software allocation.
- **Industrial Control System constraint** (SEM 3.4.6): the NAS/NextGen is a designated US Critical Infrastructure and an Industrial Control System, so cybersecurity requirements (via the AIS-300 security authorization process) must be considered no later than alternatives analysis at IID — and the architect must anticipate the security posture likely in force at deployment given long development cycles.
- **Models across the lifecycle** (SEM 3.5.3): the same modeling/simulation/prototyping methods are reused from Service Analysis through Solution Implementation, but yield richer analysis as the program matures — from shortfall identification, to alternative trade-offs and business-case/cost estimates, to refining the design and verifying requirements against simulated environments.
- **FAA M&S tools** (SEM 3.5.4): **System Architect (SA)** for architecture products (UML-based, links to the WITNESS simulation product, paired with "Screen Architect" for interface prototypes) and **CORE** for connected system models with an integrated simulation engine spanning requirements through V&V.

## Mental Models
- **Three products, one consistency triangle.** Requirements are *derived from* the functional architecture and *allocated to* the physical architecture; the RAM keeps all three two-way traceable. Break any edge of the triangle and the design is no longer coherent.
- **PRS is the skeleton, MRS is the sentence.** Strip a requirement to `Name + Relation + Value` to make it unambiguous and machine-checkable; clothe it in a `shall` sentence with a paragraph number and verification reference to make it a contract. The discipline lives in the skeleton; the legibility lives in the sentence.
- **Two loops, two diagnoses.** When alternatives fail, the *pattern* of failure tells you which loop to run: a *common* failure across all alternatives indicts the functional architecture (design loop); a *divergent* failure pattern indicts the requirements (requirements feedback loop). The cause is read from the shape of the non-compliance.
- **Verb choice is a control surface.** *Shall* binds the builder; *will* states government intent; *may* grants an option; *should* is too soft to be a requirement and is banned. The mood of the verb is the obligation.
- **CPRs are the program's load-bearing requirements.** Keep them to the minimum that characterizes the mission need, copy them verbatim into the baseline, give them precedence in early test, and read their V&V status as the program's pass/fail at each milestone.
- **Fidelity is a cost dial, not a virtue.** Resources spent building and validating a model should match the value of the information it yields; rapid prototypes trade fidelity for speed and are deliberately *not* the planned system, while traditional prototypes pay for faithfulness when risk demands it.
- **A model is only good relative to a purpose** (cited from Lankhorst 2009): assess a model against the purpose it was built for and its audience — there is no purpose-free notion of a "good" model.

## Anti-patterns
- **Advancing on an unstable requirements set** — committing to full investment (or pushing a set through the AMS) while requirements are still in flux; the SEM's explicit guard is the 80%-stable threshold and the no-change-for-a-quarter recommendation before FID.
- **Solution-specific early requirements** — writing implementation-bound requirements in the pPRD that restrict the search for solutions; preliminary requirements must stay implementation-agnostic.
- **Letting constraints "manage design by default"** — failing to identify, document, and manage design constraints, so they silently shape the solution instead of being engineered against (SEM 3.4.1).
- **Banned constructs** — using *should* in a requirement, using *and/or*, or hiding behind vague "minimum/maximum" instead of explicit relational operators.
- **Invalid (unverifiable) requirements** — a requirement with no associated verification method is, by the SEM's own rule, not a valid requirement.
- **Mistaking a rapid prototype for the product** — treating a menu-assembled rapid prototype as the planned system, when it only approximates system elements; and treating any prototype as a first production draft rather than a learning artifact to be set aside.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Requirements Analysis = Development + Management**: develop functional/performance requirements from the functional architecture and stakeholder analysis, then control them under CM across the lifecycle.
2. **PRS → MRS** is the FAA's authoring pipeline; `Name + Relation + Value` first, then a `shall`-form specification sentence, every one of which carries a verification method and lands in the **VRTM**.
3. **Requirements quality is a checklist** — necessary, concise, implementation-free, attainable, complete, consistent, traceable, unambiguous, verifiable, allocatable — used to both write and audit.
4. **The PRD matures pPRD → iPRD → fPRD**; an approved fPRD with an 80%-stable set is the gate to a full investment decision, after which Type A/B/C specifications govern the contract.
5. **Architectural Design Synthesis** develops alternatives, allocates requirements (via the **RAM**), and evaluates alternatives on cost/schedule/performance/risk — branching into a **design loop** or **requirements feedback loop** depending on how alternatives fail.
6. **Architecture is documented in ISEF/FEAF/DoDAF views** (AV/OV/SV/TV products), traced vertically to the enterprise architecture and horizontally to linked architectures.
7. **CPRs** are the minimal, make-or-break performance requirements — written as MRSs, copied verbatim into the APB, and gating milestone decisions.
8. **Modeling, simulation, and prototyping** buy down risk before fielding; choose model type and simulation fidelity by purpose and value-of-information, and reuse the methods across the lifecycle with growing payoff.
