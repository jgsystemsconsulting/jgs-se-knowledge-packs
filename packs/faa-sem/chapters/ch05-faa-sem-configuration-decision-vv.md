# Chapter — Configuration Management, Information Management, Decision Analysis, and Verification and Validation

> Civil-agency SE process companion to NASA/DAU. This chapter synthesizes four technical-management disciplines (sections 4.4–4.7) of the FAA Systems Engineering Manual. It describes how a regulator-operator runs configuration control, information stewardship, structured decision-making, and V&V inside the FAA's Acquisition Management System (AMS) and across the National Airspace System (NAS). Where NASA and DAU frame these as generic 15288-style processes, the FAA binds them to its own orders, boards, and baseline taxonomy — read this as the airspace-specific instantiation, not a replacement for the broader canon.

## Core Idea

These four disciplines share one job: keeping the truth about a system stable, traceable, and decision-ready as it moves through the AMS lifecycle. Configuration Management (CM) holds the *product* truth — what the system is, version by version, under board control. Systems Engineering Information Management (SEIM) holds the *project* truth — the documents, data, and records that surround the product. Decision Analysis (DA) is the structured method for *choosing* among alternatives when the truth admits more than one path. Verification and Validation (V&V) is the discipline that *confirms* the truth — that the product was built right (verification) and that the right product was built (validation). Together they form the evidence backbone that the FAA's governance bodies rely on to allocate money and accept systems into the NAS.

The unifying mechanic across all four is the **baseline-and-control loop**: establish an agreed reference point, then manage every change to it through a defined authority. CM applies that loop to product configuration. SEIM applies a Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) variant to information products. DA applies it to decisions through documented criteria and a decision report. V&V applies it through traceability against criteria captured in earlier work products. In every case the FAA treats "uncontrolled change" as the primary enemy of system integrity.

## Frameworks Introduced

The chapter names specific FAA instruments. Each is listed below with what it is and when/how the manual invokes it.

- **FAA Order 1800.66, Configuration Management Policy** — the governing CM order. It prescribes FAA CM requirements and procedures, governs how new products/services enter the NAS or how existing ones change, and is a standalone document within the AMS. The manual points to it for sample CM-plan content, for CCB charter and operating-procedure requirements, and (paragraph 3.3.2.5, Data Management) for data-management procedure. Invoked throughout section 4.4 as the authoritative source.
- **Configuration Control Board (CCB)** — the FAA-authorized formal decision-making forum that establishes CM baselines and adjudicates changes to them. Each CCB writes operating procedures to fit its mission. Introduced as part of the agency's governance structure and as a required element of any CM plan.
- **NAS CCB / Joint Resources Council (JRC)** — the NAS CCB is the highest-ranking CCB, chartered by the JRC (or its designee), with authority to charter subordinate boards. Named where the manual explains how baseline-change authority cascades.
- **Configuration Control Decision (CCD)** — the official, legally binding FAA notice of a CCB's decisions and directives; it names required actions and the responsible organizations. Closure of a CCD marks completed change implementation.
- **The five FAA baselines — Functional, Allocated, Product, Facility, Operational** — the FAA's specific baseline taxonomy (section 4.4.2, step 4). Distinct from the generic functional/allocated/product triad: the FAA adds Facility (as-built facility space and critical power-panel data) and Operational (installed hardware/software, a product baseline adapted to local NAS conditions). Each ties to specific milestones; the Product baseline is established on successful completion of the Functional Configuration Audit (FCA) and Physical Configuration Audit (PCA).
- **Change vehicles — ECP, RFD/RFW, MOU, NCP, PTR, HDR** — the named instruments that carry change requests. Engineering Change Proposals (ECP) and Requests for Deviation/Waiver (RFD/RFW) are contractor-side, against contractor-controlled baselines. The NAS Change Proposal (NCP) is the internal vehicle for FAA-managed NAS baselines, processed through the National CM support tool. Program Trouble Reports (PTR) and Hardware Discrepancy Reports (HDR) let operational-support staff fix defects without touching a baseline.
- **Configuration Status Accounting (CSA) / CSAR / Master Configuration Index (MCI)** — CSA is the systematic recording and reporting of configuration status; CSARs are its on-demand or scheduled reports; the MCI is the index holding baseline change history. Captured automatically in the NAS CM support tools for the NCP process.
- **CDRLs and DIDs** — Contract Data Requirements Lists and Data Item Descriptions, named as the primary means of monitoring and enforcing contractor CM deliverables during solution implementation.
- **PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act)** — invoked as the cycle structure governing SEIM activities (introduced earlier in the manual's Section 1.4). SEIM is organized as Plan / Perform / Review-and-Update, mapping to PDCA.
- **Integrated Multidisciplinary Team (IMT)** — the cross-discipline team that triggers and tailors the Decision Analysis process and must reach consensus on criteria, weights, and ratings. Named throughout section 4.6.
- **Decision Analysis techniques** — the manual catalogs, without prescribing one: **Prioritization Matrix** and **Quality Function Deployment (QFD)** (for criteria and weights); **Morphological Matrix** and **Baseline Reference Method / Pugh Matrix** (for generating alternatives); **Relative Rank Method**, **TOPSIS** (Technique for Order of Prioritization by Similarity to Ideal Solution), **Decision Trees**, **Influence Diagrams**, and **Modeling and Simulation** (for evaluating alternatives). Critical Performance Requirements (CPR) are noted as both an input and an output of criteria/weighting.
- **The "V" model / V diagram** — the FAA's V&V depiction (Figure 49). The left leg is work-product V&V owned mostly by systems engineers (concepts, requirements, architectures); the right leg is product/component V&V where SE supports T&E teams during Solution Implementation.
- **T&E activities — DT, OT, IOA** — Development Test (supports verification), Operational Test and Independent Operational Assessment (support validation). Governed by the FAA Test and Evaluation Process Guidelines and the ATO T&E Handbook, which the manual says complement one another.
- **V&V Guidelines, VRTM, TEMP** — the FAA AMS Lifecycle V&V Guidelines (the authoritative V&V reference), the Verification Requirements Traceability Matrix (the checklist tying every requirement to a validated need and a verification method), and the Test and Evaluation Master Plan. Verification planning lives mainly in the SEMP.
- **SEIM tools** — DOORS (requirements versioning/traceability), NAS EA Portal (enterprise architecture products), Knowledge Sharing Network (KSN, access-controlled document repository), Documentum (official document versions), System Architect (architecture products). Named as concrete information-management technology.

## Key Concepts

**A baseline is a dated agreement, not a snapshot of opinion.** The manual defines a baseline as an agreed-to description of a product's attributes at a point in time that serves as the basis for defining change. Establishing baselines is what makes a system manageable through its lifecycle; the absence of them produces confusion, version errors, and unauthorized change. The five FAA baselines progress as a series: Functional (first formal baseline, after concept exploration), Allocated (design requirements, typically after the System Requirements Review), Product (the delivered configuration, set after FCA and PCA), Facility (as-built facility and power data), and Operational (the deployed-and-adapted product baseline plus all in-service changes). The Operational baseline is explicitly the product baseline localized to NAS conditions.

**Configuration control is a closed-loop, eight-step process.** CM is iterative and present in every AMS phase. Its steps run from developing a CM plan, identifying configuration, selecting Configuration Items (CIs — aggregations of hardware/software/firmware serving an end-use function), establishing and maintaining baselines, managing approved changes, providing status accounting, verifying and auditing, through monitoring contractor CM. Change management itself decomposes into identify/describe → evaluate → disposition → monitor implementation, with reviews required *before* adjudication and CCBs serving as the adjudication forum.

**Work products are managed but not part of the product's configuration.** Data Management is a deliberate carve-out: program work products requiring a manager's signature get coordination and version control, but they are not formally part of the product baseline. This separates the lighter discipline applied to project paperwork from the formal board control applied to product configuration — a distinction that SEIM then picks up.

**SEIM is a deliberately renamed discipline.** The FAA explicitly notes it uses "Information Management" differently from the INCOSE handbook and international standards, so it coins **Systems Engineering Information Management (SEIM)** for what INCOSE simply calls Information Management. SEIM collects, manages, stores, and distributes all project information plus needed enterprise information, ensuring the right information is available, accurate, and secure when needed. It distinguishes data (raw, unorganized facts) from information (organized data with meaning to a recipient), while noting the manual uses the two terms interchangeably.

**The SEIM lifecycle is acquire → validate → maintain → distribute → archive → retire.** Validation here means filtering among multiple versions for the current or correct past-baseline copy — keeping "frozen" and "working" marked-up sets current. Distribution is request-driven and may be denied based on access rights, sensitivity, security, or availability; not every request is granted. Archive preserves records for lessons learned and risk identification; retire disposes of stale products, but even retirement carries security, privacy, and legal obligations because improper disposal can threaten the integrity of records that remain.

**Decision Analysis fidelity must match decision stakes.** The FAA does not prescribe a single tool — it stresses that every technique carries assumptions and limits, and that the simplest, most common tools make the most assumptions and so suit only simpler decisions. The engineer must pick a technique whose output fidelity matches what the decision needs: too little fidelity yields incomplete information, too much wastes resources. DA is most emphasized at the Investment Analysis decision gates, where the FAA decides whether to fund an alternative.

**Criteria define "what matters," weights define "how much."** Evaluation criteria should be measurable where possible; when a criterion is qualitative, the DA plan should assign it a quantitative value for quality. Common criteria span development and lifecycle cost, requirements compliance, RMA, safety, human factors, and many programmatic and technical-risk dimensions. Weighting is acknowledged as subjective; common approaches are a 1-to-10 rank or a percentage allocation across the criterion set. Cost is evaluated separately from other criteria throughout the process.

**Four-to-six alternatives is the working band.** The manual recommends down-selecting until alternatives are meaningfully distinct, citing roughly four to six as enough representation; if six alternatives still lack distinction, perform sensitivity analysis. All alternatives considered must be documented, and options failing essential criteria should be eliminated early.

**Verification is incremental and continuous; validation is iterative and early.** Verification confirms the product is "built right" against requirements, architectures, specifications, design constraints, and standards — it runs throughout development and does not require a test for every requirement, only a valid verification method at an appropriate level. Validation confirms "the right product is built" — that the work product or product will fulfill its purpose in its intended environment, performed as early as possible to reduce risk. The four verification method categories are Inspection, Analysis, Test, and Demonstration.

**V&V works against criteria captured upstream.** Each work product is verified or validated against criteria set in a prior step, then becomes the criteria basis for later products — the mechanism the V diagram illustrates. The ConOps and NAS Enterprise Architecture are major sources of validation criteria; the EA can in turn be refined from V&V results.

## Mental Models

**The baseline as a "save point."** A baseline freezes a known-good configuration so every later change is measured against a defined reference rather than against shifting memory. Each milestone takes a new save point; the chain of save points is the system's history.

**Two ledgers, one truth.** CM keeps the product ledger (board-controlled, formal). SEIM keeps the project ledger (managed, version-controlled, but not board-controlled). Work products move on the SEIM ledger; CIs move on the CM ledger. Knowing which ledger an artifact belongs to tells you what control rigor applies.

**The funnel and the filter.** Decision Analysis is a funnel: many candidate alternatives enter, screening against essential criteria filters them, down-selection narrows to four-to-six distinct options, evaluation scores them, and sensitivity analysis tests whether the winner is robust. The output is a recommendation to the FAA decision authority — DA recommends; the authority decides.

**Fidelity dial.** Picking a DA technique is setting a dial between assumption-heavy/cheap and assumption-light/expensive. The right setting is the lowest fidelity that still answers the decision — the same logic the manual applies to choosing the least complex model that gives the most visibility.

**The V as a mirror.** The left leg defines and decomposes (validate the criteria); the right leg integrates and confirms (verify against those criteria). Every point on the right verifies against the matching point on the left. Crossing the bottom of the V is where SE hands the lead to T&E.

**Three sentence test for V&V outcomes.** "Built right" = verification. "Right thing built" = validation. "Proven on paper before proven in metal" = validate work products early so defects don't propagate.

## Key Takeaways

- The FAA runs CM, SEIM, DA, and V&V as four faces of one principle: establish an agreed reference, then control every change to it through a defined authority and a traceable record.
- CM is governed by FAA Order 1800.66 and enforced by CCBs; the NAS CCB (chartered by the JRC) sits at the top and the CCD is the legally binding record of a board's decision.
- The FAA uses five baselines — Functional, Allocated, Product, Facility, Operational — extending the generic triad with airspace- and facility-specific references; the Product baseline depends on passing both the FCA and PCA.
- SEIM is a deliberate FAA rename of INCOSE's Information Management, run as a PDCA cycle over six steps (acquire, validate, maintain, distribute, archive, retire), with distribution gated by access and security.
- Decision Analysis is tool-agnostic by design: the FAA catalogs many techniques (Prioritization Matrix, QFD, Pugh/Baseline Reference, Morphological Matrix, Relative Rank, TOPSIS, Decision Trees, Influence Diagrams, M&S) but prescribes none, demanding the engineer match technique fidelity to decision stakes and document every alternative.
- V&V is anchored in the "V" model, the VRTM, and the AMS V&V Guidelines; verification (built right, incremental) and validation (right thing, iterative and early) are kept distinct, with DT serving verification and OT/IOA serving validation.
- Across all four disciplines, documentation is the deliverable: CSARs, the SEIM information products, the Decision Report, and controlled-and-archived V&V records are what let FAA governance bodies make funding and acceptance decisions with confidence.
