# Chapter — Specialty Engineering I: RMA, Life Cycle Engineering, E3 and Spectrum, and Human Factors

> Scope note: This chapter synthesizes Section 5 (Specialty Engineering) of the FAA Systems Engineering Manual, covering Reliability/Maintainability/Availability, Life Cycle Engineering, Electromagnetic Environmental Effects and Spectrum Management, and Human Factors Engineering. The SEM is a civil-agency SE process companion to NASA and DAU material — it does not restate generic SE theory but shows how a safety-of-life airspace operator institutionalizes these specialty disciplines through its own acquisition machinery (the Acquisition Management System, NAS architecture, and FAA orders and handbooks). Read it alongside, not instead of, the NASA Systems Engineering Handbook and the DAU SE Guidebook.

## Core Idea

Specialty engineering in the FAA exists to make a system *operationally suitable*, not merely functionally correct. The four disciplines in this chapter each attach a non-functional dimension to a system — its dependability over time (RMA), its total cost and supportability across its whole life (LCE), its behavior in a shared electromagnetic and radio-frequency environment (E3 and Spectrum), and its fit to the people who run and maintain it (Human Factors). What unifies them in the FAA treatment is that the agency's mission *is* safety, so each discipline is bound back to safety and to the National Airspace System (NAS) rather than treated as an isolated analysis.

The most consequential idea in the RMA material is a deliberate rejection of availability as a contractual specification for modern software-intensive systems. The manual argues that availability hides a reliability-versus-maintainability trade-off, cannot be predicted credibly before software exists, and cannot be demonstrated within any practical test window. Instead the FAA allocates dependability top-down through *Service Threads* — end-to-end "sensor-to-glass" reliability block diagrams of the equipment delivering a service — and classifies each by how badly its loss would hurt safety and efficiency. The other three disciplines share the same posture: push the analysis as early as possible (cheaper to fix), tie every output back into the standard SE processes (requirements, risk, verification), and recognize that the real failure modes of NAS systems are latent software defects, supportability gaps, environmental interactions, and human error — not simple hardware breakage.

## Frameworks Introduced

The manual names specific FAA instruments and methods. Listed here with where and how each is used.

- **FAA RMA Handbook (FAA-HDBK-006A)** — the authoritative companion the SEM defers to. The SEM's RMA section is explicitly built on it; the handbook holds the term definitions, the detailed allocation methodology, sample requirements, and analytical appendices. The SEM repeatedly points to handbook sections (e.g., the preliminary requirements analysis methodology, the procurement-package guidance, and the appendix tables used to check an offeror's RMA model) rather than duplicating them.

- **Service Threads and the Service Thread Loss Severity Category (STLSC)** — the core RMA allocation construct. A Service Thread is a reliability block diagram of all equipment needed to deliver a NAPRS service to an air traffic specialist. Each thread carries one of three criticality categories with an associated availability target: *Essential* (loss reduces capacity with localized efficiency impact; A = .9999), *Efficiency-Critical* (loss carries economic efficiency impact, possibly system-wide; A = .99999), and *Safety-Critical* (loss would present an unacceptable safety hazard during the transition to reduced capacity). The manual stresses that no single thread may carry a safety-critical service; such a service must be split into two independent threads each at .99999, because one thread can never be trusted to reach the needed level. Notably, there is no "routine" category — a top-down allocation for routine service would yield unacceptably low requirements and unreliable, costly systems.

- **NAPRS services and FAA Order 6040.15** — the operational anchor for Service Threads. Threads are defined against the National Airspace Performance Reporting System services in that order, which lets engineers tie NAS-level requirements to real, well-understood fielded services and "close the loop" by comparing requirements against the measured performance of deployed systems.

- **The three availability constructs** — *Inherent Availability (Ai)*, the theoretical maximum considering only hardware under ideal support with perfect failure coverage and no scheduled downtime; *Equipment and Service Availability (Aes)*, an operational measure including unscheduled outages, logistics, and administrative delays but excluding scheduled downtime; and *Operational Availability (Aop)*, which includes all downtime. Aes and Aop for reportable facilities are tracked by NAPRS.

- **The six RMA process tasks** — the acquisition-aligned workflow: (1) Preliminary Requirements Analysis, building the bridge from enterprise requirements to procurement specs via Service Threads; (2) Procurement Package Preparation, populating the System-Level Specification (SLS), Statement of Work (SOW), and Information for Proposal Preparation (IFPP); (3) Proposal Evaluation, judging reliability modeling, fault-tolerant design, and performance modeling; (4) Contractor Design Monitoring, through formal reviews, Fault Tolerance Technical Interchange Meetings, and fault-tolerance risk management; (5) Design Validation and Reliability Growth; and (6) RMA Requirements Analysis and Maintenance, keeping the NAS-RD and thread allocations current.

- **Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** — a top-down graphical logic method that maps fault and hazard paths to an undesired top-level event, supporting both qualitative and quantitative evaluation. The manual prizes its visible inter-relationships and its accessibility to analysts unfamiliar with the system, while warning that its top-down direction can let a non-obvious top-event hazard slip past unnoticed.

- **Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA) and FMEA with Criticality (FMECA)** — a systematic, bottom-up identification of failure modes and their effects on the next-higher design level, qualitative or quantitative. Adding a criticality figure of merit and severity levels turns an FMEA into an FMECA; severity cannot be assigned until the FMECA's purpose is fixed. The manual notes related techniques such as dependence diagrams and Markov analysis.

- **Reliability growth and "expunging"** — the validation mechanism replacing infeasible pass/fail demonstration. Reliability growth is a continuous test-and-correct cycle; MTBF is computed from operating hours over failures, but a failure whose cause is shown corrected (or which cannot recur within 30 days) is "expunged." The metric is acknowledged as statistically meaningless for true MTBF but useful as a lever on contractor responsiveness, since the Government decides when to expunge and thereby controls the MTBF reported upward.

- **Life Cycle Engineering (LCE) and its six steps** — Identify Needs, Technical Assessment, Technology Insertion, Analyze Performance, Operational Assessment, and Establish Service Environment. LCE maps onto AMS lifecycle phases and decision points and uses Earned Value Measurement to set cost and schedule targets, with a Basis of Estimate (BOE) feeding the yearly Resource Planning Document (RPD).

- **Integrated Logistics Support (ILS / NAILS) and its nine elements** — these cover: maintenance planning; the maintenance support facility; direct-work staffing for maintenance; supply support; support equipment; training (with its support and the personnel skills it builds); technical data; packaging, handling, storage and transportation; and computer-resources support. The manual treats ILS and NAILS as interchangeable terms and ties these elements to each AMS phase.

- **In-Service Performance Review (ISPR), Operational Environment Assessment (OEA), and the In-Service Review / In-Service Decision (ISR/ISD)** — the periodic technical and operational health checkpoints across the in-service life: the ISPR (typically every two years post-commissioning) characterizes in-service health; the OEA gauges the operational environment's capacity to support the deployed configuration; the ISR checklist and ISD gate deployment to the field.

- **FAA COTS Risk Mitigation Guide** — referenced for managing COTS obsolescence stages and limiting their effect on system performance and sustainment across the out-years.

- **E3 vocabulary and governing references** — Electromagnetic Environment (EME), Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC), Electromagnetic Susceptibility (EMS), Hazards of EM Radiation (RADHAZ, including HERF to fuels and HERP to personnel), EM Pulse (EMP), Electrostatic Discharge (ESD), Lightning, and Precipitation Static (P-Static). Governing references named include FCC Title 47 Part 15 of the CFR, the FCC "digital device" definition, ANSI C63.14 for E3 definitions, and FAA-G-2100 (General Requirements for Electronic Equipment, whose EMC paragraph makes EMC a requirement for any acquisition).

- **Spectrum Management instruments** — FAA Order 6050.19 (declaring aeronautical spectrum a scarce, limited resource), the NTIA as administrator of the Federal portion of the spectrum, ATC Spectrum Engineering Services (formerly ASR) as the FAA owner, Frequency Management Officers (FMOs) as the regional RFI points of contact, and the Spectrum Planning Subcommittee approval that gates the initial requirements document.

- **Human Factors Engineering (HFE) instruments** — the FAA Human Factors Acquisition Job Aid (in the FAA Acquisition System Toolset, FAST), the Human Performance Interfaces taxonomy (Table 27), the 24 Human Factors Areas of Interest (Table 28), the HFE process tasks, the Human Factors Coordinator role, the Human Factors Working Group (HFWG), and the Human Factors Integrated Program Plan. Referenced companion guides include the FAA Human Factors Integration Guide for Mission and Service Area Analysis and the FAA Guidelines for Human Factors Requirements Development.

## Key Concepts

**Why the FAA distrusts availability as a requirement.** The manual lays out three reasons availability fails its own criteria for a good requirement. First, equal availability numbers can hide wildly different operational realities — it offers a paired example where a 3-minute restart with a 5,000-hour MTBF and a 3-hour restart with a 34-year MTBF both compute to .99999, yet the 34-year figure is uncheckable and the operational impacts differ enormously. Second, availability cannot be predicted credibly before the software exists, because the dominant driver for fault-tolerant software-intensive systems is the effectiveness of fault detection and recovery software, which hinges on undiscovered defects. Third, contractual compliance cannot be verified, because a statistically valid demonstration would have to run longer than the system's lifetime and would be contaminated by ongoing software fixes and disputed failure attribution. Availability remains useful as a planning tool, a trade-study aid, and an operational metric — just not as a primary contractual spec.

**Three FAA system categories drive different RMA treatment.** *Information systems* (software-intensive ATC automation and communications) get the strict top-down availability allocation and the most attention, given their criticality and software cost/schedule risk. *Remote and distributed systems* (sensors fanning in, services fanning out to many workstations) achieve availability through diversity and overlapping coverage; the manual explicitly rejects top-down allocation for them because availability is a binary up/down measure ill-suited to many independent elements — forcing an arbitrary "r of n" rule (e.g., 49 of 50 radars up) misrepresents operational reality. Their element requirements are better set by lifecycle-cost reasoning and expert judgment on number and placement. *Infrastructure systems* (power, HVAC) violate the independent-failure assumption underlying RMA math and so also resist top-down allocation; the answer is standardized configurations consistent with the threads they support.

**The hazard interval during capacity transition.** When a critical service is interrupted, the move from full to reduced-capacity operation is not instantaneous; during that transition controllers increase separation and clear airspace, and a window of elevated safety-hazard risk can open. In most cases this interval is negligible and only efficiency is affected — but for surveillance or voice communications the safety hazard is real until traffic density is reduced, which is exactly why any service identified as critical to safe separation must have an independent backup.

**Fault tolerance must be embedded in application software, not bolted on.** A recurring concern is that recovery infrastructure alone cannot mask software faults unless the application software is itself written to handle unanticipated or erroneous inputs. Because application developers are driven by functionality under schedule pressure, embedding fault tolerance is routinely deferred and becomes very hard to add later — so the manual prescribes separate risk-management tracks for the fault-tolerant infrastructure, for error handling in applications, and for performance monitoring, plus standards that application developers must follow.

**Reliability growth accepts that systems ship "not yet good enough."** Because a valid reliability demonstration at the William J. Hughes Technical Center (WJHTC) is impossible — there are simply not enough operating hours to burn down latent defects to the required level — the manual concludes systems will be fielded before they meet reliability targets, with the large fleet of installations accumulating the hours that drive MTBF growth. Success depends on the contractor aggressively fixing defects (incentivized by initial and final reliability targets set in the SLS and by Government control of expunging) and on field personnel concurring that the system is stable enough to deploy.

**Life-cycle cost is decided early and dominated by support.** LCE's premise is that early-phase decisions lock in most lifetime cost; procurement cost is the visible part, but later maintenance cost traces directly to planning and procurement choices. The manual flags that second-level engineering and maintenance support can exceed 70% of a program's operations budget, which is why the technical assessment evaluates the Maintenance Support Facility so heavily and why sustainment favors evolutionary upgrades and technology insertion over periodic wholesale replacement.

**E3 is bidirectional and environment-relative.** Electromagnetic compatibility is not a single property of a box; it is the relationship between a system and its electromagnetic environment in both directions — what the environment does to the system (susceptibility, vulnerability) and what the system emits into the environment (conducted and radiated emissions). Because the EME can change across a system's life, E3 analyses are redone, and the manual recommends running them on COTS as well as new systems. A key engineering decision point: compute the system's electrical dimensions first — if it is electrically large, simple circuit math (e.g., Kirchhoff's Laws) is insufficient and Maxwell's Equations must be used.

**Regulatory compliance is necessary but not sufficient.** FCC rules focus on a device's *generated* emissions, not its susceptibility, and most NAS RF/electronic devices fall under the less-stringent Class A (commercial/industrial) regulations. The manual is explicit that meeting FCC requirements does not guarantee compatibility with the intended operational environment — developers must still address the actual EME, their own emissions into it, and their own susceptibility, or face costly redesign that grows more expensive the later it is discovered.

**Spectrum is a scarce, governed, shared resource.** The FAA is among the top three Federal spectrum users with more than 50,000 frequency assignments, and a new RF system cannot be introduced without a frequency assignment. Spectrum Management runs the whole life: initial band assignment, RFI detection and reporting (new systems tested so they do not interfere with others; external RFI reported to the regional FMO), and band modifications driven by new systems, customer-need changes, national allotment adjustments, or international issues. Project teams must engage ATC Spectrum Engineering Services early; the initial requirements process is not complete until the Spectrum Planning Subcommittee approves.

**Human factors treats operators and maintainers as first-class system elements.** HFE's stance is that the operators and maintainers of a system matter every bit as much as the equipment and code they run, and that an effective program must characterize not only the system but the users, their attributes, and the use environment. The discipline is justified economically as well as operationally: applied early, HFE raises performance/safety/productivity and lowers staffing and training costs; applied late, operational/maintenance/design changes become expensive, high-risk adjustments. The Human Performance Interfaces taxonomy (functional-role, information, environmental, operational, organizational, cooperation, cognitive, and physical interfaces) and the 24 Areas of Interest (from Allocation of Functional Roles and Anthropometrics through Human Error, Situation Awareness, Workload, and Work Space) give the program a structured checklist so the breadth of human concerns is not lost. HFE also explicitly feeds human reliability analysis back into RMA engineering.

**Every specialty output re-enters the standard SE processes.** The disciplines are not terminal analyses. RMA, E3, Spectrum, and HFE all generate requirements that feed Requirements Management, concerns and issues that feed Risk Management, and verification criteria that feed Verification and Validation. E3 results, like other specialty analyses, are captured in a Design Analysis Report; HFE produces a Human Factors Plan or input to the SEMP and a family of analysis reports (planning, analysis, design/development, test and evaluation, and management/coordination).

## Mental Models

**The reliability block diagram as the unit of dependability.** Stop thinking of a "system" as a box with an availability number and start thinking of an end-to-end *thread* — every piece from sensor to glass that the controller depends on. Dependability is a property of the thread, allocated by how much its loss would hurt safety and efficiency, and it stays stable as the NAS evolves because most architecture changes just swap one block for another.

**Two ways to hit a dependability goal — design it in, or support it well.** The manual frames the basic RMA choice as either building high-reliability systems up front or optimizing logistics to tolerate lower maintainability/supportability. Reliability and maintainability are the tradeable knobs; availability is the higher-level requirement they serve. Use this to reason about where money should go on a given program.

**Cost is front-loaded, pain is back-loaded.** Across all four disciplines the same curve appears: the decisions are cheap and easy early and the consequences are expensive and rigid late. An E3 problem caught in requirements is a spec line; caught after deployment it is a redevelopment. An HFE concept change in late acquisition is a high-risk program adjustment. This is the single strongest argument the manual makes for pulling specialty engineering to the front of the lifecycle.

**Top-down where math holds, expert judgment where it doesn't.** Top-down availability allocation is right for software-intensive information systems and wrong for distributed and infrastructure systems whose independence assumptions break. The mental discipline is to ask whether the failure-independence assumptions hold before reaching for the allocation formula; when they don't, defer to lifecycle-cost reasoning and subject-matter-expert judgment on placement and configuration.

**Compatibility is a relationship, not a property.** For E3, picture the system inside an environment full of other emitters and susceptibilities, and ask both questions: can it tolerate what's there, and is it a good neighbor? The same relational framing applies to spectrum (your assignment versus everyone else's) and even to human factors (the human inside the system, not beside it).

**The contractor-incentive lens on reliability growth.** When you cannot prove reliability statistically, you instead engineer the contractor's behavior: set initial and final targets in the spec, hold the expunging lever, and report a controlled MTBF upward. The "number" is a management instrument for responsiveness, not a measurement of truth — keep those two purposes separate in your head.

## Key Takeaways

- The FAA's mission is safety, so RMA is bound tightly to system safety and risk management, and dependability is allocated top-down with safety and efficiency as the primary considerations.
- Availability is excluded as a primary contractual requirement for complex software-intensive systems for three reasons — it hides the reliability/maintainability trade, it cannot be predicted credibly pre-software, and it cannot be demonstrated within a practical window.
- Dependability is allocated through Service Threads classified by STLSC (Essential .9999, Efficiency-Critical .99999, Safety-Critical split into two independent .99999 threads); there is deliberately no "routine" category.
- Three system categories get different treatment: information systems take top-down allocation; remote/distributed and infrastructure systems do not, because their failure-independence assumptions break.
- The primary causes of unscheduled outages in modern NAS information systems are latent software defects and maintenance/restoration delays — not hardware failures — and restart time often dominates restoration.
- Reliability growth (with expunging) replaces infeasible pass/fail demonstration; systems are knowingly fielded short of target and matured across the fleet, with field-personnel concurrence on readiness.
- FTA (top-down, may miss non-obvious top hazards) and FMEA/FMECA (bottom-up, criticality added for FMECA) are the named hazard/failure analysis methods.
- Life-cycle cost is locked in early and dominated by support; second-level engineering and maintenance can exceed 70% of operations budget, so sustainment favors technology insertion and evolutionary upgrades over wholesale replacement, with COTS obsolescence actively managed.
- E3 is bidirectional and environment-relative; FCC compliance (mostly Class A for NAS devices) addresses only emitted EMI and does not guarantee operational compatibility, so the EME and the system's own susceptibility must still be engineered.
- Spectrum is a scarce governed resource; no RF system may be fielded without an assignment, engagement with ATC Spectrum Engineering Services must start early, and management continues for the full life.
- HFE treats operators and maintainers as first-class system elements, is justified by both performance and lifecycle cost, and uses the Human Performance Interfaces taxonomy and 24 Areas of Interest to keep its breadth disciplined.
- Every specialty discipline routes its outputs back into the standard SE processes: requirements, risk (concerns and issues), and verification/validation.
