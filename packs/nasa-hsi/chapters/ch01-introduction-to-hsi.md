# Chapter 1: Introduction to NASA Human Systems Integration

## Core Idea
HSI is a systems engineering discipline that treats the human as a sub-system on par with hardware and software, applied throughout a system's life cycle to optimise total system performance and reduce life-cycle cost (LCC).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Human Systems Integration (HSI)**: An interdisciplinary management and technical process that integrates human considerations into system acquisition and development to enhance design, reduce LCC, and optimise total system performance.
  - When to use: Applicable to all NASA programs and projects from Pre-Phase A onward; mandatory per NPR 7123.1B.
- **Human-Centered Design (HCD)**: An iterative approach to interactive system development that makes systems usable by ensuring human needs, abilities, and limitations are met through frequent user evaluations.
  - When to use: When designing hardware/software with direct human interaction, especially human space flight programs (required by NASA-STD-3001 V2 3005).
- **Total System Performance**: The measurable outcome of the integrated interaction of hardware, software, and human elements.
  - When to use: As the top-level success criterion when conducting trade studies and evaluating design alternatives.

## Key Concepts
- **HSI Domains**: The six functional areas NASA uses to structure HSI work — Human Factors Engineering (HFE), Operations Resources, Maintainability and Supportability, Habitability and Environment, Safety, and Training. Each domain has the potential to interact with all others.
- **Human Factors Engineering (HFE)**: The central HSI domain; responsible for characterising human capabilities and constraints and applying that knowledge to engineered systems. Three guiding principles: demands shall be compatible with human capabilities; systems shall exploit human capabilities in non-routine situations; systems shall tolerate and recover from human error.
- **Life Cycle Cost (LCC)**: Total ownership cost from conception through operations. HSI's primary economic justification: LCC is "locked in" early in a program, so inadequate early HSI leads to expensive operations-phase corrections.
- **Function Allocation**: The process of assigning functions to hardware, software, or humans during system design. A key HSI activity performed from Pre-Phase A onward.
- **Systems Engineering Engine (SEE)**: NASA's 17-process SE framework defined in NPR 7123.1B. HSI runs in tight synchronisation with the SEE rather than as a parallel discipline.
- **HSI Practitioner**: The lead person responsible for implementing HSI within a program or project; must have SE or human-centered design expertise and be capable of integrating multiple domain SMEs.
- **Key Concept #4 — Early Application**: HSI must be established in P/P planning during early concept and design phases. After a system is designed, implementing HSI oversight or workarounds is very expensive.

## Mental Models
- Use HSI as a cross-cutting process embedded in the SEE, not a separate parallel workstream — this is how a systems engineer becomes an HSI practitioner.
- Use HFE when you need to apply human capability/limitation data to a specific hardware or software design decision; use HSI when you need to integrate multiple human-centered domains across the full life cycle.
- Use HCD when you need iterative user evaluation loops to inform design; it is the method; HSI is the management framework that contains it.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating HSI as "somebody else's job"**: NASA's approach explicitly integrates HSI with SE workflow so that it becomes another best practice for systems engineers, not a separate speciality.
- **Deferring HSI to post-design phases**: INCOSE data cited in the guide shows that LCC is locked in during early phases; applying HSI oversight after design is completed is very expensive and often ineffective.
- **Applying only HFE without HSI integration**: Prior to HSI, separate human-centered domains had to interact with P/P management as independent disciplines, producing uncoordinated inputs. HSI coordination resolves domain conflicts before they reach management.

## Key Takeaways
1. The human is a sub-system on par with hardware and software; total system performance requires all three to be considered concurrently.
2. All personnel who interact with a system across its entire life cycle — users, operators, maintainers, trainers, logistics personnel — are within scope of HSI.
3. HSI domain integration is managed by a lead HSI practitioner who resolves cross-domain conflicts before they escalate to program management.
4. Early application of HSI (Pre-Phase A) produces the maximum LCC savings; late application produces costly workarounds.
5. HSI, HFE, and HCD are nested: HSI is the management and process framework, HFE is one domain within it, and HCD is the design methodology employed.
6. NASA formally mandated HSI through NPR 7123.1B (2013), which requires an HSI Plan for all programs and projects.

## Connects To
- **NPR 7123.1B** (NASA Systems Engineering Processes and Requirements): Primary regulatory document defining HSI as a required process; Appendix G specifies HSI Plan as a recommended deliverable at lifecycle milestones.
- **NASA-STD-3001** (Space Flight Human-System Standard): Mandatory HMTA standard; Volume 2 section 3.5 requires a human-centered design process for all human space flight programs.
- **NPR 8705.2B** (Human-Rating Requirements): Requires an HSI team to be formed before SRR for human-rated programs.
- **INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook**: Source of the "locked-in LCC" model cited in section 1.4.
