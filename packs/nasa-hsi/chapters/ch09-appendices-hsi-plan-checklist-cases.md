# Chapter 9: Appendices — HSI Plan Outline, Checklist, and Case Studies

## Core Idea
The three operational appendices translate HSI concepts into practitioner-ready artifacts: Appendix A provides an annotated HSI Plan template, Appendix B a domain-by-domain implementation checklist, and Appendix C real program case studies demonstrating the cost of inadequate HSI and the return on effective HSI investment.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Annotated HSI Plan Outline (Appendix A)**: A six-section template for a major program HSI Plan, with annotated key points under each section; the primary reference for writing any new HSI Plan.
  - When to use: When initiating or updating an HSI Plan for a new P/P; tailor to P/P size per Chapter 4 (section 4.2) guidance.
- **HSI Implementation Planning Checklist (Appendix B)**: A domain-by-domain checklist to assess scope of the HSI effort during early life-cycle phases; initiated immediately upon assuming HSI lead responsibility.
  - When to use: At P/P inception and revisited at each phase transition to identify new or changed HSI scope; documents the basis for the chosen HSI effort scale.
- **HSI Case Studies (Appendix C)**: Empirical examples drawn from real NASA programs illustrating negative outcomes from inadequate HSI (F-22, Shuttle Ground Processing, F-119, Constellation) and positive outcomes from effective HSI, plus an ideal-state vision for a future large-scale human space flight program.
  - When to use: When building the business case for HSI investment with program management; when identifying lessons to apply to a new P/P.

## Key Concepts
- **HSI Plan as Audit Trail**: The HSI Plan may serve as the audit trail for HSI data sources, analyses, activities, trade studies, and decisions not captured in other P/P documentation — a critical governance function for traceability.
- **HSI Plan as Dynamic Document**: The HSI Plan is updated at every key life-cycle milestone; it is not a static deliverable but a living management tool.
- **HSI Relevance Section (3.2)**: The most persuasive section of the HSI Plan for program management; must describe how human performance characteristics drive total system performance and mission success, and identify known LCC risks from human element gaps.
- **HSI Strategy Section (4.1)**: Identifies P/P-specific HSI key decision points, critical enabling technologies for human performance, and the update schedule for the plan itself; links HSI products to SE products, analyses, and trade studies.
- **HSI Domains Section (4.2)**: Identifies which of the six NASA HSI domains are applicable to the specific P/P, provides rationale for inclusion, and describes the process to apply, validate, and integrate domain knowledge.
- **HSI Requirements Section (5.1)**: References applicable requirements and standards, describes how each contributes to mission success and affordability, captures program-specific HCD process per NASA-STD-3001 V2 3005, and documents derivation rationale for any program-specific requirements not derived from NASA standards.
- **F-22 Hypoxia-Like Incidents (C.1)**: Negative case study illustrating failure to adequately investigate and resolve human physiological performance issues during operations; demonstrates the consequence of inadequate HSI in the Operations and Sustainment phase.
- **Shuttle Ground Processing (C.2)**: Negative/mixed case study illustrating how ground crew operations complexity and crew time can escalate dramatically when human elements are not designed into the system from the start.
- **ISS Emergency Lighting Case (ISS EELS → EEGS)**: Positive case study (in body of guide, Ch. 4): original battery-dependent Emergency Egress Lighting System (EELS) required excessive crew time and logistics; replacement with photo-luminescent Emergency Egress Guidance System (EEGS) reduced both crew time and cost to near zero.
- **Constellation HSI Experience (C.4)**: Mixed case study showing the value and the limitations of HSI implementation; the HSI Group operated via "influence leadership" within SE&I, demonstrating that authority structure matters for HSI effectiveness.
- **Agile at NASA (C.6)**: Positive case study demonstrating how incremental/Agile development can be successfully hybridised with NASA's plan-based SE culture for software assurance.

## Mental Models
- Use Appendix C case studies when you need to make the business case for HSI: "Standing on requirements documents alone may not carry as much leverage as being able to cite examples and case studies where HSI makes (or could have made) a difference."
- Use the ISS EELS/EEGS example as a concrete crew-time cost-avoidance illustration: a design decision made without HSI consideration generated years of crew-time and logistics cost; a subsequent HSI-informed redesign eliminated both.
- Use the Appendix B checklist as a self-auditing tool at each phase: if new HSI requirements emerge that were not captured in the initial checklist, the HSI Plan must be updated.

## Anti-patterns
- **HSI Plan written once and not updated**: The guide explicitly states the HSI Plan is "a dynamic document that will be updated at key life cycle milestones." A static HSI Plan drifts from the program reality and loses its authority as a management tool.
- **Treating the Appendix A outline as a mandatory template**: The outline is annotated guidance for "a major program — e.g., space flight or aeronautics." Smaller P/Ps must tailor aggressively; applying the full outline to a small-scale project creates unnecessary overhead.
- **Excluding the HSI Relevance section (3.2) from program management briefings**: This section is the primary management-facing justification for HSI; it translates technical HSI concerns into program risk, cost, and mission success language.

## Key Takeaways
1. The six-section HSI Plan structure (Introduction / Applicable Docs / HSI Objectives / HSI Strategy / Requirements-Organisation-Risk / Implementation) provides a complete, auditable record of how HSI is being managed for the P/P.
2. The HSI Plan must be updated at every major life-cycle milestone; its value as a management tool depends on currency.
3. The Appendix B checklist is the scoping instrument: initiate it immediately, document the result, and use it to justify the chosen scale of HSI effort.
4. Case studies (Appendix C) are the practitioner's business-case toolkit; the ISS EELS/EEGS example, the Shuttle ground processing experience, and the Constellation HSI effort are the most direct analogues for future program justification.
5. The HSI Relevance section (3.2) is the most persuasive management-facing content in the HSI Plan; it should explicitly connect known human performance challenges to mission success probability and LCC.
6. Agile/incremental programs require explicit HSI adaptation: the feedback-loop principle remains intact, but the cadence must align to sprint boundaries rather than traditional phase milestones.
