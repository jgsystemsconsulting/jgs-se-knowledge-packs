# NASA HSI Practitioner Guide Glossary

**Applicable Documents** — The set of standards, NPRs, and program documents that are invoked by HSI implementation on a P/P; listed in HSI Plan section 2.0. (Ch 9)

**CDR (Critical Design Review)** — Phase C milestone (KDP D for projects) confirming that detailed design is mature enough to proceed to full-scale fabrication; the final HSI design-influence gate. (Ch 5)

**CERR (Critical Events Readiness Review)** — Phase E review evaluating readiness for a specific high-criticality mission event (e.g., EVA); HSI provides domain readiness inputs. (Ch 6)

**ConOps (Concept of Operations)** — The system description from the user's perspective, covering nominal and off-nominal operational scenarios; established in Pre-Phase A and the left-side anchor of the SE "V" for validation. (Ch 3, Ch 8)

**Control Board** — Coordination mechanism between institutional TAs and programmatic authorities for reaching agreements on specific P/P HSI issues. (Ch 2)

**COTS (Commercial-Off-The-Shelf)** — Pre-manufactured hardware or software selected to meet a P/P need; requires explicit HSI assessment beyond functional form/fit/function. (Ch 7)

**Crew Time KPP** — A Key Performance Parameter expressed as the percentage of crew work-day hours devoted to maintenance, preparation, and training; the premier HSI metric for space systems. (Ch 8)

**DR (Decommissioning Review)** — Late Phase E review confirming decision to terminate the system; HSI provides final TPM values and late-mission lessons learned. (Ch 6)

**DRR (Disposal Readiness Review)** — Phase F gate confirming readiness for final system disposal; HSI inputs address human-system aspects of decommissioning. (Ch 6)

**ETA (Engineering Technical Authority)** — TA function of the Agency's OCE, delegated from the Chief Engineer; primary TA for NPR 7123.1B and the SEHB. (Ch 2)

**FRR (Flight Readiness Review)** — Pre-launch Phase D milestone; HSI provides objective evidence of human-system acceptability in the operational mission context. (Ch 6)

**Function Allocation** — The iterative assignment of specific functions to hardware, software, or humans; the most critical early-phase HSI design decision, influencing LCC and operational risk. (Ch 3, Ch 8)

**HCD (Human-Centered Design)** — An iterative design approach ensuring systems are usable by meeting human needs, abilities, and limitations through frequent evaluations with users; the design methodology employed within HSI. (Ch 1)

**HFE (Human Factors Engineering)** — The central NASA HSI domain; applies knowledge of human capabilities and constraints to hardware/software design. Three principles: compatible demands, exploitation of capabilities in non-routine situations, tolerance of human error. (Ch 1)

**HIDH (Human Integration Design Handbook)** — NASA/SP-2010-3407R1; companion to NASA-STD-3001; provides HCD methods and techniques guidance. (Ch 1)

**HIDP (Human Integration Design Processes)** — NASA/TP-2014-218556; captures NASA human engineering lessons learned and complex HCD processes (e.g., net habitable volume); lists structured activities for human-centred design. (Ch 5, Ch 8)

**HITL (Human-in-the-Loop) Testing** — Empirical testing involving representative human subjects in realistic operational conditions; primary SEE 7/8 method; early HITL can be pre-declared for Phase D V&V credit. (Ch 3)

**HMTA (Health and Medical Technical Authority)** — TA function of the OCHMO; maintains NASA-STD-3001 and related documents; for human space flight, JSC CMO is HMTA for all centres. (Ch 2)

**HSIP / HSI Plan** — The systematic management document for applying HSI concepts to a P/P; may be standalone, a SEMP section, or part of project documents; updated at every major lifecycle milestone. (Ch 7, Ch 8, Ch 9)

**HSIPG (HSI Practitioner's Guide)** — NASA/SP-2015-3709; this document; primary NASA reference for implementing HSI within SE processes. (Ch 1)

**HSI (Human Systems Integration)** — An interdisciplinary management and technical process integrating human considerations into system acquisition and development to enhance design, reduce LCC, and optimise total system performance; formally defined in NPR 7123.1B (2013). (Ch 1)

**HSI Domains** — The six functional areas structuring NASA HSI work: Human Factors Engineering, Operations Resources, Maintainability and Supportability, Habitability and Environment, Safety, and Training. (Ch 1)

**Human Rating Certification Package** — Formal evidence package submitted to TAs for endorsement of end-product readiness for crewed operations; HSI provides human-system acceptability evidence. (Ch 6)

**IPT (Integrated Product Team)** — The primary collaboration vehicle within a P/P; HSI practitioners must participate from Phase A onward to provide timely human-centered design input. (Ch 2)

**KDP (Key Decision Point)** — A NASA governance gate (KDP A through F) authorising progression to the next lifecycle phase; HSI Plan updates and review participation are tied to each KDP. (Ch 3)

**KPP (Key Performance Parameter)** — A high-visibility program-level metric derived from overarching goals; must have defined threshold and objective values; crew time is the primary HSI KPP for space systems. (Ch 8)

**LCC (Life Cycle Cost)** — Total ownership cost from conception through disposal; a primary HSI value driver because LCC is locked in during early phases — inadequate early HSI leads to expensive operations-phase corrections. (Ch 1)

**MCR (Mission Concept Review)** — End-of-Pre-Phase-A milestone (KDP A for projects); HSI deliverables include initial ConOps draft, preliminary MOEs, initial function allocation, and HSI planning initiated. (Ch 4)

**MDR (Mission Design Review)** — The robotic mission equivalent of the SDR; Phase A milestone for robotic programs; HSI is applicable to robotic missions. (Ch 4)

**MOE (Measure of Effectiveness)** — Customer-satisfaction criteria established in Pre-Phase A under SEE 1/2; used to derive MOPs and TPMs in subsequent phases. (Ch 3)

**MOP (Measure of Performance)** — Engineering-level metric derived from MOEs; baselined by SRR; feeds TPM definition. (Ch 3)

**NPR 7120.5E** — NASA Space Flight Program and Project Management Requirements; defines KDPs, milestone reviews, P/P categories, and human rating requirements. (Ch 2)

**NPR 7123.1B** — NASA Systems Engineering Processes and Requirements; the primary SE regulation defining the 17 SEE processes, HSI as a required process, and the HSI Plan as a recommended deliverable. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**NPR 8705.2B** — Human-Rating Requirements for Space Systems; requires an HSI team to be formed before SRR for human-rated programs; source of the Human-Rated Product Maturity Matrix. (Ch 2)

**ORR (Operational Readiness Review)** — Pre-launch Phase D milestone confirming readiness for operations; HSI provides endorsement of all human-system aspects including training and certification. (Ch 6)

**PDR (Preliminary Design Review)** — Phase B milestone (KDP C for projects); HSI success criteria include agreed human requirements, complete requirement flow-down, sound ConOps, and completed trade studies. (Ch 5)

**PFAR (Post-Flight Assessment Review)** — Phase E review evaluating mission objective achievement; HSI describes human-system successes, lessons learned, and needed improvements. (Ch 6)

**PLAR (Post-Launch Assessment Review)** — Phase E review of readiness to proceed with full routine operations; HSI evaluates human-system readiness. (Ch 6)

**PRR (Production Readiness Review)** — Phase C review confirming readiness for full-scale production; HSI inputs include finalised manufacturing, logistics, and training plans. (Ch 5)

**SAR (System Acceptance Review)** — Phase D acceptance gate; HSI deliverables include full V&V reporting, validated analytical models, and operations support products. (Ch 6)

**SDR (System Design Review)** — Phase A KDP B milestone for projects; establishes baseline design. HSI requirements updated for all new human allocation since SRR. (Ch 4)

**SEE (Systems Engineering Engine)** — NASA's 17-process SE framework; HSI runs in lock-step synchronisation with the SEE. (Ch 3)

**SEMP (Systems Engineering Management Plan)** — Primary top-level technical management document; HSI Plan may be a standalone document or an embedded section within the SEMP. (Ch 2, Ch 7)

**SIR (System Integration Review)** — Phase C review confirming integrated HSI inputs have evolved from component-level to system-level inputs, ready for Phase D assembly. (Ch 5)

**SMA TA (Safety and Mission Assurance Technical Authority)** — TA function of the OSMA; manages NPR 8705.2B; requires HSI team formation before SRR for human-rated programs. (Ch 2)

**SME (Subject Matter Expert)** — A domain specialist (e.g., human factors, medical, radiation, acoustics) engaged by the HSI practitioner to provide depth of knowledge in a specific HSI domain. (Ch 2, Ch 8)

**SRR (System Requirements Review)** — First formal Phase A gate; HSI success criteria include baselined ConOps, HSI team established, HSI requirements addressing all domain areas, MOPs/TPMs established. (Ch 4)

**"Test as You Fly"** — NASA validation principle requiring that HITL validation events be conducted in conditions representative of actual operational use for results to count toward V&V closure. (Ch 6)

**TLI (Technical Leading Indicator)** — Program-level progress indicators ensuring proper advancement; defined and tracked per NASA/SP-2014-3705. (Ch 8)

**Total System Performance** — The measurable outcome of the integrated interaction of hardware, software, and human elements; the overarching HSI success criterion. (Ch 1)

**TPM (Technical Performance Measure)** — Engineering margin metric tracked from Phase A through Phase E; final TPM values reported at the Phase F DR. (Ch 3, Ch 8)

**TRR (Test Readiness Review)** — First Phase D milestone ensuring readiness for major system-level testing; HSI inputs include test objectives, requirements, plans, and procedures. (Ch 6)

**V&V (Verification and Validation)** — Verification (SEE 7): confirms the system meets HSI requirements. Validation (SEE 8): confirms the system meets the operational intent defined in the ConOps. Both are required and distinct. (Ch 3, Ch 8)
