# NASA R&M Standard Glossary

Key terms from NASA-STD-8729.1A — NASA's R&M (Reliability and Maintainability) standard for spaceflight and support systems (Revision A, 2017-06-13) — alphabetical, with chapter references. Definitions are paraphrased from the standard's Section 3 and surrounding text.

**Appendix A (R&M Objectives Hierarchy)** — the standard's flowchart-and-table catalog mapping each R&M objective and sub-objective to the strategies that accomplish it; the SMA Plan is expected to address it. (Ch 3, Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Appendix B (Scope Identification tables)** — the tabular view that sets, per bottom-level strategy, the suggested evidence methods and the minimum scope appropriate to each mission class. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Availability** — the degree to which a system is ready to perform when called upon; split by the standard into Inherent (Ai) and Operational (Ao) forms. (Ch 2)

**Criticality** — a program-defined measure of a failure's significance for mission performance, hazard, and maintenance cost; classifications are set by each program/project. (Ch 2)

**Dependability** — the umbrella property of avoiding service failures more frequent or more severe than acceptable. (Ch 2)

**Evidence (evidentiary method)** — the type of analysis or activity suggested to demonstrate that a bottom-level strategy's objective is satisfied; catalogued as R&M Evidentiary Methods. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Failure** — non-performance or incorrect performance of an intended function, often the manifestation of one or more faults; treated as permanent. (Ch 2)

**Failure cause / mechanism / mode / effect** — the failure causal chain: the underlying defect (cause), the degradation process (mechanism), the way failure shows up (mode), and the immediate operational consequence (effect). (Ch 2)

**Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)** — a bottom-up, inductive analysis identifying failure modes and their effects, typically used to find critical single failure points. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Failure Mode Effects and Criticality Analysis (FMECA)** — an FMEA extension adding a study of each failure mode's relative mission risk or criticality. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Failure tolerance** — the ability to keep functioning despite a specified number of coincident, independent failure causes. (Ch 2)

**Fault** — an undesired state or the immediate trigger of failure; envelops software and operational anomalies as well as hardware. (Ch 2)

**Fault management** — the engineering process for containing, preventing, detecting, isolating, diagnosing, responding to, and recovering from off-nominal conditions. (Ch 2)

**Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** — a deductive, top-down analysis estimating the probability of a defined top event and tracing all causes that lead to it. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Inherent Availability (Ai)** — availability considering only operating time and corrective maintenance, computed as MTTF / (MTTF + MTTR); rests on quantities the designer controls. (Ch 2)

**Level of Repair Analysis (LORA / ORLA)** — an analysis deciding the maintenance level at which components are replaced, repaired, or discarded, weighing economic and readiness constraints. (Ch 2)

**Maintainability** — a measure of how easily and quickly a system can be returned to operational status. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Mission class** — the risk classification (e.g., human spaceflight / Class A robotic at the top level) that sets how far evidence methods are scoped. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**NPR 7120.5** — the NASA program/project management requirements document that mandates the SMA Plan and defines the critical-technical-facility scope test; the lone applicable government document. (Ch 1, Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Objective (Objective block)** — a necessary characteristic or attribute the system or design must have; the Top Objective is the single highest-level goal. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Objectives-driven approach** — the standard's defining philosophy: mandate the R&M objectives and strategies, but leave method selection to the program. (Ch 1, Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Operational Availability (Ao)** — availability folding in logistics, ready, and administrative downtime plus preventive and corrective maintenance, computed as MTBF / (MTBF + MDT); the link between readiness and supportability. (Ch 2)

**Operational readiness** — the system's ability to respond and perform on demand. (Ch 2)

**Order of precedence** — the rule that the standard does not supersede established Agency requirements; conflicts are resolved by the responsible Technical Authority. (Ch 1)

**Redundancy** — using more than one independent means to achieve a function; the design mechanism behind failure tolerance. (Ch 2)

**Reliability** — the probability an item performs its intended function over a stated interval under stated conditions. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Reliability Block Diagram Analysis (RBDA)** — a deductive, top-down model in success space representing reliability/availability via series, parallel, and combined paths. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Reliability Centered Maintenance (RCM)** — an ongoing process settling the mix of corrective and preventive maintenance to hit a required reliability at minimum cost. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Risk** — the triplet of scenario(s) leading to degraded performance, their likelihood(s), and their consequence(s), with uncertainty folded in. (Ch 2)

**Risk acceptance** — the formal, documented decision not to mitigate a risk. (Ch 2)

**Scope** — the extent to which an evidence method should be performed; suggested at the top-level mission class and reduced for higher-risk classes. (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Single point failure** — an independent hardware, software, or human element whose failure causes loss of objectives, hardware, or crew. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**SMA Plan** — the Safety and Mission Assurance Plan (required by NPR 7120.5) into which R&M requirements are established; there is no separate freestanding R&M plan. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**SMA Technical Authority** — the assurance role that must concur the plan is sufficient, verify that independent evaluation happened, and concur in the evidence at review gates. (Ch 1, Ch 4)

**Strategy (Strategy block)** — the ways or methods used to accomplish a parent objective or sub-objective; tailorable guidance that sets the minimum expectation. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Sustainability / Sustainment** — maintaining the level and duration of logistics support, and providing the logistics and personnel services, to keep operations going to mission accomplishment. (Ch 2)

**Tailoring** — adjusting or seeking relief from a prescribed requirement (producing deviations and waivers per NPR 7120.5); the standard scales rigor to accepted risk. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Testability** — the design characteristic that lets you determine status (operable, inoperable, degraded) cost-effectively and with confidence. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Validation** — corroborating that the product reflects requirements and is acceptable to the user ("building the right system?"). (Ch 2)

**Verification** — determining whether a system meets its requirements ("building the system right?"). (Ch 2)

**Worst Case Analysis (WCA)** — analysis verifying performance under combined part variation, environment, and circuit variation. (Ch 6)
