# Chapter 6 — Evidentiary Methods: Reliability & Maintainability Analysis

## Core Idea
NASA-STD-8729.1A treats reliability and maintainability not as numbers to be claimed but as **objectives that must be earned with evidence**. This chapter's source slice is the standard's catalogue of *R&M Evidentiary Methods*: a set of analyses and engineering activities that have a track record on past programs and that generate the evidence supporting R&M objectives. The catalogue is split into two tables — **Reliability Analysis Methods** and **Maintainability Analysis Methods** — and every method is described along a common five-part skeleton: *what is done*, *why it is done*, *in what circumstances it is called for*, *when in the life cycle it is performed*, and the *NASA Preferred Reliability Practice* that documents the technique in depth. The unifying logic is objectives-driven: you do not run a method because it is on a list, you run it because it produces evidence that a specific R&M objective is being met, and the standard tells you the conditions under which each method is worth its cost.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **R&M Evidentiary Methods catalogue** — the section's organizing framework: a tabular reference of analyses and activities, each summarized along four cuts — the activity itself, its rationale, the conditions that call for it, and its timing within a program or project. *When/how to use:* as a menu for selecting the right evidence-generating activity for a given objective, life-cycle phase, and risk classification — not as a mandatory checklist.
- **The five-column method descriptor** — every method is captured as *Activity / What Is Done / Why It Is Done / In What Circumstances It Is Called For / When It Is Performed*, cross-referenced to a **NASA Preferred Reliability Practice** number. *When/how to use:* read across the row to decide whether a method applies to your situation before committing resources.
- **Reliability Analysis Methods table** — the reliability-side catalogue, spanning Alert Reporting, Approved Parts List, Availability Analysis, Human Error Risk Assessment, Human Factors Task Analysis, Deep Dielectric Charging / Internal ESD (IESD), FMEA/FMECA, Fault Tree Analysis (FTA), Ground Handling Analysis, Limited-Life Item Analysis, Micrometeoroid/Debris Analysis, Parts Control Plan, Parts Traceability, Part Electrical Stress Analysis (PSA), Physics of Failure Analysis, Reliability Assurance Plan, Reliability Modeling (Prediction/Allocation), Reliability Tradeoff Studies, Single Event Effects (SEE) Analysis, Sneak Circuit Analysis, Structural Stress Analysis, Surface Charging/ESD Analysis, Thermal Analysis of Electronic Assemblies to the Part Level, Thermal Stress/Fatigue Analysis, Trend Analysis, and Worst Case Analysis (WCA). *When/how to use:* draw from this table when the objective at stake is failure avoidance, fault tolerance, or quantified reliability performance.
- **Maintainability Analysis Methods table** — the maintainability-side catalogue, spanning Link Analysis, Logistics Support Analysis/Plan, Maintainability Modeling (Prediction/Allocation), Maintenance Concept, Maintenance Engineering Analysis, Maintenance Plan, Reliability Centered Maintenance (RCM), Testability Analysis, and Tradeoff Studies. *When/how to use:* draw from this table when the objective is repairability, supportability, readiness, or maintenance cost.
- **NASA Preferred Reliability Practices (by number)** — each method points to a named practice document (for example PT-TE-1428 for alert reporting, AT-3 for availability prediction, PD-AP-1316 for FMECA, PD-AP-1312 for the team approach to fault-tree analysis, PD-AP-1313 for reliability assessment using block diagramming, PD-AP-1314 for sneak circuit analysis, and AT-2 / PM-3 / PM-4 / the RCM practice on the maintainability side). *When/how to use:* the table is the index; the referenced practice is where the actual procedure lives.

## Key Concepts
- **Evidence over assertion.** Each method exists to produce defensible evidence for an R&M objective. The "why it is done" column is consistently about demonstrating something — fault-tolerant capability, single-point-failure absence, adequate design margin, supportability — rather than about completing a deliverable.
- **Conditional applicability is explicit.** The "in what circumstances" column is a gate. Availability Analysis is called for when operations-and-support costs dominate life-cycle cost or when supportability and readiness are major concerns. Sneak Circuit Analysis is reserved for the most safety- or mission-critical equipment. Micrometeoroid/Debris Analysis is invoked when impacts present a risk to safety or the mission. The standard expects you to check the gate before spending effort.
- **Timing is tied to design maturity.** Methods are sequenced against life-cycle phase. FMEA/FMECA starts as soon as a system block diagram exists and is updated through design and operations, with functional-level FMECAs in architectural design and piece-part FMECAs once candidate flight schematics are available. FTA runs during system design — for complex systems during architecture development, then refined for detailed design. Reliability Modeling is performed early to support FMECA and FTA quantification.
- **Single-point-failure and fault-tolerance evidence.** FMECA's stated purpose includes evaluating fault-tolerant capability such as redundancy, identifying single-point failures needing corrective action, identifying critical items, and supporting failure/anomaly triage during an anomaly.
- **Design-margin verification runs through the thermal/stress family.** Part Electrical Stress Analysis verifies derating margin at anticipated part temperature during qualification; Thermal Analysis to the part level verifies thermal margin across the path from control surface to heat source; Structural Stress Analysis and Thermal Stress/Fatigue Analysis build in and verify margin against fatigue, fracture, and thermal cycling on solder joints, coatings, and other critical materials. Worst Case Analysis verifies performance under combined part variation, environment, and circuit variation.
- **The space-environment cluster.** Several reliability methods are specific to the spaceflight threat environment: Deep Dielectric Charging / IESD (energetic-electron environments), Surface Charging/ESD Analysis (differential charging of nonconductive surfaces), Single Event Effects Analysis (high-energy particle upsets, latchups, gate ruptures), and Micrometeoroid/Debris Analysis (particle-collision severity and frequency under a mission profile).
- **Parts pedigree as a reliability control.** Approved Parts List, Parts Control Plan, and Parts Traceability form a chain: restrict parts to those with known failure rates and lifetimes, control how part lots and characteristics are managed, and trace pedigree from manufacturer to user so a failure can be traced to its source and production lot.
- **Maintainability is predicted, modeled, and planned — not assumed.** Maintainability Modeling estimates mean-time-to-repair requirements through prediction and allocation; the Maintenance Concept establishes the overall approach to meeting operational, logistics, and maintenance objectives; the Maintenance Plan details how the support program is conducted; and Logistics Support Analysis assesses required logistic support and feeds back to influence system design.
- **RCM balances maintenance types against cost.** Reliability Centered Maintenance determines the mix of corrective and preventive practices that delivers the required reliability at minimum cost, using diagnostic tools and measurements to judge when a component is near failure and should be replaced — and the standard notes a Maintenance Plan may substitute for it on smaller programs where maintainability prediction and analysis are not required.
- **Trend Analysis spans the life cycle and changes purpose by phase.** It forecasts future events from past results; before launch it assesses system or process maturity in terms of failure/anomaly occurrences, quality processes, and delivery; in flight it tracks system health and estimates future performance.
- **Tradeoff studies appear on both sides.** Reliability Tradeoff Studies and the maintainability Tradeoff Studies both compare realistic alternative design approaches against cost, schedule, risk, and performance impacts — the method is the same shape, the objective differs (reliability characteristics versus the preferred support/maintenance approach).

## Mental Models
- **The method is the instrument; the objective is the measurement.** Picture each method as a sensor pointed at one R&M objective. You choose the sensor by asking what objective you need evidence for, then check the "circumstances" gate to confirm the sensor is worth deploying here.
- **Read the row, not the title.** A method's name tells you little; the five columns tell you whether it applies, when to run it, and where the detailed procedure lives. The standard is built to be queried row-wise.
- **Evidence accumulates along the V.** Early-phase methods (Approved Parts List, Maintenance Concept, functional FMECA, FTA during architecture) seed the objectives; later-phase methods (piece-part FMECA, PSA, thermal-to-part-level, WCA on candidate flight designs) confirm them against the maturing design. The same objective is touched repeatedly at increasing fidelity.
- **Gate before you spend.** Because R&M analysis competes for limited resources, the "in what circumstances" column is the cost-control mechanism: high-risk classification may justify less formality, and the cheapest, always-on methods (PSA is noted as part of the basic electronics design process and low-cost) are distinguished from the reserved-for-critical ones (Sneak Circuit Analysis).
- **Parts pedigree is a backward-traceable chain.** When a part fails, traceability lets you walk from the failed unit back to manufacturer and production lot — so reliability evidence is not just predictive but forensic.

## Key Takeaways
1. The section is a **two-table catalogue** of R&M evidentiary methods — Reliability Analysis Methods and Maintainability Analysis Methods — each method described by what/why/circumstances/when plus a referenced NASA Preferred Reliability Practice.
2. Methods are **objectives-driven**: you select one because it generates evidence for a specific R&M objective, not because it is mandatory.
3. The **"in what circumstances" column is a gate** — applicability depends on cost drivers, risk classification, criticality, and the threat environment; many methods are explicitly reserved for safety- or mission-critical hardware.
4. **Timing is keyed to design maturity** — FMECA, FTA, reliability modeling, and the thermal/stress/WCA family all sequence against block diagrams, architecture, and candidate flight designs.
5. The **reliability table** centers on failure avoidance, fault tolerance, single-point-failure elimination, design-margin verification, parts pedigree control, and the space-environment threats (IESD, surface ESD, SEE, micrometeoroid/debris).
6. The **maintainability table** centers on predicting and allocating MTTR, defining the maintenance concept and plan, logistics support, testability, and RCM's cost-optimal maintenance mix.
7. Every method **points to a NASA Preferred Reliability Practice** by number — the table indexes the method; the practice holds the procedure.

## Connects To
- **The objectives-driven R&M framework (this pack's earlier chapters):** these methods are the evidence-generating layer beneath the R&M objectives — the analyses that earn the objective claims rather than assert them.
- **FMEA/FMECA and Fault Tree Analysis (reliability core):** the two anchor analyses for fault tolerance, single-point-failure identification, and qualitative/quantitative cause assessment — both cross-referenced to NASA Preferred Reliability Practices (PD-AP-1316, PD-AP-1312).

- **NASA NPR 7123 / DoD & NASA SE process packs:** these R&M analyses are the discipline-specific evidence feeding the broader systems-engineering technical processes — design, verification, and technical reviews — where R&M objectives are assessed against the maturing design.
