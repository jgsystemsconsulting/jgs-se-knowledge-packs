# Chapter — R&M Terminology & Core Definitions

## Core Idea

NASA-STD-8729.1A grounds its reliability and maintainability (R&M) practice on a shared, precise vocabulary before it asks anyone to plan an activity. Section 3 of the standard supplies that vocabulary: a list of acronyms and a definition set that the document explicitly says exists to support planning R&M work and interpreting the R&M considerations laid out in its Appendix A. In other words, the terms are not encyclopedic trivia — they are the working language in which the standard's objectives-driven framework is expressed.

The most important thing to internalize from this slice is that the standard is **objectives-driven** rather than activity-mandating. The definitions reflect this: terms like *availability*, *risk*, *dependability*, *operational readiness*, and *sustainability* describe outcomes a program is trying to achieve, while terms like *FMEA*, *FTA*, *RBDA*, and *RCM* describe the tools and processes used to get there. The vocabulary draws a clean line between the goals (what good looks like) and the means (how you analyze and assure it). Reading the definitions this way — outcome words versus method words — is the mental key to the entire standard.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

The definitions section names a coherent set of analysis frameworks and methodologies. Each is introduced as a defined term, with its character (inductive vs. deductive, failure-space vs. success-space, top-down vs. bottom-up) called out:

- **Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)** — introduced as a bottom-up, inductive, methodical analysis that identifies and documents failure modes at a chosen level and states the resulting effect of each. The standard notes it is typically used to find critical single failure points in hardware and that, relative to formal hazard analyses, it sits as a subsidiary analysis.
- **Failure Mode Effects and Criticality Analysis (FMECA)** — introduced as an extension of FMEA: the same examination of how failures occur and their effects across system elements and the mission, plus a study of each potential failure mode's relative mission risk or criticality.
- **Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** — introduced as a deductive reliability tool yielding both qualitative and quantitative measures of failure probability; it estimates the chance of a defined top event, traces all causes that lead to it, and records the analysis as a baseline for later studies of design alternatives.
- **Reliability Block Diagram Analysis (RBDA)** — introduced as a deductive, top-down method that builds a symbolic-logic model in *success space*, representing reliability and availability relationships among the system and its elements through series, parallel, and combined configurations. Successful operation corresponds to an unbroken path from model input to output.
- **Reliability Centered Maintenance (RCM)** — introduced as an ongoing process that settles the blend of corrective and preventive maintenance needed to hit a required reliability at minimum cost, using diagnostics to act before failure.
- **Level of Repair Analysis (LORA)** — introduced as an analytical method (also called Optimum Repair Level Analysis, ORLA) for shaping maintenance concepts and deciding the repair level at which components are replaced, repaired, or discarded, weighing economic and readiness constraints.
- **Reliability Analyses** — introduced as the umbrella concept: a set of conceptual tools, with FMEA and FTA cited as failure-space examples and RBDA as a success-space example.

The standard also names the acronyms for related analyses and constructs without full definitions in this slice — including FRACAS (Failure Reporting and Corrective Action System), PRA (Probabilistic Risk Assessment), PRACA (Problem Reporting and Corrective Action System), WCA (Worst Case Analysis), PSA (Parts Stress Analysis), MTA (Maintenance Task Analysis), HALT, HAST, and ESS (Environmental Stress Screening) — signaling the broader toolset the considerations in Appendix A draw upon.

## Key Concepts

**The reliability / maintainability / availability triad.** *Reliability* is defined as the probability that an item performs its intended function over a stated interval under stated conditions, with the note that a top-level reliability value can be apportioned across subfunctions. *Maintainability* is defined as a measure of how easily and quickly a system can be returned to operational status, shaped by design, installation, skilled personnel, procedures, test equipment, and the maintenance environment. *Availability* ties the two together and is split into two precise variants:

- **Inherent Availability (Ai)** — operational capability considering only operating time and corrective maintenance time, excluding logistics, administrative, and preventive-maintenance downtime. It derives from engineering design analysis and is computed as MTTF / (MTTF + MTTR). The standard stresses that it rests on quantities the *designer* controls.
- **Operational Availability (Ao)** — the broader figure that folds in logistics time, ready time, administrative/waiting downtime, and both preventive and corrective maintenance. It is computed as MTBF / (MTBF + MDT). The standard frames Ao as the quantitative bridge between readiness objectives and supportability, extending availability to factors the logisticians and mission planners control (such as how many spares and how close they are).

That Ai/Ao split is one of the most load-bearing distinctions in the chapter: the same word, *availability*, means something different depending on whether you are answering to the designer's world or the logistician's world.

**The failure causal chain.** The definitions assemble a vocabulary of failure that flows from cause to consequence: a *failure cause* is the underlying defect in design, process, quality, or part application; a *fault* is an undesired state or the immediate trigger of failure (and notably envelops the word "failure," since faults also cover software and operational anomalies); a *failure mechanism* is the physical, chemical, electrical, or thermal degradation process; a *failure mode* is the particular way a failure shows up (short, open, fracture, excessive wear), independent of why; a *failure* is non-performance or incorrect performance of an intended function, often the manifestation of one or more faults and treated as permanent; and a *failure effect* is the immediate operational consequence. Two propagation terms extend the chain across boundaries: *failure propagation* (a failure inside a product triggering failures outside it) and *fault propagation* (effects of one fault spilling into other faults and potentially failures).

**Risk as a triplet.** Risk in mission execution is defined operationally as a set of three things: the scenario(s) that lead to degraded performance, the likelihood(s) of those scenarios, and the consequence(s) if they occur — with uncertainties folded into the likelihood and consequence evaluation. The surrounding terms operationalize this: *risk management* (an organized decision process that identifies, analyzes, plans, tracks, controls, communicates, and documents risk), *risk reduction* (modifying a process or system to lower probability, severity, or uncertainty, or to shift timing), and *risk acceptance* (the formal, documented decision *not* to mitigate a risk).

**Severity, criticality, and the failure-significance terms.** *Criticality* and *severity* are both defined as measures of a failure's significance for mission performance, hazards to material or personnel, and maintenance cost — and the standard is explicit that programs and projects set their own criticality and severity definitions and classifications. A *single point failure* is an independent hardware, software, or human element whose failure causes loss of objectives, hardware, or crew. *Failure tolerance* is the ability to keep functioning despite a specified number of coincident, independent failure causes, and *redundancy* (using more than one independent means to achieve a function) is the design mechanism behind it.

**Fault management and maintenance support concepts.** *Fault management* is defined as the engineering process for containing, preventing, detecting, isolating, diagnosing, responding to, and recovering from conditions that interfere with nominal operations; *fault isolation* narrows down a fault's approximate location. On the maintenance side, *maintenance* covers all actions to retain or restore an item to a specified condition, *spares* are maintenance replacements for parts or assemblies, *support equipment* is what keeps systems operable in the field, and *testability* is the design characteristic that lets you determine status (operable, inoperable, degraded) cost-effectively and with confidence.

**Logistics and readiness outcomes.** Several terms describe sustainment-level goals: *operational readiness* (the system's ability to respond and perform on demand), *sustainability* (maintaining the level and duration of logistics support to meet objectives), and *sustainment* (providing the logistics and personnel services to keep operations going to mission accomplishment). *Dependability* sits above these as the umbrella property — the ability to avoid service failures that are more frequent or more severe than acceptable.

**V&V and the program scaffolding.** The slice also fixes the standard's verification/validation language: *verification* asks "are we building the system right?" (determining whether a system meets its requirements), while *validation* asks "are we building the right system?" (corroborating that the product accurately reflects requirements and is acceptable to the user). Surrounding management terms — *program*, *project*, *milestone*, *review*, *requirements*, *task*, *contract*, *contractor*, *supplier*, and *tailoring* — anchor the organizational context in which R&M activities are planned.

## Mental Models

**Outcome words vs. method words.** Sort every defined term into one of two buckets. Outcome words (reliability, availability, dependability, operational readiness, sustainability, maintainability) describe the goal state the program is steering toward. Method words (FMEA, FMECA, FTA, RBDA, RCM, LORA) describe how you analyze and assure your way there. This sorting reflects the standard's objectives-driven posture — it cares first about what you must achieve, then about the analyses that serve those objectives.

**Failure space vs. success space.** The analysis frameworks split along this axis, and the definitions make it explicit. FMEA and FTA work in *failure space* — they reason about how things break and how those breakages propagate. RBDA works in *success space* — it models the conditions under which the system keeps working, treating an unbroken input-to-output path as success. When choosing a tool, first ask which space the question lives in.

**Inductive bottom-up vs. deductive top-down.** A second axis cuts the methods. FMEA is inductive and bottom-up: start at the part, build up to the effect. FTA and RBDA are deductive and top-down: start from a defined top-level event (failure for FTA, success for RBDA) and decompose downward. Knowing the direction tells you what you'll discover and what you must already know to begin.

**The two availabilities are two stakeholders.** Whenever "availability" appears, ask: designer's world or logistician's world? Inherent availability (MTTF/(MTTF+MTTR)) lives entirely inside the designer's control. Operational availability (MTBF/(MTBF+MDT)) folds in spares, logistics, and administrative delay — the logistician's and mission planner's world. The gap between the two numbers is precisely the slack that supportability decisions, not design decisions, govern.

**The failure causal chain as a left-to-right pipeline.** Read failure terms as a flow: cause → fault → mechanism → mode → failure → effect, with propagation terms describing how that flow crosses product boundaries. Locating an observed problem on this pipeline tells you whether you are looking at a root issue, an intermediate state, or a downstream symptom — and which analysis (FMEA, FTA) to point at it.

## Key Takeaways

- The standard front-loads a shared vocabulary because the whole objectives-driven framework is written in it; the definitions exist specifically to support planning R&M activities and interpreting the Appendix A considerations.
- Availability is two distinct quantities: **Ai = MTTF / (MTTF + MTTR)** (designer-controlled) and **Ao = MTBF / (MTBF + MDT)** (the link between readiness objectives and supportability, including logistics and spares).
- The named analysis frameworks divide cleanly: FMEA/FMECA (bottom-up, inductive, failure space), FTA (deductive, failure space), RBDA (deductive top-down, success space), with RCM and LORA covering maintenance strategy and repair-level economics.
- The failure vocabulary forms a causal chain — cause, fault, mechanism, mode, failure, effect — and propagation terms describe how problems cross product boundaries.
- Risk is defined as a triplet of scenario, likelihood, and consequence, with uncertainty built into the last two; risk management, reduction, and acceptance are the actions taken around it.
- Criticality and severity are program-defined: the standard deliberately leaves their classifications to each program/project, reinforcing the tailoring-and-objectives stance.
- Verification ("building the system right?") and validation ("building the right system?") are sharply separated questions, not interchangeable terms.
