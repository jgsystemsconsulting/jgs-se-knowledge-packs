# Chapter 1: Introduction & DMSMS Fundamentals

## Core Idea
DMSMS — Diminished Manufacturing Sources and Material Shortages — is the loss, or coming loss, of the manufacturers and suppliers of the items, raw materials, and software a system depends on. Because the technology lifecycle is short while defense systems are long-lived, these losses are not anomalies to be eliminated but an inevitability to be managed. SD-22 frames DMSMS management as a risk-based, multidisciplinary process that runs across the entire system lifecycle: identify obsolescence-driven issues, assess their potential impact on schedule and readiness, analyze mitigation options, and implement the most cost-effective one. The discipline exists for a simple reason — it protects systems, and proactive management is the cheapest, most efficient way to blunt the cost, schedule, performance, and ultimately readiness impacts of obsolescence.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The five-step DMSMS management process (Prepare, Identify, Assess, Analyze, Implement)** — the spine of the entire guidebook; the document itself is organized around these five steps, each detailed in its own section (Sections 3 through 7).
  - When to use: across the whole lifecycle, from early technology development through sustainment. The steps are tailorable and can be entered at any point, though starting early is best. They form a repeating cadence — resolve one issue, move to the next, and when the list is exhausted, begin again because the market will have shifted.
- **"Strategize" as an encompassing sixth activity** — depicted in Figure 3 as a circle around the five steps, because strategic processes (system-modification planning, plus using evaluation results to improve the program's own effectiveness) relate to all five and raise the odds of low-cost resolutions while delaying or preventing issues.
  - When to use: continuously, as the overlay that ties the five steps into deliberate planning rather than reactive firefighting.
- **First-order vs. lower-order (derived) obsolescence** — the two broad DMSMS mechanisms. First-order is driven directly by market forces and regulation changes affecting DoD's ability to buy, license, support, or use hardware and software. Lower-order is *functional* obsolescence caused by the changes made to resolve first-order issues, to perform a proactive upgrade, or to meet a new requirement.
  - When to use: when diagnosing why an item is failing — to distinguish a supplier/market loss from a downstream incompatibility introduced by your own change.
- **Hardware/software symmetry of mechanisms** — Figure 2 presents first- and lower-order mechanisms as symmetrical across hardware and software, deliberately drawing "monitoring" and "resolution identification" as single shared boxes to stress that hardware and software obsolescence are one process and can occur simultaneously, not in sequence.
  - When to use: when scoping monitoring so software is not treated as a separate or lesser concern than hardware.

## Key Concepts
- **DMSMS issue**: the loss, or impending loss, of the manufacturers or suppliers of items, raw materials, or software — triggered when a source discontinues production or support, or when a raw material is no longer available.
- **Obsolescence vs. DMSMS**: nearly synonymous, used interchangeably in the guidebook. The small difference: DMSMS also covers items not technically obsolete but in short supply, and obsolete items out of production that are still in demand. Because the management *process* is identical, the terms are distinguished only when clarity demands it.
- **Universality of the problem**: every system is susceptible. DMSMS spans short- and long-lived systems; reparables and consumables; and space, air, ground, and sea equipment, including support and test gear. It is not confined to piece parts — obsolescence can occur at the part, module, component, equipment, or system level.
- **Beyond electronics**: although traditionally seen as an electronics problem, DMSMS can hit any item, including software and MaSME (materials and structural, mechanical, and electrical) components. COTS items are a major obsolescence source precisely because they are most exposed to market forces. MaSME items behave differently from electronics — they stay on the market longer and are often repairable — but are still vulnerable to hazardous-material regulation (e.g., RoHS), supplier exit, broken business cases, supply-constrained or restricted specialty metals (10 U.S.C. restrictions), and loss of tooling.
- **Functional obsolescence**: an item still available commercially that no longer works as intended because of hardware, software, or requirements changes elsewhere in the system. This is the heart of lower-order obsolescence — e.g., a COTS operating-system upgrade that breaks existing firmware, or legacy software that fails on new hardware.
- **Software is license-and-support bound**: the ability to use software is tied to holding a license and receiving support. When support ends, security patches stop, bugs go unfixed, and no further modifications are possible — so software obsolescence is as operationally serious as hardware obsolescence.
- **DMSMS objectives during the lifecycle**: build resilience in during design; keep any unavoidable out-of-cycle redesign as small in scope as possible; drive obsolescence-driven hits to the production schedule to zero; and stop the discipline's namesake shortages from degrading readiness.
- **Not a standalone function**: DMSMS management is inherently linked to reliability, maintainability, supportability, and availability, and benefits many stakeholders rather than one. The intended audience extends beyond practitioners to PMs, engineers, product support managers, and life-cycle logisticians.
- **Design enablers of resilience**: modular open systems architecture (standardized, identified major interfaces makes swapping in alternative technologies easier) plus secured data rights and technical data for items likely to face DMSMS — these enable competition and make technology refreshment feasible.

## Mental Models
- **Manage the inevitable, don't try to eliminate it.** DMSMS issues are unavoidable and can strike at any lifecycle point; the goal is resilience and cheap recovery, not a fantasy of zero obsolescence.
- **Short-tech-clock vs. long-system-clock.** The root driver is the mismatch between technology's fast lifecycle and a defense system's long one — a Black Hawk slated for sustainment to 2075 will outlive many generations of its parts.
- **Your own fixes breed new problems.** First-order resolutions and proactive upgrades can *create* lower-order obsolescence; every change is also a potential new obsolescence source.
- **Proactive beats reactive, with hard dollars behind it.** The guidebook's case examples make the payoff concrete: THAAD's one-time seeker redesign avoided roughly $100M in redundant future redesign; a Black Hawk DMT closed ~900 cases for over $156M in cost avoidance; a C-130 enterprise team's risk-based schedule analysis helped secure more than $700M in appropriated funding; the Navy resolves 3,000+ cases yearly with $100M+ annual cost avoidance.
- **The cost of doing nothing is a number too.** The counter-example: a contract that failed to require the contractor to manage obsolescence for future lots produced 19 DMSMS contract actions and $40M in resolution costs — proactive practice (six-month health assessments, supplier audits, refresh trade studies) followed.
- **DMSMS results should drive technology refreshment.** Technology refreshment is a planned, periodic change of items to keep them accessible; the ultimate aim is to replace items *before* they go obsolete.

## Anti-patterns
- **Letting a DMSMS situation reach mission impact.** Allowing obsolescence to progress until items are unavailable and a system goes non-mission-capable is contrary to DoD policy and a marker of both ineffective DMSMS management and poor sustainment planning.
- **Treating obsolescence as purely reactive / late.** The Air Force-led joint program example shows the cost of omitting proactive obsolescence obligations from the contract: the contractor delivered units but had no duty to preserve future production capacity, so pop-up obsolete parts threatened the ramp to full-rate production.
- **Designing closed, non-modular systems.** The 2022 National Defense Strategy excerpt cited by SD-22 flags that systems not designed as open create longer-term obsolescence, interoperability, and cost-effectiveness challenges — closed design leaves little room to incorporate new technology.

## Key Takeaways
1. A **DMSMS issue** is the loss or impending loss of sources for items, materials, or software; every system is susceptible, across all domains, levels of assembly, and item types — including software and MaSME, not just electronics.
2. DMSMS management is a **risk-based, multidisciplinary, lifecycle-long** process with five tailorable steps — **Prepare, Identify, Assess, Analyze, Implement** — wrapped by a continuous **Strategize** overlay.
3. The root driver is the mismatch between **short technology lifecycles and long defense-system lifecycles**; obsolescence is inevitable and must be managed, not wished away.
4. Mechanisms split into **first-order** (market/regulation-driven loss of sources) and **lower-order/derived** (functional obsolescence caused by your own changes), and they apply symmetrically and simultaneously to hardware and software.
5. **Proactive, robust management protects systems and saves real money** — the case studies show hundreds of millions in cost avoidance, while neglect drives escalating cost and readiness loss.
6. DMSMS is **not standalone**: it is bound to reliability, maintainability, supportability, and availability, and enabled by **modular open systems architecture, secured data rights, and planned technology refreshment**.
