# Chapter — Assess: Resolution Need, Timing & Level

## Core Idea
The **Assess** step of a DMSMS management program weighs the potential effect a diminishing-manufacturing-sources-and-material-shortages (DMSMS) issue, at any level of a system, could have on **cost, schedule, availability, and readiness**. Most obsolescence issues produce a blend of these effects, and — left unresolved — eventually trigger all of them. Assessment exists to answer three decision questions before resources are committed: (1) Should a resolution be pursued at all (should a case be opened)? (2) Which problem comes first / when should the resolution start? (3) At what level of assembly should the resolution be applied? Note the scope caveat for this whole step: it lives in **sustainment/obsolescence** decision-making, where availability and readiness impacts normally surface and where data-driven timing analysis governs whether — and when — a fix is worth doing.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The three Assess questions (need / timing-priority / level)** — the source frames the entire step as answering "should a resolution be pursued," "which problem first / when," and "at what level of assembly." Used as the organizing spine of Section 5; each subsection maps to one question.
- **Four assessment data categories** — *programmatic*, *availability*, *criticality*, and *logistics* data, plus the output of an item availability analysis, feed the assessment and ultimately the resolution choice. Used when gathering inputs; data is refreshed as monitored systems change and as EOL notices or refreshed predictive-tool/vendor-survey results arrive.
- **The "when NOT to pursue a resolution" situations (Situations 1–4)** — four risk patterns the source uses to decide a resolution may be unnecessary: no historical demand in sustainment (Situation 1); enough on-hand inventory to reach retirement or a tech refresh (Situation 2); reclamation can recover enough stock (Situation 3); and the design/production phase, where a constant supply is normally needed and the exception (everything already procured) carries enormous uncertainty (Situation 4). Used to bound the resolution-need question by identifying low-risk cases.
- **The Health Assessment** — a structured, by-year accounting of starting balance, predicted/actual usage, and ending balance per item over a horizon (e.g., ten years), reported in a tailorable template (the source's Table 11). Used after the design matures and then regularly through development, production, and sustainment to estimate when an item can no longer satisfy demand. The source notes the community has not standardized the name; equivalents include obsolescence/DMSMS impact assessment, tombstone charts, sustainability assessment, supportability analysis, and operational impact assessment.
- **The Appendix K methodology** — the fuller calculation that extends a basic health assessment from the *component* view to the *system* view, recommended as a contractual requirement on primes/OEMs/sustainment providers (because DoD may lack visibility into contractor-held stock). Used to estimate when a DMSMS issue affects the **system**, not merely the part, and to inform the level-of-resolution decision.
- **Supportability progression model** — the source's Figure 18 logic showing how a component that can no longer be procured drives failures upward: component failure forces assembly replacement, assembly depletion forces unit replacement, and unit exhaustion finally degrades the system/subsystem MICAP rate. Used to explain *why* timing matters and to locate where the impact lands.
- **The four-step prioritization process (piece-part / CCA / black-box)** — a ranking-and-adjustment procedure for circuit-card obsolescence, extensible by analogy to mechanical and COTS assemblies. Used to answer "which problem first." Step 1 ranks by obsolete-part counts and adjusts by days of supply / demand; step 2 adjusts for near-term obsolescence risk and number of sources; step 3 adjusts for maintenance philosophy; step 4 adjusts for planned product improvements or modernization.
- **The five level-of-resolution factors** — how many (and how hard) the DMSMS problems sitting in a given assembly are; how reliable the items at that level are; the cost of repair set against redesign or replacement; how much remaining life the neighboring items have; and whether a redesign or replacement could also raise mission capability. Used to decide whether to fix an item or to redesign/replace its next-higher assembly (NHA).

## Key Concepts
- **The three impact types in sustainment.** *Cost* impacts can appear in any life-cycle stage and are measured as the spend to resolve, the change in support cost, and the price difference between the item before and after resolution (which may rise or fall). *Schedule* impacts attach mainly to design/production phases or to sustainment-phase modifications, because obsolescence can delay design or manufacturing. *Availability and readiness* impacts normally occur in sustainment and can reduce — or entirely prevent — mission capability.
- **Assessment is a joint effort.** The DMSMS community does not answer the three questions alone. Program-office and prime/OEM logisticians supply the inventory and demand data needed to judge *when* an impact lands; where demand data are not field-based, program-office and prime/OEM engineers must assess reliability. Determining the resolution level draws on engineering judgment about feasibility/complexity plus DMSMS knowledge of other items in the higher assemblies.
- **What triggers an assessment.** Predictive-tool or vendor-survey results flagging a problem; receipt of a Product Discontinuance Notice (PDN); or a change in a health assessment caused by rising demand for already-obsolete inventory.
- **Data quality drives confidence.** Best practice is to gather as much data as possible to raise analytic rigor, but data are often incomplete; the DMT does the best it can and, where it substitutes assumptions, the result carries greater uncertainty. Data collection also varies by acquisition phase: early design may rely on notional, preferred-parts-list data and predicted reliability, while actual logistics data exist only in sustainment.
- **Programmatic data inputs.** Life-cycle phase (obsolescence found in design/production drives total ownership cost more than an issue found near disposal); planned and *funded* technology insertions/refreshments for the item and its NHA (DMSMS leverages but does not drive refresh); planned end of system life (used in inventory math, but uncertain because of service-life extensions that can turn a previously harmless issue into an operational one); number of systems in use over time; and planned average operating hours (used to compute demand).
- **Availability, criticality, and logistics data.** Availability is identified at the lowest practical level with impact rolled up to higher levels; the DMT separates currently-unavailable items from those forecast obsolete within ~2–3 years, and notes that authorized substitutes remove immediate risk. Criticality reflects mission/safety priority, the criticality of an item's function (microprocessors, microcontrollers, memory, ASICs, FPGAs are cited examples), and item cost. Logistics data include item cost, demand, reliability, inventory (with the system's share separated from other platforms'), maintenance philosophy, repair history, survival rates (fraction of time repair is economical), and wearout rates (fraction of time repair is futile because the item would fail quickly after return to service).
- **Obsolescence does not equal an issue requiring action.** A predictive tool calling an item obsolete (now or by a date) does not by itself justify pursuing a resolution. The program office first validates risk against availability timing, stock on hand, and expected time to implement a fix — the latter being especially important for a **MaSME** item, where a long resolution lead time may justify buying extra inventory or pre-positioning an action to shorten that lead time even before the item is obsolete.
- **Software and material behave differently from hardware.** Hardware-electronic and MaSME stockpiles can buy time, so a resolution may not be needed depending on days of supply. By contrast, **loss of a software license** has an immediate impact, and a resolution should be pursued when the software is mission/safety critical; an information-assurance lapse is also immediate, because the software cannot be used without a waiver. **Loss of software support** is more nuanced: unchanged, error-free software may run safely to end of system life as long as its underlying layers are sustained, but requalification after a software change can exceed that after a hardware change because of the testing involved. Firmware changes hinge on the risk of altering a functional group, and material **environmental-compliance** issues hinge on how long the host item remains available.
- **From component impact to system impact.** A health assessment assumes spares are stocked at every level; if a level is unstocked, that step in the progression disappears and impact arrives sooner. Estimating future shortage requires projecting future demand (using accepted logistics math at the component or NHA level) against existing stock plus due-ins and planned procurements — a calculation whose two riskiest assumptions are projected operating hours and reliability, with risk always highest pre-deployment when no field reliability data exist.
- **Level-of-resolution economics.** When one item is among many with DMSMS issues in a hierarchy, replacing or redesigning the assembly may beat fixing each item. The decision is largely cost arithmetic (if the cost of many item-level fixes exceeds an NHA redesign, redesign is considered), complicated by the fact that a single item may sit in multiple NHAs — so the cheapest answer can be a *mix* of item-level and higher-level resolutions. Software level-of-resolution is harder: hardware impact is roughly linear up the hierarchy, but software has non-linear secondary/tertiary effects and far-reaching dependencies, so it is decided case by case.

## Mental Models
- **Assess is a gate, not a reflex.** The point of the step is to *avoid* spending on resolutions that days-of-supply, reclamation, or an imminent retirement/refresh make unnecessary — and to time the ones that are necessary. "Obsolete" is an input; "issue worth resolving" is a conclusion.
- **Days of supply is the clock.** A snapshot of inventory and usage rates yields the window available to identify and implement a fix while a replacement item still exists. The risk of a negative impact is the race between that window and the time to field a resolution.
- **Push the analysis up the hierarchy until the impact lands on the system.** Component-level obsolescence only matters once it propagates — component → assembly → unit → system/subsystem — so the health assessment's job is to find *when* the issue reaches the system, not just the part.
- **Match the rigor to the data you actually have.** In design/production, reliability stands in for demand and contractor stock stands in for inventory; in sustainment, field demand and organic logistics data exist. The same four-step prioritization runs in both, but its inputs (and the weight of its later steps) shift with phase.
- **Software flips the default.** For hardware you can often wait on a stockpile; for a software *license* or IA lapse you generally cannot — address it immediately, then let stable underlying layers determine how long an unsupported-but-working binary can persist.

## Anti-patterns
- **Recording demand only at the assembly level.** The source explicitly names this a **bad practice**: when a depot-level reparable (DLR) is repaired but the specific components swapped out are not recorded, demand for individual components is masked, which inhibits data-driven DMSMS management (discussed further in the source's Appendix K.2).
- **Treating a predictive-tool "obsolete" flag as an automatic resolution trigger.** The source warns that obsolescence — current or forecast — does not by itself translate into an issue a program office must resolve; risk must be validated first against availability timing, stock, and resolution lead time.

## Key Takeaways
1. Assess decides three things — **whether** to resolve, **when/which first**, and **at what level** — by weighing cost, schedule, availability, and readiness, primarily in the sustainment/obsolescence context.
2. The decision rests on four data families — **programmatic, availability, criticality, logistics** — refreshed as systems change; missing data is filled with assumptions that raise uncertainty.
3. A resolution may be unneeded when on-hand stock, reclamation, low/absent demand, or an imminent retirement/refresh covers projected need (Situations 1–4) — but software-license, IA, and firmware-functional-group issues usually demand immediate action.
4. The **health assessment** (basic, per the Table 11 template) plus the **Appendix K methodology** translate component-level balances into a system-level timeline of when the issue actually bites; best practice is to make these a contractual deliverable from primes/OEMs.
5. Prioritization uses a **four-step ranking** (obsolete-part counts → near-term risk/sources → maintenance philosophy → planned modernization), adaptable from circuit cards to COTS and mechanical assemblies, and skippable when an external organization will resolve a common item.
6. Level-of-resolution turns on **five factors** and on cost arithmetic — fix the item or redesign the NHA, often a blend across levels — with software requiring case-by-case judgment because its dependencies are non-linear and far-reaching.
