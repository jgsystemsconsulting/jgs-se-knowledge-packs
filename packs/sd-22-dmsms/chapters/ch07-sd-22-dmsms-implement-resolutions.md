# Chapter 7 — Implement: Implementation of DMSMS Resolutions

## Core Idea
Picking a resolution option is not the finish line. The DMSMS Management Team (DMT) stays engaged through a final Implement step built on three processes: programming and budgeting for the chosen resolution, integrating resolution funding with modification funding, and actually executing the resolution. The throughline is money and follow-through — because DMSMS issues are inevitable and recur every year, advance planning for resolution funds is treated as a best practice, and the DMT must shepherd execution through the program office's standard configuration- and engineering-change machinery rather than assuming those processes will run cleanly on their own. (Caveat domain: sustainment / obsolescence.)

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Implement step — three final processes** — (1) Program and budget for DMSMS resolutions, (2) Integrate DMSMS resolution and modification funding, (3) Implement DMSMS resolutions.
  - When/how: the closing phase of the DMSMS management process, after the PM has selected a preferred resolution option; the DMT participates in all three.
- **Modification Programming and Budgeting Best Practices (1–3)** — a named set of practices for leveraging funded modification POMs and budgets to carry DMSMS resolutions, depicted on the source's Figure 25.
  - When/how: whenever a DMSMS resolution can ride alongside, or be funded through, a planned modification effort.
- **The DMT's implementation-involvement checklist** — a best-practice list of six DMT actions during execution (roles understood, steps defined, technical actions verified, process monitored, status feedback obtained, BOMs updated).
  - When/how: applied as the case enters the implementation phase, layered on top of the program office's standard update / Engineering Change Proposal (ECP) process.
- **The bona fide need statement (LON buy exception)** — a documented justification prepared for the General Counsel to obtain an exception to the 31 U.S.C. §1502(a) limitation when buying in advance of need.
  - When/how: required when a Life-of-Need (LON) buy reaches beyond an appropriation's normal period of availability.
- **Statutory limits cited as constraints** — 31 U.S.C. §1502(a), the "Balances Available" rule on period-limited appropriations, and 10 U.S.C. §2213, which caps how much excess supply may be acquired; §2213 carries a written-determination exception path.
  - When/how: invoked when sizing large LON buys, especially for stock-fund-managed items.

## Key Concepts
- **End-of-life vs. end-of-need.** A contractor required to buy items normally cares only about demand through the contract's period of performance; the government typically wants coverage through the end of need. If the government does not explicitly define this, it stays open to interpretation — and the government should not assume a contractor will cover the longer horizon without extra funding.
- **Contractor-funded resolutions are driven by the contractor's business case.** When a contract makes the contractor responsible for resolving DMSMS, the contractor optimizes against its own calculation, not the program office's most-cost-effective option. Demanding the program-office-optimal resolution in an RFP can inflate the bid price unless a contractual incentive offsets the contractor's financial risk; program offices should expect to negotiate and possibly add funding.
- **Program and budget early, not in the execution year.** Specific future DMSMS problems are unknowable, but their occurrence is certain and recurring. Relying on execution-year funding is not a best practice; without advance planning, resources may be unavailable, and unmitigated DMSMS can degrade reliability and availability, slip schedule, and raise cost.
- **When budgeting best practices do and don't apply.** Dedicated DMSMS programming and budgeting is needed only when a resolution stands alone or when DMSMS is the principal driver of a larger effort. Resolutions are frequently bundled with capability enhancement, technology insertion/refresh, planned maintenance, life extension, or planned modifications — because it is convenient for availability and often cheaper on the same subsystem. When DMSMS is not the driving purpose, some of the document's budgeting best practices may not apply.
- **Modifications create resolution opportunities.** A modification can change a subsystem's obsolescence picture (the source's "tombstone" charts shift from red/yellow toward green). Where resolution funding rides with a modification, programming and budgeting typically belong to the modification planners.
- **The three modification budgeting best practices.**
  - *BP1 — size LON buys against approved modification plans.* If a funded modification will eventually retire an obsolete item, the LON's period of need runs only from when the issue first bites to when the modification lands; that window sets the quantity. LON resolution cost over time is small (apart from holding cost) assuming the items get used, but the buy creates an execution-year budgeting spike, especially for high-cost items. Modification-plan uncertainty (start dates, scope, funding probability) feeds the safety-level decision.
  - *BP2 — account for funded modifications to avoid duplication.* The DMSMS community should be able to state how much is spent on resolutions. A modification may resolve an issue outright (the obsolete item is removed anyway) or be augmented to resolve one far more cheaply than a standalone fix. Knowing the former avoids wasted analysis; proposing the latter is good practice — together they prevent double-counting and duplicated effort.
  - *BP3 — adjust modifications to absorb execution-year surprises.* DMSMS issues also surface unexpectedly mid-execution and can demand near-term resources. A funded modification is one potential source: the technical/engineering authority may rescope or slow the modification to free funds for the pressing issue, but the program office's financial decision-makers must approve. The DMT needs relationships with both.
- **Implementation runs through the standard change process.** Once accepted and funded, the case follows the program office's normal configuration-update and engineering-modification procedures. Minor changes may be largely clerical; major configuration or engineering changes flow through the appropriate ECP level, which confirms changes and qualifications meet system requirements.
- **The champion.** A high-level champion is critical to implementation success — senior enough to command attention, knowledgeable enough to own and justify the obsolescence program, and positioned to pull the functional disciplines together and clear obstacles the DMT cannot.
- **Recurring implementation issues the DMT advises on:**
  - *Contracting-officer priority* — invite the contracting officer to DMT meetings; make the need and its urgency clear.
  - *Buying in advance of need* — 31 U.S.C. §1502(a) caps what a period-limited appropriation can fund, which can limit LON quantity; an exception generally needs a documented bona fide need statement (problem statement; analyses showing no acceptable alternate part / no low-CM-impact fix; analysis that the LON is most cost-effective; expected impacts of not buying; quantity forecast with assumptions; discontinuation and impact dates; identified funds).
  - *Two-year stock-fund supply limit* — 10 U.S.C. §2213 bars stock-fund obligations likely to leave more than two years of operating stock on hand, with a written-determination exception (e.g., economical order quantity up to three years, or industrial-base / national-security need). For DLA-managed items the exception path is triggered automatically given the right justification, purchase-limitation evidence, and approvals; Service-managed items rarely use LON buys because Services typically handle expensive Depot-Level Reparables (DLRs).
  - *Calculating stock to end of need* — with well-understood demand this is a statistical-distribution calculation (the Poisson distribution is the normal choice) using confidence limits, operating tempo, units in service, and failure rates, anchored on a known end-of-need date (system retirement, a funded modification's removal of the item, or a longer-term resolution).
  - *Contract vehicle* — a contract must exist with whoever implements the resolution; appropriation restrictions mean some efforts need procurement funds and others need RDT&E funds (redesign, substitution, new-source qualification). The contracting office advises.
  - *Inventory management* — receiving, inspecting, and storing items can block an LON buy if unsolved; storage options include suppliers, primes, and DoD component facilities (the source cites White Sands Missile Range), and program offices have at times contracted commercially for storage with their own funds.

## Mental Models
- **End-of-need is the budgeting anchor — and the biggest source of error.** Almost every LON sizing decision turns on the end-of-need date. If that date later moves (life plans or modifications change), you over- or under-buy. Underestimation may force a second, costly interim resolution with few options left; overestimation leaves excess inventory. Because the cost of under-buying is almost always far higher than the cost of some excess, make conservative assumptions and consider a safety level.
- **Demand you can't see still has to be bought for.** A WCF-managing organization sizing an LON buy sees only its own customer demand. Past buys made outside WCF channels (logistics support contractors, direct commercial purchases), provisioning errors that miss a system as a user, production-line draws, and Foreign Military Sales (FMS) demand can all be invisible — and a shelf-life limit can pull the quantity the other way. Pull in as many of these inputs as possible before fixing the number.
- **Two horizons, one negotiation.** The contractor reasons to its period of performance; the government reasons to end-of-need. Strong government–industry relationships and full government awareness of the DMSMS services the contractor provides are what make the gap negotiable.
- **The modification is a funding river you can divert.** A funded modification is simultaneously a resolution (it may remove the obsolete item), a budget you can avoid duplicating, and an emergency reservoir you can rescope or slow to cover a surprise. Reading a modification only as "a separate program" wastes all three options.
- **WCF efficiency can work against your program.** A WCF-managed storage location that flags low-demand stock as excess may dispose of items you still need, and items used across systems can be procured out from under you by another program office. Implementation isn't done when the buy lands — assets in a shared, demand-optimizing system may need active protection.

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming the standard process will be problem-free.** The source names it explicitly: it is usually a mistake for the DMT to assume the program office's standard processes will run without trouble — hence the DMT's six-point involvement checklist during implementation.
- **Relying on execution-year funding.** Counting on obtaining resolution funds in the year of execution is called out as not a best practice; advance programming and budgeting is the expectation.

## Key Takeaways
1. The Implement step has three processes — program/budget, integrate with modification funding, and execute — and the DMT participates in all three; the work is not done when the option is chosen.
2. Plan resolution funding in advance. DMSMS recurs every year; execution-year reliance risks unavailable resources and degraded reliability, availability, schedule, and cost.
3. Leverage modifications for resolutions via the three modification budgeting best practices: size LON buys against funded plans, avoid duplication, and tap modification scope/timing for execution-year surprises (with financial-authority approval).
4. Execute through the standard ECP / configuration-change process, but actively manage it: confirm roles, define steps, verify technical actions, monitor, get status feedback, and update monitored BOMs.
5. A senior champion is critical — to command attention, own the obsolescence program, unify the disciplines, and clear the DMT's obstacles.
6. LON buys are bounded by statute and data quality: 31 U.S.C. §1502(a) and 10 U.S.C. §2213 constrain them; a bona fide need statement and written determinations unlock exceptions; size to a known end-of-need with conservative assumptions and a safety level, since under-buying almost always costs more than excess.
