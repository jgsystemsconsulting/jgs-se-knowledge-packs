# Chapter 6: Analyze — DMSMS Resolution Determination

## Core Idea
Once an obsolescence issue has been assessed, the **Analyze** step finds the best way to resolve it. SD-22 frames resolution determination as an **iterative, cost-driven analysis**: enumerate the viable ways to keep the affected item supportable, price each one by summing its applicable cost elements, then pick the alternative that delivers the best return on investment once risk and second-order effects are accounted for. The process is identical regardless of whether the obsolete item is electronic hardware, a mechanical or material item (MaSME), or software — and it repeats continuously, with new issues typically folded in at every DMT meeting. A central early check is whether some **external organization** is already pursuing a resolution that meets the program office's schedule and technical needs; if so, the program office only has to monitor it rather than fund its own.

This is fundamentally a **sustainment/obsolescence** activity: the goal is to avoid negative impacts on operational availability at the lowest life-cycle cost, not to add capability.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Resolution cost elements** (Section 6.1) — a checklist of roughly twenty cost categories (non-recurring engineering; engineering/engineering-data revision; purchase of engineering, design, or technical data; qualification of new items; revision of test procedures; software changes; start-up costs; testing; tooling/equipment; computer programs and documentation; interim support; spares; supply/provisioning data; support and test equipment; technical manuals; training/trainers; and an "other" catch-all).
  - When to use: to build up a resolution's **total implementation cost** as the sum of every applicable element. Use *actual* costs where known; otherwise each program office should develop **average costs** per element.
- **Three resolution categories and their option types** (Section 6.2, Table 12) — *existing material (logistics)*, *substitutes (engineering)*, and *redesign (engineering)*, ordered by increasing research, cost, and engineering involvement. Within the **existing-material** category the options run from needing no fix at all, to using an already-approved item, to a life-of-need stockpile, to recovering supply by repair/refurbishment/reclamation or by extending the producer's production or support. The **substitute** category spans a straightforward drop-in, a more involved substitute requiring engineering, and qualifying a brand-new source. The **redesign** category climbs from a design refreshment, through redeveloping the item, to redesigning the next-higher assembly, up to a complex redesign amounting to system replacement.
  - When to use: to enumerate candidate resolutions. SD-22 recommends working **top to bottom** through Table 12, since lower rows imply more research and higher implementation cost.
- **Cost-element-to-resolution mapping** (Table 13) — a matrix marking which cost elements typically apply to each resolution option, so an estimator knows which elements to price for a given option.
  - When to use: to scope the cost build-up for a specific resolution option without missing or over-counting elements.
- **AoA / BCA** (Section 6.3.4) — the analytic decision tools. A **Business Case Analysis** is a formal, structured examination of the costs, benefits, and risks of alternatives, compared on **net present value (NPV)** (discounted benefits minus discounted costs); the highest-NPV (lowest life-cycle-cost) option wins because the benefit — avoiding readiness impact — is the same across alternatives. An **Analysis of Alternatives** is the lighter-weight version used when a low-cost, low-risk resolution can be estimated confidently up front.
  - When to use: BCA for high-cost or high-uncertainty decisions; AoA when the answer is clear and cheap to bound.
- **Average resolution-cost table** (Table 15) — per-option average costs in FY24 dollars, derived mainly from a 2014 Department of Commerce DMSMS survey, ranging from roughly \$1,258 for an approved item up to multi-million-dollar redesign/system-replacement figures.
  - When to use: for preliminary estimates, sanity-checking a program office's own cost estimates, and cost-avoidance calculations when nothing better is available. Appendix L breaks these down by environment, commodity, and item type — to be used with caution where sample sizes are small.

## Key Concepts
- **Total implementation cost = sum of applicable cost elements.** A resolution might be as light as updating drawings and technical manuals or as heavy as developing and testing a whole new design; the estimate must capture whichever elements apply.
- **Counterfeit and Pb-free realities raise cost.** Buying an electronic item from anyone other than an authorized source (OCM, OEM, authorized/franchised distributor, or approved after-market manufacturer) forces extra testing to keep counterfeits out of the supply chain — so average testing cost must be folded in. SD-22 points to the SAE AS5553/AS6081/AS6171A/AS6174A/AS6462C/AS6496 family and DoDI 4140.67 for counterfeit-prevention guidance.
- **Categories scale with effort.** Existing-material options need actions to secure availability of existing supply; substitutes need engineering to qualify or implement; redesigns need the full engineering-and-qualification cycle for new or heavily modified equipment.
- **Viability is per-issue.** Every applicable resolution type can in principle apply to any commodity (mechanical, material, software, electronic), but not all are *viable* for a given issue. A viable resolution must meet **all** requirements — performance, safety, security — and must address all second-order derivative effects. For a forged impeller body, for instance, the only realistic options may be a new source or a redesign.
- **Commodity skews frequency, not process.** Survey data (Table 14) shows obsolescence resolutions concentrated in electronics (about 63%), with electrical near 28% and mechanical near 8%; *which* options get used varies by commodity even though the determination process does not.
- **Resolve at the lowest practical level of indenture — but check higher.** Consider resolutions at the lowest level of assembly possible; however, a higher-assembly fix can sometimes yield a higher ROI by resolving many issues at once or delivering reliability gains.
- **Roadmaps drive timing and scope.** Product, technology, and supportability roadmaps tell the DMSMS community how long an item is needed and when planned modifications land. They are used to **size LON buys**, **evaluate competing resolutions**, and **plan multi-phase resolutions** that defer or avoid a costly redesign by riding along with a planned modification.
- **Health assessments set the refresh point.** A notional health assessment colors part inventories red/yellow/green over time; the optimal technology-refresh date is the point where the summed cost of individual interim resolutions exceeds the cost of a redesign. The DMT can recommend interim resolutions to delay a refresh for a limited time, but does not own the refresh schedule itself.
- **System security engineering (SSE) reshapes alternatives.** Because resolving an issue usually changes configuration, BOM, suppliers, or items, it can replace critical items carrying security requirements, introduce new items with their own requirements, or bring in new suppliers/foreign investors with new supply-chain vulnerabilities. SD-22 lists SSE disciplines — anti-tamper, hardware/software assurance, SCRM, program protection, cybersecurity (plus DEF) — and instructs that selecting a resolution should **trigger a re-look at risk**. The Program Protection Plan (PPP) is the primary source for critical items, but practitioners must coordinate with SSE SMEs because the PPP may be incomplete or out of date.
- **Risk weighting beats raw cost.** After breakeven and ROI are computed, the cheapest option may not be best. Capture technical, supply-chain, financial, and schedule risks, weight them, and trade cost against risk — sometimes a high-cost/low-risk option (e.g., a substitute using *current* technology) beats a cheap option that will soon go obsolete again.
- **Multi-phase resolutions cover the gap.** When the best-ROI option can't be implemented before stocks deplete (e.g., a new source needs a year to qualify), pair it with an interim option such as an LON buy to bridge the time increment.
- **Cost avoidance redefined.** SD-22 replaces the older cost-avoidance method with one grounded in *what would have been done reactively*: the avoidance equals the cost of the reactive resolution that was avoided minus the actual proactive resolution cost. There is **no** avoidance if the proactive and reactive resolutions would be the same, or if the issue was found reactively.
- **Leadership approval is a feedback loop.** Preferred options go to program office leadership, who may add constraints (often around the "time to implement" risk) and force another iteration of viability analysis and AoA/BCA before funding is secured and final approval is granted.

## Mental Models
- **Two-axis sort: research/cost on one axis, viability on the other.** Table 12 ranks options by how much research and engineering they demand; the situation analysis filters that list down to what is actually viable for *this* issue. You walk the ranked list top-down and keep only the feasible rows.
- **Cost is built, not looked up.** A resolution's price is assembled element by element (Table 13 tells you which elements apply), preferring actuals and falling back to program-developed averages — then Table 15's survey averages serve only as a preliminary or sanity-check figure.
- **Same benefit, so compare on cost.** Because every viable resolution buys the *same* outcome — sustained operational availability — the decision collapses to a life-cycle-cost (NPV) comparison, adjusted for risk. That is why AoA/BCA only need a consistent ordinal ranking of cost, not perfectly exact numbers, to choose.
- **The fixed-cost cliff for restart.** The non-recurring cost of standing a production line back up for an obsolete item can exceed the per-unit manufacturing cost by more than two orders of magnitude. That asymmetry favors a slightly oversized LON buy (risking some excess inventory) over an undersized one that forces an expensive future restart — and favors amortizing redevelopment NRE over a larger initial buy plus obtaining the TDP to cut future re-order costs.
- **Ride the modification wave.** Resolving obsolescence *in conjunction with* a planned modification or technology insertion is cheaper than a standalone, out-of-cycle redesign — so synchronize resolution timing with roadmap events, and let DMSMS health assessments refine modification plans rather than the reverse.
- **Dominated options fall out first.** An option that is both costlier *and* riskier than another is "dominated" and can be discarded immediately, narrowing the field before any finer cost-to-risk tradeoff is attempted.

## Anti-patterns
- **Skipping the PPP-is-authoritative assumption check.** SD-22 explicitly calls it *not a best practice* for the DMSMS practitioner to read the PPP (or other documents) alone to decide whether and how security requirements apply — because the practitioner may not be positioned to interpret it, the PPP may be stale, or key components may fall outside its scope. The correct move is to coordinate with SSE SMEs.
- **Sizing an LON buy without the roadmap.** Estimating the lifetime quantity in isolation, ignoring a planned modification that will retire the item, over-procures and wastes money.
- **Treating cost as the sole decision criterion.** Choosing the lowest-cost option without weighting risk can lock in a resolution (e.g., an alternate item on near-obsolete technology) that fails or re-obsoletes quickly.
- **Naïve/over-broad cost-avoidance claims.** The prior methodology is criticized for letting reactively-found problems count, assuming a next resolution that might never have happened, and leaving the avoided-resolution cost undefined — producing inflated or inconsistent figures.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Resolution determination is iterative and cost-driven**, identical across hardware, mechanical/material, and software, with new issues added at each DMT meeting.
2. **Build total implementation cost from the cost-element checklist** (Section 6.1), preferring actuals over program-developed averages; add counterfeit-testing cost whenever buying outside authorized sources.
3. **Three categories, increasing effort**: existing material (logistics) → substitutes (engineering) → redesign (engineering); walk Table 12 top-to-bottom and keep only viable options that meet *all* requirements and second-order effects.
4. **Roadmaps and health assessments set timing** — they size LON buys, enable multi-phase/interim resolutions, and identify the optimal point to fold obsolescence fixes into a planned technology refresh or insertion.
5. **System security engineering must inform every resolution** — any configuration/BOM/supplier change triggers a re-look at assurance, SCRM, CPI protection, and cybersecurity risk, coordinated with SSE SMEs, not the PPP alone.
6. **Choose by AoA or BCA on life-cycle cost (NPV), then adjust for risk** — capture technical, supply-chain, financial, and schedule risk; discard dominated options; sometimes pay more to buy down risk.
7. **Leadership approval can force another iteration**, often around the time-to-implement risk; combined/multi-phase resolutions are valid outcomes when no single option fits the timeline.
