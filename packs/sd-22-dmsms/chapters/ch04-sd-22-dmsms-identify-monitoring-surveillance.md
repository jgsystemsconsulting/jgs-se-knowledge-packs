# Chapter 4: Identify — DMSMS Monitoring and Surveillance

## Core Idea
The **Identify** step is where a program office watches its items and materials for end-of-life (EOL) notices and other early signals of pending discontinuance, ideally starting in the design phase and running across the full system life cycle. Because monitoring everything is prohibitively expensive, the whole step is governed by a **risk-based, "yes/no" decision** about which portions of the system to actively monitor, when, and how often. Identify chains six processes — prioritize systems, procure tools, collect and prepare item data, analyze item availability, assess preliminary designs, and forecast technology obsolescence — to convert raw bills of material (BOMs) into a list of items with immediate or near-term obsolescence problems, plus an early forecast of issues still over the horizon.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The six Identify processes** — system prioritization; identification and procurement of monitoring/surveillance tools; collection and preparation of item data; analysis of item availability; assessment of preliminary designs for DMSMS risk; and forecasting technology obsolescence. (Section 4 opening.)
  - When/how: the first three are largely **one-time** (start-up) activities; the latter three **recur** throughout the system's life whenever new notices, surveys, or scheduled tool updates warrant. The source notes the one-time processes sit close to the Prepare step and the overall flow is unchanged if they are placed at the end of Prepare or the start of Identify.
- **Two-layer prioritization (the two filters)** — a first filter screens **subsystems** to focus the DMSMS effort (Section 4.1); a second filter screens **individual items within those subsystems** before loading BOMs into predictive tools (Section 4.3.2). Each filter narrows scope to control cost while keeping the highest-risk items in view.
- **Three strategic determinations for "what to monitor"** (carried in from the Section 3.1 foundations and applied here): (1) apply algorithms based on life-cycle estimates; (2) further analyze uncategorized items; (3) decide whether to investigate critical materials not visible on the BOM.
- **Three-category triage of BOM items** — *definitely monitor*, *do not monitor*, and *uncategorized* (not enough known). For the uncategorized group, three options exist: monitor all (low risk, high cost), monitor none (high risk, low cost), or analyze further to optimize.
- **Three risk categories + the risk cube** (Section 4.3.2.1, Figure 12) — for previously uncategorized items, score on **item criticality**, **supply chain vulnerability**, and **time to implement a resolution**. The best practice is to proactively monitor only items that are high on all three axes (the "red/red/red" corner).
- **Four item-availability methods** (Section 4.4) — predictive tools, vendor surveys, critical materials analysis, and product discontinuance notices (PDNs) — each of which can trigger a refresh of an item's availability status.
- **Preliminary design assessment template** (Section 4.5, Table 10) — a risk-area worksheet scoring early preparation, ability to monitor, and item obsolescence against cost/schedule/performance impact and a red/yellow/green rating.
- **Roadmap-driven forecasting** (Section 4.6, Figure 16) — product, supportability, and technology roadmaps feed forecasts of future DMSMS issues, aggregated in the program's IMS/IMP.

## Key Concepts
- **DMT determination, not a ranking.** Prioritization is a binary scoping decision by the DMSMS management team about *what to actively monitor*, not an ordered list. Funding can force acceptance of more risk than desired — and the source warns that accepting too much DMSMS risk has produced large, unexpected resolution costs.
- **Prioritization drivers.** Subsystems are screened on safety (critical characteristics whose failure could be catastrophic), mission criticality (including single points of failure and impact on next-higher assemblies), DMSMS-related cost history, known troublesome/historically problematic areas, life-cycle phase, sustainment strategy, availability of technical data, and **vulnerability to supply chain exploitation** (short supply pushes buyers toward less trustworthy sources, raising counterfeit and compromise risk).
- **BOMs are the indispensable data type.** Without a parts list or BOM, item availability analysis and continuous discontinuance prediction are impossible. An **indentured BOM** (showing component→assembly→unit→system relationships) is preferred over a **flat BOM**, because it reveals whether multiple obsolete items share an assembly — which can make a minor redesign cheaper than several life-of-need (LON) buys. The source notes the government need not always hold the BOM; DMSMS can be managed by the prime contractor.
- **System-item hierarchy** (Figure 9): unit → assembly → component, with synonyms such as LRU/WRA, SRA, SRU, piece part, and device.
- **Different data for different item types.** Hardware (electronic and MaSME) relies on parts lists/BOMs; for MaSME items a Provisioning Master Record (PMR) can trim the monitored set after production. **Software** needs a contracted list (CDRLs/DIDs), plus interdependency mapping — software relationships can be cross-system rather than hierarchical and are best surfaced through engineers/developers or interface control documents, not the BOM.
- **The "definitely monitor" set.** Includes electronic COTS assemblies, active and RF components, programmable devices, memory, microprocessors, ASICs, hybrids, custom electronic and custom passive assemblies, sole-source low-demand assemblies, design-driving/sole-source/environmentally-threatening materials, and all electromechanical items.
- **The "do not monitor" sets.** Standard/common industrial items (most mechanical parts, connectors, cabling, certain consumables) that are easily replaced; and custom-fabricated items not produced after final delivery — for which logistics managers/PMs must instead lay in enough stock and retain documentation to enable reacquisition.
- **Database-derived risk signals.** The source maps specific DLA/provisioning fields to risk: Weapon System Essentiality Code (1, 5, 6, 7 critical), Criticality Code (E, F, H, M, S), Special Procedures Code (1, 3, 4, 5), requisition/PDC/RDD indicators of MICAP/NMCS, Reference Number Variation Code (9 = inactive), Acquisition Advice Code (V/Y/X), Acquisition Method Code (3/4/5 sole-source) and the AMSC data-rights flags (A/D/P/R), and CAGE codes beginning with an alpha character (foreign source).
- **Predictive tools are imperfect and disagree.** An informal study found two tools recognized only ~71% and ~72% of researched items and disagreed on obsolescence status for ~4% of items. The guidance: use more than one tool (or a multi-source tool) when funding allows, and **manually confirm** findings — even agreement between tools should be verified, and "unknown availability" must never be read as "available."
- **Vendor surveys** fill gaps predictive tools cannot — COTS assemblies, MaSME items, and materials (alloys, epoxies, adhesives, cooling fluids). Best practice frequency: roughly twice yearly for electronics; program-determined for MaSME items (with arguments for keeping cadence aligned to avoid extra work and to catch fast-moving regulatory/geopolitical material disruptions); for software, quarterly (high risk), semi-annually (moderate), at least annually (low).
- **Critical materials analysis** targets the "unknown unknowns" — hazardous, exotic, or supply-constrained materials embedded in BOM items or used in their manufacture, often introduced low in the supply chain by firms unaware their product is destined for a DoD system. Because such issues span many programs, the work is best done **centrally and collaboratively**, anchored by a targeted watch list (CMRMP emerging chemicals, DLA Strategic Materials Office SCM watch list) and standing DMT engagement rather than labor-intensive lower-tier vendor surveys.
- **PDN sources.** Pull automated alerts from predictive tools, **GIDEP**, and **DLA** — all three, since each carries data the others lack — and always validate a PDN before acting. GIDEP cannot predict obsolescence but offers no-cost batch matching of parts lists against existing discontinuance notices and an Urgent Data Request service (source-of-supply and request-for-information queries). DLA alerts add usage/weapon-system coding and link to emulation options (GEM/AME) for managed microcircuits.
- **Software has its own monitoring logic.** COTS software is tracked via licenses/support agreements, roadmaps, user groups, and surveys; custom and open-source software hinge on continuation-of-support and configuration control; COTS firmware is treated as a hardware-software functional group that goes obsolete when *either* element does; custom firmware needs no monitoring because the program already controls its changes.
- **Preliminary-design assessment** is a best practice to keep obsolete or hazardous/exotic items out of new and revised designs, ideally timed to major technical design reviews and conducted whenever new parts lists arrive — though a fuller system health assessment fits better once the design is mature.
- **Forecasting** rests on roadmaps. Predictive-tool EOL estimates and vendor-survey EOL answers are supplemented by product, supportability, and technology roadmaps; technology management provides an early-warning view of industrial-base health for the system both as-configured and as it may be modified.

## Mental Models
- **The risk cube as a filter, not a scorecard.** Walk the three axes in order: if an item is neither mission- nor safety-critical, operational impact is low and you can resolve reactively; if it is critical but the supply chain is robust, demand can still be met; even for a critical item with a fragile supply chain, proactive monitoring is only warranted when the **time to implement a resolution exceeds the on-hand inventory runway**. Only the red/red/red corner earns scarce proactive-monitoring dollars.
- **Two filters, then load.** Subsystem filter (Section 4.1) → item filter (Section 4.3.2) → load into predictive tools. If you cannot say which filter a decision belongs to, you are conflating "which subsystems matter" with "which parts on those subsystems matter."
- **Reactive is a legitimate choice — when the system can wait.** Choosing reactive monitoring is an explicit bet that the system will suffer no severe ill effects from waiting until an item is unobtainable before resolving it. That bet is sound *unless* significant risks are present; an SME should understand and periodically revalidate every "do not monitor" decision.
- **Indenture buys options.** Seeing obsolescence in context of the item hierarchy expands resolution choices (redesign one assembly vs. multiple LON buys); a flat BOM hides those options.
- **A snapshot, refreshed.** Availability status is a point-in-time picture that must be re-taken on a cadence matched to commodity volatility — daily electronics alerts where possible, since a 30-day LON-buy window can close before a quarterly COTS survey ever runs.
- **Find the unknown unknowns by talking, not surveying.** For lower-tier critical materials, the cost-effective move is standing DMT engagement and tacit-knowledge sharing among primes, OEMs, depots, and material/environmental SMEs — not exhaustive (and often unanswered) lower-tier vendor surveys.

## Anti-patterns
- **Monitoring everything.** The source repeatedly frames comprehensive monitoring as prohibitively expensive and explicitly directs a risk-based approach; treating every part as worth proactive monitoring squanders limited resources and starves the high-risk items of attention.
- **Accepting too much DMSMS risk to save monitoring cost.** Named directly: under-monitoring has led to large, unexpected resolution costs downstream.
- **Trusting a single predictive tool, or trusting tool output without manual confirmation.** Given documented recognition gaps (~71–72%) and inter-tool disagreement (~4%), and the warning that an alternative flagged by a tool may not actually work in a legacy system, single-tool reliance and unverified output are flagged risks.
- **Reading "unknown availability" as a false sense of security.** The source explicitly warns that items with unknown status must not be assumed safe.
- **Leaving obsolete items in a product design.** Not eliminating obsolete items pushes buyers toward higher-risk distributors, increasing the odds of counterfeit parts entering the DoD supply chain and fielded weapon systems.
- **Treating provisioning data as sufficient for DMSMS management.** The source cautions that provisioning errors/inconsistencies can themselves trigger shortages or apparent DMSMS issues, and that provisioning data alone does not suffice.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Identify = monitoring and surveillance**, started in design and sustained across the life cycle; six processes turn BOMs into a list of immediate/near-term obsolescence issues plus a forward forecast.
2. **Prioritization is a risk-based yes/no scoping decision** by the DMT — driven by safety, mission criticality, cost history, troublesome areas, life-cycle phase, sustainment strategy, data availability, and supply-chain-exploitation vulnerability.
3. **BOMs are the most important data type**, and **indentured beats flat** because it reveals assembly-level relationships that widen resolution options.
4. **Two prioritization filters** (subsystems, then items) keep cost down; items sort into *definitely monitor*, *do not monitor*, and *uncategorized*.
5. **Score uncategorized items on the risk cube** — criticality × supply-chain vulnerability × time-to-resolve — and proactively monitor only the **red/red/red** items.
6. **Four availability methods** — predictive tools, vendor surveys, critical materials analysis, PDNs (GIDEP + DLA + tool alerts) — refresh status; tool output is imperfect and must be manually confirmed.
7. **Critical materials and software** need dedicated, tailored approaches; lower-tier material risk is best surfaced through centralized, collaborative DMT engagement.
8. **Assess preliminary designs and forecast with roadmaps** to keep obsolete items out of designs and to time modifications before items run out.
