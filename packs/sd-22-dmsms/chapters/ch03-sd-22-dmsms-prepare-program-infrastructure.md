# Chapter — Prepare: DMSMS Management Program Infrastructure (DMT, DMP, Operational Processes)

## Core Idea
**Prepare** is the first of the five DMSMS management process steps, and it lays the groundwork for the other four. It builds the infrastructure a program office needs for robust, **risk-based, proactive** sustainment of a system against obsolescence: leadership sets the strategic foundations, a chartered and trained DMSMS Management Team (DMT) is formed, a DMSMS Management Plan (DMP) is written and approved by the PM, and a set of operational processes is stood up. These activities rarely happen in a strict line — the DMT typically begins forming while the DMP is still being drafted, and standing up the operational processes feeds back into DMP content. The recurring theme is that obsolescence is inevitable across a system's life cycle, so the program office must decide *up front and on the basis of risk* where to be proactive, where to be reactive, and how to fund and oversee the work — much of which is often performed by production or sustainment contractors with the DMT in an oversight role.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **The Prepare step** (Section 3) — the first of the five DMSMS management process steps. Use it at program initiation and revisit it whenever circumstances change. It encompasses establishing the foundations, forming the DMT, developing the DMP, and establishing the operational processes (secure resources, advocate for resilient designs, evaluation process, QMS, case monitoring/tracking, supporting contracts).
- **Foundations for DMSMS management** (Section 3.1) — the strategic direction the PM sets *before* the DMP is finalized: objectives, DMT roles/responsibilities and operating guidelines, meeting cadence, and risk-based operating guidelines. Used to keep the DMP's scope of effort matched to the funding actually available and to leadership's expectations.
- **DMSMS Management Team (DMT)** (Section 3.2) — chartered by the PM or PSM; the entity holding the lead and oversight for DMSMS management. Use the ARCI chart to assign responsibility across roles and activities.
- **ARCI chart** (Section 3.2.1, Table 5) — Accountable / Responsible / Consulted / Informed / **Not Informed** assignment of roles to DMSMS activities. Exactly one "A" per decision at each organizational level; multiple R/C/I/N allowed. Used to depict relative roles and responsibilities of each DMT member.
- **Parts and Material Life Cycle Management credential** (Section 3.2.2) — the DAU credential mapped to DMSMS management, built from a named set of DAU courses (LOG 0640/0650/0660/0630/0670/0320/0380/0390/0400/0470/0510, CON 0040, CLE 019, CLE 026). Table 6 maps recommended courses to each DMT role; the DMT lead tailors it.
- **DMSMS Management Plan (DMP)** (Section 3.3) — required of all program offices per **DoDI 4245.15**; a living document developed by the DMT and approved by the PM. Follows the expected outline: Purpose, Scope and Applicability, DMSMS Management Approach, DMT, DMSMS Management Operations, Funding, Contract Requirements, Metrics/Reporting/Quality, Process Capability. A template is on the DKSP; SYSPARS can guide development.
- **Process capability matrix** (Appendix D referenced from 3.3) — four levels of capability maturity for the key DMSMS processes; the DMP and the matrix are kept aligned, with rationale for any gap between current and desired capability.
- **DMSMS operational processes** (Section 3.4, Figure 5) — the named operational processes; this chapter covers the Prepare-step ones: Secure Resources (3.4.1), Establish Interfaces to Advocate for DMSMS-Resilient Designs (3.4.2), Establish a DMSMS Management Evaluation Process (3.4.3), Establish a QMS (3.4.4), Establish a Case Monitoring and Tracking Process (3.4.5), and Establish Supporting Contracts (3.4.6).
- **Record keeping framework** (Section 3.4.3.1, Table 8) — Level 1 and Level 2 data elements split into cost-related and operations-efficiency data; every program office collects Level 1, robust ones add Level 2. A data dictionary is in Appendix H.4.
- **Quality Management System (QMS)** (Section 3.4.4) — the overarching framework controlling DMSMS processes; includes a quality plan, compliance metrics, and process analysis. Lean Six Sigma (LSS) using the DMAIC cycle is the named process-analysis method.
- **Supporting documents** — **SD-26** (DMSMS and Parts Management Contracting Guide) for contract requirements; **SD-19 / MIL-STD-11991B** for parts management; **EIA-933C** (COTS Assembly Management Plan); **DI-MGMT-82275A** DID for DMSMS metrics data; **DoDI 5000.88** establishing parts selection to consider DMSMS.

## Key Concepts

### Foundations: leadership sets direction before the DMP
Program office leadership should not delegate the strategic direction to the DMT. If it does, the DMP can end up describing a scope of effort that the available funding cannot support or that does not match leadership's expectations. Leadership engages early to define objectives, roles, operating guidelines, and meeting cadence, and refines the four overarching objectives of DMSMS management. It then sets **risk-based operating guidelines** by answering questions such as which subsystems get monitoring priority (driven by mission criticality, safety, or known problem areas), which items within them to monitor proactively versus reactively, and when DMSMS management should begin.

### Start early — even for slower-moving commodities
As a best practice, proactive DMSMS management starts as early as the **SRR**, when preliminary parts lists appear. This holds for electronic items (where designing in already-obsolete parts almost guarantees later sustainment problems) and — perhaps counter-intuitively — for **MaSME** (material, mechanical, and similar) items too, even though their market forces move more slowly than the commercial electronics market. Starting early widens the window of opportunity, gives a larger menu of cheaper resolutions, lowers the chance of schedule/readiness impacts, and lets high-risk MaSME monitoring ride along with electronic-item monitoring.

### Software obsolescence is real (just different)
Software does not become "obsolete" in the traditional sense, but its availability degrades: vendor support ends, the underlying hardware obsoletes, build/test tooling or the development environment disappears, or the skills to sustain it are lost. Hardware and COTS software form a symbiotic supply chain where each drives the other's obsolescence. A software obsolescence program surfaces these issues before system impact and improves awareness of cybersecurity weaknesses.

### The DMT: composition, roles, and the ARCI chart
The PM/PSM charters the DMT and authorizes its activities. Composition scales with system complexity and DMSMS severity, and evolves over the life cycle — a partial team can suffice before CDR. The party that will ultimately own sustainment should sit on the DMT through *all* phases. The ideal roster includes a **DMT lead** (the DMSMS champion, ideally full-time), **program office representative**, **DMSMS SME**, **engineering activity representative**, **System Security Engineer (SSE)** (present except where security is genuinely not a concern; the SSE works from the Program Protection Plan), **logistics representative** (often the PSM), **sustainment/maintenance representative**, **software SME**, plus ad-hoc members (supply support / DLA, value engineering, contracting office, prime/subcontractor, FMS, IP SME, software-license group, environmental/materials engineering, CCB). Roles can be combined and change over time. The **ARCI chart** depicts who is Accountable (exactly one per decision per level), Responsible, Consulted, Informed, or Not Informed for each activity. Ultimately the PM is accountable for DMSMS management of the system.

### DMT training and competency
Not every member must be a DMSMS SME, but the DMT lead sets minimum training per role based on the approach, resources, and each member's responsibilities; new members are trained on joining. Training maps to the DAU **Parts and Material Life Cycle Management** credential. The source enumerates the knowledge, skills, and abilities a DMSMS SME should hold — spanning logistics/SE, DoD policy, case-tracking and metrics, BCA processes, supply chains, technology roadmapping, part-selection criteria, conflict resolution and stakeholder communication, and the ability to chair a DMT and advise on DMPs, SOWs, contract language, and ILAs.

### The DMP: living plan, not a tutorial
The DMP documents the foundations and identifies the risks of deviating from standard DMSMS processes. It is tailored to the program office (infrastructure, record keeping, QMS, resources, priorities, constraints), has no prescribed length, and should not be a tutorial — it references other documents to avoid duplication. It begins early (preferably right after program initiation) and is revised as conditions change: role changes, contract revisions, tool/process changes, a service-life extension, procurement-plan changes, or operating-tempo shifts. The PM approves it; the DMT develops it and has authority to execute it. The expected outline maps each section to tailorable questions and to the Appendix D capability matrix — e.g., *Purpose* fixes near/long-term objectives (keeping cost/schedule/readiness/availability impacts within an acceptable level), *Approach* sets the proactive/reactive split and the government-vs-contractor roles while preserving a life-cycle perspective, *Contract Requirements* cites CDRLs/DIDs/SOW language and addresses **true LON (life-of-need, not life-of-contract) buys**, managing across contract breaks, and exit strategies.

### Securing resources: operations funding drivers
Operations funding is distinct from resolution-implementation funding. Drivers fall into three categories: (1) one-time **program-initiation** tasks (develop the DMP, stand up quality processes, obtain analysis tools and case-management databases); (2) **data collection / research / forecasting** (obtain or build BOMs, clean and load them, analyze, research, survey vendors, process PDNs, open cases); and (3) recurring **data analysis and oversight** (contract language, budgets, roadmap forecasting, health assessments, design reviews, oversight of implementation). Table 7 contrasts **startup** effort (entire BOM, all vendors, all items) against **steady-state** effort (only BOM changes, periodic data cleanup, surveys only when conditions are met) — startup costs more, and the spike recurs as new subsystems phase in.

### Advocating for DMSMS-resilient designs
The DMSMS community advocates for resilience during design, framing DMSMS not as a single-issue cause but as one thread woven into reliability, maintainability, availability, and supportability — and leveraging other communities' access to PMs and chief engineers. Early design decisions lock in a large share of life-cycle cost (Figure 6). Resilience requirements include criteria such as selecting parts with forecasted availability of five years or more, to the maximum practical extent, for new designs and changes (per illustrative requirement 17 in SD-26). The source lists design concepts to reduce DMSMS risk: weighing **future resolution cost** (a shorter-EOL but cheaper-to-resolve item can be the better choice), thoughtful **technology and item selection** (avoiding end-of-functional-life parts and sole-source where possible), **standardization and reuse** (preferred parts lists, standardized modules), **open systems architecture** for both hardware and software (modular, consensus-based interfaces enabling F3 substitution and multi-vendor competition), cautious **use of COTS assemblies** (faster, cheaper up front but short commercial life cycles and requalification costs), and judicious **use of alternative-grade parts** (e.g., automotive grade, with added screening/derating). Parts management (per SD-19 / MIL-STD-11991B / DoDI 5000.88) is the closely related SE discipline.

### Evaluation process and the record-keeping framework
The DMT continually evaluates effectiveness against the program office's defined objectives by recording and analyzing data to generate metrics — which also point to where to investigate further. The record-keeping framework (Table 8) divides data into cost-related and operations-efficiency information, at **Level 1** (collected by every program office) and **Level 2** (richer analyses for robust programs). Some elements (marked #) lie outside what a DMSMS contractor can see, so the program office must gather them — which is why they do not appear in DID DI-MGMT-82275A. The framework's benefits are programming/budgeting (better, more defensible cost estimates), process improvement (faster case processing, better monitoring and LON-buy processes), and **ROI** (quantifying the cost of being reactive versus the cost avoidance of being proactive). Figure 7 captures the central value proposition: identifying an issue earlier widens the window of opportunity, which lowers readiness/schedule/counterfeit risk and resolution cost. The data are useful not only to the program office but to PEOs, Service HQ, and OSD — who today cannot answer basic enterprise questions (how many program offices have DMPs, how much DoD spends on resolutions) for lack of aggregated data.

### Quality Management System (QMS)
DMSMS processes run inside a QMS so quality and consistency survive personnel changes. The **quality plan** defines checks, documents responsibilities and methods as procedures/work instructions (a top tier common across DMSMS management, a second tier of platform-specific work instructions), and inserts **quality checks** at optimal points to catch errors before later work or the customer is affected. **Compliance metrics** measure whether processes are followed, against five criteria — time, cost, resources, quality, actions — and against a target value. **Process analysis** can use **Lean Six Sigma** with the **DMAIC** cycle (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) to identify and remove "waste"/defects; the source cites a DMT that found 131 COTS-surveillance defects in about a year and achieved roughly 25–30% efficiency improvement after process changes, and recommends building a value stream map (VSM) and an automated database.

### Case monitoring and tracking
The community works two case types: cases from *known* obsolescence (a PDN or predictive-tool result) and cases from *review of a preliminary design or forecasted* obsolescence (used to inform designers so obsolete items are designed out). Case management both tracks an issue from identification through resolution implementation and provides a data repository for budgeting and process improvement. A program office decides its criteria for *when* to open a case (ideally on every item with an EOL notice, for completeness). A tracking tool/database built on a **case sheet** records part data, higher-assembly impact, research notes, selected resolution, cross-platform effects, action items, and timing relative to key dates (e.g., when a bridge buy is needed). Case status runs from **Open** (with variants Under Investigation, Decision Pending, Implementation Pending, Under Implementation) to **Closed** to **Watch List** (closed but monitored, e.g., to confirm demand assumptions behind an LON buy still hold).

### Supporting contracts
Contracts (and inter-government agreements) enable the operations. The program office should not assume a contractor's "standard" DMSMS process mirrors this document — it is based on the contractor's own internal guidance. Leadership should use its own people efficiently (don't divert in-house engineers to routine day-to-day DMSMS tasks) and leverage the prime/OEM's deep system knowledge across the life cycle. Employing **independent SMEs** is a best practice even when a prime is engaged: they help oversee the prime, provide independent verification and neutral third-party validation, bring specialized tools/data/supplier relationships, advise on contract language and BOM acquisition, link to other program offices' best practices, and can carry DMSMS work during organic sustainment. Centralized DMSMS SME teams across DoD can be leveraged so a program office need not build expertise from scratch. Across all of this, the **BOM is the single most important data element** — without knowing what items are on the system, even reactive DMSMS management is extremely limited.

## Mental Models
- **Prepare is the foundation, not a phase you finish.** It is the first of five steps and feeds the other four; its products (foundations, DMT, DMP, processes) are living and get revised as risk and circumstances change.
- **Foundations precede the plan.** Leadership sets strategy *before* the DMP is finalized so scope matches funding — flipping the order produces an unfunded, misaligned plan.
- **Risk dial, not an on/off switch.** Robust DMSMS management does not mean "proactive everywhere." The job is to balance always-monitor high-risk items, broadly-available long-life items, and everything in between — and to document the reactive/proactive split and its accepted risk.
- **Window of opportunity.** Earlier identification = longer window = more (and cheaper) resolution options and lower readiness/schedule risk. This single idea justifies funding proactivity (Figure 7).
- **One "A" only.** On the ARCI chart, accountability is singular per decision per level; diffuse accountability is a planning defect.
- **Government owns the life-cycle perspective.** Contractors' responsibility spans only their period of performance; the DMP must give the government the information and provisions (true-LON buys, exit clauses, managing across contract breaks) to bridge the gaps.
- **Build it once, leverage it everywhere.** Don't build or buy bespoke tools, don't duplicate the prime's work, and don't build SME capability from scratch when centralized teams, independent SMEs, and existing DMSMS management systems already exist.
- **The BOM is the keystone.** Every monitoring, assessment, and resolution function depends on knowing what items are on the system; missing or incomplete BOMs cripple the whole effort.

## Anti-patterns
- **Delegating the strategic direction to the DMT.** Leadership that hands off the foundations risks a DMP whose scope of effort exceeds the funding obtained or diverges from leadership's expectations (Section 3.1).
- **Optimistic assumptions in the DMP.** Plans built on overly optimistic assumptions — e.g., simply assuming a contractor will sustain the system for life — can leave the government without the DMSMS data it needs if the actual approach turns out to be organic support (Sections 3.3, 3.3.3).
- **Stopping case tracking at funding rather than fielding.** Some program offices stop tracking a resolution once it is funded instead of through fielding; the source calls this a **bad practice**, because the DMT needs feedback on whether (and which) resolution was actually implemented to make good future decisions (Section 3.4.5).
- **Tolerating or ignoring process defects.** In the QMS, the quality criterion focuses on whether appropriate action is taken when a defect is found; compliance metrics should reveal whether defects are tolerated or, worse, ignored (Section 3.4.4.2).
- **Modifying COTS without weighing the implications.** Modifying a COTS assembly or its software can make it nonstandard and incompatible with the supplier's standard updates, forcing a later choice between costly re-revision and high support costs plus inevitable obsolescence (Section 3.4.2).
- **Chasing technical data and IP rights only when an issue arises.** Aggressively pursuing such rights only after a problem surfaces is discouraged; the data should instead be secured whenever it can be obtained (Section 3.3.6 footnote).

## Key Takeaways
1. **Prepare** is the first of the five DMSMS steps; it stands up the foundations, the DMT, the DMP, and the operational processes, and its outputs are living and iterative.
2. **Leadership sets the foundations before the DMP is finalized** so the documented scope matches the funding actually available and leadership's expectations.
3. **Start proactive, risk-based DMSMS management early** — as early as SRR — for both electronic and slower-moving MaSME items; treat software obsolescence (availability, not classic obsolescence) as real.
4. The **DMT** is chartered by the PM/PSM, scales and evolves with the system, and uses an **ARCI chart** (exactly one Accountable per decision per level); the PM is ultimately accountable.
5. A **DMP is mandatory** (DoDI 4245.15), developed by the DMT and approved by the PM, tailored, living, and aligned to the four-level **process-capability matrix** (Appendix D).
6. **Operations funding ≠ resolution funding**; budget without fiscal constraint first, then accept and document risk if funding falls short; expect a **startup spike** over steady-state effort.
7. **Advocate for DMSMS-resilient designs** (≥5-year forecasted availability where practical, open systems architecture, standardization, careful COTS/alternative-grade use) because early design choices lock in life-cycle cost.
8. **Record keeping** (Level 1 for all, Level 2 for robust programs) underpins budgeting, process improvement, and the **ROI** case for proactivity; aggregated, it serves PEO/Service HQ/OSD too.
9. Run DMSMS processes inside a **QMS** with a quality plan, compliance metrics, and LSS/DMAIC process analysis to keep quality consistent across people.
10. **Track cases from identification through fielding** (Open variants → Closed → Watch List); stopping at funding is a bad practice.
11. **Leverage primes, OEMs, independent SMEs, centralized SME teams, and existing tools**; the government keeps the life-cycle perspective via contract provisions (true-LON buys, exit clauses).
12. The **BOM is the most important data element** — without it, even reactive DMSMS management is extremely limited.
