# Chapter 5: A Framework of Digital Systems Engineering — Core Concepts

> Source note: synthesized from the arXiv preprint of *Towards Digital Engineering* (Huang et al.), Section 5 ("A Framework of Digital Systems Engineering," core-concepts material). This pack is grounded in the open arXiv PDF only, not the paywalled journal version. This chapter covers a thin slice of roughly 28 pages, so it stays close to the source's own definitions rather than extrapolating.

## Core Idea
Digital engineering is framed here as the *end state* of digitally transforming engineering practice — the place that digital transformation is heading toward. That transformation has two defining traits: engineering artifacts become digital, and engineering work happens inside a digital, connected environment. In that environment, one engineering process consumes digitalized products and services produced by other processes and emits new digitalized products and services of its own, with digital *models* sitting at the center. The author's central move in this section is to pin down a precise vocabulary — above all, separating *digitize* from *digitalize* — so that the rest of the framework (unique identification, digital augmentation, digitalized models) rests on a defined foundation rather than loose usage of "digital."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Digital transformation of engineering (Figure 3 in the source)** — a high-level picture in which a traditional engineering process is transformed into a *digital engineering process*. Inputs (natural resources, products and services from other processes, domain scientific knowledge, a specialty-plus-digital workforce) flow through the process and emerge as digitalized products and digitalized services, where each product is the original product plus a digital augmentation.
  - When/how to use: as the orienting diagram for what "going digital" actually changes — both *what* is produced (digitalized outputs) and *where* the work happens (a digital, connected environment fed by enabling technologies).
- **The four-part definition of "Digitalize"** — the source's formal definition (Section 5.1.1): (1) represent the item in a standard form with well-defined semantics so any type of machine can access it; (2) assign and keep a unique identifier for it; (3) create standard, well-defined-semantics metadata so digital technologies can manipulate it automatically; (4) bind that identifier and metadata uniquely to the item.
  - When/how to use: as the test for whether something is *digitalized* versus merely *digitized*. The author notes the unique binding of identifier and metadata to an artifact can be realized with mechanisms such as digital signatures and Blockchain.
- **Digitalized artifact = artifact + digital augmentation** — the source's structural definition (Section 5.1.3): a digitalized artifact is the artifact together with a *digital augmentation* made of three parts — a digital representation (standard form, machine-accessible), a uniquely associated identifier (for traceability and accountability), and associated metadata (standard form, well-defined semantics) enabling automated operation.
  - When/how to use: as the schema for describing any artifact once digitalized, whether the artifact is itself a digital object (model, dataset, document, picture) or a physical object (product, part).
- **Digitalized model + its metadata schema** — a specialization of the digitalized-artifact definition (Section 5.1.4) for models specifically, with a richer metadata breakdown: generic attributes; inputs/outputs/parameters; provenance; a utilization guide; security properties; and machine-processible access-control policies.
  - When/how to use: when the artifact being digitalized is a model — which the author argues is the most important artifact class for digital engineering, echoing the DoD Digital Engineering Strategy's emphasis on digital representation of the system of interest.
- **"Model of models"** — the author's name for using knowledge representation to explicitly capture the properties of, and logical relations among, different model types.
  - When/how to use: as the groundwork for building the ontologies that express models' digital augmentations.

## Key Concepts
- **Digitize vs. digitalize**: drawing on the Gartner glossary, *digitization* is the conversion from analog to digital form, while *digitalization* (in Gartner's business-oriented sense) is using digital technologies to change a business model and create new value. Technically, the author sharpens this: a merely *digitized* item may be computerized but is not yet in a standard form or annotated with the metadata that lets different machines access and operate on it automatically. So "digitalize" goes beyond "digitize."
  - Worked examples from the source: a loose-format image file is a digitized photo but not digitalized; a standardized-format photo carrying metadata (camera settings, device, time, place) is digitalized. Likewise, a scanned or typed-in book is digitized; an eBook with metadata for cross-reader display and rights management is digitalized.
- **Degrees of digitalization**: digitalization is a spectrum, not a binary. How far along an item sits depends on the fidelity of its digital representation, the depth of its semantics, the compatibility of its form, and the richness of its metadata — and those in turn set how much automated machine operation and interoperation is possible. The vague phrase "digital xxx" can land anywhere on this spectrum.
- **Unique identification (Section 5.1.2)**: a necessary component of digitalization because it underpins traceability and accountability. The author broadens "traceability" beyond requirement traceability in systems engineering to include tracing information flow, material flow, chains of causality, and chains of faults. Barcodes and RFID are cited as everyday illustrations of how powerful unique identification is.
- **Digital representation by artifact type**: for a digital object, the digital representation *is* the object itself; for a physical object, the representation can range from a full digital twin down to a picture or text description, its identifier can be a barcode or RFID code attached to it, and its metadata covers both the object's properties and those of its representation.
- **"Standard" means shared, precise syntax and semantics**: a "standard" form is one following precisely defined, community-shared syntax and semantics — which can mean conformance to an officially issued standard *or* to ontologies a community commonly uses.
- **Centralized standards vs. distributed ontologies**: officially issued standards are a centralized approach — efficient in the short term but possibly only a local optimum long term. Ontologies are a decentralized, crowdsourced, evolutionary, fine-grained route to standardization: a concept may appear in several published ontologies, and the most-reused version becomes the de facto standard. Short term, ontologies are less efficient (ontology mapping is often needed and can be costly); long term, they harness collective intelligence, incentivize innovation, and allow micro-level revision. A mature cluster of ontologies can eventually be adopted and issued as an official standard by an authority.
- **Scientific foundations**: the author elevates *formal logic* (the basis of knowledge representation & reasoning, ontologies, and formal semantics) together with *computing science* to the status of scientific foundations for digital engineering — observing that logic has been an essential but under-acknowledged foundation for conceptualization in systems engineering, with Dickerson & Mavris (2010) cited as a rare exception that engaged the issue.
- **Enabling technologies**: digital engineering is enabled by a stack that includes IoT, smart cyber-physical systems, Big Data technologies, AI and machine learning, digital twins, distributed trust, and Blockchain — and, critically, KR&R, ontology engineering, and the semantic web (all AI branches) for representing artifacts, especially models.

## Mental Models
- **"Digital" is a spectrum, not a switch.** Before claiming something is "digital," locate it on the digitization-to-full-digitalization continuum using four dials — representation fidelity, semantic depth, form compatibility, metadata richness. Those dials predict how much machines can actually do with it automatically.
- **An artifact is the thing plus its digital shadow.** Every digitalized artifact decomposes into representation + identifier + metadata. If any of the three is missing, you have a digitized thing, not a digitalized one.
- **Identity precedes accountability.** Traceability and accountability are downstream of unique identification — you cannot trace a flow, a cause, or a fault chain across organizations and life-cycle phases without first being able to name each item uniquely.
- **Standards and ontologies trade time horizon.** Reach for an official standard when you need short-term efficiency and central coordination; reach for ontologies when you want long-term evolvability and collective intelligence — and expect the mature ontology cluster to eventually *become* the standard.
- **Models deserve special treatment.** Because digital representation of the system of interest is the central theme of digital engineering, the model is the artifact class to digitalize most carefully — hence its richer metadata schema (provenance, utilization, security, access-control policies).

## Key Takeaways
1. Digital engineering is the destination of digital transformation: digitalized artifacts produced and consumed inside a digital, connected environment, with digital models at the center.
2. *Digitize* ≠ *digitalize* — to digitalize is to give an item a standard, machine-accessible representation, a unique identifier, and standard metadata, all uniquely bound together (the four-part definition).
3. A digitalized artifact = the artifact + a digital augmentation comprising digital representation, unique identifier, and associated metadata.
4. Unique identification is foundational because traceability and accountability — broadly meaning information, material, causality, and fault chains — depend on it.
5. Models get a specialized definition and a metadata schema spanning generic attributes, I/O and parameters, provenance, utilization guidance, security properties, and machine-processible access-control policies.
6. "Standard" means precisely defined, community-shared syntax and semantics — achievable via centralized official standards (short-term efficient) or distributed ontologies (long-term evolvable); the two are endpoints of one continuum.
7. Formal logic and computing science are positioned as the scientific foundations of digital engineering, with KR&R, ontologies, and the semantic web as the AI machinery for representing artifacts.
