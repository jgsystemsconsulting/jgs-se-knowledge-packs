# Chapter 5: Assessing MOSA, Managing Technology Change, and Product/Technology Roadmaps

> **Authority note:** This pack is built from the OUSD(R&E) *Implementing a MOSA in DoD Programs* guidebook (February 2025), which is the current MOSA implementation authority and supersedes the 2013 Open Systems Architecture (OSA) guidebook. Where this chapter discusses how to assess, measure, and roadmap MOSA, it reflects the February 2025 guidance, not the retired 2013 OSA framing.

## Core Idea
A Modular Open Systems Approach is only worth adopting if a program can show it is paying off and can keep it paying off across the system's life. This chapter covers the three activities that close that loop: **assessing** a MOSA against the benefits it is supposed to deliver, **managing technology change** so the modular structure actually enables refresh and insertion (and shields the program from obsolescence), and **roadmapping** product capability and technology so the timing of change is planned rather than reactive. The unifying logic is that modularity is the mechanism — isolating volatile technology areas behind stable, open interfaces — and assessment metrics, DMSMS mitigation, and roadmaps are how that mechanism is governed.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **MOSA benefit classes (the five-benefit assessment frame)** — Interoperability, Technology Refresh, Enhanced Competition, Innovation, and Cost Avoidance / Cost Savings. The guidebook states that MOSA metrics should align with these benefits and that most metrics will fall into one or more of these five classes.
  - When/how to use: The Program Management Office (PMO) selects the few metrics that matter for a given MOSA application — chosen by use case, by the level of system design being looked at, and by the phase of maturity — and aligns each to one or more benefit classes. A proposed architecture can be judged simply as pass-or-fail — except when a particular MOSA implementation is being evaluated and the supporting mechanisms are present (for example, a Work Breakdown Structure exists and the desired MOSA features have been identified), in which case a finer, metrics-based assessment applies.
- **Appendix A assessment methodology** — Appendix A of the guidebook lays out criteria and a methodology that PMOs and Program Managers (PMs) apply when they need to gauge a program's or system's MOSA.
  - When/how to use: Apply when a program needs a structured MOSA assessment; the specific metrics depend on the primary stakeholder's MOSA implementation and on the benefits that stakeholder desires or expects from it.
- **Product roadmap (e.g., AAF Software Product Roadmap)** — a high-level, adaptable view of the target value to be delivered over a designated period, based on technology insertion as indicated in an accompanying technology roadmap. The named exemplar is the DoD Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) Software Product Roadmap on the Defense Acquisition University AAF site.
  - When/how to use: To plan capability delivery over time and tie each increment of value to the technology that must be inserted to enable it.
- **Technology roadmap** — the companion to the product roadmap; it indicates the technology insertions that the product roadmap's value delivery depends on.
  - When/how to use: Paired with the product roadmap to answer the timing questions below — when capabilities are needed, when the enabling technology must be demonstrated, and when hardware/software changes must be producible.

## Key Concepts
- **Metrics align to benefits, not to ceremony.** The collection of possible metrics across the many MOSA use cases, design levels, and maturity phases is reduced to the *necessary few* for the specific application. Most existing general engineering metrics can be adapted to MOSA needs, so programs rarely need to invent metrics from scratch.
- **PMO ownership of metric selection.** For the use cases judged most probable, the PMO sets the metrics, pulling from DoD, Service, and other Government guidance as well as industry practice and existing metric sources.
- **Pass/fail vs. measured assessment.** An organization can evaluate a proposed architecture pass/fail, *unless* it is assessing a specific MOSA implementation with the supporting mechanisms in place (e.g., a WBS, identified desired MOSA features) — in which case a more granular, metrics-based assessment is appropriate.
- **Technology management is life-cycle-critical.** Successfully managing technology is vital to acquiring and maintaining a complex system throughout its life cycle. MOSA is one important approach because it supports technology updates and integration — adopting newer technologies to improve reliability, lower sustainment cost, and enhance warfighting capability against evolving demands.
- **DMSMS as the central obsolescence risk.** Diminishing Manufacturing Sources and Material Shortages (DMSMS) complicate technology insertion and refreshment. Mitigation requires involving DMSMS management practitioners in the insertion/refresh planning process; working with program offices, they help identify issues early and develop strategies to address them.
- **Open standards as the supply-chain lever.** Consensus-based open standards and interfaces let components from multiple vendors integrate and interoperate, enabling alternative sources for critical components and reducing single-supplier dependence. Equivalent components can be substituted from other suppliers, which directly mitigates DMSMS risk.
- **MOSA's compounding supply-chain effects.** MOSA reduces vendor lock-in (easier to switch suppliers if a vendor discontinues support or exits the market), promotes competition (encouraging innovation and reducing single-source dependence), and simplifies maintenance and upgrade because individual components can be replaced or upgraded without disturbing the whole system. The net effect is a more resilient supply chain.
- **DMSMS oversight coordination.** A program's DMSMS planning should be coordinated upward with USD(A&S) — the acquisition-and-sustainment office under the relevant Under Secretary of Defense — which sets oversight and guidance on acquisition and sustainment policy (DMSMS management included), so that program plans stay aligned with DoD-wide policy and best practice. Referenced sources for DMSMS detail are DoDI 4245.15 along with SD-19, SD-22, and SD-26.
- **Roadmaps isolate volatility.** Product and technology roadmaps manage MOSA by using modularity to isolate the technology areas expected to evolve rapidly or to offer capability-improvement opportunities. They help programs identify capability improvements, safety enhancements, and other new requirements.
- **The roadmap timing questions.** Roadmaps are built around five timing questions: when new capabilities need to be in place; when the enabling technology must be proven out in a relevant or operational setting; when the related hardware and software changes have to be producible; what funding is planned; and how the mission profile shifts.
- **MOSA and technology management are mutually reinforcing.** Identifying modular interfaces makes substituting newer/alternative technologies easier — reducing the time and cost to integrate. Conversely, refreshment and insertion plans can inform a priority scheme for *which* parts of the system to make modular first.

## Mental Models
- **The "necessary few" filter.** Don't ask "what can we measure?" — ask "which few metrics, mapped to the five benefit classes, actually tell us whether this specific MOSA implementation is delivering for its primary stakeholder?" The universe of candidate metrics collapses once use case, design level, and maturity phase are fixed.
- **Modularity is the verb; refresh is the payoff.** The interface boundary is what you design; technology refresh, competition, and DMSMS resilience are what that boundary buys you. If a module's boundary doesn't make substitution easier, it isn't earning its place.
- **Two roadmaps, one clock.** The product roadmap says *what value, when*; the technology roadmap says *what must be ready, when*, to deliver it. Reading them together turns "we'll upgrade eventually" into dated, funded, demonstrable commitments.
- **Bidirectional priority.** MOSA decides where technology change is cheap to absorb; technology plans decide where MOSA is worth investing in first. Let each inform the other rather than treating modularity decisions and refresh plans as separate workstreams.

## Anti-patterns
The source does not explicitly name anti-patterns in this slice. The following are stated as risks the guidance is designed to counter, framed as conditions to avoid:
- **Vendor lock-in / single-source dependence** — the source presents MOSA as reducing vendor lock-in and the likelihood of single-source dependencies; the implied failure mode is a closed design that traps the program with one supplier.
- **Unmanaged DMSMS** — entering technology insertion/refresh without DMSMS practitioners involved leaves obsolescence and material-shortage risk unaddressed, which the source explicitly warns complicates these efforts.

## Key Takeaways
1. Align MOSA metrics to the **five benefit classes**: Interoperability, Technology Refresh, Enhanced Competition, Innovation, and Cost Avoidance/Cost Savings — and reduce them to the necessary few for the specific application.
2. The **PMO** owns metric selection, drawing on DoD/Service/Government guidance and industry practice; most existing engineering metrics can be adapted rather than newly invented.
3. Use **pass/fail** for a proposed architecture, but switch to metrics-based assessment when evaluating a specific implementation with mechanisms (e.g., a WBS, identified MOSA features) in place; **Appendix A** provides the criteria and methodology.
4. MOSA is a primary tool for **managing technology change** across the life cycle — it supports updates, refresh, and integration to improve reliability, cut sustainment cost, and enhance capability.
5. **Open standards and interfaces** mitigate **DMSMS** by enabling multi-vendor sourcing, component substitution, reduced lock-in, and easier component-level maintenance/upgrade — building a more resilient supply chain.
6. Coordinate DMSMS planning with **USD(A&S)**; consult **DoDI 4245.15, SD-19, SD-22, SD-26** for detail.
7. **Product and technology roadmaps** (e.g., the AAF Software Product Roadmap) use modularity to isolate volatile technology and plan change around capability need, technology demonstration, producibility, funding, and mission-profile changes.
8. MOSA and technology management are **mutually beneficial**: modular interfaces ease technology substitution, and refresh/insertion plans can prioritize where to apply MOSA first.
