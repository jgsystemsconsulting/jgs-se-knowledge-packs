# Chapter 4: Technical Considerations — WBS Taxonomy, Requirements, Standards, Software, Cybersecurity, and COTS

> Source: *Implementing a MOSA in DoD Programs* (OUSD R&E, February 2025), §3.2 Technical Considerations. This Feb 2025 guidebook is the **current DoD authority** on MOSA implementation and supersedes the 2013 Open Systems Architecture (OSA) guidebook — treat earlier OSA guidance as historical context, not standing direction.

## Core Idea
Implementing a Modular Open Systems Approach is not only a management decision; it forces specific technical choices that the program manager (PM) and lead systems engineer must make as they build the system's architecture. §3.2 walks through six technical levers — a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) used as an architectural taxonomy, how MOSA reshapes requirements, the selection and profiling of standards, software-specific modularity, cybersecurity, and the special hazards of commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) parts. The unifying message is that modularity and openness must be *designed in* through these technical decisions, not bolted on afterward.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) as a MOSA taxonomy** — built and maintained by the PM or lead systems engineer across the life cycle, structured so that severable major components and modular systems can be added, removed, or replaced incrementally. *When/how:* prepared early via the systems engineering process; Table 3-1 lays out a step sequence from aligning MOSA principles through monitoring and integrating modules.
- **MIL-STD-881F, *Work Breakdown Structures for Defense Materiel Items*** — the mandatory WBS standard for programs subject to DoDI 5000.02. *When/how:* applied by the PMO or lead systems engineer to set the proper system hierarchy and architecture, locate hardware/software interface points, and support functional decomposition and data-flow traces; its product-oriented structure anchors the MOSA taxonomy and can also guide software MOSA decomposition.
- **MIL-STD-196G system taxonomy** — the naming hierarchy of System, Subsystems, Centers, Centrals, Sets, Group, and Units. *When/how:* used to pick consistent taxonomic conventions when defining levels of modularity for a successful MOSA implementation.
- **DoDI 5000.02, *Operation of the Adaptive Acquisition Framework*** — the acquisition framework that triggers MIL-STD-881F applicability.
- **10 U.S.C. 4401** — statute directing a preference for consensus-based standards when choosing among competing options.
- **ASSIST database (assist.dla.mil) and its MOSS area** — the defense/federal repository of specifications, standards, handbooks, and data item descriptions; its **Modular and Open Standards and Specifications (MOSS)** area holds MOSA-specific guidance. *When/how:* searched by PMs/engineers when identifying and selecting standards.
- **Systems Engineering Guidebook (2022)** — referenced for MOSA requirements and their verification/validation at technical design reviews.
- **API Technical Guidance (SE&A, July 2024 or latest)** — referenced for the API-first software design approach.
- **DoDI 5000.87, *Operation of the Software Acquisition Pathway*** — referenced for software data-rights and software acquisition handling.
- **FACE® (Future Airborne Capability Environment) and OMS (Open Mission System)** — named as prescriptive, consensus-based integration standards in the novel software-cybersecurity approach.

## Key Concepts
- **WBS aligned to MOSA principles**: Table 3-1's first step is to align program end goals with the MOSA principles that support them — modularity, openness, scalability, flexibility, and reusability — then refine the WBS to call out modular components, interfaces, data-exchange needs, and testing/validation. Later steps prioritize modules, define module boundaries (I/O requirements, data formats and protocols, APIs and SDKs, test requirements), allocate tasks and resources, establish a modular development process (Agile, CI/CD, modular test), monitor module development (progress, dependencies, risk), and finally integrate and test against business and technical requirements.
- **Modularity spans hardware and software**: the same WBS approach applies to hardware, software, or an integrated assembly of both, so corresponding modules can be replaced or upgraded together.
- **Traceability and programmatic consequences**: capturing interfaces and modules in the WBS increases life-cycle traceability. Beyond technical criteria, the PMO must weigh programmatic consequences from intellectual property (IP) to the supply chain at every level of the hierarchy, and the WBS should articulate the level of modularity needed to support competition and technology refresh.
- **MOSA changes how requirements are written**: requirements for modularity and openness affect both technical solutions and business strategy. Whether stated at the SoS, system, subsystem, or lower level, they remain ordinary requirements to be managed within the design specification — but their expression and composition parameters depend on the design level, development phase, and existing maturity or constraints. MOSA success is verified and validated at each technical baseline through design reviews. Figure 3-2 depicts an iterative dual-path process (a design path and a funding path) for building MOSA attributes in rather than adding them on.
- **Standardization purpose**: standards underpin compatibility, interchangeability, commonality, and interoperability across DoD and allies/partners, and enable rapid, cost-effective technology insertion. Standards are foundational to MOSA because they enable modularity, interoperability, and flexibility.
- **Standard selection and profiling**: aside from a small set of standards required by law (life/safety, hazardous-materials transport, treaty, environmental), engineers select standards that best fit program requirements, favoring consensus-based ones per 10 U.S.C. 4401. When multiple standards are in play, the PMO should build a **profile** that limits the variability that would otherwise impede interoperability. Standards applied at key interfaces support supplier competition; standards guidance should specify implementation, conformance, and which organizations verify it, backed by a life-cycle test plan and verification methods.
- **Software modularity with hardware-grade rigor**: software should be designed with the same modular discipline as hardware so both can be upgraded concurrently; interfaces must be defined precisely to cut complexity at technology refresh. Software partitioning starts at high capability levels and drives down into specific DoD domains, separating functionality into independent, interchangeable modules that enable product-line common software libraries.
- **API-first design**: when architecting MOSA software, the PMO should develop an Application Programming Interface (API) approach emphasizing modularity, scalability, and reusability — designing the API *before* the underlying system.
- **Software reference architecture and data model**: a software architectural lexicon or reference architecture portrays the levels of software decomposition; MIL-STD-881F can guide software MOSA taxonomy; a data model is identified at varying fidelity, and modular software **data rights** are declared at the appropriate levels of abstraction.
- **Hardware vs. software cybersecurity**: hardware cyber needs (cross-domain guards, firewalls, encrypt/decrypt chips) can be met through multiple suppliers of form-fit-function items. Software cybersecurity is harder: the historical proprietary-transport-service approach only works if there are multiple competing proprietary solutions that avoid costly re-integration. A **novel approach** models software cybersecurity requirements using a life-cycle DevSecOps toolchain with MBSE, prescriptive consensus-based integration standards (FACE, OMS), and Agile methods (CI/CD, containers), letting the toolchain convert models into transport-service functionality (Figure 3-3).
- **COTS adaptation**: applying MOSA features to COTS components or existing systems requires first understanding the products thoroughly; industry recommends a **business case analysis** to decide whether and how far to apply MOSA requirements to commercial products.

## Mental Models
- The WBS is the *taxonomy that turns MOSA principles into structure*: principles (modularity, openness, scalability, flexibility, reusability) go in at the top of Table 3-1, and a product-oriented hierarchy with explicit interfaces and modules comes out — the same artifact then carries traceability, competition, and technology-refresh decisions.
- "Built in, not added on" (Figure 3-2): MOSA requirements are written into the specification at the right design level from the start, and verified at each technical baseline — retrofitting modularity onto a mature design fights the constraints rather than using them.
- Standards are *options waiting to diverge*: two implementations of the same standard can fail to interoperate because of differing options and defaults, so a **profile** that pins down the variability is what actually delivers interoperability.
- For software cybersecurity, model first and let the toolchain generate the secure transport — the DevSecOps + MBSE + consensus-standards path is offered as the modern alternative to locking in a single proprietary transport service.
- COTS is a *benefits-vs-fragility trade*: a design change to a COTS-based item can trigger heavy regression testing or recertification of the base platform, which can erase the savings MOSA was meant to capture — so run the business case before committing.

## Anti-patterns
- **Poor software development practices that obscure modular components** — the source warns that weak practices can hide a system's modularity, so programs should hold to the foundational practices of modularity and interface transparency in both software and hardware.
- **COTS design changes that negate MOSA benefits** — the source notes that, for COTS implementations, a design change may force substantial regression testing or even recertification of the base platform, negating any projected MOSA benefit (one reason for the up-front business case analysis).

## Key Takeaways
1. Use the **WBS as a MOSA taxonomy** — built by the PM/lead systems engineer, aligned to MOSA principles (modularity, openness, scalability, flexibility, reusability), and structured so modules can be added, removed, or replaced over the life cycle (Table 3-1).
2. **MIL-STD-881F** is the mandatory, product-oriented WBS standard for DoDI 5000.02 programs and anchors both hardware and software MOSA decomposition; **MIL-STD-196G** supplies the system-naming taxonomy.
3. MOSA-related **requirements** are managed like any others but expressed according to design level, phase, and constraints, and are verified/validated at each technical baseline — built in, not added on (Figure 3-2).
4. Select standards that fit the program, favor **consensus-based** standards (10 U.S.C. 4401), search **ASSIST/MOSS**, and write a **profile** to limit interoperability-killing variability; require implementation, conformance, and verification details.
5. Design **software** with hardware-grade modular rigor, take an **API-first** approach, use a reference architecture and data model, and declare modular **data rights** at the right abstraction (DoDI 5000.87).
6. Handle **cybersecurity** through multi-supplier form-fit-function hardware and, for software, a DevSecOps + MBSE + consensus-standard (FACE/OMS) + Agile approach instead of a single proprietary transport service.
7. Treat **COTS** cautiously — run a **business case analysis** first, because obsolescence, proprietary data, multiple configurations, and recertification risk can negate MOSA benefits.
