# Chapter 8: AI RMF Profiles and Use

## Core Idea
AI RMF Profiles are context-specific implementations of the framework's functions, categories, and subcategories; Current and Target Profiles reveal gaps to prioritise, and the voluntary, non-prescriptive design of the AI RMF allows organisations to adapt it to their sector, size, and risk tolerance.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **AI RMF Profile**: An implementation of GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions tailored to a specific organisation, sector, technology, or use case.
  - When to use: Operationalising the AI RMF for a specific application (e.g., hiring, fair housing, large language models) or benchmarking current against target state.
- **Current Profile**: A description of how AI is currently being managed and the related risks and outcomes in terms of present activities.
  - When to use: Baselining an organisation's existing AI risk management posture.
- **Target Profile**: A description of the outcomes needed to achieve desired AI risk management goals.
  - When to use: Setting improvement objectives and planning gap remediation.
- **Cross-Sectoral Profile**: A profile covering risks of models or processes common across multiple sectors (e.g., large language models, cloud services, procurement).
  - When to use: Governing AI technologies or business processes that cut across industry boundaries.
- **AI RMF Playbook**: Companion online resource providing suggested tactical actions organisations can apply within their own contexts to navigate the Core functions.
  - When to use: As a practical implementation guide alongside the AI RMF; voluntary and adaptable.

## Key Concepts
- **Gap analysis**: Comparison of Current and Target Profiles to reveal the outcomes that must be achieved to meet risk management objectives; drives prioritised action planning.
- **Temporal profile**: A profile characterising either current state or target state at a given point in time within a specific sector, industry, organisation, or application context.
- **Use-case profile**: A profile instantiated for a specific application setting (e.g., AI RMF hiring profile, AI RMF fair housing profile) based on the organisation's requirements, risk tolerance, and resources.
- **Voluntary adoption**: The AI RMF is non-mandatory; organisations may select from categories and subcategories according to their needs and resources.
- **Non-prescriptive design**: The AI RMF provides a catalogue of outcomes rather than one-size-fits-all requirements, allowing flexible application across all sectors and organisation sizes.
- **Living document**: The AI RMF is intended to be updated as technology, standards, and community experience evolve; formal review expected no later than 2028.

## Mental Models
- Use Current Profile → Target Profile → Gap Analysis → Action Plan as the operational cycle for any organisation adopting the AI RMF; this transforms the framework from reference material into a managed improvement programme.
- Use cross-sectoral profiles when the same AI capability (e.g., an LLM) is deployed across multiple business units or sectors; avoid duplicating governance effort by establishing shared standards.
- Use Playbook suggestions as starting points for subcategory implementation; they are voluntary and can be customised or replaced with organisation-specific equivalents.

## Key Takeaways
1. Profiles are the mechanism by which the AI RMF becomes organisation-specific; without a profile, the framework remains generic guidance.
2. The Current-to-Target gap analysis is the engine of continual improvement; it converts framework aspirations into prioritised, resourced action plans.
3. The AI RMF does not prescribe profile templates; this flexibility is intentional and allows for adaptation across sectors, organisation sizes, and use cases.
4. Cross-sectoral profiles are valuable for foundation model deployments or cloud-based AI services used across multiple business contexts.
5. The AI RMF is a living document; organisations should plan for periodic re-profiling as the framework evolves and as their AI portfolios mature.
6. Framework effectiveness should be periodically self-evaluated by users against their own policies, processes, practices, and expected outcomes.

## Connects To
- **GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE functions**: Profiles are instantiations of these functions for specific contexts; all Core content feeds into profile construction.
- **NIST AI RMF Playbook**: The tactical companion resource that provides suggested actions for each subcategory; available at the NIST Trustworthy and Responsible AI Resource Center.
- **ISO/IEC 42001**: AI management system standard whose plan-do-check-act cycle aligns with the Current-Target-Gap-Action profile methodology.
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework**: Profiles are structurally equivalent to CSF profiles; organisations already using CSF profiles can extend the model to AI risk.
