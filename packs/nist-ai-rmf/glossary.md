# NIST AI RMF 1.0 Glossary

**Accuracy** — Closeness of results of observations, computations, or estimates to the true or accepted values (ISO/IEC TS 5723:2022); must be paired with realistic, deployment-representative test sets. (Ch 3)

**Accountability** — Organisational obligation to answer for AI system decisions and outcomes; presupposes transparency and is shared across all AI actors involved in the lifecycle. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Affected individuals/communities** — Individuals, groups, or communities directly or indirectly impacted by AI systems or their outputs; they do not necessarily interact with the deployed system. (Ch 2)

**AI actor** — Any individual or organisation that plays an active role in the AI system lifecycle, including designers, developers, deployers, operators, evaluators, and impacted communities (OECD 2019). (Ch 2)

**AI RMF Core** — The four-function structure (GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE) that organises AI risk management activities at the highest level. (Ch 3, Ch 4–7)

**AI RMF Playbook** — Voluntary companion online resource providing suggested tactical actions for navigating each AI RMF function, category, and subcategory. (Ch 8)

**AI RMF Profile** — A context-specific implementation of the AI RMF functions, categories, and subcategories tailored to an organisation, sector, technology, or use case. (Ch 8)

**AI system** — An engineered or machine-based system that, for a given set of objectives, generates outputs such as predictions, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments; operates with varying levels of autonomy (OECD 2019; ISO/IEC 22989:2022). (Ch 2)

**Bias (computational/statistical)** — Bias in AI datasets or algorithmic processes, often stemming from systematic errors due to non-representative samples. (Ch 3)

**Bias (human-cognitive)** — Bias in how individuals or groups perceive and act on AI system information, or how they think about AI system purposes; present throughout the AI lifecycle. (Ch 3)

**Bias (systemic)** — Bias present in AI datasets, organisational norms and practices, and broader society; can occur without prejudice or discriminatory intent. (Ch 3)

**Context of use** — The specific application setting, user population, legal environment, and societal factors that shape an AI system's risks and benefits. (Ch 5)

**Cross-sectoral profile** — An AI RMF profile covering risks of models or processes common across multiple use cases or sectors. (Ch 8)

**Current Profile** — A description of how AI risk is currently being managed and the related outcomes in terms of present activities. (Ch 8)

**Emergent risk** — Risk that arises as AI systems adapt, evolve, or are used beyond intended scope; may not be detectable at initial deployment. (Ch 1)

**Explainability** — A representation of the mechanisms underlying an AI system's operation; answers the question "how" a decision was made. (Ch 3)

**Fairness** — An AI trustworthiness characteristic addressing equality, equity, harmful bias, and discrimination; systems with balanced demographic predictions are not necessarily fair. (Ch 3)

**GOVERN function** — The cross-cutting AI RMF function that cultivates organisational risk management culture, establishes policies and accountability, and enables MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE. (Ch 4)

**Gap analysis** — Comparison of Current and Target Profiles to identify outcomes that must be achieved to meet risk management objectives; drives prioritised action plans. (Ch 8)

**Human-cognitive bias** — See Bias (human-cognitive). (Ch 3)

**Inscrutability** — Opacity of AI systems arising from limited explainability, lack of documentation, or inherent uncertainty; complicates risk measurement. (Ch 1)

**Interpretability** — The meaning of AI system output in the context of its designed functional purposes; answers the question "why" a decision has that meaning to the user. (Ch 3)

**Living document** — The AI RMF's design principle that the framework will be updated as technology, standards, and community experience evolve; formal review expected by 2028. (Ch 8)

**MANAGE function** — The AI RMF function that allocates resources to prioritised risks, executes risk treatment plans, monitors deployed systems, and communicates about incidents. (Ch 7)

**MAP function** — The AI RMF function that establishes contextual knowledge — purposes, stakeholders, capabilities, impacts, risks — needed to frame risks before measurement or management. (Ch 5)

**MEASURE function** — The AI RMF function that applies quantitative, qualitative, or mixed-method tools to analyse, benchmark, and monitor AI risk and trustworthiness characteristics. (Ch 6)

**Privacy-Enhanced** — An AI trustworthiness characteristic reflecting design that safeguards human autonomy, identity, and dignity through anonymity, confidentiality, and consent-based data practices. (Ch 3)

**Reliability** — Ability of an item to perform as required, without failure, for a given time interval, under given conditions (ISO/IEC TS 5723:2022). (Ch 3)

**Residual risk** — Risk remaining after risk treatment; directly impacts end users and affected individuals and communities (ISO GUIDE 73). (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**Risk** — Composite measure of an event's probability of occurring and the magnitude of the consequences of the corresponding event (ISO 31000:2018). (Ch 1)

**Risk culture** — Organisational attitudes, norms, and practices that prioritise identification and management of AI risks; driven from senior leadership downward. (Ch 4)

**Risk management** — Coordinated activities to direct and control an organisation with regard to risk (ISO 31000:2018). (Ch 1)

**Risk prioritisation** — Allocating risk management resources according to the severity and likelihood of identified risks; highest-risk systems require most urgent and thorough treatment. (Ch 1)

**Risk tolerance** — The organisation's or AI actor's readiness to bear risk in order to achieve its objectives; contextual, use-case specific, and subject to change (ISO GUIDE 73). (Ch 1)

**Robustness (generalizability)** — Ability of a system to maintain its level of performance under a variety of circumstances, including unanticipated uses (ISO/IEC TS 5723:2022). (Ch 3)

**Safe** — An AI trustworthiness characteristic: the system does not, under defined conditions, lead to a state in which human life, health, property, or the environment is endangered (ISO/IEC TS 5723:2022). (Ch 3)

**Secure and Resilient** — An AI trustworthiness characteristic: secure systems maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability; resilient systems withstand adverse events and degrade gracefully. (Ch 3)

**Socio-technical** — Describing AI systems and their risks as shaped by both technical factors and societal dynamics, including human behaviour, organisational norms, and deployment context. (Ch 1)

**Target Profile** — A description of the outcomes needed to achieve desired AI risk management goals; paired with a Current Profile for gap analysis. (Ch 8)

**TEVV (Test, Evaluation, Verification, and Validation)** — Integrated assurance process performed throughout the AI lifecycle by actors separate from front-line developers; confirms system trustworthiness and tracks risks. (Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Third-party risk** — Risk introduced by external data, software, or hardware components; the deploying organisation retains responsibility for managing this risk regardless of component origin. (Ch 1, Ch 4)

**Transparency** — The extent to which information about an AI system and its outputs is available to individuals interacting with it; answers the question "what happened" in the system. (Ch 3)

**Trustworthiness** — A social concept reflecting the degree to which an AI system can be relied upon; determined by the balance of all seven trustworthiness characteristics in context; only as strong as its weakest characteristic. (Ch 3)

**Validation** — Confirmation, through objective evidence, that requirements for a specific intended use or application have been fulfilled (ISO 9000:2015). (Ch 3)
