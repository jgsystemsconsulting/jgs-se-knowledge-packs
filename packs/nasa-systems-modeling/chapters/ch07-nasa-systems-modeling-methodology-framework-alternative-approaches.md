# Chapter — Methodology, Framework, and Alternative Modeling Approaches

## Core Idea

The methodology and framework that NASA-HDBK-1009A puts forward are not invented from scratch — they are borrowed and tailored from two named, externally published sources, and then bound to NASA's own SE structure. The methodology side comes from OOSEM; the framework side comes from the MBSE Grid. Both are introduced in the body of the Handbook (sections 4.3.2 and 4.3.3) and then given their full background treatment in Appendix B, which is where this chapter's source slice lives. The purpose of that appendix is explicitly to explain where these two pieces came from and how they were adapted to fit the NASA SE Engine.

Sitting underneath the borrowed methodology and framework is the same anchoring principle that runs through the whole document: the metamodel of NASA SE elements and relationships (section 7, Figures 7-1 and 7-2) is presented as *one* approach to modeling in support of the NASA SE Engine — not the only one. Appendix D then makes that explicit by walking through several alternative modeling approaches that implement the same NASA SE elements differently. The throughline is that the method stays tool-agnostic SysML tied to NPR 7123.1: SysML v1 is used to illustrate, but the SE-level structure is what governs, and multiple modeling patterns can populate it. This is the current Revision A baseline.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **OOSEM (Object-Oriented Systems Engineering Method).** Introduced in Appendix B as the source of the Handbook's modeling methodology. The appendix describes OOSEM as a systems-level development method that fuses object-oriented concepts with conventional systems-engineering practice. Its top-level process is shown in the OOSEM System Development Workflow figure, with the primary process steps in blue and secondary steps in white. Two specific OOSEM steps — Update Modeling Plan and Setup Model — are the ones the Handbook pulled into its section 4.3.2 methodology. The appendix notes that the System Design Process steps from the NASA SE Handbook closely resemble the steps in OOSEM's "Specify and Design System" process. OOSEM is cited to the INCOSE OOSEM Process Baseline model (the 1/2020 baseline).

- **MBSE Grid (and its Traceability view).** Introduced as the source of the Handbook's modeling framework. The Handbook leverages the MBSE Grid and tailors it to the NASA SE Engine. In its native form the Grid lays out project life-cycle phases down the rows and the SysML modeling pillars across the columns; each cross-section holds the metamodel content — the system modeling elements and their relationships captured across multiple SysML diagrams. The Grid is cited to the 2017 INCOSE International Symposium paper by Morkevicius, Aleksandraviciene, Mazeika, Bisikirskiene, and Strolia — their simplified, SysML-based approach to modeling complex systems (titled "MBSE Grid").

- **MBSE Grid Metamodel.** A companion figure (B.2-2) that captures the same element types and relationships as the Grid, but rendered in the same notation the Handbook uses for its own NASA SE metamodel in section 7 (Figures 7-1 and 7-2). Its purpose is to let you put the Grid's metamodel and the NASA SE metamodel side by side in one consistent format.

- **MBSE Grid with Diagram Call-Outs / Grid in Grid Format (Appendix C).** A populated example showing NASA SE Processes on the rows and the four SysML pillars on the columns, with the actual diagrams and tables from this Handbook dropped into the cross-sections. Appendix C is the worked instance of the framework.

- **Property-Based Requirement (PBR) approach (Appendix D.2).** An alternative for requirements modeling. PBR classes let a requirement carry structure, numerical attributes, and constraints so it can support requirements analysis. The PBR element is described as an extension of the SysML abstractRequirement, extendedRequirement, and Block. When mapped onto the section 7 metamodel, every element in the Requirements pillar is typed as a PBR Requirement.

- **Scenario Modeling approach (Appendix D.3).** An extension to behavior modeling, useful for ConOps development. It introduces a Scenario Modeling Context Block that acts as a bridge between Activities and State Machines, using directed composition to the system of interest (any Structure-pillar element) and to relevant Activities (Behavior-pillar elements). It supports nominal, off-nominal, and multi-level scenarios and can feed simulation and analysis. Sourced to The OpenSE Cookbook.

- **System Specification Modeling approach (Appendix D.4).** A pattern relating Structure-pillar elements to the Requirements pillar. It adds Logical Design, Logical Node Design, and Physical Design blocks that trace to Systems, Subsystems, and Components by directed composition, plus a System Specification Block that ties those structure blocks to requirements (via generalization to the structure elements and directed composition to PBR-based requirement elements). Sourced to The OpenSE Cookbook.

- **Verification Modeling approach (Appendix D.5).** The OpenSE Cookbook's requirements verification pattern, built to support V&V simulation. It uses a Verification Context Block linked to the System Context element, owning a parametric diagram; the System Context's part property defines the verification scope, which can include the System Specification Block, the Scenario Modeling Context Block, or any other Structure-pillar element. Sourced to The OpenSE Cookbook (the Thirty Meter Telescope work, Karban et al., 2018).

- **NASA SE Competency Model (Appendix A).** Referenced as the descriptive backing for the common technical processes shown in the SE Engine. It restates that NPR 7123.1 organizes work into three process sets — system design, product realization, and technical management — and is sourced to the NASA SE Handbook (NASA/SP-2016-6105, Revision 2).

## Key Concepts

**The methodology is borrowed, not invented.** The Handbook's modeling method does not originate inside NASA — it lifts two concrete steps (Update Modeling Plan, Setup Model) from OOSEM and slots them into the NASA approach. The justification is structural similarity: OOSEM's "Specify and Design System" process and NASA's System Design Process steps look alike, so the graft is natural rather than forced.

**The Grid is a problem/solution matrix.** In the MBSE Grid, the life-cycle phases split horizontally into two sections. The "problem" section defines and builds understanding of the problem; the "solution" section supplies one or more design alternatives to that problem. The problem section subdivides into two rows: "black box" (the conceptual representation) and "white box" (the technical description).

**The Grid maps cleanly onto NASA's four System Design Processes.** The mapping is explicit. Stakeholder Expectation Definition occupies the Black Box row. Technical Requirements Definition and Logical Decomposition map to the White Box. Design Solution Definition maps to the Solution layer. The Grid's life-cycle phases correspond to processes 1 through 9 of the NASA SE Engine, and extra rows can be appended to cover the product realization process steps.

**The metamodel differences are additive and traceability-focused.** When you compare the MBSE Grid's native metamodel against the NASA-SE-element metamodel of section 7, the differences are mostly additions that sharpen traceability: an explicit call-out of goals and objectives traced from stakeholder needs; mission-level behavior and structure elements; validation requirements/statements tracing to objectives; verification requirements/statements tracing to technical requirements; refined "refines" relationships linking requirements and behavior at the same decomposition level; allocations between the requirements and structure pillars; a decompose relationship from a level above to component-level behavior and structure; and the addition of Measure of Performance (MOP) and Technical Performance Measure (TPM) value-property types.

**The cross-section is where diagrams get filed.** Once the Grid is tailored to NASA — processes on the rows, the four SysML pillars on the columns — its cross-sections become organizing slots. The Handbook uses them to hold the model's SysML diagrams and tables, and Appendix C shows a populated grid using this Handbook's own figures. The same diagram or table can legitimately appear in more than one row (for example, behavior content shows up in both the Logical Decomposition and Design Solution rows), reused with content tuned to each process.

**The alternative approaches all extend a single pillar.** Appendix D is organized by pillar. PBR extends the Requirements pillar; Scenario Modeling extends the Behavior pillar; System Specification adds a bridge from Structure to Requirements; Verification Modeling adds a V&V/parametric analysis layer over the System Context. They are not competing whole methodologies — they are targeted extensions that still resolve back to the section 7 metamodel elements.

**The interface metamodel is its own depiction.** Appendix E adds a metamodel of functional and structural interfaces — how functions interface with functions, how structural elements interface with other structural elements, and how both relate to interface requirements. It reuses the Handbook's convention of putting the SysML-specific element or relationship type in square brackets (block, activity, and so on), keeping the SE-level structure separable from the language used to express it.

## Mental Models

**Two dials, two sources.** Hold the methodology and the framework as separate dials with separate suppliers. The methodology dial (what order you do things in) is set by OOSEM. The framework dial (where things go) is set by the MBSE Grid. NASA tailored both, but knowing the origin tells you where to look when you need depth — OOSEM's baseline for the workflow steps, the Morkevicius et al. paper for the Grid.

**Black box / white box / solution is the spine of the Grid.** When you read the Grid, read it top to bottom as a narrative: black box states the problem conceptually, white box describes it technically, and the solution layer answers it with one or more design alternatives. NASA's four System Design Processes drape directly over those rows, so the Grid is really NASA's design flow turned sideways into a matrix.

**The metamodel "differences" are a traceability upgrade.** Don't read the bullet list of differences between the Grid metamodel and the NASA SE metamodel as disagreements. Read them as additions that wire more threads of traceability into the model — goals to needs, validation to objectives, verification to requirements, allocations across pillars, MOPs and TPMs as first-class values. The Grid version is the more traceability-rich rendering of the same intent.

**Appendix D is a menu, not a doctrine.** Treat the alternative approaches as a menu you select from per project, each one a pluggable extension to a single pillar. Because the Handbook's metamodel is declared to be just one approach, picking PBR for requirements or the OpenSE verification pattern for V&V is a legitimate substitution — provided you can still trace your constructs back to the NASA SE elements. The right move is to record whichever choices you make in the project's modeling plan.

**The model stays the source; the language stays swappable.** Everything in this slice reinforces the document's tool-agnostic stance. The Grid's columns are SysML pillars and the alternatives are written in SysML v1, but the bracket convention in the interface metamodel and the "one approach" framing of section 7 keep the SE structure independent of the notation. SysML is the illustration; NPR 7123.1's elements are the contract.

## Key Takeaways

- The Handbook's methodology is OOSEM-derived: it pulls the Update Modeling Plan and Setup Model steps from the OOSEM System Development Workflow, justified by OOSEM's "Specify and Design System" process resembling NASA's System Design Process.
- The Handbook's framework is the MBSE Grid (Morkevicius et al., 2017), tailored to the NASA SE Engine, with life-cycle phases on rows and SysML pillars on columns and the metamodel content held in the cross-sections.
- The Grid splits into problem (black box / white box) and solution sections; NASA's four System Design Processes map onto those rows, and the Grid corresponds to NASA SE Engine processes 1 through 9.
- The Grid's native metamodel differs from the NASA SE metamodel mainly by additions that strengthen traceability — goals/objectives, mission-level elements, validation and verification statements, refined refines relationships, cross-pillar allocations, a decompose relationship, and MOP/TPM value types.
- Appendix C shows a populated grid using this Handbook's own diagrams and tables, with some artifacts deliberately reused across multiple rows.
- Appendix D offers alternative, pillar-targeted modeling approaches — PBR (requirements), Scenario Modeling (behavior), System Specification (structure-to-requirements), and the OpenSE Verification pattern (V&V) — each an extension that still maps back to the section 7 metamodel.
- The metamodel in section 7 is explicitly one approach among several; the method remains tool-agnostic SysML tied to NPR 7123.1, and this is Revision A.
