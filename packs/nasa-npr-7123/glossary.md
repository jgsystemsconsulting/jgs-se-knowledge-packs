# NASA NPR 7123.1D Glossary

**Acceptable Risk** — Risk understood and agreed to by the program/project and governing bodies such that no further specific mitigation is required. (Ch 7)

**Activity** — A set of tasks describing the technical effort to accomplish a process and help generate expected outcomes. (Ch 3)

**Affordability** — The practice of balancing system performance and risk with cost and schedule constraints over the system life while satisfying operational needs. (Ch 7)

**Baseline** — An agreed-to set of requirements, designs, or documents under formal change control; changes require an approved monitoring and approval process. (Ch 7)

**Bidirectional Traceability** — The ability to trace any requirement to its parent requirement and to its allocated children requirements; mandatory output of the Requirements Management process. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**Brassboard** — A medium-fidelity functional unit that uses as much of the final product design as possible and begins to address scaling issues; operated in simulated operational environments. TRL 5 artefact. (Ch 8)

**Breadboard** — A low-fidelity unit demonstrating function only, without respect to form or fit; uses commercial/ad hoc components; not intended to provide definitive operational performance data. TRL 4 artefact. (Ch 8)

**Certification Package** — The body of evidence from verification activities (reports, models, waivers, etc.) evaluated to certify the design for flight/use. (Ch 7)

**Common Technical Processes** — The 17 processes mandated by NPR 7123.1D, grouped into system design (4), product realization (5), and technical management (8); collectively the SE Engine. (Ch 3)

**Concept of Operations (ConOps)** — A high-level description of how the system will be used to meet stakeholder expectations, typically time-sequenced; developed early in Pre-Phase A; provides validation criteria. (Ch 7)

**Configuration Item** — An aggregation of hardware, software, or firmware that satisfies an end-use function and is designated for separate configuration management with specified requirements. (Ch 7)

**Corrective Action** — Action taken on a product to correct and preclude recurrence of a failure or anomaly (design change, procedure change, training). (Ch 7)

**Customer** — The organisation or individual that has requested and will receive a product; each product in the system hierarchy has a distinct customer. (Ch 7)

**Customizing** — Selecting how SE requirements are implemented without changing what must be done; does not require waivers or deviations; must be documented in the SEMP. (Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Cybersecurity** — Prevention of damage to and restoration of computers, electronic communications, and information systems, ensuring availability, integrity, authentication, confidentiality, and non-repudiation. (NPD 2810.1, referenced Ch 2)

**Decision Authority** — The individual authorised to make important decisions for programs and projects under their authority. (Ch 7)

**Decision Analysis Process** — Technical management process applying criteria identification, alternatives identification, analysis, and selection to resolve technical issues across the life cycle. SE-23. (Ch 3)

**Derived Requirements** — Requirements arising from constraints, implied needs, or factors introduced by selected architecture and design rather than from explicit higher-level direction. (Ch 7)

**Deviation** — Authorised relief from meeting a requirement *before* the requirement is placed under configuration control. Contrast with Waiver. (Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Enabling Products** — Life-cycle support products and services (production, test, deployment, training, maintenance, disposal) that facilitate the operational end product through its life cycle. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**Engineering Technical Authority (ETA)** — Individuals with formally delegated, independently funded engineering authority flowing from the NASA Chief Engineer; responsible for engineering processes, specifications, rules, and best practices. (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Engineering Unit** — A high-fidelity unit closely resembling the final product; built and tested to establish confidence the design will function in expected environments; may become the final product if traceability is maintained. TRL 7 artefact. (Ch 8)

**Entrance Criteria** — Minimum accomplishments a program/project must fulfil before a life-cycle review is convened. (Ch 5, Ch 9)

**Formulation Phase** — The first part of the NASA management life cycle where system requirements are baselined, feasible concepts are identified, and preparation for the Implementation phase is made. (Ch 7)

**Human Systems Integration (HSI)** — Required interdisciplinary integration of the human as a system element to ensure hardware, software, and human components cooperate effectively; co-owned by ETA, SMA TA, and HMTA. (Ch 2, Ch 5)

**Implementation Phase** — The part of the NASA management life cycle where detailed design is completed, products are fabricated/assembled/integrated/tested, and deployed to customers or users. (Ch 7)

**Insight** — Government surveillance that monitors contractor compliance using metrics and milestones; a monitoring activity that does not require the Government to concur in contractor decisions. Contrast with Oversight. (Ch 4)

**Iterative** — Application of a process to the same product or set of products to correct a discovered discrepancy; distinguished from Recursive. (Ch 7)

**Joint Confidence Level (JCL)** — A process and product informing management of the likelihood that a project's cost will be at or below target AND schedule will be at or before target date simultaneously. (Ch 7)

**Key Decision Point (KDP)** — The event at which the Decision Authority determines readiness of a program/project to progress to the next life-cycle phase. (Ch 5, Ch 7)

**Key Performance Parameter (KPP)** — A capability or characteristic most essential for mission success; failure to meet a KPP threshold can cause reevaluation or termination of the program/project. (Ch 7)

**Laboratory Environment** — An environment that does not address the operational environment; tests in a laboratory environment are solely for demonstrating underlying technical principles. (Ch 8)

**Leading Indicator** — A subset of TPMs providing forward-looking insight into potential future states; allows management to act before problems are realised; mass and power margins are mandated examples. (Ch 6, Ch 7)

**Logical Decomposition** — Decomposition of technical requirements by functions, time, and behaviours to determine logical/data architecture models and derived requirements; outputs include functional flow block diagrams, timelines, state/mode diagrams. SE-09. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**Measure of Effectiveness (MOE)** — A qualitative or non-directly-designable stakeholder-facing measure of product acceptability; drives validation; defined during Stakeholder Expectations Definition. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**Measure of Performance (MOP)** — A quantitative design target; when met, helps ensure the associated MOE is satisfied; generally two or more per MOE; defined during Technical Requirements Definition. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**NPR (NASA Procedural Requirement)** — A mandatory Agency-level directive with which programs and projects must comply or from which they must obtain approved tailoring. (Ch 1)

**Operational Environment** — The environment in which the final product will be operated (space for spacecraft; ground/airborne for other systems); TRL 7 requires demonstration here. (Ch 8)

**Operations Concept (OpsCon)** — A detailed description (baselined at PDR) of how flight and ground systems are used together to achieve the mission; feeds from ConOps; may be a single or separate document. (Ch 7)

**Oversight** — Government surveillance in which the Government retains and exercises the right to concur or non-concur with contractor decisions; more directive than Insight. (Ch 4, Ch 7)

**Product Layer** — A horizontal slice of the product breakdown hierarchy comprising an end product and its associated enabling products; the SE Engine is applied once per product layer. (Ch 3, Ch 7)

**Prototype Unit** — A unit demonstrating form, fit, and function at a representative scale to permit validation of analytical models predicting full-scale system behaviour. (Ch 7)

**Recursive** — Repeated application of processes to design next lower layer products or realise next higher layer products within the system structure; also applied across life-cycle phases. (Ch 7)

**Relevant Environment** — The specific subset of the operational environment needed to demonstrate critical risk aspects of the technology; not the full operational system. (Ch 8)

**Repeatable** — A process characteristic allowing consistent application at any level of the system structure or within any life-cycle phase. (Ch 7)

**Request for Action (RFA)** — A formally documented action item from a life-cycle review; each Center defines its own RFA disposition process; reviews are not complete until all RFAs are dispositioned with TA concurrence. (Ch 5, Ch 7)

**Requirement** — An agreed-upon need, capability, or demand expressed as a "shall" statement; must be individually clear, correct, feasible, unambiguous, and verifiable; collectively non-redundant and non-conflicting. (Ch 7)

**Review Item Discrepancy (RID)** — A formally documented finding from a life-cycle review capturing a reviewer's comments, concerns, or issues; must be dispositioned with TA concurrence before a review is considered complete. (Ch 5, Ch 7)

**Risk** — The potential for performance shortfalls with respect to safety, technical, cost, or schedule mission execution domains. (Ch 7, NPR 8000.4)

**SE Engine** — The NASA model of 17 common technical processes applied to each product layer to drive the development of system products; evolved from the SE Vee. (Ch 3)

**SEMP (Systems Engineering Management Plan)** — The living technical planning document establishing what SE processes will be used, how they are applied, who is responsible, and what resources are required; ETA-approved; updated through SIR. (Ch 6)

**Single Point Failure** — An independent system element (hardware, software, or human) whose failure would result in loss of objectives, hardware, or crew. (Ch 7)

**Stakeholder** — A group or individual affected by or having an interest in a program or project; includes customers, other interested parties, and relevant stakeholders. (Ch 7)

**Success Criteria** — Specific accomplishments that must be satisfactorily demonstrated for a review to be considered complete; documented in the review plan. (Ch 5, Ch 7)

**System** — The combination of hardware, software, equipment, facilities, personnel, processes, and procedures that function together to produce the capability required to meet a need. (Ch 7)

**System Security** — Prevention or limitation of the impacts of system vulnerabilities from known or postulated threats, including cybersecurity vulnerabilities; classified as a technical risk within NASA. (Ch 7)

**Tailoring** — The process of adjusting or seeking relief from prescribed NPR requirements via waiver or deviation; requires ETA approval; results documented in the SEMP. (Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Technical Authority (TA)** — Part of NASA's checks-and-balances system; individuals with formally delegated, independently funded authority; three lines: ETA (Engineering), SMA TA (Safety and Mission Assurance), HMTA (Health and Medical). (Ch 7)

**Technical Performance Measure (TPM)** — Performance measures monitoring current actual achievement against anticipated plan; selected from MOPs; used for progress tracking and corrective action; mass and power margins are mandated leading-indicator TPMs. (Ch 6, Ch 7)

**Technology Readiness Level (TRL)** — A nine-point scale (1–9) measuring technology maturity from basic research (1) to flight-proven operational system (9); separate hardware and software criteria defined per level. (Ch 8)

**Transition (Product Transition Process)** — Delivery of a verified and validated end product to the next higher product layer customer or end user; includes packaging, handling, storing, transporting, installing, and sustainment activities. SE-15. (Ch 3)

**Validation (product)** — Proof that the product accomplishes its intended purpose in its intended environment based on stakeholder expectations; answers "Am I building the right product?" (Ch 7)

**Validation (requirements)** — Ensuring requirements are well-formed (clear), complete (consistent with needs), consistent (conflict-free), and individually verifiable. (Ch 7)

**Verification** — Proof of compliance with requirements/specifications by test, analysis, demonstration, or inspection; answers "Did I build the product right?" (Ch 7)

**Waiver** — Authorised relief from meeting a requirement *after* the requirement is placed under configuration control. Contrast with Deviation. (Ch 2, Ch 7)
