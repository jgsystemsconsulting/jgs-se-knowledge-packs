# Chapter 2: GOVERN (GV) Function

## Core Idea
GOVERN is the central Function of the CSF wheel: it establishes, communicates, and monitors the cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy that inform what an organization does across all other five Functions.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **GOVERN (GV)**: The Function that sets organizational context, risk strategy, roles, policy, oversight, and supply chain risk management as the foundation for all cybersecurity activity.
  - When to use: as the starting point for any cybersecurity program; outcomes here must be in place before the other Functions can be prioritized coherently.
- **Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management (GV.SC)**: A systematic program to identify, manage, monitor, and improve cybersecurity risk across the supply chain ecosystem.
  - When to use: whenever the organization acquires technology products, engages suppliers, or relies on third-party services.

## Key Concepts
- **Organizational Context (GV.OC)**: Understanding the mission, stakeholder expectations, dependencies, and legal/regulatory/contractual requirements that surround cybersecurity risk decisions; includes both what external stakeholders depend on the organization for (GV.OC-04) and what the organization depends on from others (GV.OC-05).
- **Risk Management Strategy (GV.RM)**: The documented priorities, constraints, risk tolerance and appetite statements, and assumptions that guide operational risk decisions; includes a standardized calculation method (GV.RM-06) and explicit recognition of positive (opportunity) risks (GV.RM-07).
- **Roles, Responsibilities, and Authorities (GV.RR)**: Defined and enforced accountability structures for cybersecurity risk, from organizational leadership (GV.RR-01) through resource allocation (GV.RR-03) and human resources integration (GV.RR-04).
- **Policy (GV.PO)**: Formal, enforced organizational cybersecurity policy that is established based on context and strategy (GV.PO-01) and reviewed and updated to reflect changes in requirements, threats, and technology (GV.PO-02).
- **Oversight (GV.OV)**: The feedback loop by which cybersecurity risk management performance results are used to review and adjust the risk management strategy (GV.OV-01 through GV.OV-03).
- **Risk Appetite**: An organization's statement of the amount and type of risk it is willing to accept in pursuit of its mission; established and maintained under GV.RM-02.
- **Risk Tolerance**: The acceptable deviation from the risk appetite; communicated alongside risk appetite under GV.RM-02.
- **C-SCRM (Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management)**: A systematic process for managing cybersecurity exposure throughout supply chains, captured in the GV.SC Category and linked to SP 800-161r1.
- **Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)**: The integrated, organization-wide approach to balancing a portfolio of risk types (financial, privacy, cyber, reputational); GV.RM-03 requires cybersecurity risk activities to be included in ERM processes.
- **Positive Risk (Strategic Opportunity)**: GV.RM-07 explicitly includes the characterization of opportunities (upside risks) in cybersecurity risk discussions, not only threats.

## Mental Models
- Use GV.OC before GV.RM: you cannot set sensible risk appetite without first understanding mission, stakeholder expectations, and legal obligations.
- Use GV.SC continuously through the supplier relationship life cycle: due diligence before engagement (GV.SC-06), risk monitoring during the relationship (GV.SC-07), and provisions for post-relationship activities (GV.SC-10).
- Use GV.OV as the governance feedback loop: strategy sets direction, execution generates data, oversight adjusts strategy — preventing cybersecurity from becoming a static, tick-box exercise.

## Anti-patterns
- **Separating cybersecurity governance from ERM**: GV.RM-03 requires integration; siloed cybersecurity risk management leads to competing priorities and resource conflicts.
- **Ignoring supplier risk until a breach occurs**: GV.SC-06 and GV.SC-07 require proactive due diligence and ongoing monitoring — not reactive supplier audits.
- **Treating policy as a one-time document**: GV.PO-02 requires policy to be reviewed and updated when requirements, threats, technology, or organizational mission change.

## Key Takeaways
1. GOVERN is not a Function to "complete once" — it provides the continuous strategic foundation for all other CSF activity.
2. Risk appetite and tolerance statements (GV.RM-02) are the critical link between executive strategy and operational risk decisions.
3. Leadership accountability (GV.RR-01) and adequate resource allocation (GV.RR-03) are explicit GOVERN outcomes, not implied prerequisites.
4. Supply chain risk (GV.SC) is a first-class GOVERN concern, covering the full supplier life cycle from pre-engagement due diligence through post-relationship obligations.
5. Oversight (GV.OV) creates the feedback loop that makes risk management adaptive rather than static.
6. GOVERN is positioned at the center of the CSF Functions wheel because every other Function's prioritization depends on the strategy, policy, and direction it establishes.

## Connects To
- **IDENTIFY (ID)**: GOVERN's risk strategy and priorities directly inform which assets and risks IDENTIFY focuses on.
- **NIST IR 8286 series**: Detailed guidance on integrating cybersecurity risk management with enterprise risk management programs.
- **SP 800-161r1**: In-depth cybersecurity supply chain risk management practices that operationalize GV.SC outcomes.
- **NIST RMF (SP 800-37)**: Governance structures and risk strategy in GOVERN feed directly into the RMF's system-level risk management activities.
