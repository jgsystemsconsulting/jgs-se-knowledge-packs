# NIST CSF 2.0 Glossary

**Adaptive (Tier 4)** — The highest CSF Tier; organization-wide, risk-informed cybersecurity practices that adapt based on lessons learned, predictive indicators, and real-time supply chain risk information. (Ch 8)

**Adverse Event Analysis (DE.AE)** — The CSF Category covering characterization of anomalies and indicators of compromise through correlation, impact estimation, threat intelligence integration, and incident declaration. (Ch 5)

**Anomaly** — An observable deviation from expected baseline behavior of assets, networks, or personnel that may indicate a cybersecurity attack or compromise. (Ch 5)

**Asset Management (ID.AM)** — The CSF Category covering identification and life-cycle management of hardware, software, data, services, people, and supplier-provided assets based on mission criticality. (Ch 3)

**Awareness and Training (PR.AT)** — The CSF Category ensuring personnel possess the cybersecurity knowledge and skills to perform their tasks, for both general workforce and specialized roles. (Ch 4)

**C-SCRM (Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management)** — A systematic process for managing cybersecurity exposure throughout supply chains and developing appropriate response strategies; governed under GV.SC. (Ch 2)

**Community Profile** — A baseline of CSF outcomes created and published to address shared interests of organizations in a particular sector, subsector, technology, threat type, or other use case; usable as the starting point for a Target Profile. (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Continuous Monitoring (DE.CM)** — The CSF Category covering ongoing monitoring of networks, physical environments, personnel activity, external service providers, and computing hardware/software to find potentially adverse events. (Ch 5)

**CSF Category** — A group of related cybersecurity outcomes that collectively comprise a CSF Function; identified by a two-part alphabetic code (e.g., GV.OC, PR.AA). (Ch 1)

**CSF Core** — The nucleus of the CSF; a taxonomy of high-level cybersecurity outcomes arranged in a Function/Category/Subcategory hierarchy applicable to organizations of any size, sector, or maturity. (Ch 1)

**CSF Function** — The highest-level organizing unit for cybersecurity outcomes; there are six Functions: GOVERN, IDENTIFY, PROTECT, DETECT, RESPOND, RECOVER. (Ch 1)

**CSF Subcategory** — More specific outcomes of technical and management activities that divide a CSF Category; identified by a numeric suffix (e.g., GV.OC-01, PR.AA-05). (Ch 1)

**CSF Tier** — A characterization of the rigor of an organization's cybersecurity risk governance and management practices; four Tiers: Partial (1), Risk Informed (2), Repeatable (3), Adaptive (4). (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Current Profile** — The part of an Organizational Profile specifying which Core outcomes an organization is currently achieving (or attempting to achieve) and to what extent. (Ch 8)

**Data Security (PR.DS)** — The CSF Category covering confidentiality, integrity, and availability protection of data at rest, in transit, and in use, and the maintenance of tested backups. (Ch 4)

**DETECT (DE)** — The CSF Function for finding and analyzing possible cybersecurity attacks and compromises through continuous monitoring and adverse event analysis. (Ch 5)

**Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)** — An organization-wide approach to balancing a portfolio of risk types (financial, cyber, privacy, reputational, etc.); cybersecurity risk management is required to be integrated into ERM under GV.RM-03. (Ch 2, Ch 8)

**Gap Analysis** — Comparison of Current and Target Profiles to identify differences and develop a prioritized action plan (e.g., risk register, POA&M) to close those differences. (Ch 8)

**GOVERN (GV)** — The central CSF Function that establishes, communicates, and monitors the organization's cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, and policy. (Ch 2)

**Identity Management, Authentication, and Access Control (PR.AA)** — The CSF Category limiting physical and logical asset access to authorized users, services, and hardware, incorporating least privilege and separation of duties. (Ch 4)

**IDENTIFY (ID)** — The CSF Function for understanding the organization's current cybersecurity risks through asset management, risk assessment, and improvement identification. (Ch 3)

**Implementation Examples** — Concise, action-oriented, notional illustrations of ways to achieve Core Subcategory outcomes; not a required baseline of actions. (Ch 1)

**Improvement (ID.IM)** — The CSF Category for identifying and implementing enhancements to cybersecurity risk management across all Functions, derived from evaluations, tests, exercises, and operational execution. (Ch 3)

**Incident Analysis (RS.AN)** — The CSF Category covering forensic investigation to establish root cause, preserve record integrity, and validate incident magnitude. (Ch 6)

**Incident Declaration Criteria (DE.AE-08)** — Defined thresholds or conditions against which adverse events are evaluated to determine whether a formal cybersecurity incident should be declared. (Ch 5)

**Incident Management (RS.MA)** — The CSF Category covering execution of the incident response plan, triage, categorization, prioritization, escalation, and recovery initiation. (Ch 6)

**Incident Mitigation (RS.MI)** — The CSF Category covering containment (preventing incident expansion) and eradication (removing the threat) activities. (Ch 6)

**Incident Recovery Communication (RC.CO)** — The CSF Category covering coordination with stakeholders and public communication during recovery operations using approved messaging. (Ch 7)

**Incident Recovery Plan Execution (RC.RP)** — The CSF Category covering structured execution of recovery activities, backup integrity verification, post-incident norm-setting, and formal recovery declaration. (Ch 7)

**Informative References** — Mappings that indicate relationships between CSF Core outcomes and existing standards, guidelines, regulations, or other content; help organizations understand how outcomes can be achieved. (Ch 1)

**Inherent Risk** — The level of cybersecurity risk to the organization before any risk responses are applied; determined by combining threat, vulnerability, likelihood, and impact information (ID.RA-05). (Ch 3)

**Least Privilege** — The access control principle that permissions are limited to the minimum necessary for a user, service, or hardware component to perform its function; explicitly required under PR.AA-05. (Ch 4)

**Organizational Context (GV.OC)** — The CSF Category covering understanding of mission, stakeholder expectations, dependencies, and legal/regulatory/contractual requirements surrounding cybersecurity decisions. (Ch 2)

**Organizational Profile** — A mechanism for describing an organization's current and/or target cybersecurity posture in terms of the CSF Core's outcomes; contains a Current Profile and/or Target Profile. (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Oversight (GV.OV)** — The CSF Category that uses results of cybersecurity risk management activities to inform, improve, and adjust the risk management strategy. (Ch 2)

**Partial (Tier 1)** — The lowest CSF Tier; cybersecurity risk management is ad hoc with limited organizational awareness and no formal policies. (Ch 8)

**Platform Security (PR.PS)** — The CSF Category covering management of hardware, software, and services of physical and virtual platforms through configuration, maintenance, log generation, and secure development. (Ch 4)

**Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M)** — A planning artifact used to document and track actions required to close gaps between an organization's Current and Target Profiles. (Ch 8)

**Policy (GV.PO)** — The CSF Category covering establishment, communication, enforcement, and periodic review of organizational cybersecurity policy. (Ch 2)

**Quick-Start Guide (QSG)** — A brief, audience-tailored supplementary resource providing actionable first steps for specific CSF-related topics. (Ch 1)

**RECOVER (RC)** — The CSF Function for restoring assets and operations affected by a cybersecurity incident to normal operating status. (Ch 7)

**Repeatable (Tier 3)** — A CSF Tier where risk management practices are formally approved as organizational policy, implemented as intended, and regularly updated based on risk management processes. (Ch 8)

**RESPOND (RS)** — The CSF Function for taking actions regarding a detected cybersecurity incident, including management, analysis, communication, and mitigation. (Ch 6)

**Risk Assessment (ID.RA)** — The CSF Category for understanding cybersecurity risk through vulnerability identification, threat intelligence intake, likelihood and impact estimation, and risk response prioritization. (Ch 3)

**Risk Informed (Tier 2)** — A CSF Tier where cybersecurity risk management is implemented on an irregular, case-by-case basis without formal policies. (Ch 8)

**Risk Management Strategy (GV.RM)** — The CSF Category establishing priorities, constraints, risk appetite, risk tolerance, and communication lines to support operational risk decisions. (Ch 2)

**Risk Tolerance** — The acceptable deviation from an organization's risk appetite; established and maintained alongside risk appetite under GV.RM-02. (Ch 2)

**Roles, Responsibilities, and Authorities (GV.RR)** — The CSF Category covering definition and enforcement of accountability structures for cybersecurity risk, from leadership through resource allocation. (Ch 2)

**Target Profile** — The part of an Organizational Profile specifying desired Core outcomes prioritized to achieve cybersecurity risk management objectives, including anticipated future changes. (Ch 8)

**Technology Infrastructure Resilience (PR.IR)** — The CSF Category covering security architectures that protect asset confidentiality, integrity, and availability and support organizational resilience. (Ch 4)
