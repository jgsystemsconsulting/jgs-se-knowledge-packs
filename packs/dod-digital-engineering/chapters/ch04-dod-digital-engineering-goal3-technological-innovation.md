# Chapter — Goal 3: Incorporate Technological Innovation to Improve the Engineering Practice

## Core Idea
Goal 3 of the DoD Digital Engineering Strategy treats technological innovation as the engine that keeps the Department's engineering practice ahead of adversaries. Building on the model-based approaches established in Goals 1 and 2, it directs DoD organizations to adopt advancing technologies and methods so they can stand up a single end-to-end digital enterprise — one that links stakeholders, processes, capabilities, and data digitally. The payoff is an organization that can analyze and adapt quickly, modernize capabilities, and make decisions that are both faster and more relevant. The goal explicitly looks toward technologies that learn, adapt, and act autonomously, positioning continuous technology infusion as a deliberate, managed practice rather than an occasional upgrade.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Focus Area 3.1 — Establish an end-to-end digital engineering enterprise**: The first of Goal 3's two focus areas. It sets the vision of an engineering enterprise that joins the digital and physical worlds across a system's entire lifecycle, applying a model-based approach inside a digitally connected environment that advanced technologies enable. The framework spans full lifecycle activities from concept through disposal.
  - When/how to use: Apply across the whole lifecycle. In the earlier phases the emphasis is on evaluating concepts, engaging the user, and identifying tradeoffs by working against a digital representation of the system of interest. In the later phases the emphasis shifts to production, delivery, and sustainment of the actual end item. The intent is to keep evolving the digital representation in step with the physical end item so the enterprise gains continuous insight and knowledge from the operational environment.

- **Focus Area 3.2 — Use technological innovations to improve the digital engineering practice**: The second focus area. It directs stakeholders to apply emerging technologies to sharpen decision making, extend system capabilities, and speed up computationally intensive engineering work. It also anticipates technology that changes how machines communicate and collaborate with one another and with people, drawing on the complementary strengths of humans and machines.
  - When/how to use: Decompose into three lines of effort the strategy names directly — (1) make better use of data to improve awareness, insight, and decision making; (2) advance human-machine interactions; and (3) improve technology insertion processes so cutting-edge capability can be infused cost-effectively. Use these as the practical entry points whenever a program asks "how do we actually improve the practice with new technology."

- **Technology insertion / infusion process (Focus Area 3.2)**: The strategy's stated approach to improving how DoD inserts technology — favoring infusion that leverages the marketplace while seeking high-payoff solutions, and implementing rigorous processes to support cost-effective technology development and selection decisions.
  - When/how to use: Invoke when selecting technologies for the digital enterprise. The strategy directs stakeholders to weigh both current and future enterprise and program needs in those selection decisions, not just the immediate program requirement.

## Key Concepts
- **End-to-end digital enterprise**: An engineering enterprise that connects the digital and physical worlds across a system's full lifecycle, running concept-to-disposal activities through a model-based approach inside a digitally connected, technology-enabled environment.
- **Evolving digital representation**: A digital representation of the system that is kept current alongside the physical end item throughout the lifecycle, so the enterprise continuously harvests insight and knowledge from the operational environment.
- **Lifecycle phase emphasis shift**: Early-lifecycle work centers on concept evaluation, user engagement, and tradeoff identification against the digital representation; late-lifecycle work centers on production, delivery, and sustainment of the end item.
- **Data-driven decision making**: Exploiting the exponential growth of data — across formats, structures, and sources — using advances in big data and analytics to support warfighters and to make better use of data in every lifecycle phase, enabling insights and faster, better decisions.
- **Continuous data assessment**: Capturing and continuously assessing data as a design evolves so that potential improvements and options can be compared and optimized in short periods of time.
- **Human-machine interaction**: The next frontier of technologies that transform how humans and machines interact; cognitive technologies arising from advances in artificial intelligence can perform tasks that once required human intelligence — building knowledge, learning continuously, understanding natural language, and reasoning and interacting more naturally than traditional systems.
- **Human-machine teaming for decisions**: The strategy's expectation that humans interacting with machines will make faster data-driven decisions and exploit data more effectively than people could on their own.
- **Technology infusion examples (Figure 6)**: Categories the strategy cites as enablers of the digitally connected enterprise — advanced computing, big data analytics, artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, and robotics, among others.
- **Authoritative source of truth (A-10 example)**: In the cited A-10 case, adopting 3-D model-based design and product lifecycle management let the program build a digital thread for the sustainment phase and identify the authoritative source of truth for the aircraft's engineering data.

## Mental Models
- **Innovation as a built-on layer, not a bolt-on**: Goal 3 sits on top of Goals 1 and 2 — it infuses technology and practice advances onto an already model-based foundation. Picture it as adding capability to an existing model-based core rather than starting a parallel effort.
- **One digital twin, two emphases**: Think of a single digital representation that travels the whole lifecycle, with the questions it answers shifting over time — "which concept and what tradeoffs" early, "how do we produce and sustain this" late — while it keeps syncing with the physical item.
- **Digital-physical loop**: Frame the enterprise as a feedback loop in which the operational environment continuously feeds insight back into the digital representation, which in turn informs lifecycle processes.
- **Strengths-of-both teaming**: Treat human-machine interaction as a division of labor that exploits what humans do well and what machines do well, rather than a straight substitution of machines for people.
- **Marketplace-leveraged infusion**: View technology insertion as a disciplined sourcing problem — pull high-payoff capability from the commercial marketplace through rigorous, cost-effective selection rather than building everything in-house.

## Key Takeaways
1. Goal 3 builds on the model-based approaches of Goals 1 and 2 and uses technology infusion to construct a single end-to-end digital enterprise connecting stakeholders, processes, capabilities, and data.
2. Focus Area 3.1 establishes the end-to-end enterprise across the full lifecycle from concept to disposal, with emphasis shifting from concept evaluation and tradeoffs early to production, delivery, and sustainment late.
3. The digital representation should evolve alongside the physical end item so the enterprise gains continuous insight from the operational environment.
4. Focus Area 3.2 improves the practice itself along three lines: better use of data for awareness and decisions, advancing human-machine interaction, and improving technology insertion.
5. Data growth plus advances in analytics make continuous, data-driven optimization possible — comparing and improving design options in short periods of time.
6. Human-machine interaction is framed around cognitive technologies that let people make faster data-driven decisions, drawing on the complementary strengths of humans and machines.
7. Technology insertion should leverage the marketplace and rigorous, cost-effective selection processes, weighing both current and future enterprise and program needs.
8. The A-10 Wing Replacement Program is the cited example: 3-D MBD, roughly 10,000 unique models, the NLign technology to map repairs onto the model, and PLM together built a sustainment-phase digital thread and an authoritative source of truth.

## Connects To
- **Goal 1 and Goal 2 (Digital Engineering Strategy)**: Goal 3 explicitly builds on the model-based approaches these earlier goals establish; the end-to-end enterprise reuses their model-based foundation.
- **Digital thread and authoritative source of truth**: Demonstrated in the A-10 example, where 3-D MBD and PLM produced a sustainment-phase digital thread and identified the authoritative source of truth for engineering data — concepts that recur across the strategy's other goals.
- **Advanced technology enablers (Figure 6)**: Advanced computing, big data analytics, artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, and robotics, which the strategy positions as the means to transform engineering practice.
- **Lifecycle activities (concept to disposal)**: The full-lifecycle scope of Focus Area 3.1 ties Goal 3 to the broader lifecycle processes the digital enterprise is meant to inform and modernize.
