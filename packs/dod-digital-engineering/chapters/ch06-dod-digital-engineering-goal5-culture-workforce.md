# Chapter 6: Goal 5 — Transform the Culture & Workforce

## Core Idea
Goal 5 is the people-and-culture half of the digital engineering transformation. The Strategy argues that the first four goals — formalizing models, an authoritative source of truth, technological innovation, and supporting infrastructure — are technical levers that go nowhere unless the organization itself changes. Culture here means the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors of the organization, and the Strategy is explicit that those norms are what actually govern how people work and run operations day to day. So this goal asks the DoD to deliberately and systematically plan, implement, and sustain the transformation by reaching past the technology into the workforce and the culture around it. The approach is framed as a set of mutually reinforcing enablers — training, education, strategic communication, leadership, and continuous improvement — rather than a one-time rollout.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Digital Engineering Culture and Workforce Enablers (Figure 8)** — the Strategy's organizing picture for Goal 5. It names the enabling levers of the cultural change: training, education, strategic communication, leadership, and continuous improvement.
  - When/how: introduced at the top of the Goal 5 section as the frame for everything that follows; the three focus areas (5.1–5.3) operationalize these enablers.
- **Three Goal 5 focus areas** — the goal is decomposed into three focus areas that move from knowledge, to leadership, to people:
  1. **5.1 Improve the digital engineering knowledge base** — codify, update, and organize the body of knowledge; advance policy/guidance/specifications/standards; streamline contracting and business practices; and establish and share best practices.
  2. **5.2 Lead and support digital engineering transformation efforts** — treat transformation as change management led from the top: communicate and execute the vision; build alliances, coalitions, and partnerships; and establish accountability to measure and improve results.
  3. **5.3 Build and prepare the workforce** — develop the knowledge, competence, and skills of the workforce, and ensure active participation so that learning turns into new habits and behaviors.
  - When/how: each focus area is a subsection of Section V for Goal 5, each broken further into named actions.
- **Acquisition-document touchpoints (RFP, SOW, CDRL, DID)** — named as the contracting artifacts that digital engineering will reshape as procurement moves off paper toward a model-based approach.
  - When/how: cited under 5.1 to show that the cultural change must reach contracting, procurement, legal, and business practices, not just engineers — and that changes are needed for planning, evaluating, awarding, and managing procurements, including post-award.
- **NAVAIR Systems Engineering (SE) Transformation** — a U.S. Navy example sidebar illustrating Goal 5 in practice.
  - When/how: presented as a real initiative whose work spans integrated modeling environments, policy and process, workforce and culture, and experimental pilot projects and demonstrations, with the aim of raising throughput and cutting the cycle time to develop weapon systems.

## Key Concepts
- **Culture as the operating system.** The Strategy defines culture as shared values, beliefs, and behaviors, and stresses that these norms fundamentally shape how people behave and perform work. Changing methods and tools without changing this layer leaves the transformation unfinished.
- **A living knowledge base that is currently uneven.** The digital engineering body of knowledge sits at mixed levels of maturity, scattered across standards, web resources, and academic and trade literature — pockets of excellence in silos. Goal 5 calls for a concerted effort to keep improving, updating, and organizing it.
- **The standards gap.** Many standards already touch digital engineering — modeling languages, processes, architecture frameworks — but no single set of digital engineering standards spans the full range of models and data that must be captured and exchanged across disciplines, domains, and lifecycle phases. The fix is to encourage common terminology, a shared understanding of concepts, and consistent rigor, starting by evaluating current policy, guidance, specifications, and standards to find the gaps.
- **Contracting and business practices must move off paper.** Procurement is largely paper-based and needs to shift to a model-based approach. That flexibility comes at the cost of reworking DoD processes for planning, evaluating, awarding, and managing procurements, and it touches the RFP, SOW, CDRL, and associated DIDs. The point: cultural change includes contracting, legal, and business teams, not only design engineers.
- **Best practices as shared, reusable assets.** To institutionalize digital engineering, the DoD should advance existing initiatives and networks so information flows across stakeholders, and make best practices available for reuse or adaptation. Sharing effective courses of action and lessons learned lets the broader community learn from one another — a Department-wide effort to inform, involve, and mobilize partners.
- **Transformation is change management.** By definition, transformation means managing change. Driving a culture of innovation, experimentation, and continuous improvement means shaping the values, attitudes, and beliefs of teams and individuals — and leaders are the ones who supply the framework for that change by energizing people to contribute and grow.
- **Leaders communicate, ally, and hold accountable.** Three leadership actions recur: (1) communicate and execute the vision and strategy through open, frequent, multi-channel communication, while removing barriers, addressing resistance, providing resources, setting priorities and milestones, defining roles, and giving people a way to ask questions and give feedback; (2) build alliances, coalitions, and partnerships across government, the Services, international partners, academia, FFRDCs, and industry to co-create and share resources; and (3) establish accountability — name champions and sponsors, generate short-term wins alongside long-term outcomes, and define metrics and success criteria to incentivize, monitor, reward, correct, and improve.
- **A changing workforce.** The future workforce is geographically dispersed, multidisciplinary, and multigenerational. As subject matter experts retire, junior engineers must work alongside them and carry the knowledge forward — making deliberate knowledge, competence, and skill transfer through training and education essential at every level.
- **"Doing" cements the change.** Training and education alone do not change culture. The DoD must drive the application of knowledge into new habits and behaviors — "doing" is what builds real experience. Engaging stakeholders, internal and external, lets them participate directly in deciding, designing, and delivering digital capabilities across the lifecycle.

## Mental Models
- **The four technical goals build the machine; Goal 5 builds the people who run it.** Models, the source of truth, innovation, and infrastructure are necessary but inert without a workforce and culture able to adopt them. Treat Goal 5 as the adoption layer that the others depend on.
- **Enablers, not a switch.** Figure 8 frames training, education, strategic communication, leadership, and continuous improvement as ongoing enablers that reinforce each other. The transformation is sustained pressure across all five, not a single launch event.
- **Knowledge → leadership → people.** The three focus areas trace a logical arc: first codify and organize what is known (5.1), then lead and manage the change with accountability (5.2), then equip and engage the people who carry it out (5.3).
- **Change is socio-technical, and the social side is the harder side.** The Strategy puts cultural change on equal footing with the technical work precisely because shared values and behaviors are what determine whether new methods actually get used.
- **Learn, then do.** Knowledge transfer is the input; new habits and behaviors are the output. Experience comes from application, so participation and "doing" are deliberately designed in, not assumed to follow from training.
- **Pull the whole acquisition system, not just engineering.** Because contracting, procurement, legal, and business practices shape behavior and incentives, transforming them is part of the cultural change — paper-based, document-centric procurement quietly anchors the old way of working.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Goal 5 = transform the culture and workforce** so the DoD can adopt and support digital engineering across the full lifecycle; it is the people-and-culture complement to the technical goals.
2. **Culture is defined as shared values, beliefs, and behaviors** that drive how work actually gets done — and changing it requires a deliberate, systematic plan, not just new tools.
3. **Figure 8's enablers** — training, education, strategic communication, leadership, and continuous improvement — are the levers; the work is sustained, not one-and-done.
4. **Focus area 5.1** improves and organizes the knowledge base, closes the digital engineering standards gap, moves contracting and business practices off paper, and shares reusable best practices.
5. **Focus area 5.2** treats transformation as leader-driven change management: communicate the vision, build cross-sector alliances, and establish metric-backed accountability with champions and short- and long-term wins.
6. **Focus area 5.3** prepares a dispersed, multidisciplinary, multigenerational workforce, transfers knowledge as SMEs retire, and — critically — converts training into new habits through active participation and "doing."
