# Chapter 5: Goal 4 — Establish Supporting Infrastructure & Environments

## Core Idea
Goal 4 is the foundation that lets the other digital engineering goals actually run. Its purpose is to stand up the infrastructure and environments where stakeholders can do digital engineering work, collaborate, and communicate. The Strategy is candid that today's DoD IT environments fall short: they are commonly stove-piped, complicated, and hard to manage, control, secure, and sustain, in part because each program ends up using them differently. The intent is to move away from that fragmentation toward a more consolidated, collaborative, and trusted environment, with solutions offered at both the enterprise level and the program level so that the full set of digital engineering objectives can be realized. Three properties recur as the success criteria for these environments: they must keep pace with technology, strengthen cybersecurity and intellectual-property protection, and improve information sharing.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Goal 4 of the DoD Digital Engineering Strategy (2018)** — to stand up the supporting infrastructure and environments in which stakeholders can perform their activities, collaborate, and communicate.
  - When/how: presented in Section V (Digital Engineering Goals and Focus Areas) as the enabling goal whose infrastructures and environments underpin all the other goals. Figure 7 (Digital Engineering Infrastructure & Environments) lays out the core elements.
- **Focus Area 4.1 — Develop, mature, and use digital engineering IT infrastructures** — the hardware, software, network, and related-equipment backbone for digital engineering.
  - When/how: framed as a crucial enabler and foundation for advancing the state of the practice, spanning multiple geographic locations and organizations while meeting security requirements.
- **Focus Area 4.2 — Develop, mature, and use digital engineering methodologies** — the methods, processes, and tools that turn a document-based shop into a model-based enterprise.
  - When/how: positioned as the transformation in how engineers work, manage, engineer, and deliver solutions once the infrastructure exists to support it.
- **Focus Area 4.3 — Secure IT infrastructure and protect intellectual property** — building cybersecurity and IP protection into the environment rather than bolting them on.
  - When/how: framed as a prerequisite for the whole transformation, since so much value now lives inside models and data that must stay protected as they are shared.
- **Engineered Resilient Systems (ERS)** — cited in the Strategy as a worked example for Goal 4.
  - When/how: described as a DoD joint program that assembles an integrated suite of modern computational engineering tools inside an architecture aligning acquisition and operational business processes. The suite spans models, simulations and related capabilities, plus trade-space assessment and visualization tools. The Strategy notes the Navy generated more than 19 million ship designs with ERS tools, and that ERS applies cost-versus-capability analysis to find the affordable capability space for a future surface combatant.

## Key Concepts
- **Enterprise- and program-level provision**: the DoD intends to offer infrastructure solutions at both levels so that consolidated, shared capability and program-specific needs are both met.
- **What "IT infrastructure" means here (4.1)**: a collection of hardware, software, networks, and related equipment that crosses geographic and organizational boundaries and still satisfies security requirements.
- **Secure connected information networks**: networks supporting digital engineering must be reliable, available, secure, and connected across the lifecycle. They have to carry computing infrastructure and enterprise services at every classification level, moving information and the authoritative source of truth securely. The payoff is better collaboration, enhanced learning, easier information sharing, and data-driven decision making.
- **Hardware and software provisioning**: the DoD plans to resource and deploy the hardware and software the workforce needs. It favors modular approaches and a broad range of solutions to gain flexible scalability, meaningful cost savings, and faster deployment when building out the digital end-to-end enterprise, and will adopt commercial cloud platforms, technologies, and Service solutions where appropriate.
- **Methodologies as a transformation, not a tool swap (4.2)**: moving to a model-based enterprise requires changing how engineers work, manage, engineer, and deliver — not merely installing software. The DoD expects to update its engineering processes, manuals, and instructions to capture the intended benefits.
- **What new methods should incorporate**: at a minimum, technological innovations, authoritative sources of truth, formalized modeling, and the workforce and cultural opportunities that improve quality, productivity, and acquisition efficiency.
- **Tool selection philosophy**: the DoD will evaluate and pick digital engineering tools for current and future stakeholder needs, aiming for a mix of scalable, enterprise-ready solutions across disciplines and domains. Crucially, the strategy concentrates on standards, data, formats, and the interfaces between tools rather than locking into specific tools — and stakeholders must weigh license agreements and data-exchange requirements when selecting them.
- **Key tool capability factors**: visualization, analysis, model management, model interoperability, workflow, collaboration, and support for extension/customization.
- **Securing the environment (4.3)**: because so much information now resides in models, the DoD must reduce cyber risk and defend digital engineering environments against both internal and external threats, protecting the classification, availability, and integrity of models and data. Cybersecurity is to be integrated into all phases of digital engineering planning and execution, with known high-risk vulnerabilities to DoD networks and data mitigated collectively.
- **Intellectual property as a shared, hard problem**: identifying and protecting IP is described as an extremely complex challenge that government and industry must take on together. Both sides share responsibility for protecting copyrights, trademarks, patents, and competition-sensitive information — while still allowing relevant information to flow between stakeholders across the lifecycle.

## Mental Models
- **Infrastructure is the substrate, not a side project**: Goal 4 sits underneath the other goals. If the environment is stove-piped and insecure, the authoritative source of truth, the models, and the innovations the other goals call for have nowhere reliable to live.
- **Standards-and-interfaces over tools**: the durable bet is on the seams between tools — standards, data, formats, interfaces — rather than any single vendor product. This keeps the enterprise from being constrained by, or captive to, particular tools.
- **Modular and cloud-leaning by default**: scalability, cost savings, and speed come from modular hardware/software choices and from adopting commercial cloud where it fits, instead of bespoke per-program builds.
- **Security and IP are facilitators, not brakes**: the framing is consistently "secure *while* facilitating" — protection is meant to enable collaboration and information flow, not shut it down. The same applies to IP: protect rights and competition-sensitive information while keeping relevant information moving.
- **Method change rides on infrastructure**: methodologies (4.2) mature *on top of* an evolving infrastructure (4.1). You cannot transform how engineers work without the connected, secure environment to work in.

## Anti-patterns
- **Stove-piped, program-by-program environments**: the Strategy explicitly diagnoses current DoD IT infrastructures and environments as stove-piped, complex, and hard to manage, control, secure, and support — precisely because their use varies from program to program. This is the state Goal 4 is meant to move away from, toward a consolidated, collaborative, trusted environment.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Goal 4 builds the foundation**: it stands up the infrastructure and environments that let stakeholders perform digital engineering activities, collaborate, and communicate — enabling the other four goals.
2. **Three success properties**: the target environments must keep pace with technology, strengthen cybersecurity and IP protection, and improve information sharing (Figure 7).
3. **Three focus areas**: 4.1 IT infrastructures (hardware, software, networks, equipment), 4.2 methodologies (methods, processes, tools), and 4.3 securing the infrastructure and protecting IP.
4. **Provide at both levels**: enterprise-level and program-level infrastructure solutions, leaning on modular approaches and commercial cloud for scalability, cost savings, and speed.
5. **Bet on interfaces, not tools**: focus on standards, data, formats, and tool-to-tool interfaces rather than being constrained to specific tools; weigh license and data-exchange terms when selecting them.
6. **Security and IP are co-equal and shared**: integrate cybersecurity across all phases, protect the classification/availability/integrity of models, and treat IP protection as a joint government–industry responsibility that still permits relevant information to flow.
7. **ERS is the cited exemplar**: a joint program offering an integrated suite of computational engineering, simulation, and trade-space/visualization tools — credited with over 19 million Navy ship designs and cost-versus-capability analysis of the affordable space for a future surface combatant.
