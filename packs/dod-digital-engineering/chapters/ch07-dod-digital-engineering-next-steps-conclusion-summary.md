# Chapter — Next Steps, Conclusion & Goal/Focus-Area Summary

## Core Idea

The DoD *Digital Engineering Strategy* (2018) closes by translating its vision into a coordinated transformation program and then consolidating its entire content into a single reference table. The closing material makes three moves. First, it assigns ownership: the DoD Components own their implementation plans, while the Office of the Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Systems Engineering (ODASD(SE)) coordinates across the enterprise so that the engineering practice improves as a whole. Second, it lays out a sequenced set of next steps — coordinate, plan, pilot, and sustain — that move the Department from strategy to institutionalized practice. Third, the appendix maps each of the five strategic goals to its supporting focus areas and sub-activities, giving practitioners a compact, traceable checklist of what "doing digital engineering" actually entails across the lifecycle. The framing throughout is deliberately decentralized: because the Department runs many missions against varied threats with a spectrum of technologies, no one approach is treated as sufficient, and success is positioned as a matter of collaboration and cultural change rather than a single mandated tool or method.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Digital Engineering Strategy** — The governing document itself. The Next Steps section frames the whole transformation as proceeding "through the implementation of this Digital Engineering Strategy," with the Components creating, sharing, and executing plans aligned to the strategy's goals.
- **ODASD(SE) (Office of the Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Systems Engineering)** — Named as the enterprise coordinator. It does not own Component plans but convenes, aligns, and works to close gaps, eliminate duplication, and share best practices across the engineering enterprise.
- **DoD Components** — Named as the owners of their own digital engineering implementation plans. ODASD(SE) supports them in developing those plans.
- **Digital Engineering Working Group (DEWG)** — Introduced in Next Step 1 as the standing collaboration body. Its remit is to address common practices and concerns, exchange information, align technical initiatives, propose policy and guidance, and pursue resolution of cross-cutting issues.
- **Digital engineering summit** — A convening mechanism ODASD(SE) will hold with representative DoD Component leaders to discuss Service implementation plans.
- **Digital engineering pilot programs** — Named in Next Step 3 as a deliberate proving ground used before full-scale adoption in major programs.
- **Appendix 1 — Digital Engineering Goals and Focus Areas** — A two-column reference table mapping the five strategic goals to their focus areas and indented sub-activities. It restates and operationalizes the strategy's core constructs, including the **authoritative source of truth** as a recurring organizing concept.

## Key Concepts

**The four next steps.** The strategy sequences its transition into four named actions:

1. **Coordinate DoD Digital Engineering Efforts** — ODASD(SE) convenes a summit of Component leaders and runs the standing DEWG to keep technical initiatives, policy proposals, and cross-cutting issue resolution aligned.
2. **Develop DoD Implementation Plans** — The Components, supported by ODASD(SE), write plans expressing the desired outcomes that achieve the strategy's goals.
3. **Implement Pilot Programs** — Components stand up a set of pilots to surface barriers and to evaluate tools, processes, and cost before scaling into major programs. The explicit purpose of piloting is to learn, measure, and optimize the digital approach for both efficiency and effectiveness.
4. **Sustain Digital Engineering Transformation** — The Department applies policy, guidance, training, and continuous-improvement initiatives to institutionalize the practice across government, industry, and academia.

**Distributed ownership, central coordination.** Plan ownership sits with the Components; coordination authority sits with ODASD(SE). The coordinator's job is integrative — closing gaps, removing duplicate effort, and propagating what works.

**The motivating problem (Conclusion).** The conclusion grounds the whole effort in mission urgency: growing threats at home and abroad, and the need to equip the warfighter quickly despite shifting conditions. It diagnoses the current engineering practice as constrained by siloed processes and schedule overruns, and argues that a digital environment enables more risk-taking and faster fielding of prototypes — benefits already realized by parts of private industry and DoD engineering centers.

**The five goals (Appendix 1).** The summary table enumerates the strategy's goals and their supporting focus areas:

- **Formalize how models are developed, integrated, and used** so that modeling informs decision making at the enterprise and program levels — focus areas span formal model planning, building/integrating/curating models that are accurate, complete, trusted, and reusable, and using models to communicate and perform model-driven lifecycle activities.
- **Provide an enduring, authoritative source of truth** — focus areas cover defining, planning, developing, and governing the authoritative source of truth (establishing access and controls, executing governance) and then using it across the lifecycle as the technical baseline, as the means to produce digital artifacts, and as the basis for reviews, decisions, and collaboration.
- **Incorporate technological innovation to improve the engineering practice** — focus areas establish an end-to-end digital engineering enterprise, infuse innovations to enable it, exploit data to sharpen awareness and decision making, and advance human-machine interaction.
- **Establish the supporting infrastructure and environments** in which stakeholders perform their work, collaborate, and communicate — focus areas develop and mature IT infrastructure (secure connected networks, hardware and software), develop and mature methodologies and tools, and secure that infrastructure while protecting intellectual property when models are shared.
- **Transform the culture and the workforce** so the Department can adopt digital engineering, and sustain it, throughout the lifecycle — focus areas improve the knowledge base (advancing policy, guidance, specifications, standards; streamlining contracting, procurement, legal, and business practices; sharing best practices), lead and support transformation (communicating the vision, building alliances across government/industry/academia, establishing accountability for measurable results), and build and prepare the workforce (developing skills and ensuring active participation in transformation).

## Mental Models

- **Strategy → plans → pilots → sustainment as a maturity ramp.** The four next steps are not parallel tasks but a progression: shared coordination first, then concrete plans, then bounded experiments that de-risk and price the change, then the policy/training/continuous-improvement machinery that makes it permanent. Pilots sit deliberately *before* full-scale rollout so failure stays cheap.
- **The coordinator as gap-closer, not gatekeeper.** ODASD(SE) is best understood as a connective layer over independently owned plans — its leverage comes from aligning initiatives and circulating best practices, not from owning execution.
- **Appendix 1 as the strategy's index card.** The goal/focus-area table is the operational distillation of the whole document. Read top-down, a goal answers "why"; the focus areas answer "what to do"; the indented sub-activities answer "what specifically to deliver." It doubles as a traceability scaffold from intent to concrete action.
- **Authoritative source of truth as a lifecycle of its own.** Within the second goal the source of truth is treated as something defined, then governed, then *used* — controls and governance precede its role as the technical baseline and as the engine for artifacts, reviews, and decisions.
- **"No single approach" as a design constraint.** Because missions, threats, and technologies vary across the Department, the strategy deliberately avoids prescribing one method or tool — heterogeneity is assumed, and collaboration plus cultural change carry the weight that a uniform mandate would in a more homogeneous enterprise.

## Key Takeaways

- The transformation is executed *through* the strategy, with Components owning implementation plans and ODASD(SE) coordinating to close gaps, remove duplication, and spread best practices.
- The four next steps — coordinate, develop plans, pilot, sustain — sequence the Department from intent to institutionalized practice, with piloting explicitly used to learn, measure, and optimize before scaling into major programs.
- Standing mechanisms — the digital engineering summit and the DEWG — give the coordination effort a durable home for aligning technical work and proposing policy and guidance.
- The conclusion frames digital engineering as a response to a real operational problem: today's siloed processes and overruns slow the Department, while a digital environment enables risk-taking and faster prototype fielding.
- Success is positioned as collaboration and business/cultural change across DoD, industry, allies, and the future workforce — not a single tool, and not a one-size-fits-all method.
- Appendix 1 collapses the strategy into five goals mapped to focus areas and sub-activities, making it the practical checklist for tracing intent to concrete digital engineering work across the lifecycle.
