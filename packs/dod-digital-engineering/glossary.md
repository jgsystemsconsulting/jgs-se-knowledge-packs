# DoD Digital Engineering Strategy — Glossary

Key terms from the *DoD Digital Engineering Strategy* (2018), alphabetical, with chapter
references. Definitions are synthesized and paraphrased from the public Strategy, not
reproduced verbatim.

**A-10 example** — cited sustainment case (Goal 3) in which 3-D model-based design and product lifecycle management built a digital thread for the aircraft and identified the authoritative source of truth for its engineering data. (Ch 4)

**Access and controls** — the governance mechanism that gives authorized users the right information at the right time while keeping data protected from unauthorized users, balancing open flow against protection. (Ch 3)

**Appendix 1** — the Strategy's two-column reference table mapping each of the five goals to its focus areas and indented sub-activities; the operational index of the whole document. (Ch 7)

**Authoritative source of truth (AST / ASoT)** — the single, trusted central reference point for models and data across the lifecycle; captures the current state and history of the technical baseline, provides traceability as the system evolves, and propagates changes downstream. (Ch 2, Ch 3)

**Continuum of models** — the idea that the same authoritative models are carried and reused from concept through disposal rather than discarded and rebuilt at each phase boundary. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Continuous improvement** — one of the Goal 5 culture/workforce enablers (Figure 8); sustained refinement of digital engineering practice rather than a one-time rollout. (Ch 6)

**Culture** — the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors of an organization that govern how people work and run operations day to day; the layer Goal 5 sets out to transform. (Ch 6)

**Cybersecurity (in digital engineering)** — protection integrated into all phases of digital engineering planning and execution to defend the classification, availability, and integrity of models and data against internal and external threats. (Ch 5)

**Design-build-test → model-analyze-build** — the methodology shift digital engineering enables, moving experimentation and testing into a virtual environment before physical build and fielding. (Ch 1)

**Digital artifacts** — multi-view, multi-perspective outputs generated from the authoritative source of truth that give stakeholders visibility into the right information across functional domains. (Ch 2)

**Digital engineering** — an integrated digital approach that uses authoritative sources of system data and models as a continuum across disciplines to support lifecycle activities from concept through disposal. (Ch 1)

**Digital Engineering Strategy (2018)** — the governing, non-prescriptive "living document" from ODASD(SE) that sets the vision and the five goals; the source this pack is built on. (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**Digital Engineering Working Group (DEWG)** — the standing collaboration body that addresses common practices, exchanges information, aligns technical initiatives, and proposes policy and guidance. (Ch 7)

**Digital thread** — the connected data linkage across lifecycle phases illustrated in the A-10 sustainment example, tying models and data into one continuous reference. (Ch 4)

**End-to-end digital enterprise** — a securely connected enterprise of people, processes, data, and capabilities in which models digitally represent the system of interest across the full lifecycle. (Ch 1, Ch 4)

**Engineered Resilient Systems (ERS)** — the cited Goal 4 exemplar: a DoD joint program offering an integrated suite of computational engineering, simulation, and trade-space/visualization tools (credited with over 19 million Navy ship designs). (Ch 5)

**Evolving digital representation** — a digital representation kept current alongside the physical end item so the enterprise continuously harvests insight from the operational environment. (Ch 4)

**Focus area** — a numbered sub-objective under a goal (e.g. 1.1, 2.2, 5.3); each goal is decomposed into focus areas in Section V. (Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Goal (digital engineering)** — one of the Strategy's five strategic aims: formalize models, authoritative source of truth, technological innovation, supporting infrastructure/environments, and culture/workforce. (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**Governance (of the AST)** — the policies, procedures, standards, and controls that keep models and data formally managed and trusted, raising data quality and stakeholder confidence so decisions can be data-driven. (Ch 3)

**Human-machine interaction** — the next frontier of cognitive/AI technologies that lets humans and machines collaborate, drawing on the complementary strengths of each to make faster data-driven decisions. (Ch 4)

**Intellectual property (IP) protection** — the joint government–industry responsibility to protect copyrights, trademarks, patents, and competition-sensitive information while still letting relevant information flow across the lifecycle. (Ch 5)

**LPDM / ePDM** — the U.S. Army's Lifecycle Product Data Management / enterprise Product Data Management system, cited as a Goal 2 example of an authoritative source of truth for weapon-system and end-item data. (Ch 2)

**Methodologies (digital engineering)** — the methods, processes, and tools (Focus Area 4.2) that turn a document-based shop into a model-based enterprise; a transformation, not a tool swap. (Ch 5)

**Model** — a precise, flexible representation of a system, phenomenon, entity, or process used to explore, analyze, test, and communicate across the lifecycle. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Model formalisms** — the foundational quality rules models conform to — syntax, semantics, lexicons, and applicable standards — that keep independently authored models internally and externally consistent. (Ch 2)

**Model provenance and pedigree** — the recorded origin and lineage of a model, captured to establish trust, credibility, accuracy, and a defensible basis for reuse. (Ch 2)

**Model-based reviews and audits** — reviews, audits, and trust judgments grounded in verification and validation attributes, treated as essential to collaboration and system evolution. (Ch 2)

**NAVAIR SE Transformation** — the U.S. Navy Goal 5 example spanning integrated modeling environments, policy/process, workforce/culture, and pilot projects to raise throughput and cut cycle time. (Ch 6)

**Next steps (four)** — the Strategy's sequenced transition: coordinate, develop implementation plans, implement pilot programs, sustain the transformation. (Ch 7)

**ODASD(SE)** — the Office of the Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Systems Engineering; the enterprise coordinator that authored the Strategy and aligns Component plans (does not own them). (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**Pilot programs** — bounded proving grounds (Next Step 3) that surface barriers and evaluate tools, processes, and cost before scaling into major programs. (Ch 7)

**System of interest** — the broad scope the Strategy models digitally: systems of systems, systems, processes, equipment, products, and parts. (Ch 1)

**Technical baseline** — the current and historical state of the system captured in the authoritative source of truth and used for decisions on cost, schedule, performance, and risk. (Ch 2)

**Technology insertion / infusion** — the disciplined, marketplace-leveraged, cost-effective process for selecting and infusing high-payoff technologies, weighing current and future enterprise and program needs. (Ch 4)

**Transformation (as change management)** — the framing of Goal 5: leader-driven change that communicates a vision, builds alliances, and establishes metric-backed accountability. (Ch 6)
