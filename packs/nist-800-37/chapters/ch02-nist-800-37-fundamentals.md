# Chapter — The Fundamentals: Organization-Wide Risk, RMF Structure, Systems, Boundaries, Controls, and Supply Chain Risk

> Source: NIST SP 800-37, Revision 2 (the RMF publication — a system-life-cycle approach to security and privacy for information systems and organizations), Chapter Two ("The Fundamentals"), sections 2.1–2.8.
>
> **Scope caveat.** SP 800-37 sits at the RMF / security-engineering edge of systems engineering. It governs how *security and privacy risk* is managed across an organization and its systems, and it deliberately rides alongside the broader SE life cycle (it cites ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 and NIST SP 800-160) rather than replacing it. Read this chapter as the security-risk overlay on top of the SE life cycle, not as a general SE methodology.

## Core Idea

Managing the security and privacy risk that information systems create is treated here as a whole-organization activity, not a system-by-system afterthought. The chapter's central claim is that risk decisions made at the level of an individual system cannot be made in isolation — they are downstream of, and constrained by, decisions about mission and business objectives, the enterprise architecture, and how the organization has prepared itself to manage risk. The chapter lays out the conceptual machinery the rest of the document depends on: a three-tier model of where risk lives, a seven-step framework for working through it, a precise vocabulary for what a "system" and its "boundary" are, the requirements-to-controls relationship, the notion of a security and privacy "posture," and the extension of all of this to the supply chain.

The recurring theme is that getting the organizational groundwork right (the "Prepare" work at the upper tiers) is what makes system-level risk work affordable, repeatable, and effective. Skipping that groundwork is named, explicitly, as a path to costly, redundant, and vulnerable systems.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Risk Management Framework (RMF).** The spine of the whole document. Section 2.2 defines it as seven steps — one preparatory step plus six main steps (Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor) — all of which the source calls essential to successful execution. Introduced structurally here; the detailed task descriptions live in the document's Chapter Three.
- **The three-level organization-wide risk approach from SP 800-39.** Section 2.1 invokes the multi-level model described in NIST SP 800-39: organization level (Level 1), mission/business process level (Level 2), and information system level (Level 3), with bi-directional communication and reporting flowing across all three.
- **Cybersecurity Framework (CSF).** Cited as a way to enhance RMF task execution (Section 2.2 flexibility discussion) and mapped task-by-task in the Prepare-step tables (e.g., constructs like ID.AM-6, ID.GV-2, ID.RM, ID.SC). The source also references Cybersecurity Framework Profiles as an organization-level output.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 ([ISO 15288]).** Section 2.4 borrows its engineering definition of a *system* — a set of interacting elements organized to achieve one or more stated purposes — and its view of the entities a system interacts with in its environment of operation.
- **NIST SP 800-160 v1.** Referenced repeatedly as the systems security engineering companion: it covers life-cycle-based systems security engineering processes, requirements engineering and traceability, and system security engineering as part of the SDLC. This is the explicit hook from RMF into SE.
- **OMB Circular A-130 ([OMB A-130]).** Section 2.3 treats the 2016 revision as the policy that *requires* federal organizations to implement the RMF and to integrate privacy into it. It supplies the document's definitions of *privacy control* and *security control*, and its definition of *supply chain risk*.
- **FISMA ([FISMA]).** Cited alongside OMB A-130 (Section 2.8) for the requirement that external providers handling federal information meet the same security and privacy requirements as agencies.
- **Supporting references named in the task structure and footnotes:** SP 800-30 (risk assessment / risk factors), SP 800-161 (supply chain risk management guidance, SCRM plans, SCRM strategy template), IR 8062 (privacy engineering), FIPS 199 and FIPS 200 (categorization / impact levels), CNSSI 1253 (categorizing national security systems), and OMB FEA (enterprise architecture guidance).

## Key Concepts

**Organization-wide risk and the three tiers (2.1).** Risk management is described as holistic — touching mission/business planning, the enterprise architecture, the SDLC, and the SE activities embedded in those life-cycle processes. The upper two tiers (organization and mission/business process) do preparatory work that "goes beyond" managing the risk of any single system: assigning risk-management roles, setting a risk management strategy and risk tolerance, identifying missions and stakeholders, prioritizing assets, understanding threats and adverse effects on individuals, running organization- and system-level risk assessments, prioritizing requirements, determining authorization boundaries, defining systems in terms of the enterprise architecture, and building security/privacy architectures with controls suitable for inheritance. Level 3 then addresses risk from the system's vantage point, *guided and informed* by the upper-tier decisions.

**Control designation: system-specific, hybrid, common (2.1).** The organization labels each control as system-specific, as hybrid, or as common (i.e. inherited), aligning that designation with the enterprise architecture, the security/privacy architecture, and any tailored baselines or overlays. Common controls can be allocated at any of the three tiers. Traceability of controls back to the requirements they satisfy is established so that every requirement is addressed across design, development, implementation, operations, maintenance, and disposition.

**The seven RMF steps (2.2).** Prepare (establish context and priorities at both organization and system levels); Categorize (the system and its information by impact of loss); Select (an initial control set, then tailor to reduce risk to an acceptable level); Implement (and describe how controls are employed in the system and its operating environment); Assess (whether each control was built correctly, runs as intended, and yields the outcome required); Authorize (a risk-acceptance determination covering operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation); Monitor (ongoing — control effectiveness, system/environment changes, risk and impact analyses, and posture reporting). Impact of loss is one of four risk factors (with threats, vulnerabilities, and likelihood) per SP 800-30.

**Non-sequential, flexible execution (2.2).** After Prepare, a first-time execution typically runs the steps in order, but the source is emphatic that the steps following Prepare can be carried out non-sequentially — driven by system type, senior-leadership risk decisions, iterative/agile cycles, or changes surfaced during Monitor. Organizations are expected to execute all steps and tasks (except those marked optional) but have wide latitude in *how*: reordering, emphasizing, or combining tasks, and using the Cybersecurity Framework to enhance execution. Because the RMF is framed as an ongoing-authorization SDLC process, an organization can enter or re-enter at whichever step an assessment of risk and current system state dictates — after a gap analysis, documented decisions, stakeholder notification, and authorizing-official approval.

**RMF task anatomy (2.2, Figures 3–4).** Each step has a purpose statement, defined outcomes, and tasks. Each task carries potential inputs, expected outputs, primary and supporting responsibilities, the SDLC phase where it occurs, a discussion section, and references — and, where applicable, the references identify the correlated systems security engineering tasks. The worked example is Task P-3 (organization-wide risk assessment), whose discussion stresses aggregating system-level results, continuous-monitoring data, connections with internally and externally owned systems, external providers, and the organization's supply chain.

**Security and privacy as distinct-but-collaborating disciplines (2.3).** Per OMB A-130, security and privacy are independent disciplines that must be coordinated. Information security guards systems and the information in them against the unauthorized acts — access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, destruction — that would breach confidentiality, integrity, or availability. Privacy programs manage risks to individuals from the *processing* of PII (creation, collection, use, dissemination, storage, disclosure, disposal, and more). The crucial asymmetry: not all privacy risk comes from unauthorized activity — some arises from *authorized* processing, so protecting privacy cannot be achieved solely by securing PII. Controls meeting both objectives are simply "controls"; the "security" / "privacy" qualifiers are used only when a control is selected for a particular objective.

**System, system elements, enabling/other systems, environment of operation (2.4).** RMF execution uses the statutory definition of an information system (a discrete set of information resources organized for collection, processing, maintenance, use, sharing, dissemination, or disposition of information) while leaning on the ISO 15288 engineering view. System elements span technology/machine, human, and physical/environmental kinds, realized via hardware, software, firmware, physical structures, or people/processes/policies/procedures (a *system component* is specifically a hardware/software/firmware element). Enabling systems support the life cycle but live outside the authorization boundary — they may supply common/inherited controls or services (identification and authentication, network, monitoring). Other systems also sit outside the boundary and merely interact with or benefit from the system. Every system operates within an environment that influences it.

**Authorization boundary (2.4, 2.5).** The authorization boundary defines the system for RMF purposes — the set of system elements (people, processes, information technologies) authorized for operation or for use by an authorizing official, or the set of common controls authorized for inheritance. The source explicitly retires the older interchangeable use of "system boundary" in favor of "authorization boundary." It is set during Prepare–System level (Task P-11). Parts of the environment of operation may be ruled in scope (e.g., a facility whose physical/environmental controls then enter the system security plan) or out of scope (e.g., when the facility provides those protections as inherited common controls). Boundary determinations are context-driven and revisited periodically during continuous monitoring. Useful grouping considerations: same mission/business function, similar operating characteristics and requirements, similar information types/impact level, same environment of operation. Elements within a boundary are generally under the same *direct management* (budgetary, programmatic, or operational authority).

**Requirements and controls (2.6).** "Requirements" carries two senses: legal/policy obligations (e.g., what A-130 imposes) and, more broadly, the expression of stakeholder protection needs for a system or organization, derivable from laws, orders, directives, regulations, policies, standards, mission/business needs, or risk assessments — together driving the required security, privacy, and assurance characteristics. Organizations may subdivide into *capability requirements* (a needed capability), *specification requirements* (capabilities tied to specific hardware/software/firmware that implement all or part of a control and can be verified/validated/tested), and *statement of work requirements* (operational or development actions). Controls are descriptions of safeguards and protection capabilities; they are selected and implemented to satisfy requirements and may need further specification via *derived requirements* or instantiated control-parameter values. The flow runs: requirements and risks → controls → specification and statement-of-work requirements in the SE context — which is precisely how systems engineers develop, derive, and decompose requirements across the SDLC.

**Security and privacy posture (2.7).** Posture is the status of systems and information resources, given the assurance resources and capabilities in place to defend the organization, comply with privacy requirements, and react as conditions change. It is established continuously, by repeatedly assessing and monitoring every control — whether system-specific, hybrid, or common. Posture is the evidence base authorizing officials use to judge whether risk to operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, or the Nation is acceptable against the organization's risk management strategy and tolerance.

**Supply chain risk management (2.8).** Organizations increasingly depend on external providers and remain accountable for the resulting risk. Threats include counterfeits, unauthorized production, tampering, theft, malicious software/hardware, and poor manufacturing/development practices — including failure to build in security or privacy capabilities. Supply chain risk can be endemic or systemic: a single use of an element may be acceptable while widespread use across a system, organization, sector, or nation pushes risk past tolerance. Organizations create an SCRM policy (tying SCRM into acquisition, security/privacy, logistics, quality, and the SDLC and defining SCRM roles) and tailored SCRM plans per program/context. The RMF can manage supply chain risk, and the conceptual system view (Figure 5) can inform security/privacy activities across all supply-chain elements. Every RMF step can be executed by a nonfederal external provider *except Authorize* — risk acceptance is an inherent federal responsibility. The *level of assurance* obtainable from a provider — driven by the degree of control (contract / SLA terms) and the evidence of control effectiveness the provider supplies — determines whether acquired products/systems/services present acceptable risk, but ultimate responsibility stays with the organization and its authorizing official, who require an appropriate chain of trust.

## Mental Models

- **Risk gravity flows downhill.** Strategy, tolerance, architecture, and boundary decisions made at the organization and mission tiers fall onto the system tier and constrain what controls get selected there. The system engineer doesn't start from a blank page — they inherit a frame.
- **The authorization boundary is a dial, not a wall.** Draw it too wide and risk management gets needlessly complex; too narrow and you multiply the number of separately managed systems and inflate cost. The "right" boundary is the one that groups elements sharing mission, requirements, information impact level, and environment.
- **"In scope vs. inherited" is the boundary question for the environment.** A facility either sits inside the boundary (its protections land in your system security plan) or supplies those protections as common controls you inherit. The same physical reality, two different accountability arrangements.
- **Controls are the hinge between requirements and engineering.** A control is a high-level protection statement; it decomposes into specification and statement-of-work requirements that engineers can build, derive, and test. This is the seam where RMF and SE requirements engineering meet.
- **Posture is the instrument panel for authorization.** Continuous assessment of system-specific, hybrid, and common controls produces the evidence; the authorizing official reads that panel to decide whether residual risk is acceptable.
- **Authorize is the one non-delegable step.** Outsource everything else in the supply chain if you must — the acceptance of risk stays federal, and it stays with a named senior official.
- **Single-use acceptable can become systemic-unacceptable.** A supply-chain element that's fine in one place can become an organization- or nation-level problem when it's everywhere. Aggregation changes the risk picture.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes directly:

- **Skipping organizational preparation.** Without adequate upper-tier preparation, security and privacy activities become too costly, demand too many scarce skilled professionals, and yield ineffective solutions.
- **Failing to implement an effective enterprise architecture.** Such organizations struggle to consolidate, optimize, and standardize their IT infrastructure — and the source notes that unmanaged complexity enlarges the attack surface and the exploitable technology footprint.
- **Letting architecture/design decisions undercut security.** The source warns that architectural and design choices can adversely affect an organization's ability to implement effective security and privacy solutions.
- **Inadequate preparation producing waste.** A lack of organizational preparation can result in unnecessary redundancy and in inefficient, costly, and vulnerable systems, services, and applications.
- **Mis-sized authorization boundaries.** Boundaries that are too expansive make risk management unnecessarily complex; boundaries that are too limited multiply separately managed systems and unnecessarily inflate security and privacy costs.
- **Treating privacy as merely "secured PII."** Because some privacy risk stems from authorized processing, securing PII alone does not protect privacy — a discipline gap the source explicitly calls out.

## Key Takeaways

- Security and privacy risk management is whole-organization work spanning three tiers; system-level decisions are downstream of organization- and mission-level ones (2.1).
- The RMF is seven steps — Prepare plus six (Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor) — all essential, executable non-sequentially after Prepare, with deliberate flexibility in how tasks are carried out (2.2).
- Every RMF task has a defined anatomy (inputs, outputs, responsibilities, SDLC phase, discussion, references) and the references point to correlated systems security engineering tasks — the explicit RMF-to-SE link (2.2).
- Security and privacy are separate but collaborating disciplines; controls can serve both, and privacy risk extends beyond unauthorized activity into authorized processing of PII (2.3).
- A "system" is a set of interacting elements (technology, human, physical) per ISO 15288; enabling systems and other systems sit *outside* the authorization boundary (2.4).
- The authorization boundary defines the system for RMF, set in Task P-11, sized as a deliberate trade-off, and revisited during continuous monitoring (2.4–2.5).
- Requirements (legal/policy and stakeholder-need senses) drive controls, which in turn drive specification and statement-of-work requirements that engineers decompose — the RMF/SE requirements seam (2.6).
- Posture, built from ongoing control assessment and monitoring, is the evidence authorizing officials use to accept or reject residual risk (2.7).
- Supply chain risk is an organizational and accountable concern; the RMF applies to it, every step but Authorize can be done by external providers, and assurance depends on the degree of control and the evidence of control effectiveness (2.8).
