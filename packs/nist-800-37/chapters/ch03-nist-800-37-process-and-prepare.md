# Chapter 3: The RMF Process and the Prepare Step

> Scope caveat: NIST SP 800-37 Rev. 2 sits at the security-and-privacy-risk edge of systems engineering. It is a risk-management process for information systems, not a general SE process model. Where it touches SE proper, it does so by aligning its own tasks with the SDLC and by borrowing requirements, architecture, and life-cycle constructs from the systems security engineering literature (notably SP 800-160 v1). Read it as the governance and assurance overlay that wraps an engineered system, not as the engineering process itself.

## Core Idea
The RMF is executed as a set of steps and tasks, each owned by a defined organizational role, and is meant to run concurrently with — ideally indistinguishably from — the organization's existing System Development Life Cycle (SDLC). The best implementation reuses SDLC artifacts (plans, assessment reports, plans of action and milestones) rather than generating risk paperwork purely to satisfy the framework. The first step, **Prepare**, establishes the organization-wide and system-level groundwork — roles, risk strategy, common controls, requirements — so that every later RMF step (Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor) starts from a consistent, resourced, and governed baseline. Although the tasks are listed in order, the process is explicitly iterative: assessment results can force re-implementation and reassessment, and monitoring continuously cycles back through the earlier work.

## Frameworks Introduced
The slice introduces or invokes these named constructs, with how and when each is used:

- **Risk Management Framework (RMF) steps and tasks** — the chapter's organizing structure. Each task carries a Primary Responsibility role, Supporting Roles, Potential Inputs, Expected Outputs, and a Discussion. Used as the operating procedure for managing security and privacy risk across the system life cycle.
- **The Prepare step (Section 3.1)** — split into **Organization-Level** preparation (Tasks P-1 through P-7) and **System-Level** preparation (Tasks P-8 through P-18). Used at the very start of an RMF engagement, and revisited as the organization or system changes.
- **NIST SP 800-160 v1 (systems security engineering / life-cycle process)** — cited as the source organizations can use to derive an initial set of security and privacy requirements, which then drive control selection. Specific 800-160 v1 processes are named as references across the Prepare tasks (e.g., Human Resource Management; Risk, Decision, Quality Assurance/Management, Project Assessment and Control; Business or Mission Analysis; Stakeholder Needs and Requirements Definition; System Requirements Definition). This is the explicit SE-to-RMF bridge.
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework [NIST CSF] (Core, Profiles, Implementation Tiers)** — used to identify, align, and deconflict security requirements across a sector, industry, or organization, and to inform control selection and tailoring. Each Prepare task maps to specific CSF Subcategories (e.g., ID.AM-6, ID.GV-2, ID.RM, ID.SC). A Cybersecurity Framework Profile links cybersecurity outcomes to mission/business requirements, risk tolerance, and resources.
- **SP 800-53 control catalog and SP 800-53B control baselines** — SP 800-53 Rev. 5 separates the catalog from the baselines, and the recommended baselines now live in the companion publication SP 800-53B. Used as the starting point for organizationally-tailored control baselines and overlays.
- **FIPS 199 / FIPS 200 and the high-water-mark concept** — referenced in impact-level prioritization (Task P-6); applying the high-water mark yields low-, moderate-, or high-impact system designations. National Security Systems instead follow CNSSI 1253, which does not apply the high-water mark.
- **Enterprise architecture, security architecture, and privacy architecture** — invoked in Task P-16. The security and privacy architectures are described as integral parts of the enterprise architecture, providing a traceability roadmap from strategic goals through protection needs and requirements to concrete people/process/technology solutions.

## Key Concepts
- **RMF–SDLC alignment**: RMF tasks are intended to be executed as part of, or concurrently with, the organization's SDLC so that security and privacy risk management is woven into routine engineering rather than bolted on. The ideal is an RMF that is hard to distinguish from the normal SDLC.
- **Iterative, non-strictly-sequential flow**: Tasks appear ordered but allow divergence — for example, controls may be assessed as soon as they are implemented (before all controls are in place), and monitoring drives a continuous tracking/assessing/reassessing/reporting loop. Regardless of ordering, the last action before operation is the authorizing official's explicit acceptance of risk.
- **Applying RMF to existing systems as gap analysis**: For legacy systems, running the steps acts as a gap analysis; if no deficiencies are found and an authorization is already in effect, the organization can jump straight to continuous monitoring.
- **Task delegation vs. accountability**: A role with primary responsibility may delegate task completion to supporting roles (unless prohibited), but it remains accountable for completion.
- **Risk tolerance and the risk management strategy (P-2)**: Risk tolerance is the degree of risk or uncertainty acceptable to the organization; it constrains decisions across the whole process. The strategy makes explicit the threats, assumptions, constraints, priorities, trade-offs, and tolerance used for investment and operational decisions, and connects security/privacy programs to the organization's enterprise risk management.
- **Common controls and inheritance (P-5)**: Common controls are controls inheritable by one or more systems; they can come from any SP 800-53 family. System owners need not assess or document inherited common controls — that is the common control provider's job — and providers need not see the internals of the inheriting systems. A control only partially satisfied centrally becomes a **hybrid control**, with the split between common and system-level parts documented.
- **Organizationally-tailored baselines and overlays (P-4)**: A fully specified set of controls/enhancements/guidance derived from the SP 800-53B baselines, usable when an organization diverges from the baseline assumptions (special risks, special mission needs, unusual environments). Tailored baselines (also called overlays) can be more or less stringent than the SP 800-53B baselines and are applied across multiple systems. Communities of interest have produced overlays for cloud/shared systems, industrial control systems, national security systems, weapons and space systems, high value assets, mobile device management, federal PKI, and privacy.
- **Impact-level prioritization (P-6)**: After categorization, organizations may subdivide each impact level for finer-grained, risk-based decisions (e.g., splitting moderate systems into low-moderate, moderate-moderate, high-moderate), focusing attention on complexity, aggregation, and interconnections, and on mission-critical systems.
- **Authorization boundary (P-11)**: Establishes the scope of what the organization agrees to protect — the set of system elements, their interconnections, and the environment that is the focus of the RMF. The whole system sits inside a single boundary for accountability. Boundaries matter most when low-impact systems connect to high-impact systems or when external providers operate part of the system; formal agreements (e.g., contracts) delineate boundaries with external providers.
- **System elements**: A system is a set of elements organized to support missions and business processes. Elements span human, technology/machine, and physical/environmental categories. The narrower term **system component** denotes elements implemented specifically via hardware, software, or firmware.
- **Assets, tangible and intangible (P-10)**: Assets are items of value to mission/business objectives. Tangible assets are physical (facilities, hardware, networks, non-digital information, people); intangible assets include processes, digital data, firmware, software, services — and also organizational reputation and individuals' privacy interests.
- **Information types and the information life cycle (P-12, P-13)**: Information types are identified (drawing on the Controlled Unclassified Information program defined by NARA CUI and on SP 800-60) as a precondition for categorization. The information life cycle covers the stages information passes through — creation/collection, processing, dissemination, use, storage, and disposition including destruction — and is documented with tools such as data maps, data flow diagrams, entity-relationship diagrams, schemas, and data dictionaries.
- **Protection needs and requirements (P-15)**: Protection needs express the protection capability required to reduce risk to an acceptable level while supporting the mission; they are inherently subjective, reflecting stakeholder priorities and negotiated trade-offs. Security and privacy requirements are the formal, more granular expression of those needs and may include **assurance requirements** — confidence that the system stays trustworthy across adversity, malicious or not.
- **Requirements allocation (P-17)**: Requirements are allocated to the organization, system, system elements, or environment of operation to determine where controls are implemented; allocation conserves resources by avoiding redundant implementation across systems when a common control suffices.
- **System registration (P-18)**: Registers the system with management offices, adds it to the organization-wide inventory, and is updated with categorization data after the Categorize step.

## Mental Models
- **RMF as an overlay, not a replacement, for the SDLC**: Treat the RMF as the risk and assurance lens laid over engineering work already happening. If a task forces you to create an artifact you do not already produce in the SDLC, ask whether the SDLC artifact can supply the evidence instead.
- **Prepare = remove friction once, centrally**: Organization-level Prepare tasks (roles, risk strategy, common controls, tailored baselines, monitoring strategy) exist to give every downstream system a consistent, pre-resourced starting point — pushing cost and decision-making up to where it is decided once rather than re-litigated per system.
- **Inheritance pushes work to whoever owns the control**: When deciding whether to implement a control at the system level, first check whether a common control provider already covers it; supplement with system-specific or hybrid controls only for the gap, or accept the residual risk with organizational sign-off.
- **Trace requirements from strategy to solution**: Use the security/privacy architecture as the spine that connects strategic goals → protection needs → security/privacy requirements → concrete people/process/technology controls, keeping reasoning and assumptions documented for future traceability.
- **Boundary first, then categorize**: Pin down the authorization boundary (and what crosses it) before relying on categorization and control selection, because how information enters and leaves the system shapes the risk assessment and the controls chosen.
- **Use synthetic data to engineer down life-cycle risk**: Because information may be processed in any SDLC phase, substituting synthetic data for live data during testing/integration preserves the test benefit while reducing security and privacy risk.

## Anti-patterns
The source frames these as cautions rather than labeled "anti-patterns," but it explicitly names the following pitfalls to avoid:

- **Conflicts of interest in role assignment (P-1)**: The same individual must not hold conflicting risk-management roles — for instance, an authorizing official cannot also be the system owner or common control provider for what they authorize. Combining security and privacy roles needs care because the disciplines require different expertise and may have competing priorities.
- **Treating ordering as rigid**: Forcing strict sequential execution when iterating would be more effective, efficient, or cost-effective contradicts the framework's intent; the process is designed to revisit and cycle through tasks.
- **Spending equal effort on low-impact, isolated systems**: The streamlining guidance calls for decreasing effort and resource expenditure on low-impact systems when they cannot adversely affect higher-impact systems through connections.

## Key Takeaways
1. Execute RMF tasks concurrently with the SDLC and reuse SDLC artifacts; the strongest implementation is nearly indistinguishable from routine engineering practice.
2. The process is iterative by design — the only fixed point is that an authorizing official explicitly accepts risk before the system goes into operation.
3. Prepare runs at two levels: organization-level (P-1 through P-7) sets governance, strategy, common controls, baselines, and monitoring; system-level (P-8 through P-18) scopes the specific system.
4. Establish roles and the risk management strategy (including risk tolerance) first; tolerance constrains every downstream risk decision.
5. Maximize common controls and organizationally-tailored baselines/overlays to reduce per-system workload and cost; document hybrid controls explicitly.
6. Derive requirements from protection needs and trace them through the security/privacy architecture; allocate them to system, elements, or environment to decide where controls live.
7. The authorization boundary defines the scope of protection and must be agreed — especially across impact levels and with external providers — for accountability and credible categorization.
8. SP 800-160 v1 supplies the systems-engineering requirements and life-cycle processes; the CSF supplies the requirement alignment/deconfliction layer; SP 800-53/53B supplies the controls.

## Connects To
- **NIST SP 800-160 v1 (Systems Security Engineering)**: The explicit SE bridge — cited as the source of life-cycle systems-engineering processes for deriving security/privacy requirements and informing tailoring; specific 800-160 processes are referenced across nearly every Prepare task.
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (Core, Profiles, Implementation Tiers)**: Used to identify, align, and deconflict requirements and to inform control selection; each Prepare task maps to specific CSF Subcategories.
- **NIST SP 800-53 / SP 800-53B**: The control catalog and the baselines/tailoring guidance that organizationally-tailored baselines and overlays are derived from.
- **FIPS 199 / FIPS 200 and CNSSI 1253**: Categorization and the high-water-mark concept underpinning impact-level prioritization; CNSSI 1253 governs National Security Systems.
- **SP 800-39, SP 800-30, SP 800-137, SP 800-161**: Multi-level risk management, risk assessment, continuous monitoring, and supply-chain risk management (SCRM) processes referenced throughout the Prepare tasks.
- **IR 8062**: Privacy risk management and the privacy risk model referenced for privacy risk assessments (Task P-14).
- **NARA CUI / SP 800-60**: Controlled Unclassified Information and information-type guidance feeding information-type identification (Task P-12) and the information life cycle (Task P-13).
- **Downstream RMF steps (Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, Monitor)**: Prepare outputs — roles, risk strategy, common controls, baselines, boundaries, requirements, registration — feed directly into every subsequent step; system registration is later updated with categorization data.
