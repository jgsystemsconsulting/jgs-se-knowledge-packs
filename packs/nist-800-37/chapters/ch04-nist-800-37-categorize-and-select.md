# Chapter 4: Categorize and Select Steps

> Scope note: This chapter sits at the security-engineering edge of systems engineering. The Categorize and Select steps are RMF activities for information systems, but they are deliberately tied into the SDLC and into the life-cycle-based systems engineering processes of [SP 800-160 v1] and [ISO 15288] — the connection points are where this material is most relevant to a systems engineer.

## Core Idea
The Categorize and Select steps convert an understanding of *what a system does and how badly its failure would hurt* into a concrete, documented, and approved set of protective controls. Categorize fixes the system's impact level by reasoning about the consequences of losing confidentiality, integrity, or availability across the missions, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation that the system touches. Select then uses that impact level to choose a control baseline, tailor it to the actual system and its operating environment, allocate the resulting controls to specific system elements, document the planned implementations, and define how control effectiveness will be monitored over time — all before the authorizing official approves the plans and the work proceeds to Implement.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **RMF Categorize step (Tasks C-1, C-2, C-3)** — *what it is, when it runs*: The step that determines adverse impact to organizational operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation from loss of confidentiality, integrity, and availability. It runs at Initiation (concept/requirements definition) for a new system, or during Operations/Maintenance for an existing one. C-1 documents the system's characteristics; C-2 produces the security categorization; C-3 reviews and approves it. Categorization is a *precondition* for selecting security controls.
- **RMF Select step (Tasks S-1 through S-6)** — *what it is, when it runs*: The step that selects, tailors, and documents the controls needed to protect the system commensurate with risk. It runs at Development/Acquisition for a new system (with allocation and review tasks reaching back into Initiation), or during Operations/Maintenance for an existing one. S-1 selects baselines, S-2 tailors them, S-3 allocates controls, S-4 documents planned implementations, S-5 defines the system-level continuous monitoring strategy, and S-6 reviews and approves the plans.
- **[FIPS 199] / [FIPS 200] high-water-mark categorization** — *when used*: For systems other than national security systems, organizations may use [FIPS 200] to set a single system impact level based on the high-water mark across information-type impact levels. [FIPS 199] supplies the impact-level scheme for the three security objectives.
- **[CNSSI 1253] three-value categorization** — *when used*: For national security systems, organizations may instead establish three separate impact values that can differ across confidentiality, integrity, and availability, rather than collapsing to a single high-water mark.
- **[SP 800-53B] control baselines** — *how used*: Federal control baselines (the predefined sets of controls that the baseline selection approach starts from) are provided here, and the tailoring process is fully described here.
- **[SP 800-53] control catalog** — *how used*: The catalog from which both baseline and organization-generated selection ultimately draw controls.
- **[SP 800-160 v1] life-cycle systems engineering processes** — *how used*: Cited as the SE process basis (alongside [ISO 15288]) for developing the well-defined security and privacy requirements that drive control selection, allocation, and documentation; the specific processes referenced shift task by task (Stakeholder Needs and Requirements Definition, System Requirements Definition, Architecture Definition, Design Definition).
- **[NIST CSF] / Cybersecurity Framework Profiles** — *how used*: Profiles can express organization-specific security and privacy requirements that guide and inform control selection from [SP 800-53]; each Categorize/Select task is mapped to CSF constructs (e.g., Profile, the Identify Function, ID.AM-1 through ID.AM-5, PR.IP, ID.GV, DE.CM).

## Key Concepts
- **System description (C-1)**: A documented characterization of the system — recorded in the security and privacy plans, attached to them, or referenced from other SDLC sources — covering everything from authorization boundary, information types, and architecture/network topology to supply-chain provenance, interconnections, users and their privileges, and authorization status. Detail is scaled to the categorization and to the security and privacy risk assessments; duplication is avoided, and the description is updated as the system evolves.
- **Security categorization (C-2)**: The determination, per information type and per security objective (confidentiality, integrity, availability), of impact levels — rolled up (for non-national-security systems) to a system categorization via the high-water mark. It is performed by the system owner and information owner/steward in collaboration with senior mission, business, and risk leaders, consistent with the risk management strategy, and it *drives* subsequent control selection.
- **Categorization review and approval (C-3)**: Authoritative sign-off. For systems processing PII, the privacy official (senior agency official for privacy) gives review-and-approval *before* the authorizing official does. The authorizing official judges the decision from an organization-wide perspective, checks consistency with other systems' categorizations and with high-value-asset requirements, and may set limits on later baseline tailoring (S-2). A rejected categorization sends the system owner back to repeat C-2.
- **High-water mark concept**: For non-national-security systems, the single system impact level is set by the most severe information-type impact level rather than by averaging — a deliberately conservative roll-up.
- **Impact-level prioritization**: Categorization results can be refined (see Task P-6) to distinguish among systems sharing the same impact level, helping owners make control selection and tailoring decisions.
- **Two control-selection approaches (S-1)**: The *baseline approach* starts from a predefined [SP 800-53B] baseline (a top-down path: start broad, then tailor down). The *organization-generated approach* builds a control set from scratch using the organization's own process — a bottom-up path that can be more efficient for highly specialized systems (a weapons system, a medical device) or limited-scope systems (a smart meter). Organizations may mix approaches across their systems.
- **Tailoring (S-2)**: Adjusting a selected baseline against factors such as missions/business functions, the threat picture, supply-chain and other security and privacy risks, system type, and risk tolerance. Tailoring actions include designating common controls, applying scoping considerations, choosing compensating controls, assigning organization-defined parameter values, supplementing with additional controls, and adding implementation-specification detail. Higher-impact systems and high-value assets warrant more rigorous tailoring rationale than low-impact systems.
- **Control allocation (S-3) — system-specific, hybrid, common**: Controls are designated as *common* (provided by the organization and inherited by one or more systems), *hybrid* (partly inherited, partly implemented at the system level), or *system-specific* (implemented for one system), then allocated to the machine, physical, or human elements that need the capability. Not every control goes to every element — controls go only to elements requiring that capability.
- **Documentation of planned control implementations (S-4)**: Security and privacy plans (or equivalent SE/life-cycle artifacts) describe each selected control with enough detail to implement and later assess it. Inherited common controls need no system-level implementation detail; for hybrid controls, the plan delineates the common-provider parts from the system-level parts. Documentation enables traceability of decisions before and after deployment.
- **System-level continuous monitoring strategy (S-5)**: A strategy, consistent with and supplementing the organizational monitoring strategy, developed early in the SDLC. It sets the assessment frequency and approach for controls the organization-level strategy does not already cover, including a time-based trigger for ongoing authorization. Volatile controls, mission-critical controls, and controls tied to plans of action and milestones may need more frequent assessment. Monitoring runs from the operational phase through disposal.
- **Plan review and approval (S-6)**: The authorizing official (or designated representative), supported by senior risk, CIO, security, and privacy officials, judges whether the plans are complete and internally consistent and whether they satisfy the system's stated security and privacy requirements. Approval is a major SDLC milestone: it commits the organization to the chosen control set and proposed implementation, lets the process advance to Implement, sets the level of effort for the remaining RMF steps, and forms the basis of security and privacy specifications for acquisition.

## Mental Models
- **Categorize is the dial that sets everything downstream.** The impact level produced in C-2 is not a compliance label — it is the input that scales control selection, tailoring rigor, monitoring frequency, and documentation depth. Get the consequence analysis right and the rest of the chain inherits the right altitude.
- **High-water mark = protect for the worst case in the bundle.** Because a single severe information type pulls the whole system up, deliberately scoping or partitioning what information a system processes is itself a risk-reduction lever.
- **Top-down (baseline) vs. bottom-up (organization-generated).** When a system is mainstream, start broad and tailor down. When it is highly specialized or narrow in purpose, building the control set from the requirements up avoids the waste of importing a broad baseline only to delete most of it.
- **Inheritance is leverage.** Designating common and hybrid controls (S-3) lets one organizational implementation satisfy many systems. The systems engineer's job is to allocate capability to the right element — and to recognize when inherited common controls are inadequate and must be supplemented with system-specific or hybrid controls (or escalated as accepted risk).
- **Requirements first, controls second.** Both selection approaches require a clearly defined body of security and privacy requirements produced by a life-cycle SE process ([SP 800-160 v1] / [ISO 15288]); controls are then chosen to *satisfy those requirements*, not assembled as an end in themselves.
- **Plan approval is a gate, not paperwork.** S-6 approval is the commitment point that authorizes movement into Implement and fixes the specification basis for acquisition — treat it with the seriousness of a design review milestone.

## Anti-patterns
- **Duplicating information across artifacts.** C-1's discussion explicitly directs that duplication of system-description information be avoided wherever possible; sprawling, repeated descriptions undercut maintainability and traceability.
- **Importing a broad baseline and deleting most of it for a specialized system.** S-1 frames the organization-generated (bottom-up) approach as the more efficient and cost-effective path for highly specialized or limited-scope systems, precisely because starting from a broad baseline and then eliminating most controls through tailoring is wasteful in those cases.

## Key Takeaways
1. Categorization is a precondition for control selection: you cannot meaningfully choose controls until C-2 has fixed the system's impact level.
2. Impact is judged per information type and per security objective (C, I, A), then rolled up — by high-water mark for non-national-security systems ([FIPS 200]) or as three independent values for national security systems ([CNSSI 1253]).
3. Categorization is collaborative and risk-strategy-bound: system and information owners decide it together with senior mission/risk leaders, and the authorizing official approves it from an organization-wide vantage point (with the privacy official approving first for PII systems).
4. Select offers two paths — baseline (top-down from [SP 800-53B]) and organization-generated (bottom-up) — and organizations may use whichever fits each system.
5. Tailoring rigor scales with stakes: high-impact systems and high-value assets demand more detailed scoping and supporting rationale than low-impact systems.
6. Control allocation classifies every control as system-specific, hybrid, or common and assigns it to the machine, physical, or human elements that actually need the capability — inheritance reduces duplicated effort.
7. The continuous monitoring strategy (S-5) is set during selection, not after deployment, and runs from operations through disposal — with volatile and mission-critical controls monitored more often.
8. S-6 plan approval is the milestone that authorizes the move to Implement and establishes the security/privacy specification basis for acquisition.

## Connects To
- **RMF Prepare step (Tasks P-5, P-6, P-14, P-15, P-17, P-18)**: Prepare supplies the common controls available for inheritance (P-5), impact-level prioritization (P-6), privacy risk assessment (P-14), the requirements derivation that feeds selection (P-15), the requirements allocation that guides control allocation (P-17), and the registration updates that record categorization results (P-18).
- **RMF Implement step**: S-6 plan approval is the explicit hand-off — it is what allows the risk management process to proceed to Implement.
- **[SP 800-160 v1] and [ISO 15288] systems engineering processes**: The life-cycle SE processes that produce the security and privacy requirements driving selection; [ISO 15288]'s "fit-for-purpose" validation outcome is invoked to judge whether a system is suitable. This is the principal bridge from RMF into mainstream SE.
- **[FIPS 199], [FIPS 200], [CNSSI 1253]**: The categorization standards that define impact levels and the high-water-mark vs. three-value choices used in C-2/C-3.
- **[SP 800-53] and [SP 800-53B]**: The control catalog and federal baselines (plus the full tailoring procedure) that S-1 and S-2 draw on.
- **[SP 800-137] continuous monitoring**: The basis for the system-level continuous monitoring strategy developed in S-5.
- **[SP 800-30] / [SP 800-39] risk assessment and risk management**: The risk-assessment results and multi-level (organization/mission/system) risk management strategy that inform categorization, tailoring, and monitoring decisions throughout both steps.
- **[NIST CSF]**: Profiles and Functions mapped across every Categorize and Select task, linking RMF outcomes to the Cybersecurity Framework.
