# Chapter 1 — Introduction: The Need to Manage Security and Privacy Risk

## Core Idea
Organizations run their missions on information systems, and the success of those missions depends on protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the information those systems handle — plus the privacy of the individuals whose data they process. Because the threat landscape spans equipment failure, environmental disruption, human and machine error, and deliberate attacks that are increasingly skilled, organized, and well-resourced, managing security and privacy *risk* is not optional housekeeping but a leadership responsibility that must be owned and accounted for at every level. SP 800-37 Rev. 2 answers that need with the **Risk Management Framework (RMF)** — a repeatable, life-cycle process for building protection into systems, watching their posture continuously, and giving senior leaders the information they need to make risk-based acceptance decisions. (Note for SE readers: this chapter is the RMF/security-engineering edge of systems engineering — it governs *how much* protection and *whether to accept residual risk*, complementing the engineering disciplines that decide *how* protection is built.)

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Risk Management Framework (RMF)** — introduced in §1.1 as the central artifact of the publication. NIST developed it jointly with three partners — the Department of Defense, the national intelligence community's Office of the Director, plus the Committee on National Security Systems — to improve information security, strengthen risk-management practice, and enable reciprocity among organizations.
  - When/how: applied across the system development life cycle (SDLC) to a federal information system; it builds security and privacy capabilities into systems, sustains situational awareness through continuous monitoring, and surfaces risk information to leaders for acceptance decisions.
- **Privacy program integration into the RMF** — referenced in §1.1 via the July 2016 revision of OMB Circular A-130, which folded privacy-program responsibilities under the RMF. This is *how* privacy entered the framework as a first-class concern alongside security.
  - When/how: whenever a system processes personally identifiable information (PII), the privacy program coordinates with the security program because their objectives around confidentiality, integrity, and availability of PII are complementary.
- **Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM)** — introduced in the opening section as a growing concern closely related to, and part of, security and privacy risk. The publication folds SCRM practices *into* the RMF rather than treating them as a parallel track.
  - When/how: invoked wherever reliance on third-party/external providers and commercial-off-the-shelf products, systems, or services creates exposure to attacks or disruptions in the supply chain; SCRM concepts are explicitly flagged at several points to show how the RMF addresses them.
- **Framework for Improving Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity (NIST CSF)** — named in §1.2 as something the RMF is designed to help federal agencies implement; per the cited Executive Order, agencies use the CSF to manage cybersecurity risk.
  - When/how: a stated purpose of these guidelines is to facilitate CSF adoption within federal agencies.

## Key Concepts
- **Information system** (per the cited statutory definition): a discrete set of information resources organized to collect, process, maintain, use, share, disseminate, or dispose of information. The term is deliberately broad — it covers general-purpose computers, industrial/process-control and cyber-physical systems, weapons systems, supercomputers, command-and-control systems, smartphones and tablets, embedded sensors, and even paper-based systems.
- **Risk**: a measure of how much an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event, combining the adverse impact if it occurs with the likelihood of occurrence. The publication enumerates many risk *types* — program, compliance/regulatory, financial, legal, mission/business, political, security and privacy (including supply chain), project, reputational, safety, and strategic-planning risk.
- **Security vs. privacy risk**: the two programs share objectives around the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of PII, but privacy risk is not merely a subset of security risk. Many privacy risks come from *unauthorized* activity that breaks CIA of PII; others arise from fully *authorized* processing — creating, collecting, using, storing, disclosing, or disposing of PII — that nonetheless harms individuals (e.g., failing to give appropriate notice, or causing embarrassment or stigma). Because privacy raises distinct concerns needing specialized expertise, organizations must maintain a robust privacy program of their own.
- **Reciprocity**: an agreement to accept another organization's security assessment results — reusing system resources or accepting an assessed posture — so that information can be shared without redundant re-assessment.
- **Continuous monitoring**: the ongoing process that maintains situational awareness of a system's security and privacy posture and underpins ongoing authorization decisions.
- **Authorization / authorizing official**: the framework connects organization- and mission-level risk management to system-level risk management through a senior accountable official for risk management and a risk executive (function), and it establishes responsibility and accountability for controls — both those implemented in a system and those *inherited* by it.
- **Policy- and technology-neutral by design**: the RMF deliberately avoids binding to specific policy or technology so that IT can be upgraded and modernized without breaking the risk process, keeping essential missions running through transitions.

## Mental Models
- **Build it in across the life cycle, don't authorize it at the end.** The RMF promotes developing security and privacy capabilities *throughout* the SDLC and integrating security/privacy requirements and controls into enterprise architecture, acquisition, and systems-engineering processes — risk management is woven through, not stapled on at the end.
- **Govern the layers, link the levels.** Risk decisions at the organization and mission/business-process levels are connected to risk decisions at the individual-system level through the risk executive (function) and a senior accountable official — so a single system's risk posture is legible to, and bounded by, the enterprise risk strategy.
- **Risk is a leadership ledger.** The framework's job is to give senior leaders timely, correct metrics so they can make cost-effective, risk-based decisions and consciously *accept* (or decline) residual risk on behalf of the organization — risk is owned, not delegated into the technical weeds.
- **Privacy is complementary but distinct.** Treat security and privacy as overlapping circles, not nested ones: they coordinate on CIA of PII, yet privacy carries its own authorized-processing harms that security controls alone won't catch.
- **The supply chain is inside your boundary.** Because external providers and COTS components carry risk into your systems, manage that risk *within* the RMF rather than pretending it lives somewhere outside.

## Key Takeaways
1. Missions depend on systems; protecting those systems' **confidentiality, integrity, availability** — and individuals' **privacy** — is a leadership accountability, not just a technical task.
2. The **RMF** is the publication's answer: a repeatable, life-cycle, risk-based process developed with DoD, ODNI, and CNSS to improve security, strengthen risk management, and enable **reciprocity**.
3. The RMF builds protection in across the **SDLC**, sustains posture through **continuous monitoring**, and feeds **risk-acceptance decisions** to senior leaders.
4. **Privacy** entered the RMF via OMB Circular A-130 (2016); it is complementary to security but raises **distinct** authorized-processing harms requiring a dedicated privacy program.
5. **SCRM** and the **NIST CSF** are integrated into the RMF's scope — supply-chain risk is managed inside the framework, and the framework helps agencies implement the CSF.
6. The framework is **policy- and technology-neutral**, applies to a broad definition of *information system*, and is scoped to **federal information systems** (with state/local/tribal and private-sector use encouraged).
