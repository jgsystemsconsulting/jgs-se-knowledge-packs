# Chapter 7 — RMF Monitor Step (Section 3.7)

> **Scope caveat.** This pack sits at the RMF / security-and-privacy-engineering edge of systems engineering. The Monitor step is where the security lifecycle becomes continuous: it is the closest RMF analogue to SE's operations-and-maintenance and in-service surveillance activities, but framed in terms of control effectiveness, risk posture, and authorization rather than mission performance.

## Core Idea
The Monitor step exists to sustain *ongoing situational awareness* of the security and privacy posture of both the individual system and the wider organization, so that risk-management decisions can be made on current evidence rather than a stale point-in-time snapshot. Where the earlier RMF steps (Categorize through Authorize) produce a one-time judgment that risk is acceptable, Monitor keeps that judgment honest over the system's operational life. It is built around the recognition that systems and their environments are never static — they drift through technology, people, and physical changes — and that the authorization granted at a moment in time decays unless it is continually re-evidenced. The step is the operational engine behind *near real-time risk management* and *ongoing authorization*: instead of waiting for a periodic re-authorization, the organization continuously assesses controls, responds to what it finds, updates the authorization artifacts, reports posture upward, and lets the authorizing official re-confirm (or withdraw) acceptance on a rolling basis.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **RMF Monitor step (Section 3.7)** — the seventh and final RMF step. Its stated purpose is to maintain ongoing situational awareness of security and privacy posture in support of risk decisions. The seven Monitor tasks and their outcomes are summarized in **Table 8: Monitor Tasks and Outcomes**, which also maps each task to applicable **Cybersecurity Framework** constructs.
- **The seven Monitor tasks (M-1 … M-7)** — System and Environment Changes (M-1), Ongoing Assessments (M-2), Ongoing Risk Response (M-3), Authorization Package Updates (M-4), Security and Privacy Reporting (M-5), Ongoing Authorization (M-6), and System Disposal (M-7). Each task carries Potential Inputs, Expected Outputs, a Primary Responsibility, Supporting Roles, and a System Development Life Cycle phase mapping (here, almost entirely Operations/Maintenance, with M-7 mapping to Disposal for existing systems).
- **Continuous monitoring strategy** — invoked repeatedly as the governing artifact for Monitor. The source distinguishes an *organization-level* strategy (set in Task P-7) from an optional *system-level* strategy (Task S-5); together they set the monitoring frequency for each control and the cadence of reporting and updates.
- **Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF) mappings** — Table 8 and the task References tie Monitor activities to CSF constructs: detection and governance (DE.CM; ID.GV) for change monitoring, supply-chain (ID.SC-4) for ongoing assessment, the Respond function (RS.AN, RS.IM) for analysis and improvement, and the full Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover core for posture reporting.
- **Ongoing authorization** — introduced through Task M-6 as the disposition that replaces fixed-term re-authorization. It produces ongoing authorization to operate / to use / common control authorization, or the corresponding *denials*, derived from continuous-monitoring results rather than a scheduled package review. The source points to **Appendix F** for time- and event-driven authorization detail.
- **Referenced companion publications** — the tasks cite a consistent supporting set: SP 800-137 (continuous monitoring / ISCM), SP 800-53A (assessment procedures), SP 800-128 (security-focused configuration management, SecCM), SP 800-88 (media sanitization, for disposal), SP 800-30 (risk assessment), SP 800-39 (the three-tier risk view), SP 800-160 v1 (verification, validation, operation/maintenance, and risk-management processes — the explicit SE bridge), IR 8011 v1 (assessment automation), and IR 8062 (privacy engineering).

## Key Concepts
- **M-1 System and Environment Changes.** Monitor the system and its environment for changes that affect security and privacy posture. The source breaks change into three element classes: technology/machine (hardware, software, firmware upgrades), human (staff turnover, reductions in force), and physical/environmental (facility relocation, changes to physical access controls). Changes introduced by external providers are flagged as especially hard to detect. The organization watches for both *unauthorized* changes (whether from adversary action or inadvertent personnel error) and *authorized* changes that nonetheless shift the privacy posture, and it pursues the **root cause** of any unauthorized change so the response in M-3 can be targeted. This task leans on configuration and change management discipline (SecCM, per SP 800-128).
- **M-2 Ongoing Assessments.** After the initial authorization, all controls are reassessed on a continuing basis at a frequency set by the continuous monitoring strategy. Assessors retain a degree of independence determined by the authorizing official; that independence is what lets ongoing assessment results be *reused* for ongoing authorization, re-authorization, and the annual FISMA assessment requirement. The source stresses reuse of current, relevant results across SDLC testing, audits, and prior assessments as a cost lever, and notes that automation expands assessment frequency, volume, and coverage.
- **M-3 Ongoing Risk Response.** Respond to risk surfaced by monitoring, risk assessments, and open POA&M items. Assessment findings flow to the system owner and common control provider; the authorizing official decides the response (or approves a proposed one). Two response paths are named: *acceptance* (the finding stays documented in the assessment reports and is watched for changes in risk factors) and *mitigation* (the planned action is tracked in the POA&M). Modified, enhanced, or newly added controls are *reassessed* to confirm correct implementation and intended operation.
- **M-4 Authorization Package Updates.** Keep the authorization artifacts current — security and privacy plans, assessment reports, and POA&Ms are updated on an ongoing basis to drive near real-time risk management. The source insists updates be accurate and timely (because they steer decisions), and that information needed for oversight, management, and auditing is *not* destroyed when artifacts are revised — change tracking through configuration management preserves transparency, traceability, and individual accountability.
- **M-5 Security and Privacy Reporting.** Report posture to the authorizing official and other senior officials on an ongoing basis per the continuous monitoring strategy. Reporting may be event-driven, time-driven, or both. Organizations have latitude in breadth, depth, formality, and format; at minimum a report summarizes what changed in the plans, assessment reports, and POA&Ms since the last report. Dashboards and automated reporting tools support timeliness. Cadence is balanced — frequent enough to be current, not so frequent as to create needless work or expense — and reports can satisfy FISMA remediation-reporting requirements.
- **M-6 Ongoing Authorization.** The authorizing official reviews posture on a rolling basis (per Task R-4 guidance) to decide whether risk *remains* acceptable. Continuous-monitoring results feed near-real-time, risk-based decisions; the AO may keep the authorization, or issue a *denial* of authorization to operate / to use / common control authorization. By exercising ongoing risk determination and acceptance, the AO maintains authorizations over time and transitions the system into the ongoing-authorization model, conveying updated determinations to the risk executive. Metrics and dashboards are called out as enablers of this decision-making.
- **M-7 System Disposal.** When a system (or a system element) is removed from operation, execute the disposal strategy. The disposal controls the source names cover sanitizing media, controlling and managing configuration, verifying component authenticity, and retaining records. Inventory and tracking systems are updated, posture reports reflect the change, and affected users and application owners are notified — critically, any **control-inheritance relationships are reviewed and assessed for impact**, since disposing of a common-control provider can leave dependent systems exposed.

## Mental Models
- **Authorization as a perishable good.** Categorize-through-Authorize stamps an expiry-free judgment at one instant; Monitor is the refrigeration that keeps it from spoiling. Without continuous evidence, an authorization silently decays even though the paperwork still says "authorized."
- **The continuous loop replacing the periodic gate.** Read M-1 → M-6 as a control loop: *sense* change and reassess (M-1, M-2), *decide* and respond (M-3), *record* (M-4), *communicate* (M-5), *re-confirm acceptance* (M-6) — then back to sensing. Ongoing authorization is what you get when this loop runs fast enough that you never need to fall back to a scheduled re-authorization.
- **Two questions the AO is always asking.** "Has anything changed?" (answered by M-1/M-2 evidence and M-5 reporting) and "Is the risk still acceptable?" (answered by the M-6 determination). Everything in the step exists to keep current answers to those two questions in front of the authorizing official.
- **Reuse is the efficiency engine.** Independence-qualified assessment results are an asset that can be spent against multiple obligations — ongoing authorization, re-authorization, and the annual FISMA assessment — so the same monitoring evidence does several jobs rather than being regenerated for each.
- **Disposal as a risk event, not a clean-up chore.** Removing a system can *create* risk elsewhere through severed inheritance relationships; M-7 treats end-of-life as something to be assessed, sanitized, and accounted for, not merely powered off.

## Anti-patterns
The source does not enumerate named "anti-patterns." It does, however, point to specific failure conditions that the step is designed to prevent, framed here only as the source frames them:
- **Undetected unauthorized change.** The discussion warns that unauthorized changes arise from both adversarial attacks and inadvertent personnel error, and that externally introduced changes are hard to detect — Monitor exists precisely so these do not go unseen and unanalyzed for root cause.
- **Destroying audit-relevant information during updates.** M-4 explicitly cautions that artifact updates must not modify or destroy information needed for oversight, management, and auditing.
- **Reporting cadence miscalibration.** M-5 warns against reporting so frequently that it generates unnecessary work and expense, while still requiring it be frequent enough to keep posture current — and against treating ongoing reports with the heavyweight formality reserved for the initial authorization.
- **Ignoring inheritance at disposal.** M-7 requires control-inheritance relationships to be reviewed for impact, implicitly warning against disposing of a system without checking what depended on it.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Monitor sustains situational awareness** of security and privacy posture for the system *and* the organization, turning a one-time authorization into a continuously re-evidenced one.
2. **Seven tasks (M-1 … M-7)** cover change detection, ongoing assessment, risk response, artifact updates, reporting, ongoing authorization, and disposal — almost all mapped to the Operations/Maintenance SDLC phase.
3. **The continuous monitoring strategy governs everything** — organization-level (Task P-7) and optional system-level (Task S-5) strategies set per-control frequency and reporting cadence.
4. **Ongoing authorization (M-6) replaces the fixed-term gate**: the AO re-confirms or denies acceptance on a rolling basis from continuous-monitoring evidence, producing ongoing authorizations or their denials.
5. **Independence enables reuse**: assessor independence lets monitoring results serve ongoing authorization, re-authorization, and the annual FISMA requirement, and automation expands assessment reach.
6. **Disposal (M-7) is a managed risk event** — sanitize media, preserve authenticity and records, update inventory, and reassess control-inheritance impacts.

- **Assess step** — Monitor's M-2 is the continuous re-run of the Assess procedures; the same assessment artifacts and POA&Ms persist into Monitor and are kept current by M-4.
