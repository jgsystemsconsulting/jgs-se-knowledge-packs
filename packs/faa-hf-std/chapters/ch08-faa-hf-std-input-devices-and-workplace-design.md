# Chapter 8: Keyboards, Input Devices, and Workstation/Workplace Design

## Core Idea

This chapter covers two adjacent layers of the human-machine interface as treated by HF-STD-001B: the devices a person physically operates to put information into a system (Section 5.7) and the physical environment that surrounds and supports that person while they do so (Section 5.8). The standard treats these as a continuum — a key is no good if the keyboard is at the wrong slope, and a trackball is no good if the console it sits on forces an awkward posture. The thread running through both halves is *fit*: the device must fit the task, the hand, and the body, and the workstation must fit the anthropometry of the people who will use it across the full range of operating postures (sit, sit-stand, stand) and the full range of body sizes in the user population.

Two characteristics distinguish this material from the HSI process guidance in the nasa-hsi pack. First, it is dimensional and prescriptive rather than procedural: where nasa-hsi tells you *when* in the lifecycle to consider the operator, HF-STD-001B tells you the actual millimetre ranges, force thresholds, and angular limits that make a design acceptable. Second, the standard is candid that much of this material is borrowed — most rules cite MIL-STD-1472G, MIL-HDBK-759C, DOD-HFDG-ATCCS, DON UISNCCS, NUREG-0700, or EPRI source documents rather than original FAA research. The FAA's contribution is the curation and consolidation of these defence and nuclear-industry sources into one design reference aimed at FAA systems, equipment, and facilities. This is HSI guidance well beyond the NASA tradition, and it complements the nasa-hsi pack by supplying the hardware-level detail that the lifecycle-level pack deliberately leaves out.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

The chapter does not introduce process frameworks so much as it inherits design criteria from a stack of named source standards. Each rule carries its provenance in a bracketed `[Source: ...]` tag, and the same handful of sources recur:

- **MIL-STD-1472G (2012)** — the dominant source for the input-device mechanics: when to use a displacement versus isometric joystick, mouse orientation tolerance, trackball rotation behaviour, stylus movement, touch-panel feedback, voice-control error recovery, and the kickspace requirement. Also cited heavily for console configuration selection and seated-armrest rules. Used as the authoritative human-engineering design criteria standard.
- **MIL-HDBK-759C (1995)** — the primary source for seated-workstation ergonomics: swivels and casters, seating dimensions, knee space, seat cushioning, armrest geometry, footrests, temporary swing-away seats, and standing-workstation/workbench dimensions.
- **MIL-HDBK-759 (1995)** — cited for the standard console dimension tables (Exhibit 5.8.2.1.1).
- **DOD-HFDG-ATCCS (1992), including V2.0** — the DoD Human Factors Design Guide for Army Tactical Command and Control Systems. Source for numeric-keypad layout, QWERTY arrangement, cursor-movement key arrangement, fixed-function key standardization, and touch-screen luminance/parallax/glare rules.
- **DON UISNCCS (1992)** — the Department of the Navy User Interface Specifications for Navy Command and Control Systems. The exclusive source for the pointer/pointing-device behavioural model: pointer functionality, single-pointer association, pointer stability, pointer shapes, the hotspot concept, and the button-function mapping (select, menu, middle-button rules, left-right reversal).
- **NUREG-0700 (1996, 2002)** — Nuclear Regulatory Commission HMI review guidance. Source for general interaction-device qualities: comfort, accidental-actuation prevention, feedback, speed/accuracy, anthropometric suitability, and environmental robustness.
- **DISA HCISG V1.0 (1992)** — Defense Information Systems Agency Human-Computer Interface Style Guide. Source for the keyboard-equivalence rules (function keys and pointing-device operations should also be reachable from alphanumeric keys).
- **ANSI (1988)** — source for the physical typing-keyboard parameters: key size, horizontal and vertical key spacing, key-depression force, and keyboard slope.
- **NASA-STD-3000A (1989)** — the NASA Man-Systems Integration Standard, cited for cursor-movement key arrangement and, in the workplace section, for the overarching "design for human activities" and "physical accommodation" rules plus the maintenance/operations separation and illumination rules. This is the explicit point of overlap with the nasa-hsi pack's underlying source tradition.
- **EPRI NP-3659/NP-36591 (1984)** — Electric Power Research Institute guidance, the source for multi-person/team console arrangement factors and for stand-console control/display placement limits.
- **Scadden & Vanderheiden (1988)** and **Casali (1992)** — the human-factors literature cited throughout Section 5.7.6 on accommodating people with disabilities.
- **Woods, Hastings, Buckle & Haslam (2002)** and **Brown (1988)** — cited for interaction-device comfort, smooth movement, ease of use, and ambidextrous operation.

When and how these are used: each is invoked at the level of an individual numbered rule, and many rules cite two or three sources jointly (for example, MIL-STD-1472G together with DOD-HFDG-ATCCS for mouse rules). The FAA standard's role is integration — it reconciles overlapping defence, nuclear, and academic sources into a single set of numbered shall/should statements.

## Key Concepts

**The control/input-device boundary is deliberately fuzzy.** The standard opens by acknowledging that the line between a "control" and an "input device" blurs in practice — pushbuttons paired with trackballs, or knobs used to adjust parameters such as range. It cross-references Chapter 5.4 (Controls and Visual Indicators) for pushbuttons, knobs, dials, and switches, treating Section 5.7 as the home for keyboards and pointing/alternative devices specifically.

**Keyboard taxonomy.** Keyboards are decomposed into six key classes: alphanumeric, dedicated formatting (Space, Tab, Enter), modifier (Shift, Ctrl, Alt — effective only while held), navigation (arrows, Home, End, Page Up/Down), fixed-function (F1, F2, …), and special-purpose (Help, Delete, Backspace).

**Quantified keyboard ergonomics.** The standard fixes the physical envelope of a typing keyboard:
- Numeric-only keypads place 1–9 in a three-by-three array with 0 centred below.
- Alphanumeric keyboards follow the QWERTY layout.
- Alphanumeric key strike-surface width: roughly 12–15 mm; non-alphanumeric keys around 64 mm². The discussion notes fastest keying and lowest error rates near 12.7 mm² keys, and that 10 mm finger-input keys remain acceptable if centre-to-centre spacing of at least 16 mm is kept.
- Horizontal key centreline spacing: about 16–19 mm; vertical centreline spacing: about 18–21 mm.
- Key-depression force: between 0.25 N and 1.5 N, with 0.5–0.6 N preferred.
- Keyboard slope: 0–25 degrees, preferably under 15 degrees.

**Keyboard behavioural rules.** Beyond geometry, keyboards must give positive feedback that the intended key registered; let users correct prior entries via delete, backspace, and insert; provide two-dimensional cursor control for text processing with cursor keys laid out spatially to mirror real cursor direction; and remain consistent across every keyboard in a multi-keyboard system. Where function keys carry operations, those operations should also be reachable from alphanumeric keys, and where both a keyboard and a pointing device exist, pointing-device operations should also be reachable from the keyboard.

**Fixed-function key discipline.** Fixed-function keys should be standardized system-wide, assigned to continuously available functions (minimizing lockout), grouped logically in distinctive locations, and — on application-dedicated keyboards — have inactive keys physically blanked rather than masked with mechanical overlays.

**Pointing-device model.** A pointing device is defined as a non-keyboard device for rapid screen navigation and object selection; a pointer is the on-screen symbol it controls, whose shape may change with function or location. The general rules require a pointing device to move the pointer, select objects, and support drag-and-drop; bind to a single pointer; move the pointer in the same direction the device moves; keep the pointer on-screen (with an exception allowing crossover to an adjacent screen); not move on its own; and hold stability within about 1.3 mm (0.25 mm preferred). Devices must be comfortable, resist accidental actuation, give visual or auditory feedback, position rapidly and accurately, move smoothly, suit the users' anthropometry, be intuitive, suit the environment (heat, cold, humidity, dust), and — if shared — work for either hand.

**Device-specific criteria.** The chapter then sets parameters per device type:
- *Mouse*: orientable within about 10 degrees of correct without looking; easy multidirectional movement without regripping; full lateral pad travel maps to full screen travel; no sharp edges.
- *Joystick*: distinguishes a **displacement joystick** (moves the way it is pushed, usually spring-loaded to recentre, less fatiguing) from an **isometric joystick** (responds to pressure without moving). Prefer displacement when accuracy beats speed or when used as a secondary control; spring-load for rate control; movement capped at about 45 degrees from centre; smooth, lag-free response without backlash or cross-coupling; mounting that supports the forearm/wrist; hand-grip length 110–180 mm with grip diameter under 50 mm and specified side/rear clearances. **Hysteresis** is defined as the lag a body shows in reacting to changing forces, and joysticks with dead-band or hysteresis must not be used with automatic cursor sequencing unless null-return-instrumented.
- *Trackball*: dimensions/resistance/clearance bounded by the exhibit; wrist or arm support for precise or continuous use; free rotation in any direction generating X/Y output without cross-coupling or backlash; used only as a position control (proportional follower movement), suitable for data pickoff and accumulative travel but offering no automatic return to origin; must tell the user how to recover a follower driven off-screen.
- *Stylus*: usable for data pickoff, point entry, and free-drawn graphics; smooth same-direction follower movement; discrete placement landing the follower at the matching coordinates and holding steady when the stylus is still.

**Pointer shapes and the hotspot.** The general-purpose pointer is an up-and-left arrow. Applications must switch to the function-specific shape when a function is invoked, redefine shape only inside their own window, and give every pointer a **hotspot** — the precise active point marking where an operation will occur. The hotspot's screen location must not shift when the pointer changes shape. New shapes (for functions without a defined shape) should be easy to see, obscure little, have an obvious hotspot, hint at purpose, and not be confusable with other screen objects.

**Button operations and mapping.** Buttons must support press, release, click, double-click, drag, and move; each button has a specific function executed on press. A one-button device gives "select"; a two-button device gives "select" (left) and "menu" (right), with the menu function raising a context menu at the pointer; a middle button may be mapped if it does not conflict. Users must be able to reverse left-right button operation.

**Alternative input devices.** Beyond keyboards and pointing devices, developers may use touch screens/panels (good for data entry and selection when typing skill is not required), optical character recognition (good for formatted printed data), and voice input (good when the user's visual and manual capacity is constrained). Alternative-device interaction should stay consistent with other devices, and selection should be the most appropriate and cost-effective for the application. Touch screens need sufficient luminance transmission, positive activation indication, immediate display feedback, and mounting that minimizes parallax and specular glare. Voice control should be tested for reliably distinguishable words under realistic conditions (balancing phonetic distinctiveness against terminology familiarity), provide easy error correction and re-try, and keep an alternative input path available if recognition repeatedly fails or the device fails.

**Interchangeability is optional, not mandatory.** If more than one input device is present, the user should be able to drive interaction with all of them — but full interchangeability is not required; the design assumes the user picks the device most appropriate to each task (direct manipulation by mouse/trackball; keyboard for text and interspersed selection).

**Accessibility.** Section 5.7.6 notes that most computer-use difficulty for people with physical disabilities stems from input devices and storage media. Provisions include: carrying out all pointing functions from the keyboard; a toggle-type select key (because holding a button while moving the device is hard for some users); delayed keystroke acceptance or a **keyguard** (a keyboard cover with holes over keys) for moderate motor disabilities; easy keyguard mounting; a connection point for an alternative input device when a standard one cannot be made accessible; treating alternative-device input identically to standard input; readable, high-contrast key lettering (with removable/replaceable keycaps for the visually impaired); non-visual indication of toggle-key state for blind users; key edges discernible by touch (flat membrane keys without ridges are flagged as difficult); tactile markings on "home" keys; non-visual alternatives to key labels; and a persistent special window for special input routines.

**Workstation versus workplace.** Section 5.8 defines a **workstation** as a place designed for a specific task from which work is conducted (desks, offices, repair benches, consoles, terminals) and a **workplace** as the broader area, room, or establishment where work is done. The section is organized to start at the single workstation and broaden to multi-workstation arrangements and whole workplaces.

**Anthropometric accommodation and the percentile convention.** Workplace and equipment design must systematically incorporate task effects, performance capabilities, and physical and viewing dimensions for both maintainers and operators, conforming to the anthropometric and biomechanical characteristics of the specific user population (data in Chapter 5.12). The standard's percentile convention is explicit: clearance dimensions that a large portion of users may use frequently or in life-threatening situations take at least the 99th-percentile value (heavy passageways, doorways); reach and strength take 1st-percentile values as limiting dimensions (so the smallest personnel can reach escape mechanisms); and most other cases use the 5th-through-95th-percentile range. Higher percentiles are typically male-dominated and lower percentiles female-dominated.

**Maintenance/operations separation.** A cluster of rules keeps maintenance from interfering with operations: maintenance workplaces, controls, and displays should be separated from operator ones; maintenance activity should not interfere with operator tasks; where simultaneous activity is unavoidable, redundant information should reach operators for diagnostic and emergency duties; maintenance-only controls and displays should be accessible to maintainers without being visible to operators; and illumination must serve both, with general plus task illumination, compatibility between maintenance and operator lighting, and assurance that maintenance lighting does not interfere with operator vision in critical cases.

**Console families.** Standard consoles come in sit, sit-stand, and stand variants, with five configuration types dimensioned in the exhibits. Console selection should weigh visibility over the top, user mobility, panel-space demand for controls and displays, leg/equipment volume beneath the writing surface, and communications demands. Task performance at a console is influenced by panel contours and slopes, viewing parallax, control/display location, and the adequacy of supporting space.

**Seated-workstation design.** Seated positions reduce workload (the seat carries body weight), support sustained attention, stabilize fine eye-hand work, allow strong pedal exertion, give more equipment access, and can swivel and roll to extend reach and visual access — at the cost of less arm force, smaller arm movements, and reduced two-arm reach versus standing. Concrete seated rules: provide swivel and casters for most non-heavy tasks; meet or exceed minimum seating dimensions with adjustment ranges; aim for about 640 mm knee-space height (more if a footrest is added); gain knee/leg space by sloping the console surface under the writing surface; cushion seats for work over an hour at a stretch or over 20% of working time; design cushions to be flat-firm-yet-deformable, shock-absorbing, weight-supporting at the pelvis, tilted back about 5–7 degrees, lumbar-shaped, non-compressive under the thighs, ventilated, and posture-shiftable (larger backrests preferred); provide undercut armrests (adjustable range about 190–280 mm above the compressed seat, at least 200 mm long) unless task-incompatible; align the armrest with the control plane for tracking tasks; provide footrests when seated long in seats above about 460 mm or at surfaces above about 760 mm, kept clear of traffic; and offer temporary swing-away seats (about 380 mm diameter, 460 mm floor-to-top, ±50 mm adjustment) where space and task frequency warrant.

**Special-purpose console geometry.** Where a seated user needs more panel than the standard tables allow, a segmented horizontal **wrap-around console** is used, with left/right segments angled from the central segment's frontal plane so a 5th-percentile stationary operator can reach them. Width limits differ by whether vision over the top is required (central segment not exceeding about 1.12 m with see-over, or 860 mm without; side segments not exceeding about 610 mm), and the total left-to-right viewing angle must not exceed 190 degrees and should be reduced where possible. Where see-over is not needed and lateral space is tight, the panel is instead divided into three **vertical stacked segments** kept perpendicular to the line of sight; only infrequently used information belongs in the upper tiers, because screens above resting eye level force awkward, painful postures.

**Multi-person/team consoles.** When a team monitors, diagnoses, or controls a large ongoing process, console arrangement is driven by a long list of primary factors (functions/tasks across normal, degraded, and emergency modes; team communication and external command-and-control links; staffing for peak workload; common and individual viewing needs; maintenance access; supervisory viewing and control; management/maintenance/operating concepts; architectural constraints; equipment space; environmental requirements; work surfaces; and storage) plus secondary support factors (supervisor privacy, security, visitor/traffic provisions, personnel conveniences, and support storage). Team-position console types are chosen from standard sit/sit-stand/stand, free-standing, built-in vertical wall, and custom consoles based on allocated functions, see-over needs, communications, mobility, reach and visual arcs, work-surface area, sharing with neighbours, posture and duration, and standardization for training and interoperability.

**Standing-workstation design.** Standing suits routine, frequent, or short tasks where the worker must face different directions or move around; standing workers apply more arm force, reach inaccessible areas, reduce fatigue by moving, use flat knee-room-free surfaces, and face fewer dimensional constraints — at the cost of constantly carrying and balancing their own weight (so a worker pinned in one position should be given a sit or sit-stand option). Standing dimensions should meet minimums without exceeding maximum preferred values and adjust for the 5th-through-95th percentile; prime controls and displays go in the prime areas within stated boundaries; hardwired/annunciator displays sit horizontally within about 45 degrees of the eye reference point (within 35 degrees for displays); a single user's discrete display-control sequence should not exceed about 1.8 m in width; sit-stand see-over consoles should not exceed about 1.40 m in height with desk surfaces sized accordingly; desk height around 910–960 mm equalizes seated and standing eye height; sit-stand seats adjust up to about 760 mm with a 460 mm footrest at a constant 460 mm below the cushion; and all cabinets, consoles, and work surfaces approached closely must include a kickspace at least 100 mm deep and 100 mm high at the base.

## Mental Models

**Device-task fit before device features.** The recurring instruction is to choose the device that "most appropriately meets the application requirements" rather than defaulting to a favourite. Joysticks and trackballs for precise direct pointing; cursor keys for discrete movement; touch for typing-free selection; OCR for formatted print; voice when hands and eyes are busy. The device is selected to the task, not the task forced onto the device.

**Displacement versus isometric is an accuracy/fatigue trade.** The joystick definitions encode a decision rule: displacement joysticks move, recentre by spring, and fatigue the user less over long periods; isometric joysticks stay put and sense pressure. Pick displacement when accuracy matters more than speed or when the stick is secondary — a clean axis for reasoning about any continuous-control device.

**The hotspot is the contract between pointer and action.** The hotspot model says: whatever the pointer looks like, there is one precise point that "means" the cursor, and that point must not drift when the icon changes shape. This separates appearance (which can signal function) from the spatial commitment (which must stay stable) — a useful mental model for any direct-manipulation interface.

**Redundant input paths as resilience.** Both the keyboard-equivalence rules and the voice-control fallback rule express the same idea: critical operations should be reachable by more than one input path so that a device failure, a recognition failure, or a user limitation does not strand the operator. The accessibility section is the strongest form of this — every pointing function reachable from the keyboard.

**Design to the population, sized by consequence.** The percentile convention is a mental model for anthropometric design: pick the percentile by what failure costs. Clearance and escape → extreme percentiles (99th for clearance, 1st for reach/strength) so nobody is trapped or unable to reach; everyday fit → the 5th–95th band. Size the safety margin to the stakes, not uniformly.

**Posture follows task duration and attention demand.** The sit/sit-stand/stand decision is framed as a function of mobility need, force need, and how long attention must stay fixed. A free-to-move worker can stand; a worker pinned by concentrated attention demands should be seated or sit-stand. Posture is an output of the task analysis, not a fixed assumption.

**Eye level is the anchor for vertical layout.** The vertical-stacked-segment discussion and the standing-display placement rules both anchor on the eye reference point: keep surfaces perpendicular to the line of sight, keep frequently used information near eye level, and push only rarely needed information above it. Vertical placement is reasoned outward from where the eyes rest.

## Anti-patterns

The source names several practices to avoid:

- **Mechanical overlays to lock out function keys.** The standard states mechanical overlays should not be used to lock out function keys; on dedicated keyboards, inactive keys should instead be physically blanked.
- **Flat membrane keys without ridges.** In the accessibility discussion, flat membrane keys lacking ridges to outline the keys are called out as particularly difficult for users who rely on touch.
- **Joysticks with dead-band/hysteresis in auto-sequencing.** Displacement joysticks with a centre dead-band or hysteresis must not be used with automatic cursor or tracking-symbol sequencing unless instrumented for null return or zero-set to the stick's instantaneous position.
- **Arbitrary spoken-command selection.** The voice-control discussion warns that command entries are not to be chosen arbitrarily; the trade-off between phonetic distinctiveness and terminology familiarity must be evaluated by testing.
- **Frequently used information placed above resting eye level.** The vertical-stacked-segment discussion warns that locating screens above resting eye level forces uncomfortable, awkward postures and pain, so only infrequently used information belongs in upper tiers.

## Key Takeaways

- Section 5.7 is a dimensional design reference for input devices; Section 5.8 is a dimensional design reference for the workstations and workplaces that house them. Both are organized as numbered shall/should rules, each tagged with its borrowed source.
- The physical typing keyboard is fully quantified by ANSI (1988): key width near 12–15 mm, horizontal spacing 16–19 mm, vertical spacing 18–21 mm, depression force 0.25–1.5 N (0.5–0.6 N preferred), slope 0–25 degrees (under 15 preferred).
- The pointing-device behavioural model (single pointer, same-direction movement, on-screen retention, hotspot, button mapping) comes almost entirely from DON UISNCCS (1992); the device mechanics (mouse, joystick, trackball, stylus) come almost entirely from MIL-STD-1472G (2012).
- Choose the input device to fit the task: displacement vs isometric joystick by accuracy/fatigue; trackball as a position-only control with no auto-return; touch, OCR, and voice as alternatives matched to specific task constraints.
- Provide redundant input paths and accessibility provisions: keyboard equivalents for pointing operations, toggle select keys, keyguards, alternative-device connection points, and tactile/non-visual cues — most cited to Scadden & Vanderheiden (1988).
- Anthropometric accommodation uses a consequence-scaled percentile convention: 99th percentile for clearance, 1st percentile for limiting reach/strength, 5th–95th for general fit (Chapter 5.12 holds the data).
- Workstation posture (sit, sit-stand, stand) is selected from task mobility, force, attention-duration, and reach demands, with detailed seating-comfort and standing-dimension criteria drawn from MIL-HDBK-759C (1995).
- Console geometry is task-driven: standard consoles by configuration table, wrap-around for excess panel space, vertical stacked segments for tight lateral space, and team consoles selected against extensive primary and support factor lists (EPRI NP-3659).

## Connects To

- **nasa-hsi pack (complement, not overlap):** This chapter supplies the hardware-and-environment detail that the nasa-hsi lifecycle pack deliberately abstracts away. Where nasa-hsi tells a programme *when* to fold the operator into the SE lifecycle, HF-STD-001B gives the concrete millimetre and force criteria that an acceptable workstation must meet. The overlap is explicit at the source level: several Section 5.8 rules cite NASA-STD-3000A (1989), the same NASA Man-Systems Integration tradition the nasa-hsi pack descends from. Use the two together — nasa-hsi for process and HF-STD-001B for design criteria.
- **Other HF-STD-001B chapters (internal cross-references):** Chapter 5.4 (Controls and Visual Indicators) for pushbuttons, knobs, dials, and switches at the blurred control/input boundary; Chapter 5.3 (Displays and Printers) for display-location guidance referenced in the vertical-segment discussion; Chapter 5.11 for illumination criteria behind the workplace lighting rules; and Chapter 5.12 for the anthropometric and biomechanical data underlying the percentile convention.
- **Caveat — HSI beyond NASA:** The borrowed-source model here (MIL-STD-1472G, MIL-HDBK-759C, DOD-HFDG-ATCCS, DON UISNCCS, NUREG-0700, DISA HCISG, ANSI, EPRI) shows that the FAA standard is a curated consolidation of defence, naval, nuclear, and standards-body human-factors work. Treat HF-STD-001B as the integration layer, and trace any specific number back to its cited source when authority or currency matters.
