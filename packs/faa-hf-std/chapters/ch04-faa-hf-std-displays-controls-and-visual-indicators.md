# Chapter — Displays, Printers, Controls, and Visual Indicators

## Core Idea

This chapter covers the two halves of the classic human-equipment loop: how a system pushes information out to a person, and how a person pushes intent back into the system. HF-STD-001B Section 5.3 governs the output side — the monitors, large-screen and projection displays, stereoscopic and head-mounted displays, see-through and heads-up displays, printers, and the accessibility provisions that make output reach the widest possible population. Section 5.4 governs the input and feedback side — the controls a user touches, the visual indicators that report state back, and the integration rules that bind a control to the indicator it drives.

The unifying argument is that a display or control is only as good as the human visual and motor system it serves. Every criterion in this slice traces back to a perceptual or biomechanical fact: the fovea is the area of highest acuity and lies in the central 30 degrees of the visual field; the resting eye sits about 15 degrees below horizontal; the fifth-percentile female reach sets the maximum control distance; contrast, luminance, and character size must be sized in minutes of arc against the worst-case viewing distance. The standard's method is to convert those facts into quantified, testable limits — viewing distances, contrast ratios, character angular sizes, control forces and displacements, flash rates, luminance ratios — so that "legible," "reachable," and "unambiguous" become requirements an engineer can verify rather than adjectives a reviewer can argue about.

This pack is human-systems-integration material that reaches well beyond the NASA-centric HSI body of knowledge; it complements `nasa-hsi` rather than duplicating it. Where `nasa-hsi` frames displays and controls inside the spaceflight HSI domains, HF-STD-001B supplies the FAA-facility and military-derived design rules — heavily anchored in MIL-STD-1472G — that apply to control rooms, air traffic control towers, TRACONs, and equipment rooms, and it quantifies them down to footcandles, millimeters, and minutes of arc. Several rules still cite NASA-STD-3000B, so the two packs share lineage even as this one extends into ground systems.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

As elsewhere in the standard, no single named framework is invented; the chapter harmonizes design criteria from a defined set of cited sources, attaching each rule to its origin. The sources recurring in this slice are:

- **MIL-STD-1472G (2012)** — the dominant source throughout, supplying the great majority of "shall" criteria for displays, large-screen and projection displays, HUDs, see-through displays, printers, every control type (rotary switches, knobs, push buttons, toggle/rocker/slide switches, cranks, levers, thumbwheels, push-pull and PC switches), all the visual-indicator types (transilluminated displays, legend lights, simple indicator lights, panel assemblies, LEDs, counters, flags, scale indicators), and the control-indicator integration rules.
- **DOD-HFDG-ATCCS (1992)** — the DoD Human Factors Design Guide for the Army Tactical Command and Control System; cited for prototype evaluation, resolution and character-formation criteria, stereoscopic-display rules, positive-versus-negative polarity, flat-panel treatment, and the analog-versus-digital coding guidance.
- **NASA-STD-3000B (1995)** and **NASA-STD-3000A (1989)** — cited for simultaneous display-control monitoring, sunlight shielding, high-ambient contrast degradation, vibration provisions, and failure indicators (overload, out-of-range, power-failure).
- **BSR/HFES (2002)** — the draft HFES/ANSI VDT standard; cited for luminance uniformity, contrast ratios, character size, stroke width, jitter, and specular-glare luminance.
- **ISO 9241** family, including **ISO 9241-3 (1992)** and **ISO 9241-303 (2006)** — cited for adjustability, character formation, color fringes, stereoscopic/binocular tolerances, and vibration stabilization.
- **NUREG-0700 (1981 and 2002 editions)** — Nuclear Regulatory Commission control-room guidance; cited for luminance range, large-screen viewing geometry, knob style, and indicator-to-switch correspondence.
- **TCO '03 / TCO '06 (2005a, 2005b, 2006)** — Swedish display-certification criteria; cited for tilt/height/swivel adjustability, off-axis contrast and color uniformity, RGB color settings, and black level.
- **Hopper, Dolezal, Schur & Liccione (1994)** — cited for surface quality, environmental impact, durability, reliability (the 10,000-hour MTBF goal), and flat-panel pixel-defect limits.
- **Vanderheiden & Vanderheiden (1991)**, **Scadden & Vanderheiden (1988)**, and **Kanis (1993)** — the accessibility sources for both display accessibility (Section 5.3.6) and control accessibility (Section 5.4.4).
- **Neale (1998)** and **Rash, Ledford & Mora (1996)** — cited for helmet-mounted-display fit, controls, weight, and head-tracking accuracy.
- Plus supporting citations to **FAA-G-2100H**, **OSHA 29 CFR 1910**, **CIE (1976)**, **ANSI (1988)**, **EPRI NP 6209**, **MIL-HDBK-759C**, **UCRL-15673**, **C012-003-016**, **Defence Standard 00-25**, **Helander (1992)**, and several human-factors research papers.

The chapter also carries an explicit **color-coding semantic framework** for transilluminated displays (Exhibit 5.4.2.2.1.27) that maps colors to meanings — flashing red for emergencies needing immediate action, red for inoperative or no-go states, yellow for marginal/caution/recheck conditions, green for in-tolerance/ready/proceed, white for neutral state with no right-or-wrong implication, and blue reserved for advisory-only use. This same color framework is referenced back by the legend-light, simple-indicator, LED, and scale-indicator sections, making it the spine of the visual-indicator material.

## Key Concepts

**Display viewing geometry.** Locate displays so a user can read them to the required accuracy without an uncomfortable, awkward, or unsafe posture, and without ladders or flashlights. The screen sits directly in front of the user in the normal working position; the line of sight to screen center falls roughly between 10 and 60 degrees below horizontal (the resting eye is about 15 degrees below, and lower placement aids accommodation and convergence, especially for users over 40 or in bifocals). The face should be perpendicular to the line of sight and tilted no more than 45 degrees from it. Critical and frequently used displays belong in the central visual field — the focal 30 degrees around the fovea — so the user moves eyes rather than head. Where a control is associated with a display, the seated user's eye-to-display distance stays at or under 635 mm so a fifth-percentile female can still reach the control; the minimum viewing distance is 330 mm (relaxable to 250 mm for short viewing or faint-signal detection), and beyond 50 cm the displayed information needs enlargement or other modification to stay legible.

**Luminance, contrast, and glare.** Critical displays must read clearly across the illumination range of their environment — and the standard tabulates those ranges by setting, from 5–50 footcandles in a control room up to as much as 6,000 footcandles in an air traffic control tower. Luminance should not vary more than 1.5:1 from center to edge; off-axis contrast should not drop more than 20 percent at plus or minus 30 degrees. Foreground-to-background contrast must exceed 3:1, with 7:1 preferred, degrading to no less than 2:1 only at peak ambient light. Displays must never be dimmable to a point indistinguishable from "off," because a user could miss a critical state. Glare comes in two named forms — diffuse (general illumination washing out contrast) and specular (unwanted mirror reflections); the standard lists countermeasures (placement, indirect lighting, many dim sources rather than few bright ones, hoods, anti-glare coatings, filters) and the iron constraint that no glare treatment may degrade the luminance, contrast, or resolution the display needs to do its job.

**Resolution, linearity, and color.** Legibility resolves to a few measurable quantities: pixel density (at least 90 dpi for high reading speed, 100 dpi for complex symbols), character-matrix size, stroke width (one-twelfth to one-sixth of character height), inter-character and inter-line spacing, width-to-height ratio, and above all angular character size — at least 10 minutes of arc for non-critical text and 16 for critical, with 22–24 preferred, measured at the longest expected viewing distance. The standard warns that swapping a monitor for one of different resolution changes the angular size of a fixed-pixel character. **Linearity** rules cap jitter (deviation under 2 mm per cm of viewing distance from 0.5 to 30 Hz), element-size variation (under 10 percent across the screen), element displacement, and row/column straightness. Color rules invoke CIE chromaticity coordinates for color temperature, uniformity, and off-axis uniformity, set a minimum RGB gamut, cap black level, and warn that red desaturates first under high illumination — when color desaturates, color coding stops working.

**Display types as a selection problem.** The chapter is organized so that each display family carries its own "when to use / when not to use" logic. **Flat panels** get pixel-defect limits (failure rate under 1 percent, no full defective rows or columns, no clusters where critical data appears) and a default to positive polarity (dark characters on light) because reflections show less on a bright field. **Large-screen and projection displays** are for shared viewing by teams who must move about or for cases where individual displays are impractical — but only if every viewer has acceptable distance, angle, and an unobstructed line of sight; viewing distance, off-centerline angle (10 degrees for fixed individuals, up to 30 for groups), luminance ratios, and character angular size are all bounded, and control of a group display must prevent inadvertent modification or deletion of critical information. **Stereoscopic and binocular displays** are justified only when the third dimension conveys real benefit, the population has normal stereo vision, and the field of view suits the number of viewers; tolerances govern interocular crosstalk, alignment, focus difference, temporal asynchrony, and disparity. **HUDs** present data without blocking the view and must be compatible with the human visual system, with bounded eye-box, exit pupil, contrast, and character size. **See-through displays** must stay distinct from any background and bright enough for direct sunlight. **Helmet-mounted displays** must be adjustable and balanced to limit neck strain, keep controls reachable while worn, minimize time lag (to avoid motion sickness), present only salient task information within the central 25 degrees, and never obstruct the view of other displays and controls.

**Printers.** Use a printer when a visual record is needed. Provide at least a 3.0 luminance contrast between print and background, internal illumination where ambient light is insufficient, a take-up device, and the ability to annotate paper still in the machine. Print must be free of misregistration, tilt, and smear; tape output must be readable as it emerges without cutting and pasting. Printers obey the standard's general control, feedback, and supply-indication rules, and printed output is located within easy reach of those who need it.

**Display and control accessibility.** Two dedicated sections (5.3.6 for displays, 5.4.4 for controls) extend the design rules to users with temporary or permanent limitations. The premise is honest: you cannot design everything for everyone, but adaptations can substantially widen the user base. For displays: maximize the number who can see the output, ensure critical information is not missed by those who cannot see, provide connection points for alternative output devices (speech synthesizers, Braille displays), offer redundant auditory or tactile output, and accommodate wheelchair reach for printouts. For controls: minimize required force and offer force adjustment, give ample inter-control space for adaptive add-ons, avoid continuous-hold and simultaneous-action requirements, support either-hand operation, allow non-time-dependent input, provide nonvisual identification (size/shape/texture coding, Braille, raised pointers), keep controls within reach including from a wheelchair, and avoid blue/green/violet coding for aging users.

**The control selection and design system.** Section 5.4.1 treats control choice as an engineering decision driven by the task. Workload spreads across limbs so none is overburdened; multirotation controls serve wide-range precision; detents serve discrete steps; limit stops bound travel. The standard supplies "characteristics of common controls" tables for discrete and continuous adjustments, then dimensions, resistances, displacements, and separations for each specific control. **Direction-of-movement** is standardized: forward/clockwise/right/up/press means on, increase, or move-forward (valve controls excepted). **Coding** of controls — by location, shape, size, mode of operation, labeling, or color — is governed by an advantages/disadvantages table; size coding uses no more than three sizes, shape coding no more than ten absolutely discriminable shapes, and the FED-STD-595 control color palette is fixed with red reserved for emergencies. Foot controls and switches have their own use/avoid logic. Hand controls span rotary selector switches, key-operated switches, thumbwheels, knobs, ganged knobs, cranks, push buttons, toggle/rocker/slide switches, legend switches, push-pull and printed-circuit switches, levers, high-force controls, and miniature controls — each with quantified specs and a positive-feedback requirement (snap, click, or light).

**Prevention of accidental actuation.** A recurring safety theme: critical controls must resist inadvertent movement through location/orientation, recessing or barriers, covers or guards (never safety or lock wire), interlocks requiring extra movement, added resistance, position locks, or rotary-action design — without ever slowing legitimate operation past its time requirement. **"Dead man" controls** that drive the system to a safe state when force or input is removed are required wherever operator incapacity could create a critical condition.

**Labeling and marking of controls.** Labels exist so users can locate and identify controls, follow procedures, and avoid hazards. The standard fixes size graduation (group labels larger than component labels, each tier about 25 percent larger than the next), hierarchical labeling, location and orientation (near and usually above the control, horizontal text, no obscuring), character design (heights by viewing distance, stroke-width ratios, sans-serif fonts, mixed case for phrases and all-caps for single words, dark-on-light above 10 lux and white-on-dark for dark adaptation), and wording (unambiguous, concise, no trade names, familiar abbreviations only). Pictorial symbols must be unambiguous and not used where rotation could confuse them.

**Visual indicators and the color-coding spine.** Section 5.4.2 governs indicators that report state. Coding (color, size, location, shape, flash) discriminates among displays, identifies functional relationships, and flags critical information. Flashing red denotes emergencies needing immediate action, at three to five equal-duty flashes per second; on flasher failure the light burns steady so the signal is never lost. **Transilluminated displays** (legend lights, simple indicators, panel assemblies that use back- or edge-lighting) follow extensive rules: lights indicate equipment *response*, not mere control position; the *absence* of light must not signify malfunction, ready, or power-off (a dark bulb is ambiguous); incandescent lamps need redundant filaments or lamps and a test means; luminance, contrast, and dimming bounds keep them legible and non-false; and color coding follows the Exhibit 5.4.2.2.1.27 framework. **LEDs** conform to the same rules, with the caveat that red alphanumeric LEDs must be kept away from red warning lights. **Counters, printers, and flags** are rated against each other for quantitative, qualitative, setting, and tracking uses. **Scale indicators** prefer moving-pointer/fixed-scale over fixed-pointer/moving-scale, prefer linear scales, standardize graduation progressions (1, 2, or 5 and decimal multiples), pointer geometry, parallax minimization, and range coding by color or pattern.

**Indicator-control integration.** Section 5.4.3 binds the two halves together. The relationship between a control and its indicator must be immediately apparent — through proximity, grouping, coding, demarcation, labeling, spacing, color, insert panels, or panel relief — and neither the control nor the user's hand should obscure the indicator (hence the convention of placing controls below indicators). Indicators give feedback the user perceives as instantaneous; where a true time lag exists, the system still signals the direction and progress of change. **Movement relationships** are standardized: clockwise/forward/up/right control motion drives clockwise/up/right pointer motion and increasing values. **Control-to-indicator movement ratios** balance slewing speed against fine-setting precision (small control motion for wide indicator range, large control motion for small range), with specific turn-per-displacement values for knobs and levers. **Failure indicators** are mandated for overload, out-of-range, power-failure, and open-circuit conditions, and a power-on indicator must extinguish when power is lost.

## Mental Models

- **The display serves the eye; the control serves the hand.** Every criterion descends from a perceptual fact (fovea, acuity, accommodation, dark adaptation) or a biomechanical fact (reach, strength, dexterity). Design to the human limit, not the device capability.

- **Design to the worst-case viewing and operating condition.** Longest viewing distance, brightest or darkest ambient, gloved hands, vibration, dark adaptation — the binding case sets the requirement, not the nominal one.

- **Quantify the qualities.** "Legible," "reachable," "unambiguous," "easy" all become numbers: minutes of arc, millimeters, footcandles, contrast ratios, forces, flash rates. If you cannot test it, you have not specified it.

- **When-to-use is part of the spec.** Choosing the display family or control type is the first design decision; each section pairs a "when to use" with a "when not to use," so selection is reasoned, not habitual.

- **Color and flash carry fixed meanings.** The Exhibit 5.4.2.2.1.27 semantic map (flashing red = emergency, red = inoperative/no-go, yellow = caution, green = ready, white = neutral, blue = advisory) is a shared vocabulary; reusing it preserves recognition and never invents private meanings.

- **Absence of a signal is not a signal.** A dark light, a dimmed-to-off display, or a missing indication must never be the only way a critical condition is communicated, because failure looks identical to "nothing wrong."

- **Bind the control to its indicator.** Proximity, consistent direction-of-movement, instantaneous feedback, and arrangement correspondence make the loop self-evident; ambiguity here is where operator error is born.

- **Layer error-proofing onto critical controls.** Barriers, covers, interlocks, resistance, and dead-man behavior keep inadvertent actuation from causing harm — without ever slowing the legitimate, time-critical action.

## Anti-patterns

The source explicitly names design choices to avoid:

- **Dimming a display indistinguishably from off.** A display must never be dimmable to a level that cannot be told apart from the OFF condition, because a user could miss a critical step.
- **Using absence of illumination to mean something.** The removal of light on a transilluminated display must not indicate malfunction, no-go, ready/in-tolerance (unless the bulb is easily testable), or maintenance power-off.
- **Hoods or glare treatments that degrade the display.** Hoods must not restrict the viewing angle in a task-interfering way, and no anti-glare treatment may violate the luminance, contrast, or resolution the display needs.
- **Saturated primary colors in stereoscopic displays.** They produce their own depth effects (chromostereopsis) that interfere with stereoscopic depth perception and should be avoided.
- **Safety or lock wire to prevent accidental actuation.** Covers and guards are acceptable; safety or lock wire is explicitly disallowed for toggle, push-button, and similar controls.
- **Push buttons where status must be shown by control position.** A push button must not be used if a function's status has to be indicated by the position of its control.
- **Three-knob ganged assemblies, and ganged knobs under demanding conditions.** Three-knob assemblies should be avoided, and ganged knobs should not be used for accurate/rapid/frequent operation, with gloves, or in weather/field conditions.
- **Commas on counters.** Counter numerals must not use commas.
- **Mirror-image arrangements of controls and visual indicators.** These shall not be used.
- **Curved labels.** Labels wrapped around a pipe or cable are to be avoided.
- **Trade names and irrelevant information on labels.** These must not appear.
- **Blue, green, and violet coding for aging users.** Avoided in systems intended for older users because of age-related color discrimination loss.
- **Fixed-pointer, moving-scale indicators.** Moving scales with fixed pointers should be avoided, and a fixed-pointer/moving-scale indicator is used only when an operation requires it and the acquisition program office approves.

## Key Takeaways

- The chapter governs both halves of the human-equipment loop — output (displays, printers) and input/feedback (controls, visual indicators) — and ties every rule back to a perceptual or biomechanical fact.
- Legibility and reach are specified as numbers: angular character size in minutes of arc, viewing distances in millimeters, illumination ranges by facility type in footcandles, contrast ratios, and the 635 mm control-reach cap derived from fifth-percentile female reach.
- Display-type selection is an explicit engineering choice; flat-panel, large-screen, projection, stereoscopic, HUD, see-through, and helmet-mounted displays each carry quantified specs and when-to-use / when-not-to-use logic.
- Control design is systematized across more than a dozen control families, each with dimensions, resistances, displacements, separations, coding rules, and a positive-feedback requirement.
- A single color-coding semantic framework (flashing red / red / yellow / green / white / blue) spans transilluminated displays, legend lights, simple indicators, LEDs, and scale indicators, giving the visual-indicator material a shared vocabulary.
- Safety is built in through accidental-actuation prevention, dead-man controls, redundant lamps with test means, mandated failure indicators, and the principle that the absence of a signal must never be the sole carrier of a critical condition.
- Indicator-control integration rules — proximity, non-obstruction, standardized direction-of-movement, instantaneous feedback, and arrangement correspondence — make the control loop unambiguous and error-resistant.
- Accessibility is a first-class concern, with dedicated display and control sections extending force, reach, redundancy, and nonvisual-identification provisions to users with disabilities.
