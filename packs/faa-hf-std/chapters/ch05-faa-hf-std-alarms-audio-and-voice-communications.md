# Chapter 5 — Alarms, Audio, and Voice Communications

> Source basis: HF-STD-001B, Section 5.5 (Alarms, Audio, and Voice Communications), covering 5.5.1 Alarms and Alerts, 5.5.2 Audio Signals and Audio Alarms, 5.5.3 Voice Signals and Voice Alarms, and 5.5.4 Voice Communication Systems. Every claim below is grounded in that section. Where a requirement names its provenance in brackets, that provenance is reproduced.

## Core Idea

Section 5.5 is the FAA standard's treatment of everything that reaches the operator through the ear: the logic of alarms and alerts, the physical and acoustic design of audio signals, spoken (voice) signals and alarms, and the equipment that carries speech between people. It is organized as a descent from cognitive principles to physical specification. At the top it asks *when* an audio channel should be used at all and *how* an alarm should behave as an information system; at the bottom it pins down hertz, decibels, durations, and microphone bandwidths. The unifying argument is that the auditory channel is a powerful but fragile attention resource — it commands notice even when the eyes are busy or the lighting is hostile, but it is easily overused, easily masked, and easily ignored once it loses credibility.

Two design tensions run through the whole section. The first is *salience versus restraint*: a signal must be loud, distinct, and intrusive enough to be detected and acted on, yet not so startling, persistent, or numerous that it provokes the wrong response, masks a more critical signal, or trains the operator to tune it out. The second is *audio as supplement, not substitute*: for nonverbal signals the standard repeatedly insists the sound be backed by a visual indication that defines the condition, with the audio's job being to direct the eye to the right display rather than to carry the meaning alone.

A note on positioning within this knowledge-pack collection. HF-STD-001B is human factors / human-systems integration guidance from a civil-aviation regulator's *engineering standard* — one instance of HSI thinking that lives outside the NASA tradition. This chapter complements the `nasa-hsi` pack rather than duplicating it; indeed several requirements here cite NASA man-systems integration standards (NASA-STD-3000 / NASA STD 3000A) alongside their military sources. Read the two as parallel HSI sources from different mission domains: spaceflight crew systems on the NASA side, FAA-operated air traffic and airway-facility systems here, where the controller's headset, the control-room loudspeaker, and the conventional telephone are first-class design objects.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

Section 5.5 is, like the rest of the standard, a harmonized synthesis: nearly every requirement carries an explicit bracketed source. The recurring named sources and how the section uses each:

- **MIL-STD-1472G — *Design criteria standard, human engineering* (Department of Defense, 2012).** The dominant source across 5.5. It backs the basic "when to use" alarm rule, the redundant-visual and supplement-visual rules, most of the audio-signal physics (frequency bands, intensity ceilings, duration, periodicity, differentiation), the headset/dichotic rules, the voice-signal nature and delivery rules, and almost the entire voice-communication-equipment subsection (transmission, reception, comfort, controls).
- **NUREG-0700 (Nuclear Regulatory Commission, 1981).** The principal source for alarm *implementation* logic: prioritized presentation, significance override, simple/understandable processed alarms, separating status indication from alarms, alarm filtering versus suppression, access to suppressed alarms, sensor input validation, and noise filtering. Also backs voice-intensity criteria, the speech-interference-level definition, and the conventional-telephone requirements.
- **NUREG/CR-6105 (Nuclear Regulatory Commission, 1994).** Cited for alarming only in the appropriate operational mode, the basis for prioritization (immediacy of action and safety impact), and the ease-of-access rule for suppressed alarms.
- **MIL-STD-411 (Department of Defense, 1997).** The source of the formal *definitions* of Caution, Warning, and Advisory signals, and of the rules that warning and caution signals be used for their defined conditions.
- **MIL-HDBK-761A (Department of Defense, 1989).** Backs auditory-coding use cases, environmental compatibility, intermittence, the acknowledge-and-reset family, the "when to use voice signals" list, and most of the word-selection and presentation rules for voice (concise/intelligible wording, words to avoid, formal words, phonetic alphabet, average-talker presentation, distinctive voices, brief content).
- **MIL-STD-1801 (Department of Defense, 1987).** Cited for help on critical/non-critical alarms, distinctiveness/consistency per event class, and the acknowledge/terminate-alarm rules.
- **DOD-HFDG-ATCCS / DOD-HFDG-ATCCS V2.0 (1992).** The command-and-control human factors guideline (a domain close to air traffic control). Backs limiting priority levels to four, distinctiveness per event class, user setting of alarm parameters (and when it must not be allowed), special acknowledgement of critical alarms, and non-critical alarm reset.
- **MIL-STD-1472G with NASA-STD-3000 (1995) / NASA STD 3000A (1989).** Jointly cited for the audibility floor (octave-band level above ambient) and for the loss-of-redundancy alarm and its acknowledgement behavior — explicit points of contact with the NASA man-systems tradition.
- **Human factors literature.** **Billings (1996)** for the clear/unambiguous alerting rule; **Wiener & Curry (1980)** for indicating degree of problem and for training across all alert combinations; **Wiener (1988)** for not using audio to signal normal conditions; **Patterson (1982)** and **Stanton & Edworthy (1994)** for how many alarms can be learned (the 4-to-7 / up-to-9 / ≤12 findings); **Sanders & McCormick (1993)** for modulation rates, mid-frequency localization difficulty, and preceding voice with a non-speech alert; **Merideth & Edworthy (1994)** for why continuous tones are easily confused and adapted to.
- **Other agency/vendor/standards sources.** **Ameritech Services, Inc. - **Named evaluation instruments (5.5.4.1).** The **Phonetically Balanced (PB) word test**, the **Modified Rhyme Test (MRT)** (per ANSI 3.2), and the predictive estimators **Articulation Index (AI)** and **Speech Transmission Index (STI)** — each positioned for a different accuracy/effort/lifecycle role.

## Key Concepts

**The four jobs of an alarm system.** Drawing on NUREG-0700, the standard frames an alarm not as a noise but as an information system with four functions: tell the user a problem exists, convey its priority and nature, guide the first response, and then confirm in good time whether that response actually fixed the problem. Closing that loop — confirming the fix — is as much a part of the alarm's job as raising it.

**Caution / Warning / Advisory as defined signal classes.** MIL-STD-411 supplies hard definitions the section then enforces. A *warning* indicates a hazardous condition needing immediate action to prevent loss of life, equipment damage, or service interruption. A *caution* indicates a condition needing attention but not immediate action. An *advisory* indicates a safe/normal configuration or carries routine information. Warning signals shall be used only for warning conditions, caution signals only for caution conditions, and the two shall be readily distinguishable from each other.

**Prioritization, override, and a capped number of levels.** Alarms should be organized and presented in priority order, with the most significant getting the highest priority and higher-significance alarms automatically overriding lower ones; when incidents occur simultaneously, the higher-priority message is presented first and the rest in descending order. Priority can be based on how immediately action is required and the safety impact. The number of priority levels should be capped at four.

**Filtering versus suppression — a glossary distinction with teeth.** The section is careful to separate two techniques. *Filtering* eliminates unnecessary alarms outright and should be applied only to alarms with no current operational significance; sensor inputs should be validated and noise filtered (with time-delay processing) so spurious alarms never reach the user. *Suppression* hides alarm messages but keeps them available on request — used when one alarmed event invariably triggers downstream events, so the primary event is shown and the predictable cascade is suppressed, provided the user can still access the suppressed information and the access method is not excessively complex.

**Status indication is not an alarm.** System status should generally live on a separate display from the alarm indicators, because status is not meant to prompt action; mixing it into the alarm field increases the demand on the user.

**Reliability as a design obligation of the audio hardware.** A cluster of rules treats the audio device and its circuit as safety hardware: the design shall preclude false alarms; warning-signal failure shall not be coupled to system/equipment failure in either direction; all audio displays shall have circuit-test means; and on total system failure the system shall report *the system failure itself* rather than a flood of component failures.

**When the ear is the right channel.** The section gives an explicit list (5.5.2.1.4) of conditions favoring audio: the information is short, simple, transitory, and time-critical; the visual modality is overburdened or vision is degraded by lighting, vibration, mobility, or inattention; redundant alerting for a critical visual signal is wanted; the user needs to be cued for a follow-on response; usage has built an expectation of an audio cue; or voice communication is needed. Conversely, audio shall be used only when it genuinely aids understanding and response, and shall not cause confusion, errors, or inefficiency.

**Tones vs. complex sounds vs. speech (Exhibit 5.5.2.1.7).** The choice of signal type is a deliberate trade. Tones are good for start/stop timing and automatic communication of limited, learned information but poor for quantitative or qualitative value; complex (non-periodic) sounds are especially suited to irregular events like alarms and can carry common meaning (a fire bell) but are poor for qualitative indication; speech gives meaning intrinsic in the signal and is most effective for rapid, complex, multidimensional information but is more easily masked and raises repeatability problems.

**How many distinct signals a person can handle.** Citing Patterson and Stanton & Edworthy, the standard caps *absolute identification* at four signals (research shows 4-to-7 can be learned reasonably quickly, with performance dropping sharply beyond that, and up to nine meanings retainable only with regular exposure), and caps *relative discrimination* at twelve.

**Signal physics: frequency, intensity, duration, periodicity.** Warning signals should sit between 200 and 5,000 Hz (preferably 500–3,000 Hz); below 1,000 Hz to carry 300 m or more; below 500 Hz to pass around obstacles or through partitions; differing from the most intense background frequencies and from the system's electrical-power frequency; and mid-frequencies (1500–3000 Hz) avoided where localization is needed. Loudness must clear ambient by at least 10 dB(A) (or a 30-second max level by 5 dB(A), whichever is louder) but with a hard ceiling — 115 dB(A) on an emergency signal, and 90 dB(A) on any other signal. Signals should be intermittent (continuous tones are easily confused and the ear adapts to them), and warning signals should have two elements — an alerting signal plus an identifying/action signal — with timing rules: a 0.5-second alert then all essential information in the first 2 seconds of the action signal, or, for a single-element signal, all essential information in the first 0.5 seconds.

**Acknowledge, reset, and shutoff without losing safety.** A whole subsection governs turning signals off. Special acknowledgement of a critical alarm must not slow the response to its cause; non-critical alarms need a simple reset that does not erase the accompanying message; corrective action (by user or system) shall auto-terminate the signal; an automatic reset shall be provided that recycles so the device can sound again if the condition reappears; and volume controls may be ganged to mode switches for noisy phases — but never if that could leave an emergency signal inaudible.

**Voice signals as the high-bandwidth option.** Voice is for situations where coded meanings are too many or too forgettable, where directions are complex, or where ambient noise would mask simple tones. A voice signal should be a brief, standardized utterance that names the condition and suggests an action, ideally preceded by a non-speech alert to capture attention; critical warnings repeat with no more than a 3-second pause until corrected or overridden; word choice is prioritized intelligibility → descriptiveness → conciseness; delivery is formal and impersonal in an average, dialect-free voice; and alphabetic data uses a phonetic alphabet (alpha/bravo/charlie) rather than bare letters.

**Voice communication systems as measured, specified equipment.** The final subsection treats two-way speech as engineered infrastructure. Intelligibility is *measured* (PB word test for highest accuracy, MRT for typical systems, AI/STI as design-phase predictors) against Exhibit 5.5.4.1.2 criteria. Microphones respond across the intelligibility-critical band (200–6,100 Hz, narrowable to 250–4,000 Hz), with noise-canceling mics and noise shields specified for loud, low-frequency environments. Reception equipment, binaural headsets (mandatory at or above 85 dB(A) ambient), loudspeaker placement and filtering for channel separation, user comfort, hands-free operation, and operating controls (volume/squelch/foot controls) are all specified, down to conventional telephone band-pass, cord, cradle, coding, and switching-priority requirements.

## Mental Models

**Audio is the redundant pointer, not the message.** For nonverbal signals the standard's repeated stance is that the sound exists to redirect attention to a visual display that *defines* the condition, with the audio supplementary or supportive. The mental model: design the meaning into the visual field; let the audio be the finger that points to it. High-ambient-illumination areas get *both* an auditory and a visual alarm precisely because neither channel can be trusted alone.

**An alarm's credibility is a depletable resource.** Several rules converge on the idea that overusing or mis-tuning sound destroys its value. Auditory signals are symbols the user must learn and interpret; when designers overuse sound, signals get ignored and become annoyance, interference, or confusion. Audio shall not signal normal conditions, shall not resemble operational sounds (navigation tones, radio transmissions, electrical hum), and shall not be so startling that it blocks the right response. The model: every unnecessary or confusable alarm spends down the operator's trust in the whole alarm system.

**Salience without startle — a tuned window.** The intensity and frequency rules describe a band you must stay inside: loud enough to clear ambient (and to compensate for parkas, hearing protection, or distance), distinct enough from background frequencies and from each other, but capped at 115/90 dB(A) and explicitly not so startling as to interfere with response or divert attention from a more critical signal. Salience is a window to hit, not a dial to maximize.

**Detection probability is the real design target.** The standard frames the purpose of an alerting/warning system as giving the user a *greater probability of detecting* the triggering condition than unaided observation would. That reframes "is it loud enough?" into "does it measurably raise detection?" — the same logic that makes mid-frequencies unsuitable where localization matters and that drives dichotic (alternating-ear) presentation to keep one essential signal from masking another.

**Two-element timing mirrors human reaction.** The alerting-then-identifying structure (0.5 s alert; essential information in the first 2 s of the action signal) models how a person actually responds: first orient, then comprehend. The single-element compression to 0.5 s is the same model under time pressure. Periodicity (intermittent, modulated at 1–8 beeps/s or warbling 1–3 cycles/s) keeps the signal from fading into perceptual adaptation while it waits to be answered.

**Intelligibility is engineered and verified, not assumed.** Voice communication is modeled as a measurable transmission problem: choose the right test for the lifecycle phase (predictive AI/STI early, MRT or the demanding PB test once hardware exists), specify the band and the signal-to-noise margin (verbal signals 20 dB above speech interference level, capped at 90 dB(A); at least a 5:1 speech-to-noise ratio), and shape the channel (pre-emphasis, de-emphasis, peak clipping, noise shields, squelch) so consonant information survives. The model treats a spoken word as a fragile payload that must be protected end-to-end.

**Separate the streams in space and spectrum.** When multiple speech channels must be monitored at once, the section reaches for spatial and spectral separation rather than louder volume: loudspeakers spread across the frontal quadrant (at least 10° apart, 45° left to 45° right), low-pass filtering on one side for two channels, and a high-pass/low-pass split with a visual channel-in-use indicator for three. The mental model is that the human auditory system disentangles concurrent sources by location and timbre, so the design should give it those cues.

## Anti-patterns

The section frames most guidance positively, but it names several behaviors to avoid:

- **Using sound for normal conditions.** A normal, healthy state should never get its own auditory alarm — signalling routine conditions by sound only trains the operator to discount the channel.
- **Overusing sound.** When designers overuse auditory signals, the signals are often ignored and become a source of annoyance, interference, or confusion; sound should be used only when it actually contributes to understanding and response.
- **Signals that resemble real operational sounds.** Auditory signals should not resemble sounds that can legitimately occur in the operational setting — e.g., tones like navigation signals or radio transmissions, or hisses and hums like electrical interference — because they will be confused with the real thing.
- **Startling signals.** Signals should not be so startling that they preclude the appropriate response or pull attention away from other critical signals.
- **Reusing standard signals for new meanings.** Standard signals shall not be repurposed to convey new meanings, and a signal's meaning should stay consistent with the meaning already established for that function.
- **Acknowledgement that slows the response.** A special/critical acknowledgement (e.g., a special keystroke combination) shall not inhibit or slow the response to the condition that raised the alarm; acknowledgement and termination procedures shall not reduce reaction speed or accuracy.
- **Reporting a flood of component failures on total failure.** When the whole system goes down, the design must consolidate the messages and announce the top-level failure itself, rather than dumping a cascade of individual component faults.
- **Persistent or escalating signals that block corrective action.** Signals that persist or grow progressively louder shall not be used if manual shutoff would interfere with the corrective action required.
- **Auditory coding where it cannot be heard.** Do not lean on auditory coding in settings where background noise is loud enough to defeat reliable listening.
- **A single audio signal pointing at many displays when identification is critical.** A lone audio signal should serve multiple visual displays only when immediate identification of the right display is *not* critical to safety or performance.
- **Binaural headsets that seal off useful sound.** Binaural (both-ear) headsets should not be used below 85 dB(A) ambient if the environment contains informative sounds (voices, wear/malfunction noises, mission-status cues) that cannot also be routed into the headset.

## Key Takeaways

- An alarm is an information system, not a noise: per **NUREG-0700** it must announce the problem, convey priority and nature, guide the first response, and confirm whether the response worked.
- **MIL-STD-411** defines Caution, Warning, and Advisory as distinct classes; warning and caution signals must be used for their defined conditions and must be readily distinguishable.
- Manage alarm load by prioritization (cap at four levels), significance override, validated/noise-filtered inputs, and the filtering-vs-suppression distinction — filter only operationally insignificant alarms; suppress predictable cascades but keep them accessible.
- Nonverbal audio is a redundant pointer: it shall be accompanied by a visual signal that defines the condition and should direct attention to the right display rather than carry meaning alone; high-illumination areas get both channels.
- Hit the salience window: 200–5,000 Hz (preferably 500–3,000), at least 10 dB(A) over ambient, capped at 115 dB(A) emergency / 90 dB(A) otherwise, intermittent and modulated, distinct from background and from other signals, and never so startling it blocks the response.
- Limit distinct signals — four for absolute identification, twelve for relative discrimination — and use two-element timing (0.5 s alert; essential information in the first 2 s).
- Acknowledge/reset/shutoff must never slow the response, erase the message, or leave an emergency signal inaudible; corrective action auto-terminates the signal and auto-reset lets it re-fire if the condition returns.
- Voice signals (per **MIL-HDBK-761A** / **MIL-STD-1472G**) are the high-bandwidth option: brief, standardized, action-suggesting, intelligibility-first wording, formal delivery, phonetic alphabet, and critical warnings repeated at ≤3-second intervals.
- Voice communication is engineered and *verified* intelligibility (PB test / MRT-per-ANSI 3.2 / AI / STI against Exhibit 5.5.4.1.2), with specified mic and reception bandwidths, binaural headsets mandatory at ≥85 dB(A), spatial/spectral channel separation, and full telephone/control specifications.
