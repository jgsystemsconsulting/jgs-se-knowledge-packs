# Chapter 6 — Computer-Human Interface: Information Presentation and Coding

> Source basis: HF-STD-001B, Section 5.6 (Computer-Human Interface) — specifically 5.6.1 (Screen Design), 5.6.2 (Text Entry and Display), 5.6.3 (Graphical Information), 5.6.4 (Concealed Information), 5.6.5 (Dynamic Information Update), and 5.6.6 (Coding). All claims in this chapter are grounded in those sections. Where a requirement names its provenance, that provenance is reproduced. Requirement numbers (e.g., 5.6.1.1.1) are cited so any claim can be traced back to the standard.

## Core Idea

This is the standard's treatment of *how information should be put on a screen* and *how meaning should be attached to it*. It governs the visual presentation layer of the computer-human interface: arranging a screen, entering and displaying text, drawing graphics and maps and charts, hiding and updating information, and — the largest and most distinctive part — the disciplined use of *coding* (color, brightness, lines, symbols, shape, size, texture, spatial position, and flashing) to carry meaning beyond the literal characters on the display.

Two organizing commitments run through the whole section. The first is that presentation must be matched to the *function of the system and the task of the user* — the designer is told up front to understand a system's primary function before designing its screens, because a database-lookup system and a real-time control system have very different presentation needs (5.6.1). The second is *restraint*: information density is to be minimized, only essential information shown, visual competition reduced, and every attention-getting technique (color, flashing, reverse video, brightness) used sparingly and for functional rather than decorative purposes (5.6.1.1.2, 5.6.1.3.3, 5.6.6.1.8, 5.6.6.1.4). The section reads as a long argument that a screen is a scarce, attention-limited resource and that coding is a vocabulary that loses its meaning the moment it is over-used.

A positioning note for this collection: HF-STD-001B is human factors / HSI guidance from a *civil-aviation regulator's* engineering standard, not from the NASA tradition. It complements the `nasa-hsi` pack rather than duplicating it. This particular section is where that complementarity is most concrete and most useful: NASA's HSI material frames the human as a subsystem and manages the discipline across the lifecycle, whereas this section supplies the *operational design rules* — the actual contrast ratios, character-height angles, color reserved-meanings, and flash rates — that an HSI practitioner would reach for when specifying or evaluating an interface. Note too that almost none of these rules are FAA inventions; the section is a harmonized synthesis of prior human-engineering sources, each requirement carrying its bracketed origin.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

Section 5.6 does not introduce a new conceptual model so much as it *aggregates* a body of display-design source documents and re-states each rule in FAA-applicable language with an explicit bracketed attribution. The recurring sources, reproduced exactly as cited:

**DOD-HFDG-ATCCS / DOD-HFDG-ATCCS V2.0 (1992)** — the DoD human factors design guide for the soldier-machine interface of the Army's Tactical Command-and-Control System. This is the dominant source across the section. It backs nearly the whole screen-design subsection (simplicity, density, context, highlighting, format, consistency, initial display), the bulk of the graphical-information and map/tactical-display rules, the graph conventions, and most of the color-coding material. Its prevalence matters because ATCCS is command-and-control human factors guidance — a domain adjacent to air traffic control, which is why so many real-time-display rules trace to it.

**MIL-HDBK-761A (1989)** — *Department of Defense human engineering handbook.* The second pillar. It anchors data-entry and editing rules (justification, unfilled fields, completion actions), much of the text-display and labeling material, the table and forms rules, the dynamic-update constraints, and a large share of the coding rules (brightness levels and ratios, line/symbol/shape/size coding, flash rates).

**MIL-STD-1472G (2012)** and **MIL-STD-783D (1984)** and **MIL-STD-961 (2014)** — DoD design-criteria and standardization standards, cited for specific points: user pacing of entry (5.6.2.3.7), word/character spacing (5.6.2.5.6.2), acronym punctuation (5.6.2.5.4.11), and the rule against capitalizing whole phrases for emphasis (5.6.2.5.8.2).

**DON UISNCCS (1992)** — *Department of the Navy* user-interface guidance. Backs short-term-memory minimization, task-oriented vocabulary, text-manipulation and document operations, text-frame and capitalization rules, list display, and several map and color-preference rules.

**National Air Traffic Services (1999)** — a UK ATC source. Backs much of the character-quality detail (luminance, contrast, dot-matrix), the underlining/bold/numeric coding rules, font limits, and many of the qualitative coding cautions (texture, size, shape, multidimensional, flashing-text rates).

**NUREG-0700 (2002)** — *Nuclear Regulatory Commission* human-system interface design review guideline. Backs minimum-displayed-lines, numeric/date-format rules, list pagination, scatter plots, bar/pie/profile/segmented charts, diagrams and mimics, and several coding rules (size-versus-font, spatial coding, flash marking).

**DSTL-95-033 (1996)** — a NASA Goddard user-interface guideline. Backs print-status feedback, text-alignment rules, list display, table scanning cues, and a cluster of color rules (color pairs to avoid, testing colors, lightness, reverse video, brightness for errors). Its presence is a direct point of contact with the NASA tradition this pack complements.

**ESD-TR-86-278 (1986); Ameritech Services Inc. (1996/1998); Apple Computer Inc. (1995); Microsoft Corp. (1992); ANSI (1988); Neilson (2000).** Supporting sources for specific items: integrated displays and abbreviation familiarity (ESD-TR); table column ordering, brightness-level discussion, and topic-sentence placement (Ameritech); the Undo command (Apple); text-frame behavior (Microsoft); the character-height visual-angle figures (ANSI); and the serif/sans-serif readability guidance (Neilson). A commercial UI guideline (Apple, Microsoft) pulled into a federal standard is the same pattern seen in the pack's foundation chapter.

**FAA Order 7340.2 / FAA 7340.1 (2000) and the U.S. Government Printing Office Style Manual.** Named as the authoritative sources for *how to construct abbreviations and acronyms* (5.6.2.5.4.9, 5.6.2.5.4.12–.15) — the one place the section points to FAA-internal and federal style references rather than human-engineering literature.

**Order of precedence reminder.** As established in the foundation chapter, where this standard's text conflicts with one of these cited references, the standard's own text governs. So the bracketed sources are provenance and rationale, not competing authorities.

## Key Concepts

**Two density metrics, distinguished.** The standard separates *information density* (the amount of information per unit area, to be minimized by showing only what the user needs now, 5.6.1.1.2) from *screen density* (the ratio of filled to blank character spaces in a text display, which should not exceed roughly 60%, 5.6.1.1.3). One is a task-relevance limit; the other is a typographic crowding limit.

**Directly usable form.** Information shall be presented so the user does not have to decode or interpret raw data (5.6.1.1.4). This pairs with the rule that numeric data appear in decimal rather than binary/octal/hex (5.6.2.7.1) and with maintaining the significant digits the task actually requires (5.6.2.7.5).

**Context and its continuous indication.** A recurring concept: displayed data should carry their context (5.6.1.2.1), the application should help the user keep track of the context a task runs in (5.6.1.2.2), the current operational mode shall be shown continuously (5.6.1.2.6), and when a control's effect depends on prior actions, current context should be displayed continuously (5.6.1.2.7). Action history with per-step Undo is offered as one mechanism (5.6.1.2.9).

**Highlighting and the two-stage selection model.** When a user operates on a selected object, that object shall be highlighted (5.6.1.2.3, repeated verbatim at 5.6.6.3.6). The discussion describes two stages: a *dwell emphasis* (typically dim white) shown when the pointer rests on an object the computer believes is about to be selected, and a brighter emphasis (typically bright white) once selection is actually made.

**Spatial priority of importance.** Several rules tie importance to screen geography: critical or immediate-response information goes in the primary viewing area (5.6.1.3.13); the most important information and controls go in the upper-left of a window, the least important at the bottom (5.6.1.3.14); the obvious starting point is ordinarily upper-left (5.6.1.5.2); layout should follow the user's natural left-to-right, top-to-bottom scan, with the most frequent menu/button choice at top or left (5.6.1.3.8).

**Luminance and character-quality figures.** The standard gives concrete legibility numbers: text-background contrast ratios by condition (e.g., greater than 7:1 for bright ambient light or continuous reading, 3:1 to 5:1 for dark ambient light; Exhibit 5.6.2.2.1); dynamic data luminance about eight times the background (5.6.2.2.2); preferred character height 20–22 minutes of arc with a 16-minute floor for legibility-critical tasks (5.6.2.5.6.5–.6); spacing, stroke-width (10–12.5% of height), and a minimum 7×9 dot matrix (5.6.2.5.6.14–.15). Visual angle, not point size, is the unit, to account for viewing distance.

**Coding as a constrained vocabulary.** Section 5.6.6 treats coding as a meaning-assignment system with hard limits per dimension: brightness no more than three levels separated by 2:1 ratios (5.6.6.3.2–.3); line widths at most three and line angles at most eleven (5.6.6.4.6–.7); shape sets ideally five and not normally over fifteen (5.6.6.6.2); sizes no more than three (5.6.6.7.2); colors capped at four on a single alphanumeric screen and seven across a related set (5.6.6.2.7.3). Each cap exists because the human can only reliably discriminate so many absolute levels of any one attribute.

**Color reserved meanings.** Color-coding shall conform to conventional associations (5.6.6.2.4.3): red for no-go/error/failure/malfunction; flashing red for emergencies requiring immediate action to prevent injury or damage; yellow for marginal conditions or needed caution/rechecking; green for in-tolerance/satisfactory/proceed; white for alternatives without operability or safety implications; blue as advisory only. Once a color is assigned a use, no other color may take that meaning, and previously established user-community color meanings shall be retained (5.6.6.2.4.2, 5.6.6.2.4.4 cross-references).

**Redundancy of color.** Color-coding should be redundant with another coding form, never the sole carrier of information (5.6.6.2.1.5; reinforced for graphs at 5.6.3.3.1.3 and textures at 5.6.6.8.1). The stated reason is the unknown prevalence of color deficiency in the user population (5.6.6.2.1.2), which also drives the rule to avoid dominant wavelengths above 650 nm (protanopic insensitivity, 5.6.6.2.2.16).

**Concealed and suppressed information.** When information is temporarily suppressed, the display shall indicate that suppression (5.6.4.1.1), the user should be notified of significant changes in suppressed data (5.6.4.1.2), and a quick easy means to restore it shall exist (5.6.4.1.3). The user may be permitted to suppress data not needed for the current task (5.6.4.1.4).

**Dynamic update bounded by perception.** Update rate is constrained at both ends: when reading changing data, items must stay up long enough to be read reliably (5.6.5.1.2); alphanumeric data the user must read shall not update more than once per second (5.6.5.1.3); for reading rate-of-change or gross values, two-to-five updates per second is given (5.6.5.1.1). Users should be able to select what updates and how fast, and to stop and resume updating (5.6.5.1.4–.5).

## Mental Models

**The screen as a scarce attention budget.** The simplest way to hold the section together is to treat display real estate and user attention as a limited budget that every design decision spends. Minimal information density, the 60% screen-density ceiling, minimized visual competition, breaking large text blocks into meaningful portions, and "color only after the achromatic design is maximized" (5.6.6.2.1.8) are all the same instinct: don't spend attention you don't have to. Coding caps are the same budget applied to *meaning channels*.

**Match the presentation to the task, then to the user's scan path.** Design starts from the system's primary function (control vs. database), then arranges the screen along the user's natural scanning order and probable selection sequence, then places the most important content where the eye lands first. Importance maps to geography (primary viewing area, upper-left), and frequency maps to position (most frequent choice first).

**Coding dimensions are independent meaning channels with low capacity.** Think of color, brightness, shape, size, line, texture, and flashing as separate channels, each able to carry only a handful of discriminable values, each best used redundantly, and each degrading when overloaded. The hard numeric caps (three brightnesses, three sizes, four colors per screen, eleven line angles, fifteen shapes) are the channel capacities. Multidimensional coding combines channels but should be used in moderation (5.6.6.10.1).

**Color carries convention; do not overwrite it.** Color is modeled less as decoration and more as a small set of culturally and operationally reserved signals. The mental move is: a color already *means* something (red = stop/failure, green = go) and the user community may have its own established meanings; the designer inherits those meanings rather than assigning fresh ones, keeps one meaning per color, and changes a status box adjacent to text rather than recoloring the text itself (5.6.6.2.10.1).

**Flashing is the strongest, most expensive signal.** Flash/blink coding is reserved for urgent attention-and-response or for marking the active data-entry point (5.6.6.11.1). The model: flashing shouts, so use it on a small area, at 2–5 Hz with at least a 50% on-interval (5.6.6.11.3), give the user a way to acknowledge it, and *never* flash the very text the user must read — flash an adjacent marker (asterisk/arrow) instead, because a blinking object is by definition not continuously visible (5.6.6.11.6–.8).

**Forms are state machines the user pilots, not the system.** The forms model puts the user in control: the user sets the pace (5.6.2.3.7), cursor movement happens on explicit action rather than auto-advancing between fields (5.6.2.11.6.6), required fields gate the Save action (5.6.2.11.2.6), defaults appear but are non-destructive (the default value survives replacement, 5.6.2.11.7.4), and a form stays on screen until an explicit completion action like pressing Enter (5.6.2.3.10). Error handling lets the user correct character-by-character and field-by-field, with two sanctioned strategies for invalid or omitted entries — block at field exit, or allow continuation and flag at completion (5.6.2.11.8.1–.3).

**Graphs encode without distorting.** The graph rules form a small grammar of honest plotting: values increase from the origin, independent variable on the horizontal axis and dependent on the vertical (5.6.3.3.1.1); numeric scales begin at zero and span the full data range so comparisons stay valid and the origin is never silently dropped (5.6.3.3.1.17); axis breaks must still include the origin and be drawn as a visible break (5.6.3.3.1.5); labels are preferred over legends and keys for identifying plotted elements (5.6.3.3.1.18); pie charts show proportions but bar charts are used when accurate magnitude judgments are required (5.6.3.3.7.2).

**Maps are layered, manipulable, and stable.** The map/tactical-display model is a base map with overlays, kept maximally large and minimally obscured, color-coded redundantly, with situation information as overlays, an inset for orientation when only a portion is shown, and full pan/zoom/freeze controls — but with the constraint that zoom must not destroy the legibility of symbols and labels (5.6.3.2.2.7), and that a frozen display must announce its frozen state and warn that resumed-from-frozen data are stale (5.6.3.2.2.18, .20).

## Anti-patterns

The section is largely written as positive rules, but several discussions explicitly name patterns to avoid:

- **Complex formats and decorative embellishment.** Complex formats and embellishments that convey no useful information shall be avoided (5.6.2.1.1), and visual coding shall be functional, not decorative (5.6.6.1.4).
- **Color as the only signal.** Relying on color alone to convey information is called out repeatedly; color-coding should be redundant with another coding form (5.6.6.2.1.5), and color should not substitute for written legends (5.6.6.2.1.11).
- **Color when the target color is unknown.** Color should not be used when other displayed items might share the target's color; the discussion warns color then acts as a distracter and degrades search performance, an effect worse at high display density (5.6.6.2.1.12).
- **O and I in arbitrary codes.** The letters O and I should be avoided in non-meaningful codes because they are confused with the digits 0 and 1 (5.6.2.6.1.7).
- **Capitalizing phrases for emphasis.** Capitalization generally shall not be used to emphasize phrases or sentences; mixed case reads better, and capitals are reserved for single words used sparingly (5.6.2.5.8.2).
- **Bad color combinations.** Designers should avoid the listed combinations such as saturated red with blue, saturated red with green, yellow on white, blue on black, and other low-lightness-difference pairs (5.6.6.2.5.2, Exhibit 5.6.6.2.5.2; 5.6.6.2.5.9).
- **Flashing the content the user must read.** Data or text the user must read should never blink, because a blinking item is not continuously displayed; mark it with a flashing adjacent symbol instead (5.6.6.11.6, .8).
- **Reverse video and reverse-video overuse.** Reverse video has good attention value but can reduce legibility, so it is to be used in moderation (5.6.6.3.10 discussion).
- **Reading patterns into scatter that aren't there.** The scatter-plot discussion warns that users often perceive patterns in scattered points where none exist (5.6.3.3.5.1 discussion).
- **Misleading axes.** Omitting the origin or letting a scale fail to span the data range distorts or invites misinterpretation; numeric scales must begin at zero and cover the range (5.6.3.3.1.17 discussion).
- **Overlapping map symbols and flow lines.** Map symbols should not overlap where overlap would obscure identity (5.6.3.2.1.27), and overlapping of flow-path lines in diagrams should be avoided (5.6.3.3.12.8).

## Key Takeaways

- Section 5.6 is the standard's *presentation-and-coding* layer: screen layout, text, graphics, maps, charts, suppression, dynamic update, and the full coding taxonomy — operational rules an HSI practitioner can apply directly.
- Restraint is the through-line: minimize information density and screen density (≈60% ceiling), reduce visual competition, and use every attention-getting device (color, flashing, reverse video, brightness) sparingly and only for functional reasons.
- Presentation is task-driven: understand the system's primary function first, then arrange to the user's natural scan path, then place importance by geography (primary viewing area, upper-left) and frequency by position.
- Legibility is specified in measurable terms: contrast ratios by ambient condition (often >7:1), character height in minutes of arc (20–22 preferred, 16 minimum), stroke width, spacing, and a minimum 7×9 dot matrix — all using visual angle to account for viewing distance.
- Coding is a constrained vocabulary with hard per-channel caps — three brightness levels (2:1 apart), three sizes, four colors per screen (seven across related screens), eleven line angles, ideally five and at most fifteen shapes.
- Color carries reserved meanings (red/yellow/green/white/blue, flashing red for emergency), must be one-meaning-per-color, must honor pre-existing user-community conventions, must be redundant with another code, and must respect color-deficiency limits (avoid wavelengths above 650 nm; cap and test color sets).
- Forms keep the user in control: user-set pace, explicit cursor movement and completion, required-field gating of Save, non-destructive defaults, easy character-and-field error correction, and two sanctioned invalid-entry handling strategies.
- Graphs and charts follow an anti-distortion grammar (origin included, full-range scales, visible axis breaks, labels over legends, bars over pies for magnitude); maps are layered base-plus-overlay with pan/zoom/freeze, an orientation inset, and legibility-preserving zoom.
- Suppressed information must announce itself and be quickly restorable; dynamic updates are bounded by human perception (alphanumeric data no faster than once per second; items readable long enough to read reliably).
- Almost every rule carries a bracketed source — DOD-HFDG-ATCCS and MIL-HDBK-761A dominate, with NATS, NUREG-0700, DON UISNCCS, DSTL-95-033, ANSI, and others — making this section a harmonized synthesis rather than an FAA original.
