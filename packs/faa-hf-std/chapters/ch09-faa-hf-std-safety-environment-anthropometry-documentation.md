# Chapter 9 — System Security, Personnel Safety, Environment, Anthropometry, and User Documentation

> Source basis: HF-STD-001B, Sections 5.9 (System Security), 5.10 (Personnel Safety), 5.11 (Environment), 5.12 (Anthropometry and Biomechanics), 5.13 (User Documentation, including interactive electronic technical manuals and accommodating people with disabilities), and Sections 6.1 (Intended Use) and 6.2 (Tailoring Guidance). Every claim is grounded in that slice. Where a requirement names its provenance, that provenance is reproduced; verbatim runs from the source are paraphrased rather than copied.
>
> Positioning relative to the rest of the collection: this is FAA human factors / human-systems integration guidance from a civil aviation regulator's engineering standard. It is one instance of HSI thinking that lives *outside* the NASA tradition and is meant to **complement** the `nasa-hsi` pack, not duplicate it. Tellingly, large parts of this chapter's anthropometry, illumination, electrical-safety, and physical-hazard rules cite NASA-STD-3000A directly — so the two packs interlock at the source level, with the FAA standard re-stating NASA man-systems data in FAA acquisition language and adding its own regulatory layer (OSHA, FAA Orders, ANSI).

## Core Idea

These five chapters are where the standard stops talking about screens and controls and starts protecting the *whole person and the whole workplace*. Section 5.9 hardens the system against hostile and accidental access; 5.10 keeps the maintainer alive while working on energized, hot, toxic, radiating, or mechanically loaded equipment; 5.11 makes the workplace breathable, comfortable, quiet, and well-lit; 5.12 sizes everything to the actual human body using population statistics rather than the designer's own arm; and 5.13 ensures the human can understand the resulting system through documentation that matches their skills and tasks. Section 6 then steps back and tells the practitioner how to apply all of it without bankrupting the program — through deliberate tailoring.

The throughline is a single design philosophy stated at the security level and repeated everywhere else: the human component and the protective/environmental component of any operational system must be treated as a *subsystem* and integrated technically from the concept phase forward, not bolted on after the equipment is built. Safety, security, environment, body-fit, and comprehensibility are not afterthoughts to be verified at the end; they are requirements that flow through procurement and implementation alongside function.

A second throughline is that this material is overwhelmingly a *maintainer-protection* and *aggregation* document. Most of Section 5.10 explicitly addresses the safety of FAA maintenance personnel, and almost every rule carries a bracketed source — OSHA's 29 CFR 1910/1926 regulations, military standards (MIL-STD-1472G, MIL-HDBK-759C, MIL-HDBK-454), NASA-STD-3000A, FAA Orders, and named researchers. The standard's value is that it curates this scattered canon into one FAA-applicable rule set with explicit provenance, while reminding the reader that OSHA and FAA health-and-safety considerations take precedence over the military-derived ones.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

Names are reproduced exactly as the standard cites them.

**FAA Order 1600.54 / 1600.54B — *FAA Automated Information Systems Security Handbook* (Department of Transportation, 1989).** The governing security reference throughout 5.9. It backs accreditation and certification (5.9.1.1), supplies the definitions of accreditation, certification, identification, authentication, and authorization, requires every FAA automated system to have a software user-ID and authentication capability, and backs password management (5.9.3.2) and security-safeguard documentation (5.9.5.5). The standard flags up front that a 2000 FAA Order (1370.82) cancelled 1600.54B and that this chapter still cites the cancelled handheld heavily pending a future revision — a candid provenance caveat the reader should carry.

**ESD-TR-86-278 (1986).** The dominant human-factors source for the identification/authentication, log-on, lock-out, and data-protection rules — task simplicity, the log-on process and prompts, the three-attempt limit, alarm-on-excess-attempts, screen-saver re-authentication, continuous recognition of identity (5.9.3.1.x), password change (5.9.3.2.1), and most of the automated data-protection cluster (5.9.5.1.x, encryption, transmission protection).

**FIPS PUB 112 — *Standard for Password Usage*** and **CSC-STD-002-85 (1985)** and **DOD 5200.28-STD (1983).** FIPS PUB 112 governs password composition, length, source, storage, and ownership. CSC-STD-002-85 co-backs password change and date/time recording. DOD 5200.28-STD backs selective auditing of users by identity or security level (5.9.4.1).

**National Research Council (NRC, 1990).** Backs automatic access control and the requirement that access-control systems record date, time, location, user identity, and reason for denial in both hard-copy and machine-readable form (5.9.2.1–5.9.2.2).

**OSHA — 29 CFR 1910 and 29 CFR 1926.** The precedence-holding regulatory backbone of 5.10 and 5.11. Specific parts cited include 1910.95 (hearing conservation), 1910.96/.97 (ionizing/non-ionizing radiation), 1910.101–119 (hazardous materials), 1910.137 (rubber insulating equipment), 1910.157 (fire extinguishers), 1910.165 (alarms), 1910.212–247 and 1910.303 (machine and electrical guarding), 1910.331–335 and 1910.333(b)(2) (lockout/tagout and safety-related work practices), 1910.1200 (hazard communication), 1910.1450 (laboratory chemicals), 1926.54 (lasers), and 1926.441 (battery acid). The standard states explicitly that FAA and OSHA health-and-safety considerations take precedence over military-derived safety considerations.

**MIL-STD-1472G — *Design criteria standard, human engineering* (DoD, 2012).** Co-backs the safety-factors mandate (5.10.1.1), workspace safety, emergency-exit force/timing criteria, guarding, plugs/receptacles, and most of the environment rules — ventilation rates, air-velocity limits, effective-temperature limits, humidity, and the anthropometric percentile rules (5.12.1.2.x).

**MIL-HDBK-759C (1995) and MIL-HDBK-454 (2012).** The two electrical- and physical-safety workhorses of 5.10. MIL-HDBK-759C backs interlock/main-power-switch design, grounding techniques, discharging devices, shock-current data, physical-hazard rounding of edges and corners, and most environment and push/pull-force data. MIL-HDBK-454 backs interlock voltage thresholds (bypassable vs non-bypassable), guarding by voltage band, bleeders, shorting rods, and electrical labeling.

**MIL-STD-1800A (1990).** Backs noise levels, ventilation air-velocity, humidity verification, and PSIL/dB(A) noise-criteria definitions in 5.10.12 and 5.11.

**NASA-STD-3000A — *Man-systems integration standards* (NASA, 1989).** Cited pervasively. It backs mechanically-stored-energy protection, static-charge and electrical-conductor rules, physical-hazard protective-cover/edge/corner rules, the entire illumination section (5.11.4 — lighting levels, dark adaptation, glare, brightness ratios, fixtures), and the anthropometric application principles (use-of-data, population-extremes, the misuse-of-the-50th-percentile and misperception-of-the-typical-person pitfalls, single-joint motion, reach envelopes). This is the standard's deepest point of contact with the NASA HSI tradition.

**DOD-HDBK-743A (1991).** Source for the static and dynamic anthropometric data tables and the rule to use them for issues not addressed elsewhere; also cited as the recommended substitute data source when this document lacks needed data.

**Israelski (1977); Roebuck, Kroemer & Thomson (1975); Kroemer, Kroemer & Kroemer-Elbert (1990); Johnson (1984); Farrell & Booth (1975).** Named human-factors researchers. Farrell & Booth back the entire radiant-energy exposure-limit set (UV through microwave, 5.10.14). Israelski backs anthropometric variability, body slump, muscle-strength factors, and tremor reduction. Roebuck/Kroemer back reach-envelope data collection and touch/grip/grasp taxonomy. Johnson backs clothing-effect allowances.

**UFAS (1988) — Uniform Federal Accessibility Standards.** Sole source for the wheelchair anthropometrics (5.12.6): forward/side reach limits, reach over obstructions, and wheelchair dimensions.

**MIL-HDBK-761A (1989).** The dominant source for Section 5.13.5, interactive electronic technical manuals (IETMs) — novice/expert detail levels, graphics, audio, warnings/cautions/notes, interaction style, troubleshooting logic, parts information, and descriptive information.

**FAA-D-2494/b (1984); MIL-M-87268 (1995); MIL-STD-961 / MIL-STD-962D (2014); AFHRL-TR-73 (1973); Angiolillo & Roberts (1991); Simpson & Casey (1988); Spyridakis & Wenger (1992); The Federal Plain Language Guidelines.** The documentation-writing sources for 5.13.1–5.13.2 — matching documents to users, tabs/TOC/headings, decimal numbering, advance organizers, paragraph and sentence structure, plain language, and readability.

**Vanderheiden & Vanderheiden (1991); Scadden & Vanderheiden (1988).** Sources for accommodating people with disabilities (5.13.6) — alternate formats, type size, alternate coding, and cognitive/language accommodations.

## Key Concepts

**Security safeguards are a subsystem, integrated from concept.** The standard defines security safeguards as the protective measures and controls prescribed to meet a system's security requirements — which may include operational procedures, physical security, or hardware/software features. Crucially, from a systems-analysis viewpoint it insists the human component and the security component be technically integrated from concept through procurement and implementation, treating safeguards as a subsystem rather than a late add-on.

**Identification, authentication, authorization — three distinct acts.** Identification recognizes a previously listed user name; authentication confirms the eligibility of a station, originator, or user to access specific information and guards against fraudulent entry; authorization grants a user or group the right to access a program, process, or information. The human-factors rules make these straightforward and consistent with the protection level, complete the log-on before any operational options unlock, prompt for all entries, cap log-on attempts at three (a margin for honest error while still resisting persistent illegitimate access), alarm beyond that, and re-authenticate after a disuse period via a screen-saver mode that resumes the prior operation on success.

**Passwords must respect human memory limits.** The standard reasons that random alphanumeric strings behave like nonsense syllables — hard to memorize or retain past about five characters — so computer-generated passwords increase memory and input errors. It therefore lets users change passwords (to adopt memorable ones and to respond when compromise is suspected) and calls for training against trivial or guessable choices.

**Lockout and tagout are not substitutes for each other, and interlocks are not a substitute for either.** In the electrical-hazard section, an interlock disables an internal hazard when a cover/case is opened; a lockout mechanically disables a control in its safe position; a tagout is a tag attached at the hazard. The standard reproduces the OSHA point that interlocks shall not be the sole means of de-energizing equipment and do not replace lockout/tagout, and that lockout and tagout are used together. Interlock thresholds are banded: doors over 70–500 V get *bypassable* interlocks; access above 500 V or to RF/microwave above 300 KHz gets *non-bypassable* interlocks; an overridden interlock must auto-reset to the protected position when the cover is replaced.

**Hazard exposure limits are numeric and source-anchored.** This material is unusually quantitative. Shock effects are tabulated by current (sensation near 1–2 mA, let-go threshold around 16–20 mA, ventricular fibrillation at 51–100 mA). Electrical systems of 30 V or more are treated as potential shock hazards. Carbon monoxide exposure is capped at 50 ppm. Tissue burns begin at 45°C skin temperature, with touch-temperature limits tabulated by material (metal, glass, plastic/wood). Radiant-energy limits are specified band-by-band from ultraviolet through microwave (e.g., microwave exposure no more than 0.01 W/cm² averaged over a tenth of an hour). Noise drives a hearing-conservation program at an 8-hour TWA of 85 dBA, with permissible-exposure limits tabulated against duration.

**The design-limits approach and its percentile rules.** Anthropometry is operationalized through the *design limits approach*: apply population/sample statistics so a chosen portion of users is accommodated. The standard's headline rules — clearance dimensions (passage of the body) sized to the 95th-percentile male; limiting dimensions (reaches, control/display locations, handrails) sized to the 5th-percentile female; adjustable dimensions spanning 5th-to-95th; clothing/personal equipment sized at least 5th-to-95th; and critical life-support equipment spanning the 1st-to-99th percentiles. Using separate male and female extremes is a deliberately conservative move that yields more inclusive dimensions than the same percentiles drawn from a composite population.

**Comfort, effective temperature, and the comfort zone.** The thermal environment is governed by *effective temperature* (ET) — an empirical index combining dry-bulb, wet-bulb, air velocity, and clothing, equal to the temperature of still saturated air producing the same sensation. The *comfort zone* is the range of conditions in which thermal comfort is achievable, shifting with work rate, clothing, and acclimatization. Permanent and semi-permanent facilities maintain an ET no less than 18°C, and detailed work over extended periods does not exceed 29.5°C, treated as the upper limit for reliable human performance.

**Documentation is part of the interface.** User documentation — guides, manuals, handbooks, job performance aids, quick-reference guides, placards — is explicitly framed as part of the interface between users and the rest of the system, contributing to cognitive understanding and serving as a job aid and a supplement to (never a replacement for) training. Its nine-step development sequence runs from characterizing users and environment, through task and information analysis, draft, technical review, usability test, to release.

**Matching documents to users and skill levels.** The procuring agency owes the writer a description of expected users (aptitude, reading level, time in job, training, experience, skills/knowledge/duties), iterated until both agree it suffices. Where users span skill levels, the document either supports multiple uses without one level's material hindering another, or ships in separate versions — beginners to tutorials/entry-level documents, experts to checklist-style coverage; IETMs make this explicit with paired novice and expert detail levels, both carrying all warnings and cautions.

**Tailoring is more than inclusion/exclusion.** Section 6.2 reframes tailoring as a multi-process discipline: selecting and excluding requirements, but also elevating "shoulds" to "shalls" or reducing "shalls" to "shoulds," assessing verifiability, deriving requirements, finding redundancies, and flagging requirements likely to become inapplicable as design choices firm up. It operates down to sub-parts of a single numbered "shall" (tailoring out part "c" of an a/b/c requirement) and uses a question-mark/note convention to mark requirements needing further evaluation.

## Mental Models

**The bracketed-source ledger.** Read every requirement together with its bracket. The chapter is a *curated synthesis*, not original research, and the bracket tells you which regulator or researcher stands behind the rule — which matters for precedence (OSHA/FAA win), for currency (some FAA Orders, notably 1600.54B, are cancelled but still cited), and for cross-pack linkage (NASA-STD-3000A brackets are your bridge to `nasa-hsi`).

**"Shall" vs "should" as a binding dial.** A "shall" is binding; a "should" is a recommendation or best practice that holds in most cases but may involve trade-offs or context-specific factors. Tailoring turns this dial deliberately — down when a requirement is too expensive to meet or verify or merely desirable, up when it is mandatory, free, or worth its cost. Reading the verb is how you know what is contractually owed.

**There is no average person — and no 5th- or 95th-percentile person.** A body at the 95th percentile in stature is unlikely to be at the 95th in reach or any other dimension; in one cited study of 4000 men, fewer than 1% were "average" across five dimensions. Two corollaries follow as hard rules: never design to the 50th percentile/mean (it accommodates only half the users), and never sum like-percentile segment dimensions to build a composite (95th-percentile arm length is *not* 95th shoulder-to-elbow plus 95th elbow-to-hand; use a distribution of the composite measure, or regression equations, instead).

**Build the envelope, don't borrow the number.** Reach is task-, motion-, and function-dependent. Static reach numbers are starting points; real reach envelopes must be constructed for the actual working position and design reference point, adjusted for grasp type (fingertip touch yields the largest envelope; full grasp shrinks it), torso/shoulder movement, seat-back angle, and clothing. Strength, fine-manipulation, and repetitive tasks belong well inside the envelope's perimeter.

**Conservative reduction of borrowed strength data.** Source strength values were collected from young military men at maximum exertion, so they are too high for design. The standard reduces them (e.g., control-force criteria are 80% of the 5th-percentile male maximum) and then further reduces for female users via fixed ratios (upper extremities ~56.5%, lower ~64.2%, trunk ~66.0% of male) until better data exists. The mental model: never design to someone's maximum, and always step down borrowed data toward the weakest likely operator.

**Defense in depth for energy and access.** Whether the hazard is electrical charge, stored mechanical energy, or unauthorized data access, the pattern repeats: a primary barrier, an independent secondary barrier, a warning, and a fail-safe state. Capacitors get bleeders *and* shorting rods; high-voltage access gets guarding *and* non-bypassable interlocks; data gets encryption *and* alarms *and* audit logs. No single safeguard is trusted alone.

**Detailed-and-early tailoring beats late-and-coarse.** Because tailored requirements are a strict subset of a chapter, imposing a whole chapter imposes *more* requirements than a careful tailored list — even though a long tailored list *looks* heavier in review. Earlier detail avoids costlier later corrections, and an accurate high-level tailoring is preferable to an inaccurate detailed one, because surplus requirements are easy to delete later but missing ones are expensive and contentious to add.

## Anti-patterns (named by the source)

The source explicitly names these errors:

**Misuse of the 50th percentile or the average.** Stated as a "shall not": neither the mean nor the 50th percentile may serve as a design criterion, because — even on a Gaussian distribution — such a midpoint accommodates at best half the users (5.12.1.3.1).

**Misperception of the typically sized person.** Treating a high percentile in one dimension as a high percentile across all dimensions. A percentile value for a body part must be used exclusively for functions relating to that body part; one cannot pick a 95th-percentile-stature person as a test subject for 95th-percentile reach requirements (5.12.1.3.2).

**Summation of segment dimensions.** Summing like-percentile values across adjacent body parts to represent a composite characteristic is prohibited; the true composite percentile is less than the erroneous sum (5.12.1.3.3).

**Using single-joint data for adjacent joints.** Range-of-motion figures measured for one joint must not be carried over to a neighbouring joint, since the ranges generally do not add up — moving an adjacent joint can sharply cut a given joint's available range (5.12.3.3.2.2).

**Excessive internal cross-referencing.** Internal cross-references should be minimized (via repetition, sequential organization, or foldouts); when used, they must point to subdivision/paragraph numbers or titles, never to page numbers (5.13.2.1.4).

**Unskilled tailoring (the worst case).** The standard names the worst-case response explicitly: tailoring performed by someone unskilled in human factors, which risks an inaccurate and incomplete result; an accurate high-level tailoring is better than an inaccurate detailed one (6.2.1).

**Treating a standard as a guarantee of good design.** Section 6.1 cautions that applying the standard cannot guarantee good design, cannot replace human-factors expertise, and cannot substitute for knowledge of user and system requirements — and that even careful designs must still be verified by prototyping and testing with representative users.

## Key Takeaways

- Security, safety, environment, body-fit, and documentation are requirement streams that flow from the concept phase through procurement and implementation; the human and protective components are a subsystem to be integrated technically, not bolted on.
- The chapter is a curated, source-attributed synthesis. OSHA and FAA health-and-safety provisions take precedence over the military-derived rules, and at least one heavily cited source (FAA Order 1600.54B) is formally cancelled — read the brackets critically.
- Authentication design balances protection against human fallibility: three-attempt limits, re-authentication after disuse, resume-on-success, and password schemes that respect memory limits.
- Maintainer protection is quantitative and layered. Shock, CO, touch-temperature, radiant-energy, and noise limits are all numeric; energy hazards get redundant barriers (bleeders plus shorting rods, guarding plus interlocks) and fail-safe defaults; interlocks never substitute for lockout/tagout.
- Anthropometry runs on the design-limits approach: 95th-male clearances, 5th-female limiting dimensions, 5th-to-95th adjustability, 1st-to-99th for critical life support — with explicit prohibitions on the average, on cross-dimension percentile assumptions, and on summing segment dimensions.
- Borrowed strength and reach data are always reduced toward the weakest likely operator and rebuilt as task-specific envelopes; there is no average, 5th-, or 95th-percentile person.
- The environment chapter sizes ventilation, effective temperature, humidity, illumination, glare, brightness ratios, and noise to comfort, health, and reliable performance — most of it on a NASA-STD-3000A and MIL-STD basis.
- Documentation is part of the interface: matched to user skills and tasks, written in plain language with advance organizers and shallow numbering, and — for IETMs — offering paired novice/expert detail levels with consistent navigation and color-coded warnings/cautions/notes.
- Accessibility is a first-class concern: alternate formats, large type, non-color-only coding, textual descriptions of graphics, and cognitive/language accommodations, alongside wheelchair anthropometrics from UFAS.
- Tailoring is the discipline that makes the standard affordable: it adjusts requirement strength, assesses verifiability and objectiveness, and is best done early, detailed, and by a skilled human-factors practitioner — because a whole chapter imposes strictly more than its tailored subset.

Read the two as parallel HSI sources from different mission domains.
