# Chapter 3: Assigning Resources and Establishing Activity Durations (Best Practices 3-4)

## Core Idea
A schedule that shows *when* work happens but not *what it takes* to do the work is only half a plan. Best Practices 3 and 4 close that gap. **Best Practice 3** says the schedule should carry the resources — labor, material, equipment, facilities, travel, and the like — needed to perform each activity, whether those resources will actually be on hand when needed, and any funding or time limits that constrain them. **Best Practice 4** says each activity's duration should reflect, realistically, how long the work will take, derived from the same data and assumptions used to estimate cost. The two practices are joined at the hip: because resources drive how long work takes, you cannot defend a duration without knowing the resources behind it, and you cannot load resources sensibly without knowing how long the activity runs. A schedule that has not been examined for resource feasibility, or whose durations were set to hit a desired finish date rather than to match the effort required, is not reliable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Best Practice 3 — Assigning resources to all activities.** The schedule captures the resources each activity requires, whether those resources are expected to be on hand at the moment they are needed, and any limits that funding or time impose on them. *When/how:* applied when building the integrated master schedule (IMS), by **loading resources** — assigning labor and significant nonlabor costs to the specific activities that consume them so total and per-period program cost can be computed.
- **Best Practice 4 — Establishing the duration of all activities.** The schedule realistically reflects how long each activity takes, using the same rationale, historical data, and assumptions as the cost estimate. *When/how:* applied when each activity is defined; durations should be short enough to allow discrete progress measurement and are subject to the activity's calendar.
- **Rolling wave planning.** Near-term work is planned in detail; far-term work is held as coarse **planning packages** that are incrementally converted into detailed **work packages** as they come into focus. *When/how:* used continuously over the program's life so the schedule carries detail only where information supports it. The variant tied to program increments or blocks rather than a fixed periodicity is sometimes called **block planning**.
- **Resource leveling.** Adjusting the scheduled start of activities or the assignment of resources to match real availability, smoothing the peaks and troughs that the activity sequence creates. *When/how:* performed by whoever controls the resources, ideally integrated into routine scheduling and updating, and only on detailed schedules with detailed, historically grounded resource estimates.
- **Calendars (activity, resource, task).** Definitions of valid working times — work days per week, hours per day, and **exceptions** such as holidays and shutdowns — that govern how a duration maps onto actual elapsed time. *When/how:* assigned to activities and resources so the software computes start, finish, and float automatically and correctly.

## Key Concepts

### Resources, effort, and duration
A resource is anything required to perform work. **Labor** is human effort, measured in hours, days, or full-time equivalents (FTEs); **nonlabor** covers subcontracts, consumable materials, machines, and purchased equipment, measured in their own units (tons, pallets, cubic feet). Resources may be **time-dependent** — costing more the longer the activity runs, as with rented equipment or space — or **time-independent**, costing the same regardless of duration, as with consumed material. Both labor and nonlabor can be **fixed** (the same amount consumed regardless of how long the activity takes — 100 square feet of flooring whether the floor takes two days or three) or **variable** (consumption scales with duration — test equipment in use the whole time a test runs).

For effort-driven activities, the work in person-days roughly equals the activity's duration times the labor units assigned; equivalently, duration is the work required divided by the people available. A 16-hour task takes two days with one FTE, one day with two FTEs, four days with a half-time FTE. But the relationship is not perfectly linear — **doubling the people does not reliably halve the duration**. **Productivity and efficiency factors** (experience, type of work, steady versus discontinuous workflow) shift the real output rate, and complex activities can actually take *longer* as you add people because coordination and communication overhead grows. **Fixed-duration activities** break the link entirely: testing a satellite in a vacuum chamber takes the same number of days no matter how many engineers watch it — but the resource information still matters, because the headcount drives the *cost* even when it does not drive the *time*.

### Loading the schedule with resources
**Loading resources** is the first step in allocating expected availability to the time each activity is planned. A fully resource-loaded schedule — labor, materials, equipment, travel, facilities, and level-of-effort (LOE) activities — provides the basis for the **performance measurement baseline (PMB)**, the time-phased budget used to run **earned value management (EVM)**. Loading budgets onto both work packages and planning packages identifies the total cost to complete at the very start of the program. A schedule with *no* resources implicitly assumes resources are unlimited and always available — almost never true.

Two structural rules: **milestones are never assigned resources** because they have no duration, and **summary activities are not assigned resources** because their duration is merely the roll-up of the activities beneath them. The best source of resource estimates is the activity owners and the most experienced people who will do the work, and those owners must be able to **explain the logic** behind their numbers — an unjustified assignment gives the schedule a false air of accuracy. Resource and cost assumptions must reconcile; the **basis of estimate (BOE)** is the documented link between time and cost, kept current as assumptions shift, so that a change in duration, resource, or productivity flows through to cost.

Labor can be loaded at three levels, each a trade-off:
- **By name** ("a specific named individual") — precise, lets managers track individuals and catch overallocation of scarce experts, but demands constant updating for leave and reduces leveling flexibility.
- **By organization** ("the agency test department") — fast, but organizations mix skill sets, complicating assignment.
- **By skill set** ("a level-3 software engineer") — the middle path that avoids both too much and too little detail, obscures individual rates, and lets management reshuffle people within a skill group.

Resource data can live inside the schedule file or in external software (financial management, manufacturing resource planning), but the **best practice is to store resources in the schedule itself**. Keep them external and you must manually push availability changes back in — raising error rates, making critical resources harder to verify, and making leveling difficult.

### Crosschecking and the resource profile
Once loaded, total resources are **crosschecked** against the program budget and contractual cost ceilings, which can be done at summary level by rolling lower levels up to key milestones. Plotting cumulative resources over time produces a profile of **peaks and troughs**. Peaks must be tested for feasibility — a plan may call for 70 software engineers in a month when facilities cap the site at 40 — and peaks landing *just after* a major milestone (rather than just before) are a signal that resources need reallocating. A subtle trap: scheduling software may quietly permit **overallocation**, marking an activity as over-resourced while letting the impossible assignment stand. If only three carpenters exist and four are assigned, the realistic answer is not "four carpenters" — it is a longer duration. Resource assignments must be reviewed for realism against the software's assumptions.

### Resource leveling in depth
Leveling smooths a jagged demand profile — 30 engineers one week, none the next, 25 the next is disruptive and inefficient because mobilizing and demobilizing people is costly. At its simplest, leveling reassigns work from overloaded to underused resources or delays an activity's start until its resources free up; at its most complex, it becomes a trade-off between plan duration and the availability of many resources. It can be automatic (software) or manual (planners), and its goal is to finish on time or early using only the resources realistically expected to be available. Leveling lets management identify **critical resources** — those whose unavailability would push the finish date.

The governing principle: **level within available float, not past the completion date.** Activities chosen for delay should be the ones with the most **free float** and the fewest assigned resources, which minimizes ripple effects and reassignments. If resources are assigned realistically and none are overallocated, the leveled schedule is identical to the original critical-path-method (CPM) schedule. But if leveling *does* delay completion, that is the signal that resources are a genuine constraint and the plan itself needs rethinking. Two cautions: level only on **detailed** schedules with detailed, historically supported estimates (never on summary schedules, where availability is meaningless), and check that the date of the last leveling run is consistent with the date of the last resource-availability change, so the model reflects reality.

Leveling can change float and the critical path, so the logic used to resolve conflicts must be documented and understood. The chapter distinguishes **preferential (soft) logic** — a desired but not strictly necessary sequence reflecting management's execution plan, often subject to change control once the plan is baselined — from **engineering (hard) logic**, an order dictated by the work itself, such as curing concrete only after it is poured.

### Establishing durations
**Duration** is the elapsed working time between an activity's start and finish, expressed in business units (working days are preferred) and governed by the activity's calendar — an activity from Thursday to Tuesday on a five-day week is four working days though it spans six calendar days. Duration is *not* work (effort): two painters on a two-day, eight-hour job give a two-day duration but 32 hours of effort. Durations should be derived from the **effort required, the resources available, and their productivity** — not from the time available to hit a target date, which is "wishful thinking." If logic and durations do not support the deliverable date, the honest responses are to add resources, cut scope, or accept a later finish — not to shrink the numbers.

Sizing rules of thumb:
- **As short as practical, to a point.** Near-term detail durations should generally be no longer than the program's reporting period; if longer, the activity needs at least one measurable event inside each reporting period. Shorter durations sharpen progress measurement and are especially important for high-cost or high-risk work.
- **Break long activities** where logical breaks exist — enough detail to identify finish-to-start hand-offs; otherwise justify the long duration in the schedule basis document.
- **But not too short.** One-day-or-less activities can signal over-detailing and force needless updates. People are rarely 100 percent productive — a real productive day is roughly 60-80 percent of eight hours once meetings, calls, and email are subtracted — and a chain of one-day tasks implicitly assumes one worker who never gets sick. Never split durations just to satisfy an arbitrary guideline.
- **Use one time unit** across the schedule (days preferred) so the critical path computes cleanly.

**Estimate under "most likely" conditions** — not optimistic, success-oriented ones, and not padded. Risk margin does not belong inside durations; it belongs in separate schedule-contingency activities. **Activity owners** estimate their own durations (drawing on analogous past work), and if a scheduler produces the estimate the owners must accept the assumptions. Every duration's basis — the method (parametric from historical data, analogy to similar effort) and the supporting data — is documented, and durations for a WBS element must map to that element's cost-estimate basis, at some level if not the lowest task.

Special cases: **LOE activities** (management, oversight) derive their duration from the discrete work they support, can span whole phases, must be clearly marked, and must **never sit on the critical path**. **Long-duration activities** can deliberately reduce complexity — 30 build activities instead of 450 step activities — but then need **incremental milestones (inch-stones)**, objective product-oriented checkpoints under change control, to make progress measurable; inch-stones used to compute performance are called **quantifiable backup data (QBD)**. **Planning packages** can run several months but still need estimated durations and must be wired into network logic.

### Calendars
Calendars specify valid working times for resources and activities — work days per week, hours per day, and **exceptions** (holidays, plant shutdowns, weather days). Properly built resource and task calendars usually remove the need for the soft date constraints warned against in Best Practice 2: rather than constraining a test activity with a "start no earlier than" date because the facility is busy, model the facility as a resource on a calendar whose exceptions are the days it is otherwise occupied — then a change in availability is a one-line calendar edit. Calendars can suspend work for weather, exclude frozen-ground or rainy seasons for outdoor construction, or add planned overtime. The payoff is automatic, logic-driven rescheduling and trustworthy float and critical-path calculations.

But calendars cut both ways: **too many calendars can distort critical-path analysis** because calendars affect float, and the administrative cost of maintaining many of them can outweigh their benefit. The classic mistake is letting every activity inherit the software's **default calendar**, which rarely has national holidays or blackout periods defined — and even a holiday-aware project calendar may not match every resource's real work pattern (a 24-hour test facility alongside staff on four ten-hour days). A best practice for the U.S. holiday stretch from Thanksgiving to New Year's is to recognize that productivity drops and many people take extended leave; schedule risk analysis can model this with **probabilistic calendars** where a given day has less than a 100 percent chance of being worked.

## Mental Models
- **A schedule with no resources says resources are infinite.** Loading them forces the honest, early conversation about whether the work can actually be staffed and funded — and surfaces it while there is still time to negotiate for more.
- **Resources, effort, and duration are three faces of one quantity.** Fix any two and the third follows; the BOE is the documented hinge between cost and time that keeps them coordinated when one changes.
- **Float is the room you level within.** Move the activities with the most free float and fewest resources first; if you run out of float and have to push the finish date, resources have become a hard constraint and the plan, not the leveling, is what needs to change.
- **Durations are derived, not desired.** A duration set to meet a target date is a wish; a duration computed from effort, resources, and productivity is an estimate. The difference is whether the finish date is an output of the schedule or an input forced onto it.
- **Calendars are constraints in disguise — the honest kind.** Model availability as a calendar exception rather than a hard date constraint, and the schedule recomputes itself correctly when reality shifts.
- **Detail follows information, not the calendar's tidiness.** Roll the wave forward to a real milestone (a design review, a test), not to an arbitrary six-month line; more detail imposed too early is not more accuracy, just more rework.

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming unlimited resource availability.** A schedule built on the premise that staff are fully dedicated and never shared conveys false accuracy; in practice, programs that share scarce people (the source's border-surveillance example shared a contracting officer across programs) suffer real delays the schedule never showed.
- **Padding durations with hidden risk margin.** Margin buried inside an activity's duration cannot be monitored; risk belongs in explicit, separate contingency activities, and biased "success-oriented" estimates typically skew one direction — toward an earlier finish to please management or the customer.
- **Leveling on summary data.** Leveling a CPM schedule using summary-level or incorrect resource assumptions overrides good logic with bad data and lends a false sense of precision and confidence to senior decision makers.
- **Defaulting the calendar.** Letting all activities ride the software's default calendar — no holidays, no blackout periods, no per-resource work pattern — silently inflates available work days. The source's TWIC example put 86 percent of activities on a holiday-blind seven-day calendar, implying about 28 percent more workdays per year than truly existed, with one activity scheduled to finish on Christmas Day.
- **Overallocation the software lets slide.** Accepting an assignment of more resources than exist because the tool merely flags it rather than blocks it; the realistic fix is a longer duration, not a phantom resource.
- **A long chain of one-day activities.** Implicitly assumes a single worker at 100 percent productivity who never takes leave — unrealistic, and a sign the schedule was decomposed past the point of useful hand-offs.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Load resources onto the activities that consume them.** A fully resource-loaded schedule (labor, material, equipment, travel, facilities, LOE) is the basis for the PMB and EVM; an unloaded schedule silently assumes infinite resources.
2. **Resources, effort, and duration are interdependent** — doubling people does not reliably halve time, fixed-duration work decouples headcount from duration but not from cost, and the BOE keeps cost and time reconciled.
3. **Never resource milestones or summary activities;** store resources in the schedule itself; have activity owners justify every estimate.
4. **Crosscheck the loaded total against budget,** examine resource peaks for feasibility, and don't trust the software to prevent overallocation.
5. **Level within float, not past the finish.** Delay the high-free-float, low-resource activities; if leveling pushes the end date, resources are a real constraint and the plan needs revisiting. Level only on detailed schedules.
6. **Derive durations from effort, resources, and productivity** — not from the date you want — under most-likely (unpadded) conditions, keeping risk margin in separate contingency.
7. **Size durations to the reporting period:** short enough to measure progress, not so short they imply over-detail; one time unit (days preferred) throughout.
8. **Use inch-stones (QBD) to progress long activities** and keep LOE activities off the critical path.
9. **Build calendars deliberately** — never default them; model availability as calendar exceptions instead of hard date constraints; beware that too many calendars distort the critical path.

## Connects To
- **Best Practice 1 (Comprehensive schedule):** rolling wave planning realizes the comprehensive-IMS idea — all work captured, but at a detail level matched to available information; planning packages must still trace to WBS elements and link into the network.
- **Best Practice 2 (Sequenced activities):** properly built resource and task calendars usually remove the need for the soft date constraints Best Practice 2 cautions against; leveling distinguishes preferential (soft) from engineering (hard) logic.
- **Best Practice 4 ⇄ Best Practice 3 (this chapter):** durations are derived from the resources of Best Practice 3; the two practices are mutually dependent and share the same data and assumptions.
- **Best Practice 6 (Critical path):** calendars and resource leveling both affect float and the critical path; too many calendars can distort critical-path analysis.
- **Best Practice 7 (Total/free float):** free float — delay that does not affect any successor — is the room within which leveling operates and the criterion for choosing which activities to delay.
- **Best Practice 8 (Schedule risk analysis):** risk margin lives in separate schedule-contingency activities, not inside durations; probabilistic calendars model uncertain working days such as the holiday season.
- **Best Practice 9 (Progress monitoring):** keeping durations shorter than the reporting period is what makes objective progress measurement possible.
- **EVM and the cost estimate (appendix III):** the resource-loaded schedule underlies the PMB; resource and duration assumptions must reconcile with the cost estimate through the documented BOE.
