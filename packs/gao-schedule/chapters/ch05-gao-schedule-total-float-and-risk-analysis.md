# Chapter — Reasonable Total Float and Schedule Risk Analysis (Best Practices 7-8)

## Core Idea

These two best practices govern how a schedule expresses its own flexibility and how a program quantifies its confidence in a completion date. Best Practice 7 treats **total float** as the schedule's built-in measure of slack: the span of time a predecessor can slip before the program's forecast finish date moves. Because float is computed straight from each activity's early and late dates, it is only as trustworthy as the network logic underneath it — and unreasonably large float values are usually a symptom of missing or broken logic rather than genuine room to maneuver. Float therefore does double duty: it tells management where flexibility exists, and it acts as a diagnostic for the integrity of the schedule itself.

Best Practice 8 begins where a sound critical-path-method schedule leaves off. Activity durations are forecasts, not facts, so the program's total duration is itself uncertain. Simply adding up path durations from a deterministic schedule tends to *understate* the real critical-path length, partly because of where parallel paths merge. A **schedule risk analysis (SRA)** feeds the program's quantified risks and uncertainties into a statistical simulation — typically Monte Carlo — to produce a probability distribution of possible completion dates. From that distribution a program can pick a finish date matched to a chosen confidence level, size the **schedule contingency** (reserve of time) needed to defend it, and rank the risks that contribute most to the exposure. The unifying thesis across both practices: **a schedule's reported dates and its reported slack are only meaningful once the logic behind the float is sound and the uncertainty around the durations has been simulated rather than assumed away.**

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Best Practice 7: Ensuring Reasonable Total Float** — Requires the schedule to identify reasonable total float (also called slack) so flexibility can be judged. It establishes the general rule that critical-path activities carry the least float and that excess float flags suspect logic. It ships with its own best-practices checklist (rendered under Key Concepts).

- **Best Practice 8: Conducting a Schedule Risk Analysis** — Requires that risk data be folded into a statistical simulation to predict confidence in the completion date, to size contingency for a chosen confidence level, and to surface high-priority risks; the results are meant to inform an executable baseline. It also ships with its own checklist.

- **Total float** and **free float** — The two float types the guide says are most commonly monitored. Total float is the time an activity can be delayed or extended before the program finish date is affected; it can be positive, negative, or zero. Free float is the slice of total float available before the activity's *immediate successor* is affected.

- **Independent float** and **interfering float** — Two additional float quantities a network may compute mathematically. Independent float is the delay an activity can absorb without delaying successors when preceding activities have already started late; interfering float is what remains when an activity's free float is subtracted from its total float. (Introduced in a footnote to Best Practice 7.)

- **Merge bias** — The added risk that accumulates where parallel paths converge on a milestone. Because the merge event is timed by its latest-arriving path, and any converging path can run late, the chance of an on-time merge falls as more paths join — a multiplicative effect first flagged under Best Practice 2 and quantified here.

- **Monte Carlo simulation** — The accepted statistical method for combining the individual path distributions according to their risks and the network logic, producing the distribution of total program duration.

- **Three-point duration estimates** — One SRA input method: minimum, most-likely, and maximum remaining durations per activity, modeled (in the worked example) as triangular distributions. Captures inherent uncertainty, estimating error, and possibly bias, but cannot attribute the spread to individual named risks.

- **The risk driver approach** — The second SRA input method: each risk from the register is given a probability of occurring and a multiplicative effect on the durations of the activities it is assigned to. Estimates activity-duration risk *indirectly*, through root-cause risks, and focuses attention on mitigation.

- **Probabilistic branching** — A modeling technique for events that add activities to the schedule only with some probability (for example, a test that may fail and trigger root-cause analysis, recovery, and retest). The guide also names **conditional branching** as a more advanced "if…then…else" variant in some SRA tools, where an action is driven by a scheduled event rather than by chance.

- **Schedule contingency (reserve)** — Margin held against quantified risk and uncertainty, sized as the gap between the deterministic finish and the simulation result at a chosen confidence percentile. Explicitly distinguished from total float.

- **The cumulative distribution / S curve** and **sensitivity outputs** — The simulation's cumulative curve maps each candidate completion date to its probability; companion outputs include a duration **sensitivity index**, a **risk tornado chart**, and **risk criticality** (how often an activity lands on the critical path across iterations).

## Key Concepts

**The sign of total float.** Positive total float is genuine slack — time an activity can absorb without moving the finish date. Zero float means any delay shifts the finish by the same amount; the activity is critical. Negative float means time must be *recovered* to avoid blowing past a constrained date, and it arises when a completion constraint is set earlier than the activity's network-calculated late finish, or when work is performed out of sequence. An activity with zero or negative total float is treated as critical.

**Float is shared along a path.** Total float belongs to a sequence of activities, not to one activity in isolation. Letting an early activity consume float removes that flexibility from every successor on the path — spending the schedule's flexibility rather than conserving it against later risk. This is why float is not the same as contingency and should never be treated as one.

**Total float vs. free float (worked through the house example).** In the guide's foundation-and-underground excerpt (its Figure 35, redrawn below), two activities each carry four days of total float, but only the inspection activity also has four days of *free* float. Any slip in the excavate/install activity drags its inspection successor with it; the inspection itself can slip up to four days touching nothing downstream. The practical consequence: resource leveling is far easier on activities that hold free float, because delaying them within that free float disturbs no successor — but leveling always consumes float somewhere along the path.

**What corrupts float.** Incomplete, missing, or incorrect logic; unrealistic durations; and unstatused work all distort total float so it no longer reflects real flexibility. Date constraints and deadlines can also break the link between low/negative float and the true driving path. The reasonableness of float therefore depends on the upstream best practices: capturing all activities (BP1), sequencing them (BP2), realistic resources (BP3), and accurate status (BP9).

**Judging reasonableness — no magic number.** Float varies by status period, by an activity's place in the flow, and by remaining program duration, so management should *not* lock onto a single target (say, "two working periods max") or a fixed percentage of duration. Large float can be legitimate — landscaping or paving may slip far more than pipefitting — and the same activity carries more float in a two-year project than in a six-month one. The discipline is to sort remaining activities by total float and ask, for each high-float item, whether it could *logically* slip that far without moving the finish, and whether the responsible manager even knows that float exists. Float judged excessive but valid should be documented as a deliberate, analyzed agreement; sudden float changes often betray a broken logic link or a completed out-of-sequence activity. Every negative-float activity should be questioned, the underlying constraint justified, and an unrecoverable slip allowed to push the milestone so the negative float clears.

**Why deterministic schedules run optimistic — merge bias.** A schedule structure has many points where parallel paths merge, and the merge takes the date of its latest path. The guide's two-meter start-up-and-test example (its Figure 36, redrawn below) makes the arithmetic concrete: if each of two testing paths has a 60% chance of finishing on time, the merge milestone needs *both* — about 36%. Add a third 60% path and the joint success falls to roughly 22%. The milestone is unlikely to be on time even though each individual path probably will be. Merge bias scales with the risk on individual paths, the free float ahead of the milestone, and the number of converging paths — and it is a core reason even a well-built schedule tends to finish later than its deterministic date.

**The simulation and the S curve.** Monte Carlo varies activity durations by their distributions, threats and opportunities by their probability and effect, and the presence of probabilistic branches — recomputing a fresh critical path and set of dates each of thousands of iterations. The output frequency distribution rolls up into a cumulative S curve linking each completion date to the probability of finishing on or before it. In the three-point house example (the guide's Figure 37), the deterministic date of February 10 carried only about a 7% chance, the expected date was February 25, and a contractor wanting 70% confidence would commit to about March 2 — roughly a calendar month later than planned.

**Two ways to feed the simulation.**
- *Three-point durations* are widely used and accepted, but because the min/most-likely/max ranges come from interviews, the resulting spread cannot be traced to specific risk events — a single pessimistic estimate may silently bundle late materials, poor productivity, weather, and design churn, plus a margin for "unknown unknowns." The output is a recommended contingency with no indication of *which* risks drive it.
- *Risk drivers* attach each register risk to the activities it affects as a multiplicative factor that applies only when the risk fires in that iteration. This focuses the analysis on root-cause risks and their mitigation, and it produces a wider spread between the low and high confidence dates than the three-point method. The guide notes the two can be combined: three-point estimates for general bias and uncertainty, risk drivers for the identifiable, mitigable events.

**Modeling uncertainty vs. discrete risk.** Beyond discrete threats and opportunities (probability under 100%), the risk-driver tables also carry items whose existence is certain (100% likelihood) but whose effect is unknown — labor productivity that could help or hurt — plus a global estimating-error uncertainty applied across activities to represent natural variability.

**Prioritizing risks.** Sensitivity indexes and correlation measures (the duration sensitivity chart and the risk tornado) show *where* risk concentrates but are limited for prioritization: the sensitivity chart points at activities rather than root-cause risks, and the tornado gives correlations without attaching days or cost. **Risk criticality** — the share of iterations in which an activity lands on the critical path — is a separate, useful output, and the guide cautions that the most-often-critical activities are not necessarily the riskiest themselves; they may be critical because some *other* activity's risk pushes their path. The rigorous prioritization is the remove-one-risk loop: run the SRA with everything in, record the date at the target percentile (say 80%), remove one risk, rerun, and the date difference is that risk's contribution in calendar days saved; repeat across all risks, then re-anchor on the top risk and iterate to build a ranked list.

**Correlation.** When two durations respond to the same external force — a shared technology-maturity assumption across design/fabrication/test, or one trade's productivity across many activities — they should be modeled as positively correlated so iterations keep them long-together or short-together. Ignoring correlation lets supposedly linked activities drift independently, which is inconsistent with reality. Correlation widens the tails: high values climb higher and low values drop lower because correlated durations reinforce down the paths. It barely moves the mean but matters at the 80th percentile (in the house example, the 80th moved from March 4 to March 9 when 90% correlation was added; the 50th was essentially unchanged). The risk-driver method induces correlation automatically — a risk assigned to several activities fires on all of them at once — sidestepping the awkward, often data-free job of estimating pair-wise correlation coefficients.

**Schedule contingency, and how it differs from float.** Contingency is the time reserve between the last activity's planned finish and the committed finish milestone; depleting it as predecessors slip is how the program "spends" reserve. It is sized by SRA — the gap between the deterministic finish and the simulation result at the preferred confidence level. The 80th percentile is a common, conservative commitment; some organizations use the 55th or 65th, but because schedule distributions are right-skewed (more tendency to finish late than early), those lower percentiles are less safe and can expose the program to overruns. Contingency is held by the program manager, allocated to contractors and partners through a formal change-control process for traceability, and best represented as explicit schedule activities — not as lags, which carry no descriptive name and let the reserve get lost in network logic. Critically, **contingency is not total float**: float comes from network logic and early/late dates, while contingency comes from quantifying risk and uncertainty against a confidence level.

**Inputs, data collection, and updating.** A valid SRA needs a good network with minimal justified constraints, because any activity can become critical once durations move. Where full detail is impractical (rolling-wave planning, limited time), a *summary schedule* that rolls detail into long-duration activities can be simulated — but it must retain enough detail to show critical hand-offs and not hide merging paths. Risk data must come from a quantitative risk assessment, not arbitrary percentages, and from interviewing subject-matter experts across all organizational levels, anchored on the existing risk register. The SRA should be rerun periodically as the schedule progresses and risks retire or emerge, around key decision points, to support budget submissions, and whenever contingency burns faster than expected; each run should be fully documented (risk data, sources, validation, methodology, prioritized risk list, completion likelihood, most-often-critical activities, and contingency derivation).

**Best Practices Checklists (paraphrased).**
- *BP7 (float):* software-calculated total float is reasonable and reflects true flexibility; the program actually has the flexibility the float implies; remaining activities are sorted by total float and high-float items examined for missing logic; excessive-but-justified float is documented as an agreed assessment; float is computed to major deliveries and milestones as well as to completion; total and free float guide which activities can be re-resourced to cover slips elsewhere; management balances float use against the fact that it is shared along a path; periodic reports show float consumed in the period and remaining on the critical and near-critical paths; and constraints causing negative float are justified, with recovery plans for significant slips.
- *BP8 (SRA):* an SRA was run to find completion likelihood, needed contingency, the most-likely-to-delay risks, and the most-likely-to-delay paths; the schedule was assessed against best practices first; optimistic/most-likely/pessimistic data fields exist; correlation is accounted for; risks are prioritized by probability and magnitude; the risk register seeded the discrete risks; data and methodology are documented; the activities most often on the critical path are identified for monitoring; risk inputs are validated and bias-free; the baseline carries SRA-derived contingency; contingency is held by the PM and allocated as needed; its derivation and sufficiency are documented; the contractor runs an SRA when forming the performance measurement baseline; and SRAs are refreshed as the schedule updates.

## Mental Models

**Float as a logic detector, not just slack.** Read a large float value first as a *question about the logic*, not as an offer of flexibility. Because float is derived from early/late dates, suspiciously high (or large-negative) values almost always mean missing or convoluted logic, dangling activities, or a constraint — fix the logic and the float problem often resolves across the whole path at once.

**The shared-float pipeline.** Picture float as a fixed pool flowing down a chain of activities. Whatever an upstream activity drinks, the downstream activities can no longer use. Spending float is borrowing against your successors' ability to absorb future shocks.

**Free float as the leveling lever.** When you must absorb a delay, reach first for activities holding free float — delaying them within that free float disturbs nothing downstream. Activities with total float but no free float still won't move the finish, but they will jostle every successor's dates and resource needs along the path.

**Merging paths multiply, they don't average.** A milestone fed by several "probably on time" paths is *not* probably on time. The probabilities multiply, so on-time confidence collapses as paths converge. Condensing a schedule that hides those merge points hides the very place most of the risk lives.

**Deterministic date = optimistic floor.** The single date your scheduling software prints is closer to a best case than an expectation. Merge bias and duration uncertainty push the realistic date later; the S curve, not the deterministic finish, is what you commit to.

**Pick your date off the curve, not the plan.** Treat the cumulative distribution as a menu of promises priced by confidence. The 80th-percentile date costs more contingency than the 50th but is far harder to miss; the right-skew of schedule distributions means low percentiles are riskier than their number suggests.

**Contingency is a managed account; float is an accounting artifact.** Float is what the network arithmetic happens to leave over. Contingency is reserve you deliberately size from simulated risk, hold centrally, and spend through change control. Conflating them lets real reserve evaporate unnoticed.

**Risk-criticality ≠ riskiness.** An activity that is often on the critical path may be a bystander on a path that some *other* activity's risk keeps lengthening. Manage the path, but mitigate the root-cause risk.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure conditions explicitly:

- **Adhering to a target float value or fixed-percentage float measure.** Forcing every activity under a single cap (e.g., a maximum of two working periods) or a flat percentage of program duration ignores that legitimate float varies by activity, status period, and remaining duration.
- **Treating float as schedule contingency.** Because float is shared along a path and is a by-product of logic, using it as reserve spends flexibility that successors will need and confuses two distinct quantities.
- **Treating nonworking periods as float.** Calendar nonworking time governs resource availability, not the flexibility of an activity's dates, and must not be counted as slack.
- **Unreasonable float from bad logic.** Missing activities, missing or convoluted logic, dangling logic, and date constraints prevent valid float calculation — making activities look slippable when they are not (large negative values likewise signal missing logic or unrealistic sequencing). The FAA Collaborative Air Traffic Management case is the named example: interim milestones tied as predecessors to a finish milestone constrained to a distant date showed enormous, meaningless float (one activity at 1,280 days).
- **A "mostly critical" complex schedule.** When most of a complex schedule's remaining activities come out as critical, the plan is not realistic and needs reassessment of its logic, the practicality of its resources, and the reasonableness of its durations.
- **Skipping the SRA on a converging schedule.** The Coast Guard NSC 3 case is the named cautionary example: an SRA was deemed not required and skipped on a schedule with thousands of activities, leaving a critical milestone with 5 days of negative float and 57 converging predecessors and only a single identified risk — exactly the compound merge risk a Monte Carlo SRA would have exposed.
- **Anchoring the SRA only on the deterministic critical path.** Because durations are uncertain, any activity can become critical; focusing risk analysis solely on the current critical path misses the path most likely to actually drive the finish.
- **Over-condensing a summary schedule.** A summary rolled up too far hides long-activity effort, the activities that should carry risk, float distribution, critical hand-offs, and — most damagingly — merging paths, which are the chief source of risk.
- **Changing durations to chase an earlier date.** Trimming effort or duration estimates without justification simply because a sooner finish is wanted is likely to *increase* the risk of delay rather than reduce it.
- **Motivational bias in risk interviews.** Collecting risk data with an authority figure present, or in a setting that punishes pessimistic input, biases the data; interviewees need anonymity, nonattribution, and freedom to brainstorm worst cases.
- **Representing contingency as a lag.** Lags have no descriptive name and let the associated reserve disappear inside network logic; contingency should be explicit activities. Dispersed contingency is also discouraged because it is less visible, harder to track, can be consumed prematurely, and may push teams toward late dates — and dispersed contingency activities must never become critical or distort total float.

## Key Takeaways

- Total float is the schedule's measure of flexibility and, simultaneously, a diagnostic for logic integrity: unreasonable float (large positive or large negative) usually means missing or invalid logic, not real slack.
- Float comes in types — total, free, and (mathematically) independent and interfering; free float is what makes resource leveling safe because spending it disturbs no successor, while float in general is shared down the whole path.
- There is no universal "reasonable" float number; judge each high-float activity by whether it could logically slip that far without moving the finish, document justified exceptions, and question every negative-float activity and its constraint.
- Deterministic schedules run optimistic largely because of merge bias — converging paths multiply, not average, so a milestone fed by several likely-on-time paths is itself unlikely to be on time.
- A schedule risk analysis simulates duration uncertainty and risk (Monte Carlo) to yield an S curve, letting the program commit to a confidence-priced date, size contingency, and rank risks; right-skew makes lower percentiles riskier than they look.
- Three-point durations and risk drivers are both accepted SRA inputs; risk drivers trace contingency to mitigable root-cause risks and induce correlation automatically, while three-point ranges blur which risks drive the spread — combining the two captures both.
- Contingency is SRA-derived reserve, held by the program manager, governed by change control, and represented as explicit activities — and it is categorically distinct from total float.
- Prioritize risks with the remove-one-and-rerun method (days saved at the target percentile), not sensitivity charts alone; remember that often-critical activities are not necessarily the risky ones.
- Refresh and fully document the SRA periodically — at decision points, for budget cycles, and whenever contingency burns faster than planned or new risks materialize.
