# Chapter — Auditing a Schedule: Objectives, Scope, and an Auditor's Key Questions and Documents (Appendices I-II)

## Core Idea

The body of the guide tells you what a reliable schedule *is*; the appendices tell you how the guide was built and how to *interrogate* a real schedule against it. Appendix I lays out the methodology — how GAO derived its scheduling criteria, who validated them, and over what window the work ran — so a reader can judge the authority behind the ten best practices. Appendix II turns those same best practices into a working audit instrument: for each of the ten practices it supplies a battery of pointed questions an analyst should ask, the documents that should exist to answer them, and the concrete consequences that follow when the practice is not satisfied.

The unifying idea is that **a best practice only becomes auditable when it is restated as a question, an evidence demand, and a stated consequence of failure.** A schedule analyst does not assess a schedule by admiring it; the analyst asks whether every activity traces to a WBS element, demands the WBS dictionary that would prove it, and knows in advance that a schedule missing activities cannot support a valid critical path or a credible risk analysis. Appendix II is the GAO method made operational — the three-part structure (Key Questions / Key Documentation / Likely Effects If Criteria Are Not Fully Met) repeats identically across all ten practices, so the auditor's workflow is the same regardless of which practice is under examination.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Objectives, Scope, and Methodology (Appendix I)** — The guide's account of how its criteria were developed. GAO's stated approach was to extract best practices from leading practitioners and to convert them into standard criteria for judging how well agency programs and projects meet industry scheduling standards. Each practice was developed and validated with a standing committee of cost-estimating, scheduling, and earned-value analysis specialists. This is the provenance layer that justifies the rest of the guide.

- **The semi-annual expert committee** — A recurring working body that meets at GAO headquarters twice a year, open to anyone with relevant interest or expertise, including program managers and agency executives. Its membership is drawn from government agencies, private firms, independent consultants, trade groups, and academia internationally. Agendas reach a mailing list of roughly 600 experts, feedback arrives via meeting discussion, telephone participation, and email, and minutes are documented and archived. This is named as the validation mechanism for each best practice.

- **The public exposure process** — Modeled on the methodology used for the **GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (GAO-09-3SP)**. A public exposure draft of the Schedule Assessment Guide was released in May 2012, with feedback solicited for two years. Each comment was screened for whether it was actionable, in scope, technically correct, and feasible. GAO reports receiving nearly 300 comments before public exposure and more than 1,000 during the exposure period, from the public, private companies, trade groups, university researchers, and especially from government agencies and working groups.

- **External standards comparison (Appendix VII reference)** — GAO compared its standards against schedule standards and best practices developed by other agencies and organizations and found them broadly comparable, with limited exceptions. The detailed comparison is held in Appendix VII (outside this slice); Appendix I only signposts it.

- **GAO's Quality Assurance Framework** — The engagement was conducted from November 2010 to November 2015 in accordance with the relevant sections of this framework, which requires planning and performing the work to obtain sufficient and appropriate evidence for the stated objectives and to disclose any limitations. GAO states this provides a reasonable basis for the guidance.

- **An Auditor's Key Questions and Documents (Appendix II)** — The audit framework proper. For each of the ten best practices it presents three fixed sections: **Key Questions** (what to ask), **Key Documentation** (what evidence to demand), and **Likely Effects If Criteria Are Not Fully Met** (what goes wrong if the practice fails). ## Key Concepts

**The audit triad repeats for every practice.** The whole of Appendix II is built from one repeating unit: ask the questions, collect the named documents, and weigh the listed failure effects. Mastering the structure once equips an auditor for all ten practices.

**Best Practice 1 — Capturing All Activities.** Key questions probe whether an IMS manages the *entire* program rather than just a block, increment, or prime contractor, and whether it is held in scheduling software and linked to external detailed schedules. The auditor checks that government, contractor, and applicable subcontractor effort are all present; that every activity traces to an appropriate WBS element and that the schedule WBS maps to the cost-estimate WBS; that milestones are consistent with contract and baseline dates; that there are clear single start and finish milestones without an excess of milestones; that activities carry unique, descriptive verb-noun names; that level-of-effort (LOE) activities are marked; and that significant risk-mitigation effort appears as discrete activities. The evidence demanded includes the WBS and its dictionary, the SOW / IMP / mission requirements, SOW-to-WBS and contractor-to-program WBS crosswalks, the activity-codes and custom-fields dictionary with LOE identification, and the engineering, software, risk, and test plans used to define activities. The named failure effects: if activities are missing, *other best practices cannot be met at all* — sequencing, resourcing, critical path, and risk analysis all inherit the gap; too-high a level disguises lower-level risk while too much detail makes progress unmanageable; a schedule not anchored to single start and finish milestones can produce an erroneous critical path; unmarked LOE can interfere with the critical path; too many milestones mask the work needed to reach them.

**Best Practice 2 — Sequencing All Activities.** This is the most heavily elaborated practice in the slice, with 22 distinct failure effects. Key questions ask whether those executing the program set the logic; whether most relationships are finish-to-start; whether predecessor or successor links are missing (the start and finish milestones excepted); whether predecessors or successors are *dangling*; whether summary activities carry logic; whether any start-to-finish (S-F) links exist; how much path convergence there is and whether converging predecessors have adequate float; whether constraints other than "as soon as possible" exist and are each justified; and whether lags or leads are present and could be replaced by better logic or more activity detail. Documentation must justify any hard or soft constraint, any lag or lead, and any activity lacking an F-S/S-S predecessor or F-S/F-F successor — used in lieu of duration and logic. The failure effects form a catalog of network pathologies: incorrect or missing links miscalculate float and invalidate the critical path; summary activities with logic hinder vertical traceability; S-F links reverse expected sequence flow and overcomplicate the network; dangling activities undermine the credibility of calculated dates; hard constraints prevent activities from responding dynamically to logic and can mask progress and delays; imposing a date because a customer demanded it is *not* a valid basis for constraining an activity; mandatory start/finish constraints are the most rigid of all; lags must be justified because they may hide variable work behind a static value and are not, in fact, risk-free; and leads — negative lags — are generally invalid because they imply negative time and perfect foresight.

**Best Practice 3 — Assigning Resources to All Activities.** Questions cover which resources are assigned and at what level of detail (labor categories, organizations, individuals); whether significant material and equipment appear; whether summary activities or milestones wrongly carry resources; how estimates were developed; whether resources are sufficient and available in each period (including scarcity and over-demand, and the plan to resolve deficiencies); whether resource leveling was done; and whether schedule resource estimates align with the cost estimate. Documentation: bases of estimate aligned with cost-estimate resources, a resource-allocation planning document with profiles and tables, and resource output across all schedules. Failure effects: a schedule without resources implies unlimited resources and unlimited availability; unrealistic late-stream resource peaks signal deferred work; automatic leveling can produce inefficient output by stalling partially resourced activities; optimistic resource assumptions lend false confidence to a leveled schedule; without resource assignments management cannot reallocate from high-float to behind-schedule critical activities.

**Best Practice 4 — Establishing Durations for All Activities.** Questions ask whether durations came from the work to be done and realistic assumptions about resources, productivity, normal interferences, and reliance on others; whether detailed durations are short enough for effective progress measurement; whether long durations are due to LOE or rolling-wave planning; whether LOE durations are driven by the activities they support; whether the responsible person estimated them; whether a target or mandated date drove program duration; whether appropriate calendars are used (with special calendars for holidays, climate, shift work, resource unavailability); and whether time units are inconsistent. Documentation must record how durations were estimated, justify nonstandard calendars, and justify excessively long durations. Failure effects: too-long activities lack detail for progress measurement; too-short activities create maintenance burden; durations set under success-oriented conditions yield unrealistic delivery dates and unreliable critical paths; the default software calendar rarely has correct national holidays or project-specific blackout periods.

**Best Practice 5 — Verifying Horizontal and Vertical Traceability.** Questions: is all logic in place and technically validated; are major hand-offs and deliverables identifiable and monitored; has horizontal traceability been demonstrated by shocking the network (delaying an activity by many days and observing the effect); are key dates consistent between lower-level detailed and higher-level summary schedules with proper roll-up; do major milestones map between the schedule and management documents. Documentation: every schedule representation timestamped to a specific time, and a demonstrated integration among summary, intermediate, and detailed schedules. Failure effects: without horizontal traceability there is little confidence in dates or critical paths and greatly extended durations have no effect on key milestones; without vertical traceability, audiences may receive inconsistent schedule information and lower-level schedules drift from upper-level milestones.

**Best Practice 6 — Confirming the Critical Path Is Valid.** Questions interrogate whether the software-calculated critical (or longest) path is valid by checking for missing logic or unjustified constraints, whether the path runs continuously from the status date to the major completion milestones, whether it starts with a constraint that makes other activities irrelevant, whether it includes LOE or unusually long non-planning-package activities, and whether lags or leads drive it; and whether management actually uses the critical path to focus on slip-sensitive activities. Documentation: identification of the deliverables/milestones needing critical paths, logic-diagram printouts showing the longest paths and total-float-based critical paths to major milestones, and identification of near-critical paths. Failure effects: a valid critical path *depends on* capturing all activities (BP1), proper sequencing (BP2), horizontal traceability (BP5), reasonable float (BP7), accurate status (BP9), and — where resources are limited — resource assignment (BP3); LOE must not drive the schedule; near-critical paths matter because they can overtake the existing critical path.

**Best Practice 7 — Ensuring Reasonable Total Float.** Questions: are computed total-float values reasonable and reflective of true flexibility; are excessive float values driven by missing logic; is total float monitored with a plan to mitigate negative float; does management rely on free float to level or reassign resources. Documentation is minimal — a list of activities sorted by total float to judge whether the values correctly reflect flexibility. Failure effects: missing activities or wrong links make float inaccurate and the critical path invalid; for instance, activities not correctly linked to successors will show *more* float than they should; negative float signals insufficient time and threatens the end date; too little built-in float leaves no room to recover from delay.

**Best Practice 8 — Conducting a Schedule Risk Analysis (SRA).** Questions: was an SRA run to find the confidence level in meeting the schedule and key dates, with checks that the schedule met best practices first, that risk-analysis data fields (optimistic / most-likely / pessimistic durations) exist, that duration uncertainties were statistically correlated, that contingency was sized against a completion probability, and that the simulation identified activities frequently landing on the critical path; was a risk register used as an input both before and after the simulation, with risks prioritized by probability and magnitude; are SRA data, assumptions, and methodology documented; are probability and impact ranges reasonable and free of bias; how is contingency controlled and authorized; and is the SRA repeated periodically. Documentation: a prioritized risk register, full SRA documentation (assumptions, methodology, data, normalization, findings), a list of interview participants with their organizations and expertise, and the SRA file itself. Failure effects: without an SRA you cannot determine completion likelihood, needed contingency, or the riskiest paths and activities; because durations are uncertain, the *true* critical path is unknown without simulation; a deterministic calculation from most-likely durations tends to *underestimate* overall critical-path duration; uncorrelated durations underestimate uncertainty on the critical path.

**Best Practice 9 — Updating the Schedule Using Logic and Progress.** Questions: is progress recorded regularly with a recorded status date; is at least one in-progress activity critical; do any activities have past planned dates without actuals, or actuals in the future; is statusing assigned to someone trained in CPM scheduling; were activities started or finished out of sequence (and was logic retained or overridden); does a schedule narrative accompany each update covering milestone and hand-off status, explanations for date changes, network-logic changes and their schedule effect, a description of critical / near-critical / longest paths versus the prior period, and any changed software options; is the structure re-examined after each update for missing logic or unnecessary constraints. Documentation: the schedule narrative, a schedule showing actual and planned dates with remaining duration and status date, and program management review (PMR) briefings to confirm status is discussed consistently. Failure effects: an unupdated schedule cannot reveal whether variances affect downstream work and cannot precede a valid SRA; out-of-sequence progress degrades the schedule and may indicate wrong logic; unfinished work left in the past destroys the plan's realism and team confidence; without a documented, consistently applied change-control process, staff may continually revise the schedule to match performance, hiding true program performance; without archived progress records, historical data for future analogous estimates is lost.

**Best Practice 10 — Maintaining a Baseline Schedule.** Questions: is the baseline the basis for measuring performance; does a schedule basis document exist describing the approach, the IMS structure (scope, responsible staff, project relationships, WBS dictionary, delivery dates, key hand-offs), the software option settings, the assumptions and ground rules (with justification for calendars, lags, constraints, and long durations), the rationale for duration estimating and the duration-effort-resource relationship, a dictionary of abbreviations and custom fields, the use of resources, the prioritized SRA risks and contingency, and the derivation of critical and longest paths with justification of excessive float; are baseline changes reviewed and approved under the change-control process; is trend analysis performed on dates, float, and contingency. Documentation: the designated baseline schedule, a description of the change-control process, the current change-control log, and the schedule basis document. Failure effects: without a formal baseline, management cannot identify or mitigate unfavorable performance; thorough documentation is essential to validate and defend a baseline and to support effective independent review; uncontrolled or undocumented changes prevent accurate measurement against the original plan and produce inconsistent stakeholder versions; without trend analysis, management loses information about how the program is performing and whether past problems will recur.

## Mental Models

**The three-column audit unit.** Picture each best practice as a three-column card:

```
+--------------------+--------------------------+-------------------------------+
|   KEY QUESTIONS    |   KEY DOCUMENTATION      |   LIKELY EFFECTS IF NOT MET   |
| (what to ask)      | (evidence that proves    | (the consequence the auditor  |
|                    |  the answer)             |  can predict in advance)      |
+--------------------+--------------------------+-------------------------------+
```

Every one of the ten practices fits this same card. An auditor reads left to right: pose the question, demand the document that settles it, and — if the document is missing or the answer is "no" — reach for the pre-written list of effects to describe the resulting risk. *(This is a text rendering of the repeating Appendix II structure; the guide presents it as running prose, not a figure.)*

**Provenance as authority (Appendix I).** The methodology is best understood as a credibility chain: leading-practitioner input → validation by a standing twice-yearly expert committee → a two-year public exposure draft screened comment-by-comment → comparison against external standards → execution under GAO's Quality Assurance Framework over a five-year window. Each link is what lets an auditor cite a best practice as a *standard* rather than an opinion.

**Failure cascades down the ten practices.** Appendix II repeatedly shows that practices are not independent. Missing activities (BP1) break sequencing, resourcing, critical path, and risk analysis. A valid critical path (BP6) is explicitly a *function of* BP1, BP2, BP3, BP5, BP7, and BP9. Float (BP7) depends on correct links (BP2). A valid SRA (BP8) requires a schedule that already meets the other practices, and cannot precede an updated schedule (BP9). The mental model: BP1 and BP2 are foundational, and a defect there propagates outward to every downstream judgment.

**Constraints, lags, and leads as logic-substitutes.** A recurring theme in BP2 (and echoed in BP4, BP6, BP10): hard constraints, lags, and leads are devices that *override* network logic. The auditor treats each as guilty until justified in documentation — a large count of constrained activities is read as a substitute for real logic and a sign the schedule may not be feasible, and a customer-mandated date is never accepted as justification.

## Anti-patterns

The source explicitly names these failure conditions (drawn from the "Likely Effects" lists):

- **Constraining to a customer-mandated date.** A customer-mandated date is named as *not* a legitimate reason to constrain an activity; doing so portrays an artificial, unrealistic view of the plan.
- **Dangling activities.** Activities lacking a driving predecessor or a driven successor reduce the credibility of calculated dates and the identity of the critical path; a slip in an activity with no logical successor will not transmit its effect downstream.
- **Start-to-finish (S-F) logic.** Named as counterintuitive — it directs a successor not to finish until its predecessor starts, reversing expected sequence flow and overcomplicating the network.
- **Logic on summary activities.** Summary activities should not carry logic; doing so obstructs the logic of lower-level activities and hinders vertical traceability.
- **Using lags as risk buffers or to represent work.** Discouraged because lags persist after the intended margin is consumed, cannot be easily monitored or included in risk assessment, take no resources, and falsely imply the represented work is risk-free.
- **Leads (negative lags).** Generally invalid — they imply negative time and exact foresight about future events.
- **Success-oriented durations.** Durations estimated under optimal conditions produce unrealistic delivery dates, unreliable critical paths, and masked risk.
- **LOE driving the schedule.** If LOE activities are on the critical path, management has no indication of which activities can slip or respond to added resources.
- **Out-of-sequence progress and unfinished work left in the past.** Degrades the schedule, signals wrong logic, destroys the plan's realism, and erodes team confidence in the model.
- **Continually revising the schedule to match performance.** Without documented, consistently applied change control, staff revising the schedule to fit actuals hides the program's true performance from the program manager.

## Key Takeaways

- **Appendix I supplies the provenance, Appendix II supplies the instrument.** One justifies the criteria; the other operationalizes them into questions, evidence, and consequences.
- **GAO's criteria were validated, exposed, compared, and frameworked.** Expert-committee validation, a two-year public exposure with over 1,000 comments screened against four tests (actionable, in scope, technically correct, feasible), comparison to external standards, and execution (Nov 2010-Nov 2015) under the Quality Assurance Framework.
- **Every best practice is audited the same way.** Key Questions → Key Documentation → Likely Effects If Criteria Are Not Fully Met. Learn the triad once; apply it ten times.
- **The "Likely Effects" lists are a pre-written risk narrative.** When a practice is not met, the auditor does not have to invent the consequence — the guide states it, which makes findings defensible and consistent.
- **The practices are coupled, not independent.** Capturing and sequencing all activities are foundational; the critical path, float, and risk analysis all depend on them. A defect early in the chain invalidates downstream judgments.
- **Constraints, lags, and leads are logic-overrides to be justified or removed.** The auditor demands documented justification for each, rejects customer-mandated dates as justification, and reads a large count of constrained activities as a symptom of a poorly planned, possibly infeasible schedule.
- **Documentation is the proof of every claim.** WBS dictionaries, crosswalks, bases of estimate, the SRA file and risk register, the schedule narrative, the change-control log, and the schedule basis document are the evidence that converts a "best practice" into a verified one.
