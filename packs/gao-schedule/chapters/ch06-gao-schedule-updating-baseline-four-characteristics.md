# Chapter — Updating the Schedule, Maintaining a Baseline, and the Four Characteristics of a Reliable Schedule (Best Practices 9-10)

## Core Idea

A schedule is only useful if it keeps telling the truth about a program as the program changes. Best Practices 9 and 10 are the two practices that keep a schedule honest over time. Best Practice 9 governs how the schedule is refreshed with what actually happened — entering real start and finish dates, real progress, and corrected network logic so that the model gives a realistic forecast of the dates that remain. Best Practice 10 governs the fixed reference point the program measures itself against — a baseline schedule placed under configuration management control, accompanied by a basis document, and compared continually to the evolving current schedule so deviations surface early.

The two practices pair tightly. Updating produces the current schedule (the latest, truest picture of where the program stands); the baseline preserves what everyone originally agreed to. Performance is the gap between the two. Without disciplined updating, the current schedule drifts into fiction; without a controlled baseline, there is nothing legitimate to measure that fiction — or that truth — against. Together they make the schedule a live management instrument rather than a stale artifact.

This chapter also closes the loop on the whole guide: GAO rolls all ten best practices up into **four characteristics of a reliable schedule** — comprehensive, well-constructed, credible, and controlled — and maps each best practice to the characteristic it supports. Best Practices 9 and 10 are the heart of the "controlled" characteristic.

The unifying thesis: **a schedule earns trust only when it is continuously reconciled with reality (statusing) and continuously measured against a controlled commitment (the baseline) — and the validity of every variance you compute depends entirely on the underlying schedule being reliable in the first place.**

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Best Practice 9: Updating the Schedule Using Actual Progress and Logic** — The practice that progress updates and logic produce a realistic forecast of activity start and completion dates, that preserving the integrity of schedule logic is required to reflect true program status, and that the person responsible for updating should be trained in critical path method scheduling.

- **Best Practice 10: Maintaining a Baseline Schedule** — The practice that a baseline schedule is the basis for managing scope, time, and resources; that the baseline is the designated target schedule under a configuration management control process; that performance is measured, monitored, and reported against it; and that a corresponding basis document explains the approach and justifies the schedule's unique features.

- **Statusing** — Introduced as the act of refreshing a plan with what actually happened — real dates, corrected logic, and recorded progress — and then re-forecasting the effort that remains. The guide draws a sharp line between *statusing* (loading the schedule with actual facts and comparing them to plan) and *revising* (adjusting future work, e.g., resequencing or adding activities). Statusing looks backward at what happened; revising looks forward at what to do next.

- **Status date (data date)** — The date marking the latest update and the boundary between completed and remaining effort. Everything through the status date is actual; everything beyond it is forecast. It is an input to the schedule calculations, not a passive label — and neither the day you happen to open the file nor the last save date is a valid substitute.

- **Retained logic vs. progress override** — The two software-governed responses to out-of-sequence progress. Retained logic stops the out-of-sequence work until the predecessor finishes (preserving original sequence); progress override lets the field reality continue and supersede the planned logic. The guide names retained logic as the more conservative and generally preferred default.

- **Progress records ("turnaround" document)** — The documented trail between real activity experience and what is recorded in the schedule. The turnaround document goes out to the activity owner with current activity data, comes back filled in with actuals, and feeds the scheduler's update.

- **Schedule narrative** — A log accompanying each status update that documents salient changes and their effect on schedule time, for decision makers and auditors.

- **Schedule basis document** — A single document defining the IMS organization, the network logic, the resource-management approach, and the basis for every parameter used to calculate dates. Created and approved by the government program management office once the baseline is approved; treated as a living document.

- **As-built schedule** — The final version of the current schedule, representing the plan as executed to completion; archived as a database of actual durations, sequences, resources, and productivity for lessons learned and future estimating.

- **Fragnet (fragmentary network)** — A subordinate network of a repetitive activity sequence (e.g., a systems-test pattern) that can be inserted into larger networks; as-built schedules help create and validate fragnets.

- **Baseline Execution Index (BEI)** — A metric: the ratio of detail activities actually completed to those that should have been completed by the status date. BEI = 1 means on plan; < 1 means under-performing; > 1 means ahead. An objective but summary-level efficiency measure.

- **Strategies for Recovery and Acceleration** — A named table of techniques (crashing, fast tracking, splitting long activities, reviewing constraint/lag and duration assumptions, adding overtime, reducing scope, allocating schedule contingency), each with stated side effects. *(The source attributes this table to GAO and NDIA; per the source caveat, the embedded table is paraphrased in Key Concepts rather than reproduced.)*

- **Replanning and rebaselining** — Named approaches to altering the plan based on knowledge gained or actual performance, distinguished by the program's change-control approach.

- **The Four Characteristics of a Reliable Schedule** — GAO's top-level synthesis: a reliable schedule is *comprehensive*, *well-constructed*, *credible*, and *controlled*. The closing table maps all ten best practices onto these four characteristics. *(Per the source caveat, the mapping table is rendered as text in Key Concepts rather than reproduced as the embedded figure.)*

## Key Concepts

**Statusing vs. revising.** Statusing loads the schedule with actual data and compares it to plan; revising adjusts future work. Both belong in the update cycle, and time for revision must be built into the update process or the schedule becomes hard to stabilize. Altering the plan based on what was learned or performed is called replanning or rebaselining depending on the change-control approach.

**The status date defines past and future.** Work through the status date is complete effort; work beyond it is remaining effort. No past date should be an estimate; no future date should be an actual. Without a valid status date, the schedule cannot reliably convey what is done versus what remains — and the file name, the viewing date, and the save date are all invalid stand-ins (the source illustrates this with a case where activities had "actual" starts in months that were still in the future relative to the file-name date).

**Update cadence is tuned, not maximized.** The interval between updates depends on program duration, complexity, risk, and IMS detail. Updating too often wastes team time for little management value; updating too rarely makes it hard to respond to real events. A useful rule of thumb in the source: track progress at a finer grain than the reporting period — if reporting is monthly, update weekly; if reporting is weekly, track daily — so the causes of reported issues are visible before the reporting period closes. This connects to keeping near-term activity durations shorter than the reporting period (Best Practice 4).

**Update by duration vs. by work.** Duration updating is the most common method because it is easiest: enter the actual time elapsed, an actual start if newly begun, and a fresh estimate of remaining duration; the software computes percent complete. The trap is that duration tracks the *passage of time from the start*, not work accomplished — an activity can burn 80% of its time and finish only 10% of its work, so realistic remaining-duration forecasts must be entered alongside actual duration. Work-based updating tracks effort by resource; it costs more time and complexity but yields better historical data and forecasts.

**Why entering percent complete is discouraged.** Recording progress as a typed-in percentage is not recommended: the software back-solves remaining duration to match the entered figure. Estimating percentage of work or time complete is an inexact science, whereas schedulers and managers are accustomed to estimating remaining duration — and that is the more natural question to ask. Subjectivity worsens as activities lengthen: 25% complete is plausible on a 4-day activity but ambiguous on a 50-day one. The exception is level-of-effort (LOE) activities, which are described as percent complete based on the total duration of the work they support.

**Consistent definitions of start, finish, and progress.** Whether marked by duration or work, data must follow the governing process's definitions. Teams need documented, shared definitions of what counts as an activity's start (preparatory research, or only significant product-affecting work?), its finish (no further charges, or simply that a successor can begin?), and meaningful progress. Opening an activity without the resources to actually do the work, just to record progress, is called out as poor practice.

**Remaining work belongs after the status date.** Time before the status date is history. Unfinished work and not-yet-started activities must be rescheduled to fall after the status date; leaving unfinished work in the past destroys the schedule's realism and the team's confidence in it.

**Progress records feed every update.** Schedules are statused from progress records for the current period — the documentation trail (often the turnaround document) between real activity experience and the schedule. At minimum each record carries: actual (or forecast) start, actual finish if finished, actual duration or work performed, and an estimate of remaining duration or work. Crucially, collect the *right type* of data: if activities are updated by duration, do not collect work updates, and vice versa. Archived progress records become historical input for estimating analogous future programs.

**Adding and deleting activities.** New activities (unplanned work, real ordering, or refinement of long activities) get a unique description, proper activity codes, and review for predecessor/successor logic, resource assignments, and critical-path/float effects — and may go through change control. Activities are *not* deleted from a baselined schedule: deletion disrupts logic and complicates baseline comparison. Instead, zero out the duration, mark it complete (keeping a record of any completed portion), and add a note explaining why.

**Out-of-sequence logic.** Out-of-sequence progress (an activity starting before its FS predecessor finishes, before its SS predecessor starts, or finishing before its FF predecessor finishes) is common and expected. The two responses are *retained logic* (stop the early work, defer the remainder until the predecessor finishes — start-stop-resume) and *progress override* (let the early work continue, with predecessor and successor now potentially parallel). The source's interior-finishing example shows progress override can shave a working day but may be infeasible — it can assume away a real dependency, demand resources that do not exist (concurrent activities needing four carpenters instead of two), and pull downstream activities earlier than is realistic. Retained logic is the conservative, generally preferred default. Either way, the underlying logic relationship should be repaired to reflect what actually happened; left unrepaired, out-of-sequence activities can distort total float, make activities artificially critical, and mask real risk or rework because predecessor outputs were not actually available.

**Verifying status updates and schedule integrity.** After statusing, management reviews the inputs. Completed activities (before the status date) should show actual starts/finishes and 100% complete with actual durations or work; in-progress activities should have an actual start with pre-status work as actuals and remaining work scheduled beyond the status date; future activities should carry no actuals at all. Out-of-sequence cases should be handled deliberately. Recalculations from changed work, resources, or durations should be verified as meaningful. Resource assignments and calendars should be reassessed for the coming period and checked for overallocation. Date constraints should be revisited and removed where possible — especially soft constraints that can no longer bind. And at least one in-progress activity should be critical; if none is, date constraints or external dependencies are probably breaking the critical/longest path, signaling weak logic and a non-credible path identity.

**Version control and the schedule narrative.** Once management approves an update, the schedule gets a version number and is archived, so updates are traceable and everyone uses the same version. Every change made during statusing is documented, and salient changes are justified with their likely effect. The schedule narrative — scaled to project and contract complexity — covers at minimum: status of key milestones (including program finish), status of key giver/receiver hand-offs, explanations for key-date changes, network-logic changes (lags, date constraints, relationship logic) and their effects, a description of critical/near-critical/longest paths compared to the prior period, and any significant software-option changes (criticality threshold, progress override vs. retained logic, whether resources progress with duration). Updated critical paths should be compared to the baseline and prior period and assessed for reasonableness; near-critical paths and total-float trends should be watched, because draining float on a near-critical path warns it may soon turn critical.

**Reporting by stakeholder.** A comprehensive schedule is effectively a database of actual, planned, and forecast activity plus resource and cost information, reportable quickly at any level of detail. Reporting regularity and depth vary by stakeholder and complexity: senior decision makers want high-level summary trend charts, key-milestone schedules, summary critical-path and contingency information, and trend indicators (milestone completions, contingency burn rate, resource availability), while complex critical-path items may be reported in greater detail than simpler noncritical work.

**Baseline vs. current schedule.** The baseline is the original configuration of the program plan — the consensus of all stakeholders on sequence, resource assignments, and acceptable key-deliverable dates — consistent with the program and budget plans, and it captures original forecasts for dates, work, resources, critical paths, and total float. It is *not* the current schedule, which is continually updated from actuals (Best Practice 9). The baseline is the commitment; the current schedule is the plan to date. Comparing the two reveals variances; deviation tells management the program is off the agreed plan and must either be steered back or have its forward plan adjusted. Without a formal baseline to measure against, management cannot identify or mitigate unfavorable performance.

**The as-built schedule and fragnets.** The final current schedule — the as-built — is the plan as executed and must be carefully archived; it becomes a database of actual events, durations, resources, and productivity, comparable to the original plan for lessons learned and usable as a basis-of-estimate input for analogous projects. As-builts also help create and validate fragnets — reusable subordinate networks for repetitive efforts (e.g., a systems-test sequence inserted into multiple product schedules).

**When and how much to baseline.** Set the baseline promptly — typically within roughly 3 to 6 months of contract award, varying with contract size, type, requirements, and risk — and operate on an interim schedule until then. Baseline detail varies with contract, industry, risk, and agency guidance: baselining the entire detailed IMS yields more insight into performance and variances but costs more to approve, change, and track than baselining only intermediate summary activities toward key milestones. Baselining can proceed in step with rolling-wave planning, with the baseline updated as planning packages are detailed. The baseline is fundamentally a commitment to contractual deliverable and program-control milestones — not strict adherence to individual activity durations, resource assignments, or logic.

**The basis document.** Thorough documentation is what lets a baseline be validated and defended — and is essential to a credible independent review. The basis document, created and approved by the government program management office after baseline approval and kept as a living document, includes (among many items): a program overview and stakeholder descriptions; the execution and contracting strategy; the IMS structure (project scope, responsible staff, inter-project relationships, a WBS dictionary, delivery dates, key hand-offs, and references to relevant contract data requirements lists and data item descriptions); software-option settings; justified ground rules and assumptions (including explicit exclusions and rolling-wave detail/planning dates); justification of every lag and date constraint, and of missing predecessor/successor logic; documentation of project/activity/resource calendars with workday-exception rationale; the duration-estimating approach and the duration/effort/resource relationship; justification of near-term long-duration activities; how LOE activities are identified and statused; acronym and custom-field definitions; the resource approach with average/peak demand and justified overallocations; a description of critical paths, longest path, and total float (with criticality-threshold and large-float justifications); the critical risks from quantitative schedule risk analysis with appropriate contingency; and a detailed account of the updating and change-management processes.

**The change process.** Both baseline and current schedules sit under change control governing when and how technical and programmatic changes are applied. Without a documented, consistently applied process, staff may continually revise the schedule to match performance — hiding true performance, breaking variance reporting, fracturing stakeholder versions, and undermining data reliability; uncontrolled changes mean performance can no longer be measured against the original plan. How much of the IMS is controlled depends on size, scope, risk, and complexity: simpler projects may control only key milestones or high-level WBS activities; complex ones control lower-level activities and dependencies. Approved current schedules are versioned and archived. The source's census example shows even error corrections (e.g., wrong activity links) routed through change control to protect integrity.

**Replanning, rebaselining, and unrealistic baselines.** Baseline changes should be limited to new/reduced scope or formal replanning. When the remaining target is judged insufficient and the baseline no longer valid for realistic measurement, rebaselining restores management control by giving a meaningful basis for performance management. Working to an unrealistic baseline can worsen a bad situation — oversized variances can obscure newer, still-fixable problems — so rebaselining normally eliminates historical variances to make new ones visible, and should be rare. Frequent rebaselining suggests scope is poorly understood or management lacks the discipline to produce realistic estimates.

**Baseline analysis and variances.** Schedules deviate as programs execute (resource shifts, early/late deliveries, added work, realized risks). Negative variances, though seen as bad, give valuable insight into risk and its causes; positive variances can also signal problems (early starts can create out-of-sequence logic and disrupt future resourcing, and finishing only easy activities early while critical ones slip is not really good news). A schedule variance means work was not completed as planned — not necessarily program delay; negative variances should be checked against the critical path (a critical-path slip delays the whole program), and large variances can create new critical or near-critical paths. Watch for variances that compress remaining durations or "stack" activities toward the end until success is no longer realistic. Crucially, **variance validity depends on schedule reliability**: variances from a poorly constructed, non-comprehensive, or non-credible schedule are themselves questionable. Variance reporting thresholds (covering days of delay and available float) should be agreed by all stakeholders and formally defined; LOE activities, because they support other work, never show a variance.

**Trend analysis and BEI.** Trend analysis plots schedule-measurement data across status periods; sustained one-direction variances should be documented to judge whether the program is heading into trouble. Useful trend measures include cumulative completed vs. planned-baseline activities, cumulative forecast vs. baseline completions, available resources over time (highlighting overallocations), and total float and contingency by period. Total float over time is a key health indicator — a quick early draw-down can foreshadow later delays and overruns. Comparing actual, forecast, and baseline completions exposes unrealistic activity "peaks" (e.g., needing 30 detail activities per two weeks when the historical rate is 15). The **Baseline Execution Index** — completed detail activities over those that should have completed by the status date — gives an objective overall efficiency read (1 = on plan), but it is a summary measure: it does not explain *why* activities slipped and weights critical-path delays the same as delays with free float. BEI can be sliced by WBS level, resource group, duration, or time window; it always reaches 1 when all activities eventually complete; and contingency activities should have durations zeroed before calculating it (otherwise they artificially depress BEI, lacking actual dates).

**Recovery and acceleration techniques (paraphrased from the GAO/NDIA table).** Recovery means recovering from interruptions to the planned schedule; acceleration means shortening overall duration. Both should focus on critical — especially long-duration critical — activities, since only shortening critical paths shortens the program. Techniques and their cautioned side effects:
- *Crashing* — add resources to time-dependent activities to finish faster; raises cost and can reduce quality if rushed with less-experienced labor.
- *Fast tracking* — reduce sequential dependencies to partial ones (e.g., FS to SS) to force parallel work; can overallocate resources and introduce risk/quality loss when naturally sequential work runs in parallel.
- *Splitting long activities* into shorter ones workable in parallel; can overallocate resources.
- *Reviewing constraint and lag assumptions* — relax forcing dates where justified; may be unrealistic if the original constraint or lag was genuine.
- *Reviewing duration estimates* using progress records as actuals accrue.
- *Adding overtime / reducing vacations* — raises labor cost above standard rates and erodes morale (and eventually quality) as overtime mounts.
- *Reducing scope* — cuts duration and cost, but scope is the reason for the work and some requirements may be undeletable.
- *Allocating schedule contingency* to absorb delay per risk-mitigation plans — using contingency too early reduces on-time likelihood, especially when the delay came from an unidentified, unquantified risk.

Whatever combination is used, archive the schedule before the change, then document the techniques and recalculate and reassess the critical path.

**The four characteristics of a reliable schedule.** GAO's research distills a high-quality reliable schedule into four characteristics:
- *Comprehensive* — includes all government and contractor activities needed to meet program objectives as defined in the WBS, with the labor/materials/travel/facilities/equipment the work needs and when those resources are available, realistic durations, and discrete progress measurement.
- *Well-constructed* — activities logically sequenced with the simplest practical logic, unusual logic used sparingly and justified in documentation, a critical path that truly models the activities driving the earliest completion date, and total float that accurately reflects flexibility.
- *Credible* — horizontally traceable (reflecting the order of events needed to reach aggregated products/outcomes) and vertically traceable (activities across schedule levels map to one another and key dates briefed to management stay in sync), with risk data predicting confidence in the completion date and a robust schedule risk analysis identifying necessary contingency and high-priority risks.
- *Controlled* — trained schedulers update it regularly using actual progress and logic from activity owners to forecast dates realistically, updates carry a schedule narrative, the current schedule is compared to a designated baseline to measure and report progress, the baseline carries a basis document, and both baseline and current schedules sit under configuration management control.

**Mapping the ten best practices to the four characteristics (text rendering of the source table).** GAO maps the ten practices onto the four characteristics as follows:
- *Comprehensive* — BP1 Capturing all activities; BP3 Assigning resources to all activities; BP4 Establishing the durations of all activities.
- *Well-constructed* — BP2 Sequencing all activities; BP6 Confirming the critical path is valid; BP7 Ensuring reasonable total float.
- *Credible* — BP5 Verifying horizontal and vertical traceability; BP8 Conducting a schedule risk analysis.
- *Controlled* — BP9 Updating the schedule using actual progress and logic; BP10 Maintaining a baseline schedule.

## Mental Models

**Status date as a one-way membrane.** Picture the status date as a vertical line dividing the timeline. Everything to its left is settled fact; everything to its right is forecast. Nothing actual leaks right of the line, and nothing estimated leaks left of it. Most statusing errors — future "actuals," past unfinished work, file-name-as-status-date — are just leaks across this membrane.

**Statusing is bookkeeping; revising is steering.** First reconcile the books with what happened (statusing), then decide what to do about it (revising). Conflating the two — adjusting the future while pretending to record the past — is how schedules quietly lose their integrity.

**Baseline as the contract, current schedule as the news.** The baseline is the signed agreement everyone consented to; the current schedule is today's headline. Variance is the editorial difference. You don't rewrite the contract every time the news is bad — you rewrite it (rebaseline) only when it has genuinely stopped being a usable yardstick, and rarely.

**Duration burned is not work done.** Time elapsed since start says nothing certain about accomplishment. The "80% of the time, 10% of the work" trap is why remaining-duration forecasts — not back-solved percent-complete — carry the real information.

**Retained logic is conservative; progress override is optimistic.** Retained logic assumes the original dependency still matters and waits; progress override assumes the field knows better and proceeds. Override can look like faster progress on the Gantt chart while quietly assuming away dependencies and resources that don't exist. Prefer the conservative model, and repair the underlying logic regardless.

**Variance is only as real as the schedule.** A variance is a measurement, and a measurement inherits the quality of the instrument. Run the comparison against a flimsy schedule and you get flimsy variances. Reliability is upstream of every metric — BEI, float trends, critical-path comparison — you might compute.

**Total float as a fuel gauge.** Watch total float over time the way you watch a fuel gauge: a fast early draw-down on critical or near-critical paths warns of trouble well before the program visibly slips. Near-critical paths losing float are activities about to "go critical."

**The four characteristics as an audit lens.** Comprehensive (is everything in it?), well-constructed (is the logic and critical path sound?), credible (does it trace and is it risk-informed?), controlled (is it kept current and measured against a controlled baseline?). The ten best practices are just the detailed checklist behind these four questions — and BP9/BP10 are the entirety of "controlled."

## Anti-patterns

The source frames these as named or explicitly cautioned failure conditions:

- **Invalid status date.** A schedule with no status date — or one that uses the file name, the viewing date, or the save date as a stand-in — cannot reliably convey what is done versus remaining; the source documents a schedule whose activities had "actual" starts in months still in the future relative to the file-name date.
- **Future actuals / past unfinished work.** Activities beyond the status date carrying actual starts/finishes, or activities before it left without actuals (or marked complete in the future), break the past/future demarcation; the source documents activities marked 100% complete two months into the future and remaining activities with no actual dates that should have started and finished.
- **Recording progress by typed-in percent complete.** Not recommended, because the software back-solves remaining duration to match the entered figure, and percent estimates are inexact and increasingly ambiguous on longer activities; estimate remaining duration instead (LOE activities excepted).
- **Inexperienced statusing.** Easy-looking scheduling software in untrained hands can hide or ignore fundamental network-logic errors and erroneous statusing assumptions; updating responsibility belongs to someone trained and experienced in CPM scheduling.
- **Deleting activities from a baselined schedule.** Disrupts schedule logic and complicates baseline comparison; zero out the duration, mark complete, keep a record of any completed portion, and add a note instead.
- **Opening activities just to record progress.** Opening an activity without the resources to actually do the work, merely to show progress, is called out as poor practice.
- **Leaving out-of-sequence logic unrepaired.** Distorts total float, makes activities artificially critical, can leave a predecessor with no real successor (free to slip to project end without consequence), and masks risk/rework when predecessor outputs were never actually available.
- **No in-progress critical activity.** If no in-progress activity is critical, date constraints or external dependencies are likely breaking the critical/longest path — a sign of weak or incomplete logic and a non-credible path.
- **Undocumented / inconsistent change control.** Continually revising the schedule to match performance, or applying inconsistent documentation requirements across contractors, hides true performance, breaks variance reporting, fractures stakeholder versions, and (the source shows) lets weaknesses in subordinate schedules degrade an integrated master schedule derived from them.
- **Working to an unrealistic baseline / frequent rebaselining.** An invalid baseline can let oversized variances obscure newer fixable problems; rebaselining should be rare, and frequent rebaselining signals poorly understood scope or undisciplined estimating.
- **Using schedule contingency too early.** Spending contingency too soon reduces the likelihood of on-time completion, especially when the delay stems from a risk that was never identified or quantified.
- **Computing variances on an unreliable schedule.** Where a schedule falls short on being comprehensive, well-constructed, or credible, every variance it computes is itself suspect.

## Key Takeaways

- Statusing (loading actual facts and comparing to plan) is distinct from revising (adjusting future work); both belong in a deliberate update cycle, and time for revision must be built in.
- The status date is the load-bearing element of updating: it is a calculation input that separates settled actuals from forecast, and the file name, viewing date, and save date are never valid substitutes.
- Tune update cadence to program characteristics, and track progress at a finer grain than the reporting period so issue causes are visible before the period closes.
- Update by remaining duration (or by work), not by typed-in percent complete; duration elapsed is not work accomplished, so realistic remaining-duration forecasts carry the real signal.
- Out-of-sequence progress is normal; prefer the conservative retained-logic model over the optimistic progress override, and repair the underlying logic either way.
- Never delete activities from a baselined schedule; zero out, mark complete, and note instead. Version and archive every approved update, and accompany it with a schedule narrative.
- The baseline is the controlled commitment; the current schedule is the live picture; performance is the variance between them — and that variance is only as valid as the schedule is reliable.
- A basis document (created by the government program office, kept living) makes the baseline defensible and is essential to credible independent review; both baseline and current schedules sit under configuration management.
- Limit baseline changes to scope changes or formal replanning; rebaselining should be rare, and frequent rebaselining signals weak scope understanding or undisciplined estimating.
- Trend analysis, total-float-over-time, and the Baseline Execution Index turn period-by-period data into early warning — but BEI is a summary measure that weights critical-path slips the same as free-float slips.
- Recovery and acceleration techniques (crashing, fast tracking, splitting, relaxing constraints/lags, overtime, scope reduction, contingency) all carry side effects and should target long-duration critical activities; archive before changing and reassess the critical path after.
- All ten best practices roll up into four characteristics — comprehensive, well-constructed, credible, controlled — with BP9 and BP10 constituting the "controlled" characteristic.
