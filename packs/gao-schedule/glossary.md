# GAO Schedule Assessment Guide Glossary

Key terms from the GAO Schedule Assessment Guide (GAO-16-89G, 2015), alphabetical, with chapter references.

**As-built schedule** — the final version of the current schedule, representing the plan as actually executed to completion; archived as a record of real durations, sequences, resources, and productivity for lessons learned and future estimating. (Ch 6)

**Backward pass** — the deterministic calculation, run from finish to start (LS = LF − duration + 1), that yields each activity's late start and late finish; combined with the forward pass it produces total float and the critical path. (Ch 8)

**Baseline schedule** — the target schedule placed under configuration management control, against which performance is measured and reported; the fixed commitment the current schedule is compared to. (Ch 6, Best Practice 10)

**Baseline Execution Index (BEI)** — the ratio of detail activities actually completed to those that should have been completed by the status date; 1 = on plan, <1 = behind, >1 = ahead — an objective but summary-level efficiency measure. (Ch 6)

**Comprehensive (schedule characteristic)** — the schedule captures all activities, resources, and durations; rolls up Best Practices 1, 3, and 4. (Ch 6)

**Controlled (schedule characteristic)** — the schedule is statused with actuals and measured against a configuration-managed baseline; rolls up Best Practices 9 and 10. (Ch 6)

**Credible (schedule characteristic)** — the schedule is horizontally and vertically traceable and informed by a schedule risk analysis; rolls up Best Practices 5 and 8. (Ch 6)

**Critical path** — the longest continuous chain of dependent activities that sets the program's earliest possible finish date; a slip on it slips the program. (Ch 4, Best Practice 6)

**Critical Path Method (CPM)** — the network technique that computes early/late dates, total float, and the critical path from logic plus durations via the forward and backward pass. (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Date constraint** — an imposed start/finish restriction (e.g., Start-No-Earlier-Than, Finish-No-Later-Than, Must-Finish-On) that overrides network logic; standardized DOD/UN-CEFACT names, but what matters is each one's effect on the forward/backward pass. (Ch 2, Ch 8)

**Driving path (longest path)** — the chain of activities that actually drives the finish milestone, traced back from it; preferred over the software-flagged critical path when constraints, multiple calendars, out-of-sequence progress, or leveled resources distort total float. (Ch 4)

**Earned value management (EVM)** — a performance-measurement discipline in which the schedule supplies the time-phasing for the performance measurement baseline; adds scheduling considerations on top of the universal best practices. (Ch 8)

**Float (slack)** — see *Total float*; also *free float* (slip available without disturbing any successor) and the mathematical *independent* and *interfering* float. (Ch 5)

**Forward pass** — the deterministic calculation, run from start to finish (EF = ES + duration − 1), that yields each activity's early start and early finish. (Ch 8)

**Fragnet (fragmentary network)** — a reusable subordinate network of a repetitive activity sequence (e.g., a systems-test pattern) that can be inserted into larger networks; validated from as-built data. (Ch 6)

**Horizontal traceability** — logical finish-to-start hand-offs across sequenced activities, including named giver/receiver milestones across linked schedules, so the products of one activity feed the next. (Ch 4, Best Practice 5)

**Integrated Master Schedule (IMS)** — the single program schedule covering the entire required scope — government, contractor, subcontractor, vendor, and external effort — from program start to finish; the focal point of program management. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Lag / lead** — an imposed delay (lag) or overlap (lead) on a logic link; a logic-override to be minimized and justified, never used to force a date. (Ch 2)

**Level of effort (LOE)** — support-type work measured by time rather than discrete output (e.g., program management); kept off the critical path and never used to drive milestones. (Ch 2, Ch 4)

**Merge bias** — the statistical tendency of a milestone fed by several converging paths to finish later than any single path's expected date, because converging paths multiply rather than average — the main reason deterministic schedules run optimistic. (Ch 5)

**Monte Carlo simulation** — the statistical method underlying a schedule risk analysis: repeated sampling of uncertain durations and risks to produce a probability distribution (S curve) of completion dates. (Ch 5)

**Near-critical activity** — an activity whose total float sits in a narrow band just above the critical-path float, so a small slip can pull it onto the critical path. (Ch 1, Ch 4)

**Progress override** — the software response to out-of-sequence progress that lets field reality continue past planned logic; the optimistic option, less preferred than retained logic. (Ch 6)

**Rebaselining** — replacing the baseline with a new target after major scope change or formal replanning; should be rare, as frequent rebaselining signals weak scope understanding or undisciplined estimating. (Ch 6)

**Resource leveling** — delaying activities (within float) to resolve over-allocation of constrained resources; if it pushes the finish date, the resource is a genuine constraint. (Ch 3)

**Resource loading** — assigning the labor, material, equipment, facilities, and travel each activity consumes; the basis for the performance measurement baseline and EVM. (Ch 3, Best Practice 3)

**Retained logic** — the software response to out-of-sequence progress that holds out-of-sequence work until its predecessor finishes; the conservative, generally preferred default. (Ch 6)

**Rolling wave planning** — progressive elaboration in which near-term work is detailed and far-term work stays summary, refined as the program proceeds; the mode that makes the reliability process cyclic. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Schedule basis document** — the living document defining the IMS organization, network logic, resource approach, and the basis for every parameter used to calculate dates; created and approved by the government program office once the baseline is set. (Ch 6)

**Schedule contingency (reserve)** — SRA-derived reserve time held by the program manager under change control and represented as explicit activities; categorically distinct from total float. (Ch 5)

**Schedule narrative** — a log accompanying each status update that documents salient changes and their effect on schedule time, for decision makers and auditors. (Ch 6)

**Schedule Risk Analysis (SRA)** — a statistical (Monte Carlo) simulation of duration uncertainty and risk that yields a confidence-priced completion date, sizes contingency, and ranks the risks driving the exposure. (Ch 5, Best Practice 8)

**Status date (data date)** — the date marking the boundary between settled actuals and forecast; an input to the schedule calculation, not the file-open or last-save date. (Ch 6)

**Statusing** — loading the schedule with actual dates, logic, and progress and comparing to plan (looks backward); distinct from *revising*, which adjusts future work (looks forward). (Ch 6, Best Practice 9)

**Ten Best Practices** — GAO's central framework: the ten practices, in no fixed order, that confirm a schedule is reliable (see SKILL.md and cheatsheet for the full list). (Ch 1)

**Total float** — the time an activity can slip before the program's forecast finish date moves; computed from early/late dates, so only as trustworthy as the network logic, and a diagnostic for that logic's integrity. (Ch 5, Best Practice 7)

**Vertical traceability** — consistency of dates, status, and scope across summary, intermediate, and detailed schedule levels, all derived from one IMS and rolled up into the *program* schedule. (Ch 4, Best Practice 5)

**Well-constructed (schedule characteristic)** — the schedule is sequenced with sound logic, a valid critical path, and reasonable total float; rolls up Best Practices 2, 6, and 7. (Ch 6)

**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** — the product-oriented decomposition of program scope; the cornerstone for capturing all activities — every activity traces to a WBS element and every element carries at least one activity. (Ch 2)
