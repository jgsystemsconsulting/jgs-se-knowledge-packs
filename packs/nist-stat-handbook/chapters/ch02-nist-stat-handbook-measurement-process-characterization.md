# Chapter — Measurement Process Characterization

## Core Idea

The NIST/SEMATECH e-Handbook of Statistical Methods treats a measurement process the way it would treat a well-run production line: measurements are the output, and the central question is how *good* that output is. "Goodness," in this chapter, is not an adjective applied loosely — it is decomposed into the specific kinds of error that contaminate a reported value, and each kind is given a statistical handle. The four pillars are bias, short-term variability (instrument precision), long-term (day-to-day) variability, and uncertainty. Characterizing a process means understanding where these errors come from, quantifying them, and then codifying their effect on any single reported value as a statement of uncertainty.

Crucially, the chapter is scoped to *ongoing* processes — calibration where similar items are measured regularly, certification of materials on a schedule, production where the metrology (tool) error itself may matter, and special studies that run long enough to collect data over time. One-time tests, special tests, and destructive tests are explicitly called out as hard to characterize, because the method depends on repeated observation of a stable surrogate. That surrogate is the *check standard*: a database of measurements on something stable that stands in for the unseen test items, letting the laboratory sample the behavior of a process whose actual workload changes with every sequence. The continuation of goodness over time is not assumed — it is *guaranteed* only by an ongoing statistical control program that watches both short-term precision and long-term variability (which in turn controls bias and day-to-day drift). The whole chapter is the machinery for setting up that program and turning its data into defensible uncertainty.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Check standard methodology** — introduced in section 2.1.2 as the foundational data-collection device. A check standard is defined as a *database* of measurements on a stable artifact (or on a process characteristic such as a bias term) structured to span a long interval and capture all sources of variation. It is the mechanism that makes statistical control, bias monitoring, and uncertainty estimation possible.
- **The three-level standard deviation model (Level 1 / Level 2 / Level 3)** — the Handbook's own naming convention for time-dependent variability: Level-1 for short-term (within-time, repeatability) variability, Level-2 for day-to-day (reproducibility) variability, and Level-3 (introduced in the gauge-study material) for very long-term (run-to-run, stability) variability. The Handbook deliberately adopts these neutral "Level" labels to avoid the contested term *reproducibility*.
- **Shewhart control chart** — introduced in 2.2.2.1 for monitoring bias and long-term variability via individual observations, with a center line at the accepted value and symmetric limits set at the accepted value plus/minus *k* times the process standard deviation.
- **EWMA (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average) control chart** — introduced in 2.2.2.1.1 as the small-shift-sensitive complement to Shewhart; the decision statistic is a recursively weighted average that discounts older data. Cited weighting-factor guidance attributes 0.2–0.3 to *Hunter*, with 0.15 also noted as popular.
- **F-distribution control chart for standard deviations** — introduced in 2.2.3.1 to monitor short-term precision; the upper control limit is the pooled level-1 standard deviation times the square root of an F critical value. The derivation is credited to *Cameron and Hailes (1974)*.
- **Calibration designs (restrained least-squares intercomparison schemes)** — introduced across 2.3.3; redundant schemes for intercomparing reference standards and test items, solved by a restrained least-squares technique credited to *Zelen*. The chapter supplies a catalog (2.3.4) of designs for mass weights, gage blocks (Doiron drift-elimination designs), electrical standards, roundness, angle blocks, thermometers, and humidity cylinders.
- **Left-right balanced designs** and **drift-elimination designs** — introduced in 2.3.3.1 as specialized calibration designs that cancel a constant instrument bias (left-right effect, observed in standard-cell work, credited to *Eicke & Cameron*) and linear instrument drift (credited to *Cameron/Hailes*).
- **Gauge R&R studies with nested designs (simple / 2-level / 3-level)** — introduced in 2.4 to characterize repeatability, reproducibility, and stability of gauges in a production setting.
- **The ISO/GUM uncertainty framework** — adopted in 2.5 as the governing structure. It rests on two ISO documents: the GUM (its *Guide* to expressing measurement uncertainty) and the VIM (the international metrology *Vocabulary* of basic and general terms). The Handbook follows GUM's Type A / Type B classification and root-sum-squares combination, adding its own statistical layer for estimating the Type A components.
- **Welch-Satterthwaite formula** — introduced in 2.5.7.1 (credited via *Brownlee*) to approximate the effective degrees of freedom of a combined standard uncertainty.
- **Treatment of uncorrected bias** — introduced in 2.5.8, a method credited to *Phillips and Eberhardt* (motivated by *Turgel and Vecchia*) for widening an uncertainty interval asymmetrically when a known bias is deliberately left uncorrected.
- **Default ASTM E691 / ISO 5725 fallback** — named in 2.5.1 as a last-resort uncertainty surrogate based on interlaboratory reproducibility standard deviation.

## Key Concepts

**Bias versus accuracy.** The Handbook separates these carefully. Accuracy is a *qualitative* term about whether a measurement agrees with the true (target or reference) value. Bias is the *quantitative* difference between the average of repeated measurements on an object and its true value — for a laboratory, the (generally unknown) gap between the lab's long-term average for a test item and what the reference laboratory would obtain on the same item. Bias is identified through calibration against a reference lab, check-standard control-limit violations, measurement assurance "blind" samples, and interlaboratory comparisons; it is reduced chiefly by calibration traced back to the reference base through a chain of intercomparisons. A caution worth flagging: errors that contribute to bias can persist even when every instrument and standard is properly calibrated and in control — temperature is singled out as the most likely culprit (a constant heat source corrupts dimensional measurements of metal), and such errors are identifiable only by those steeped in the measurement technology.

**The reference base as ultimate authority.** The most critical element of any measurement is the link between a single measurement and the reference base — the ultimate source of authority for the unit. For fundamental SI units these bases are maintained by national/regional standards labs; for other quantities, consensus values from interlaboratory tests can serve. The chapter notes that mass is the only unit historically defined by an artifact (the platinum-iridium kilogram at the BIPM), while length is defined by the distance light travels in vacuum in a fixed fraction of a second. Secondary reference standards (e.g., stainless-steel kilograms, gage blocks) are calibrated against primaries on high-precision comparators with corrections for non-ideal conditions, then themselves become the references for test items.

**Short-term variability (precision) and its four interchangeable names.** Precision describes the small random changes even a very precise instrument exhibits over the minutes-to-hours of a measurement sequence. The Handbook lists four interchangeable terms — precision, repeatability, within-time variability, short-term variability — and quantifies all of them by a single standard deviation: the short-term (repeatability) standard deviation. An individual short-term estimate is unreliable below about ten degrees of freedom, so estimates are *pooled* across days to yield the level-1 standard deviation with K(J−1) degrees of freedom.

**Long-term variability and the trap of "reproducibility."** The day-to-day standard deviation, computed over the daily means, is the process (level-2) standard deviation with K−1 degrees of freedom. A pointed caution: with very precise instruments, day-to-day variability often *exceeds* instrument precision because of small uncontrollable environmental and handling changes — so a process is not fully characterized until this is quantified. The Handbook uses *reproducibility* only generically (variability across days) precisely because national and international standards assign it conflicting specific definitions.

**Statistical control as a guarantee with a caveat.** The purpose of statistical control is to guarantee goodness within predictable limits and to *validate* the uncertainty statement. The sharp caveat: if the process is improperly specified or calibrated to begin with, control procedures can only guarantee *comparability* among measurements — not correctness. The control tests assume errors are uncorrelated over time and drawn from a single distribution; normality is assumed but the tests are robust to mild departures, so practically a bell-shaped, symmetric distribution suffices.

**Why measurement control uses individual-observations charts.** The Shewhart chart here is an *individual observations* chart, a deliberate departure from rational-subgroup charts. Subgroups of short-term repetitions characterize instrument precision but *not* long-term process variability — and measurement science cares about assessing individual measurements (or averages of short-term repetitions). Therefore the standard deviation *over time* (the process standard deviation), not the within-subgroup standard deviation, sets the limits. The choice of *k* is a deliberate trade: k = 2 gives tight control (≈5% false signals) and assumes >100 degrees of freedom for the process standard deviation; k = 3 flags only egregious excursions (≈1%). A minimum of about 100 historical check-standard values is required to establish the accepted value (baseline).

**Calibration as bias reduction, built on differencing.** Calibration assigns values to an artifact or instrument response relative to reference standards, with the explicit purpose of eliminating or reducing bias relative to the reference base. Single-point ("artifact") calibration compares an unknown to a reference of the same nominal value via a comparator, taking the difference so the instrument bias cancels — the only demand on the instrument is linearity over the small range spanned by the two artifacts. Because a single difference offers no way to assess random error, the procedure is repeated, averaged, and given a standard deviation; redundant connected schedules of such comparisons are the *calibration designs*.

**Restraint and the structure of a design.** The *restraint* is whatever is already known — a single reference standard's assigned value, or, when several references are present, those assigned values added together. A workable design must have nominally-zero differences and must be solvable for the individual items given the restraint — a property lost if references are compared only among themselves and test items only among themselves. The chapter explicitly rejects formal optimality criteria, judging a "good" design instead by practical factors: few measurements, degrees of freedom greater than three, and small enough standard deviations on the test-item estimates for their intended use. Every cataloged design includes a provision for a check standard in each measurement series, used both to control the calibration process and to quantify uncertainty. Solving a design yields values for individual references and test items, the check-standard value, a repeatability standard deviation, and the standard deviations (with covariance terms) of the estimates.

**Two special biases calibration designs are built to defeat.** First, *left-right (constant instrument) bias* — a bias independent of measurement direction, observed in standard voltage cells, not removed by reversing current; it is eliminated by taking two measurements in reverse directions so the constant cancels. These left-right balanced designs are valid *only* for comparing standards in the same environment/enclosure. Second, *bias from linear instrument drift* over the measurement time (e.g., temperature build-up from body heat or handling); drift-elimination schemes assume linear drift and equally spaced measurements in time, and the substitution-weighing example shows how a symmetric measurement order lets the drift-free difference and the drift size both be estimated.

**The unifying standard-deviation equation.** For a calibrated value, the standard deviation combines repeatability and between-day components through s_test = sqrt(K1²·s1² + K2²·s_days²), where K1 and K2 are design-specific factors read from the solution tables and s_days derives from the historical level-2 check-standard standard deviation. The day-to-day term can be negligible, simplifying the arithmetic; both s1 and s_days must come from historical data, not from the current design alone.

**Gauge R&R: the four behaviors and the nested data structure.** A gauge study characterizes gauges in their production environment across repeatability, reproducibility, stability, and bias. Time-dependent variability is collected through nested designs at one to three levels (level-1 short-term precision; level-2 across days; level-3 across runs separated by months). Two levels suffice for most systems; three are recommended where the error sources are poorly understood. The reproducibility standard deviation s_R (the uncertainty of a single test-item measurement) combines the day-to-day and short-term components. The chapter stresses these designs need *no* interaction terms for time-dependent errors and should use a single operator unless operator effect is itself being studied (in which case operators substitute for runs at level-3, and the design must remain truly nested — each operator measuring on the same schedule).

**Bias analysis within a gauge study.** Beyond variability, a gauge study probes resolution, linearity, drift, differences among gauges, and geometry/configuration differences. Whether a difference (e.g., between instruments or operators) is treated as a *random* component or a *bias* component depends on the intended scope of the uncertainty statement — random if it should apply to any instrument drawn from the batch, bias if it applies to one specific instrument relative to the group.

**The ISO/GUM uncertainty definition and tenets.** Uncertainty is defined (per GUM/VIM) as the parameter characterizing the dispersion of values reasonably attributable to the measurand. The Handbook adds that precision and bias are properties of a *method*, whereas uncertainty is a property of a *specific result* on a single test item in a specific configuration. The governing ISO tenets: every component is a standard deviation; all biases are assumed corrected (the uncertainty being that of the correction); zero corrections are allowed when a bias cannot be corrected but an uncertainty is assessed; and uncertainty intervals are symmetric. Components are grouped not by error type but by data source — Type A (evaluated by statistical methods) and Type B (evaluated by other means) — and combined identically.

**Type A versus Type B, precisely.** Both can apply to random error *and* bias; the sole distinguishing feature is whether the component comes from a statistical analysis of data (Type A) or not (Type B). Type B sources include reference standards calibrated elsewhere, physical constants, unsampled environmental effects, configuration/geometry misalignment, and limited instrument resolution. When no documented distribution exists, a worst-case half-width *a* is estimated from experience, judgment, or scant data, and converted to a standard deviation under an assumed distribution: a/√3 for uniform (most conservative — largest), a/√6 for triangular, a/3 for normal (least conservative — smallest, with ±a covering 99.7%). Following GUM, such judgment-derived standard deviations are assigned *infinite* degrees of freedom.

**Top-down estimation versus propagation of error.** The Handbook favors a *top-down* approach — estimating uncertainty directly from repetitions of the final result — because it automatically captures covariances and unsuspected error sources and is independent of any propagation model. Propagation of error (combining standard deviations of auxiliary measurements through partial derivatives) is reserved for when the result cannot be replicated directly; the partial derivatives are the *sensitivity coefficients*. The chapter supplies tabulated formulas for functions of one and two variables (credited to *Ku*) and the exact product-variance formula (credited to *Goodman, 1960*), and works a multi-variable example on a fluid-flow discharge coefficient. Covariance terms should be included only when estimated from sufficient data; reported values from calibration designs typically carry non-zero covariances that matter when summing items (e.g., two weights, or two gage blocks end to end).

**Uncertainty budgets, standard and expanded uncertainty.** Components are tabulated with their sensitivity coefficients, standard deviations, and degrees of freedom (a typical budget lists repeatability, reproducibility, stability, and instrument bias). The standard uncertainty is the root-sum-of-squares u = sqrt(Σ aᵢ²sᵢ²). The expanded uncertainty U = k·u uses k as the t critical value for ν degrees of freedom; for large ν, k = 2 approximates 95% coverage, giving Y − U ≤ True Value ≤ Y + U. When the combined degrees of freedom are unknown — troublesome when large components have few degrees of freedom — the Welch-Satterthwaite formula approximates ν.

**Treating bias you choose not to correct.** GUM assumes all biases are corrected, but at the factory floor that may be impractical, expensive, or commercially pointless. The Phillips-and-Eberhardt method reports the measurement as taken and *adjusts the uncertainty* instead, producing an asymmetric interval: a known bias δ shifts the limits to U₋ = U + δ and U₊ = U − δ (floored at zero to keep limits non-negative). If δ is itself estimated, its standard deviation enters the combined uncertainty as u_c = sqrt(u² + u_bias²). The method is designed so the final uncertainty is never smaller than the corrected-case uncertainty, reduces to it when correction is applied, preserves coverage, and remains transferable to downstream uncertainty statements.

## Mental Models

**The measurement process as a production line.** The opening and recurring frame: measurements are products, and a measurement process should be monitored exactly like a production process expected to yield identical results within limits across time, instruments, operators, and conditions. The difficulty is that you cannot sample the "product" directly because the test items change every sequence — which is the entire reason the check standard exists as a stable, repeatable surrogate.

**The check standard as a surrogate for the unseen.** Measurements on the check standard, spaced at regular intervals, stand in for the measurements you *would* make on test items if you had unlimited time and resources. This reframes quality control as: keep measuring something that should never change, and any change you detect is a change in the *process*, not the item.

**Levels of time as nested containers of error.** Time is treated as the master indicator of random error, organized into nested shells: within a sequence (level-1 precision), across days (level-2 reproducibility), and across runs/months (level-3 stability). Each shell has its own standard deviation and its own degrees of freedom, and the right number of shells depends on how well the process is understood. This nesting is the conceptual backbone linking the characterization, control, gauge-study, and uncertainty sections — the same s1/s2/s3 reappear throughout.

**Two charts, two sensitivities.** Shewhart and EWMA are framed as a deliberate pair: Shewhart reacts to the *current* measurement and catches large jumps intuitively; EWMA blends the recent past and catches small, persistent shifts (around one to one-and-a-half standard deviations) that a Shewhart chart would miss until they accumulate. Because the EWMA statistic is a weighted average, its standard deviation — and thus its control limits — are narrower than Shewhart's. The mass-calibration comparison (baseline pre-1985, monitoring from 1985) is the canonical illustration: the small post-1985 shift is barely visible to Shewhart but produces multiple EWMA violations.

**Precision control is one-sided.** For the F-distribution chart on short-term standard deviations, only the *upper* limit matters — the concern is degradation, not improvement. As long as new short-term standard deviations stay under the limit derived from historical data, instrument precision is presumed intact (common-cause variation). This mirrors a one-sided hypothesis test that the current precision has not worsened relative to a "known" historical sigma.

**Sensitivity coefficient as the lever between an error and the result.** The sensitivity coefficient (a partial derivative, or a design-derived factor like a1, a2, a3) is the model for how much a given source's standard deviation actually moves the reported value. The budget tables show these coefficients shrinking as the reported value averages over more repetitions, days, or runs — the explicit knob for how averaging buys down uncertainty.

**Conservatism as a dial across assumed distributions.** When data are absent, the choice of distribution (uniform → triangular → normal) is itself a conservatism dial: uniform spreads probability evenly to the endpoints and yields the largest standard deviation; normal concentrates it and yields the smallest. The experimenter is explicitly asked to consider how that assumption flows into the final number.

## Anti-patterns

The source names several recurring failure modes:

- **Correlated measurements taken too close in time.** When measurements are spaced so tightly that the instrument cannot recover between them, errors become correlated over time, violating the independence assumption; a time-lag plot is the recommended check.
- **Histogram pathologies signaling a broken process.** A double-humped check-standard histogram means errors are being drawn from two or more distributions; long tails indicate outliers; a flat or end-humped pattern indicates the process is out of control or improperly specified. An S-shaped probability plot signals outliers or non-normal data.
- **No within-day redundancy.** Collecting only one measurement per occasion (J = 1) leaves no way to check short-term precision; the rule is at least J = 2 repetitions per occasion.
- **Treating an interlaboratory reproducibility standard deviation as a true uncertainty.** The ASTM E691 / ISO 5725 default yields a *prediction* interval for a randomly drawn lab, not a confidence interval for the lab at hand; it can reward poor performers and punish good ones, ignores sources outside the interlaboratory test, and is only a snapshot in time. It is explicitly a strategy of last resort.
- **Reading the calibration control chart point-by-point instead of as a whole.** Small but significant shifts (the post-1985 mass example) are invisible in isolated points and emerge only when the chart is viewed in its entirety over time.
- **Building an unsolvable or trivial calibration design.** Comparing references only among themselves and test items only among themselves, with no cross-intercomparison, produces a design that cannot be solved for individual items under the restraint.
- **Assuming bias is gone because everything is "calibrated."** Bias-contributing errors (temperature foremost) survive full calibration and control, and are catchable only by domain experts — not by the statistics alone.

## Key Takeaways

- Characterize a measurement process by four named quantities — bias, short-term (level-1) variability, long-term (level-2) variability, and uncertainty — and remember the method applies to ongoing, not one-shot or destructive, processes.
- The check-standard *database* is the linchpin: it supplies the surrogate data for control charts, the historical baseline for limits, and the level-2 standard deviation feeding uncertainty.
- Pool short-term standard deviations across days (they are unreliable individually below ~10 degrees of freedom); use the process (level-2) standard deviation, not within-subgroup spread, to set measurement-control limits via an individual-observations Shewhart chart.
- Pair Shewhart (large, current shifts; k = 2 for tight, k = 3 for egregious-only) with EWMA (small, gradual shifts; λ ≈ 0.15–0.3); monitor short-term precision separately with an F-distribution chart whose only meaningful bound is the upper limit.
- Calibration reduces bias through differencing and redundant *designs* solved by restrained least squares; the restraint anchors the solution, the check standard rides along in every series, and special designs (left-right balanced, drift-elimination) defeat constant-instrument and linear-drift biases.
- Combine repeatability and between-day terms through the unifying equation s_test = sqrt(K1²s1² + K2²s_days²), drawing s1 and s_days from history.
- Follow GUM: every component is a standard deviation, classify as Type A (statistical) or Type B (other), combine by root-sum-squares into u, and expand to U = k·u (k = 2 ≈ 95% for large degrees of freedom), using Welch-Satterthwaite when combined degrees of freedom are uncertain.
- Prefer top-down uncertainty estimation over propagation of error; use propagation (with sensitivity coefficients and, where justified, covariances) only when the result cannot be replicated directly.
- When a known bias is left uncorrected, widen the interval asymmetrically (Phillips-Eberhardt) rather than ignoring it.
