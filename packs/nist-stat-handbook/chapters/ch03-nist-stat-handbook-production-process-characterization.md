# Chapter 3: Production Process Characterization

## Core Idea
Production Process Characterization (PPC) is a disciplined activity for building **data-based models** of a manufacturing process: identify its key inputs and outputs, observe their behavior across the full operating range, estimate steady-state behavior at the preferred operating point, and capture the input/output relationships in mathematical models that can then drive monitoring and improvement. The overriding purpose of a PPC study is to **identify and quantify the sources of variation** in a process so that variation can be reduced and the process can be shown to be both predictable (stable) and controllable (capable). Everything in the chapter — the planning steps, the model families, the sampling theory, the analysis sequence, the two worked case studies — exists to serve that single goal.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The three-step characterization activity (Screening → Mapping → Passive)** — *Screening* uses screening experiments to reduce a long candidate list of inputs/outputs to the key few and to form initial relationship models; *Mapping* uses response-surface experiments to chart the key outputs over their operating ranges; the *Passive* step runs the process at nominal settings to estimate stability and capability.
  - When to use: the first two steps are needed only for a new process or after a significant engineering change; the passive step recurs throughout a process's life (initial qualification, control-chart setup, after minor adjustments, after scheduled maintenance).
- **The four PPC steps (Plan → Collect → Analyze/Interpret → Report)** — a project-management spine for any study; planning is named the single most important step because faithful planning is what keeps data collection efficient under scarce factory access.
  - When to use: every PPC study, regardless of size; all decisions about how the characterization is conducted are meant to be fixed during planning, with every deviation later recorded.
- **The black-box process model** — a process is drawn with controllable inputs entering one side, observable-but-uncontrolled inputs entering top/bottom, and measurable outputs leaving the other side; inputs are *Factors* (explanatory variables), outputs are *Responses*.
  - When to use: the first modeling step in planning, to enumerate what could be sampled and how the pieces connect.
- **The fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagram** — places one characteristic on the center line and branches the influencing categories (material, machine, method, environment) into progressively finer detail.
  - When to use: after the black box, to decompose the complexity of a process and document which factors plausibly influence a chosen response.
- **The Continuous Linear Model (CLM)** — \(y = a_0 + \sum a_i f(x_i) + \epsilon\); "linear" refers to the coefficients summing linearly, not to the terms themselves, so curvature (e.g., a quadratic time term) fits within it. Coefficients are estimated by least squares.
  - When to use: the workhorse model in PPC — Shewhart control charts (\(y = a_0 + \epsilon\)), response-surface models, predictive models over a linear operating range. Estimation needs no assumption beyond the general ones; *testing* additionally assumes Gaussian error.
- **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) models** — the comparison engine of PPC, taught through **value-splitting**: every observation is decomposed into a common value plus level/interaction effects plus a residual. The chapter develops one-way, two-way *crossed* (every level of A pairs with every level of B; estimates main effects and interaction), and two-way *nested* (each level of the outer factor contains its own distinct subset of the inner factor's levels rather than the full set, so no interaction can be estimated).
  - When to use: comparing multiple levels of one or more factors (tools, recipes, operators, shifts). Crossed when all combinations are achievable; nested when a constraint forbids crossing (e.g., operators cannot swap machines), or for random-effects situations such as units sampled within lots.
- **Discrete models (contingency-table / chi-square)** — for discrete responses with discrete explanatory variables, build an \(r \times s\) count table, compute expected counts under independence (\(E_{ij} = N_{i.}N_{.j}/N\)), and test with a chi-square goodness-of-fit statistic. A log-linear model is named for mixed discrete/continuous predictors but placed outside the handbook's scope.
  - When to use: yield (good/bad), bins, survey-style outcomes; the chi-square result degrades when any cell count is small (roughly under 5).

## Key Concepts
- **Distribution = location, spread, shape.** Because no process output repeats exactly, values scatter about a central tendency. Location is the expected output value (the level a stable process settles on); spread is the expected variation; shape describes whether that variation is symmetric, skewed, or multimodal. Demonstrating constant location, constant spread, and a stable shape is the precondition for control charts and designed experiments.
- **Controlled vs. uncontrolled variation.** Controlled variation is a stable, random, consistent fluctuation about a constant level; uncontrolled variation changes over time, is unpredictable, and typically shows structure (e.g., a drifting trend). A process is **stable** only if it shows controlled variation; a PPC study aims to eliminate every source of the uncontrolled kind.
- **The Empirical Rule and the variance/standard-deviation pairing.** Spread is represented numerically by variance and graphically by a histogram. The Empirical Rule gives rough coverage bands: about 60–78% of data within one standard deviation, about 90–98% within two, and over 99% within three.
- **Error propagation — add variances, never standard deviations.** Observed variance at a step is usually an accumulation from prior steps plus measurement error. Through a linear relationship, variance propagates as \(\mathrm{Var}(y)=\alpha^2\mathrm{Var}(y_1)+\beta^2\mathrm{Var}(y_2)+2\alpha\beta\,\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2)\); when sources are uncorrelated (e.g., independent process and measurement error) the covariance term vanishes and total variance is the simple sum. Breaking an overall variance back into its parts is *components-of-variance* estimation, handled by ANOVA.
- **Populations and sampling.** We sample a target population to make inferences; facts about a sample are not automatically facts about the population. Sample adequacy depends on four attributes: representativeness (is it random?), size, population variability, and the desired precision of the estimates.
- **Factors, responses, and variable type.** Factors and responses are further classified — *Measurement* (continuous, ordered, infinite range; most information), *Ordinal* (discrete, ordered, finite), *Nominal* (discrete, unordered, few values; least information). The type dictates which analysis technique is valid.
- **Correlation vs. causality.** Two variables are correlated when a change in one accompanies a change in the other; causal when a change in one *produces* the change. Correlation does not imply causality (a hidden third factor may drive both). The practical path is to find correlations first (a property of the data) and then work to establish causality (which additionally requires engineering judgment), using screening designs to thin candidates and response-surface designs to model the survivors.
- **Sampling-plan precision and systematic error.** A sampling scheme is judged on two principles: **precision** (the standard error of an estimate, driven by inherent process variability, measurement error, the number of independent replications, and the efficiency of the scheme) and **avoidance of systematic error** (confounding, where a factor's levels coincide with some unaccounted-for variable). **Stratification** (segmenting to minimize within-stratum variability) and **randomization** (random ordering of treatment combinations) together defeat confounding and raise precision.
- **Sample-size determination.** Sample size flows from a probability statement linking desired precision to size. For proportions, \(n = z_\alpha^2(pq/\delta^2)\); for absolute error on a location estimate, \(n \approx z_\alpha^2(\sigma^2/\delta^2)\). Unknown population parameters in these formulas are where prior information helps; the result is then checked for practicality, and the only way to shrink an impractical sample size is to accept less precision.
- **The analysis sequence.** Gather all data into one variables-in-columns file → quality-check with scatter plots and histograms → estimate location/spread/shape → explore relationships (responses vs. responses, responses vs. factors, then conditional plots for interactions) → build models (polynomial for empirical prediction, physical when the underlying physics is known) → analyze variance structure → assess stability → assess capability → check assumptions.
- **Stability vs. capability.** A process is *stable* when its response parameters have constant mean, constant variance, and a constant distribution over time (numerically, *stationarity*, evaluable via the autocovariance function, though graphics usually suffice). *Capability* compares performance to specification: \(C_p = (\mathrm{USL}-\mathrm{LSL})/6s\) measures how much process spread the specs cover, and \(C_{pk}=\min[(\mathrm{USL}-\bar{x})/3s,\ (\bar{x}-\mathrm{LSL})/3s]\) corrects \(C_p\) for an off-center mean. A process must be proven stable before capability can even be discussed.
- **Checking the normality assumption.** Many techniques here (hypothesis tests, control charts, capability indices) assume normality. Non-normality can come from mixed data sources (multimodal — split and analyze separately), an unstable process (essentially unanalyzable), or a genuinely non-normal but stable mechanism such as particle counts (treatable with a power transformation, \(\lambda\) typically in \([-2, 2]\), common values giving the log, square-root, and inverse).

## Mental Models
- **Spend on planning, save on data.** Factory access for experiments is scarce and expensive, so the leverage is front-loaded: a faithful plan (goals, process model, sampling plan, data-handling scheme, analysis outline) makes collection mechanical and analysis honest. Treat planning as the place where every decision is made and every later exception is logged against.
- **Black box first, fishbone second.** Reach for the simplest possible representation — inputs in, outputs out — before decomposing any single characteristic into its cause-and-effect branches. The two tools are sequential, not alternatives.
- **Value-splitting is ANOVA you can see.** Before trusting an F-table, picture each observation as common value + effects + residual. The turned-pins example (split .125….128 into a mean of .126 plus residuals) is the whole of ANOVA in miniature; everything else is more elaborate bookkeeping over the same idea.
- **Match the error term to the structure.** The denominator of an F-ratio depends on the layout. In the nested case the error term for the top factor is the *nested* mean square (MSB), not the residual (MSE) — using the wrong denominator answers the wrong question. Crossed vs. nested is therefore a modeling decision, not a formatting choice.
- **Masked effects are a structure problem, not a signal problem.** In the furnace example a one-way analysis on zone found nothing because lot-to-lot variation swamped it; re-modeling as a nested layout (zone within lot) revealed both lot and zone as significant. When a known driver looks insignificant, suspect the layout before concluding the effect is absent.
- **Graphically interpret, numerically confirm.** Box plots and nested box plots surface main effects and interactions, but the eyes deceive — any interaction spotted visually is meant to be verified numerically. Conversely, when interactions are real, main effects must be read conditionally, because they no longer tell the whole story alone.
- **Stable before capable.** Capability indices are meaningless on an unstable process, because the distribution they summarize is not fixed. Prove constant mean/variance/shape first.

## Anti-patterns
- **Adding standard deviations.** The source is explicit: never add standard deviations — add variances. Combining spreads by summing standard deviations overstates or misallocates the propagated error.
- **Systematic sampling error / confounded effects.** Running one lot on coolant A and the next on coolant B confounds coolant with lot and tool wear; cleaning two cassettes by different methods and loading them at different tube positions confounds pre-clean method with cassette and tube-location effects. Whenever a factor's levels line up with an unaccounted-for variable, the effects cannot be separated.
- **Holding a review of the data pipeline too late — i.e., not knowing the data path.** In larger facilities data passes through automatic acquisition, storage, reformatting, and retrieval before anyone sees it; common silent failures named in the source include automated machines rejecting outliers, only summary statistics (mean/SD) being saved, explanatory-variable values (location, operator) being dropped, and missing values being handled invisibly. The countermeasure is to understand the full collection process during planning and to do dry runs.
- **Skimping on planning and then drifting from the plan.** Because planning is the high-leverage step, executing loosely against it — or failing to note exceptions — undermines the whole study.

## Key Takeaways
1. PPC builds **data-based mathematical models** of a process; its primary goal is to **identify and quantify sources of variation** so they can be reduced.
2. The activity has **three steps** (Screening, Mapping, Passive) and any study runs through **four steps** (Plan, Collect, Analyze/Interpret, Report) — with **planning** carrying the most weight.
3. Model the process with the **black-box model** (factors → responses) and refine causes with **fishbone diagrams**.
4. The core model families are the **CLM** (control charts, response surfaces; least-squares fit), **ANOVA** via **value-splitting** (one-way, two-way crossed, two-way nested), and **discrete/contingency-table** analysis (chi-square).
5. Sampling rests on two principles — **precision** (standard error) and **avoidance of systematic error/confounding** — and is defended by **stratification** and **randomization**; sample size is derived from a precision-vs-size probability statement.
6. **Add variances, not standard deviations**; decompose total variance into **components of variance**.
7. **Stability** (constant mean, variance, distribution over time) is a prerequisite for **capability** (\(C_p\), and \(C_{pk}\) for off-center processes).
8. **Interpret graphically, confirm numerically**; choose **crossed vs. nested** structure deliberately, because it sets the correct F-ratio error term — and a wrong layout can **mask** a real effect (furnace zone within lot).
