# Chapter 8: Tailoring & CM Templates by Lifecycle Phase

> Source basis: MIL-HDBK-61B, Appendix B (SAE EIA-649-1 Tailoring Guidance) and Appendix C (CM Templates). Use the current **MIL-HDBK-61B**, not the 2001 MIL-HDBK-61A(SE).

## Core Idea
Configuration management is not a fixed bundle of activities applied uniformly to every program; it is a menu that must be *selected from* and *scaled* to fit where a program sits in its life cycle and what kind of item is being managed. MIL-HDBK-61B addresses this in two complementary appendices. Appendix B gives the acquirer a tailoring method: a phase-by-requirement matrix that marks each CM activity as Recommended, Tailorable, or Not Recommended for a given acquisition phase, anchored to the "shall" statements in SAE EIA-649-1. Appendix C gives the program a set of ready-made templates — one per life-cycle phase — that lay out, for each CM function, the objectives, government and contractor actions, and the benefits of doing the work versus the risks of skipping it. Taken together, the appendices answer two distinct questions: *which* CM requirements belong on this contract in this phase (Appendix B), and *what does each CM function actually look like* once selected (Appendix C). The recurring discipline behind both is that requirements drive cost, so only the requirements genuinely needed for the current phase should be placed on contract.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **EIA-649-1 lifecycle applicability matrix (Figure B-1, "Configuration management for DoD Contracts")** — a table that cross-references each major CM requirement (General, CM Plan, Configuration Identification, Software, Change Management, ECP, Request for Variance, Configuration Status Accounting, Audit, FCA, PCA) against a set of life-cycle and support columns (MSA/TMRR, EMD, LRIP, FRP, Service Spares, DLA Spares, and CLS/PBL), keyed to specific EIA-649-1 paragraph numbers.
  - When/how: each cell is marked **R (Recommended)**, **T (Tailorable)**, or **NR (Not Recommended)**. The acquirer uses it as a quick reference to see, at a glance, which CM activities to require, which to scale, and which to leave off for the phase being executed. The handbook notes the marking can shift across phases — a supplier's general CM requirements are tailorable during the early Material Solution and Technology phase but become required during EMD and LRIP.
- **EIA-649-1 Annex A — Tailoring Worksheet** — the named instrument for performing the actual tailoring. The acquirer works through the worksheet to pick the specific requirements appropriate to their program rather than importing the whole standard.
  - When/how: the handbook is explicit that the worksheet is a selection aid — the listed requirements are not meant to be placed on contract wholesale, but to help the acquirer reason conceptually about which requirements are actually needed. Notably, while the EIA-649 principles themselves are copyright-protected and reference-only, Annex A may be used and quoted directly in contracts.
- **CM lifecycle requirements flow (Figure B-2, "CM lifecycle requirements flow chart")** — a four-step process for turning a determined life-cycle phase into contractual CM tasking.
  - When/how: the sequence is (1) determine recommended CM requirements from the table, (2) tailor them by selecting the corresponding EIA-649-1 "shall" statements, (3) incorporate those "shall" statements into the tasking document, and (4) execute day-to-day CM, with GEIA-HB-649 cited as the working reference for the execution step.
- **Appendix C phase templates (Figures C-1 through C-4)** — one template per acquisition phase that portrays CM objectives, typical metrics, activities, actions, benefits, and risks. The four templates are TMRR (Figure C-1), EMD (Figure C-2), Production and Deployment (Figure C-3), and Operations and Support (Figure C-4).
  - When/how: each template is organized by CM function (Management/Planning, Configuration Identification, Configuration Control, Configuration Status Accounting, Configuration Audits) and within each function splits actions into Government, Government-and-Contractor, and Contractor lanes, paired with a Benefits / Risks-if-not-done column. Use them as a phase-specific road map and as a justification tool — the risk column states what goes wrong when the function is omitted.
- **GEIA-HB-649A, Table 3 — Tailoring considerations** — the cited source for worked examples of how CM processes and resources can be tailored.
  - When/how: referenced as the place to find examples of process and resource tailoring considerations; the handbook adds the caution that tailored practices must remain appropriate to the product's complexity.

## Key Concepts
- **R / T / NR semantics.** Every cell in the Figure B-1 matrix carries one of three dispositions: Recommended (apply it), Tailorable (scale it to the program), or Not Recommended (do not impose it for this phase). The same requirement can carry different dispositions in different columns — the matrix is read per phase, not as a single global ruling.
- **Requirements drive cost.** A theme repeated across Appendix B: each selected "shall" statement is a requirement, and requirements drive program cost. Failing to tailor the EIA-649-1 statements imposes unnecessary requirements and unnecessary cost; the appendix frames careful selection as a cost-control discipline, not paperwork.
- **Tailoring factors.** The handbook names the program characteristics that ought to shape tailoring decisions: which life-cycle phase the program is in; how the contract is typed and structured; the method used to acquire or procure; the item's complexity, size, and intended use; how mission-critical it is; and what logistics support the affected CIs (hardware and software alike) demand.
- **Author or adapt the "shall" statements.** When building statement-of-work requirements, the acquirer either writes their own "shall" statements or tailors the existing EIA-649-1 ones to best capture their CM needs.
- **MSA has no Appendix C template — by design.** Because material solutions analysis occurs prior to Milestone A under DoD Instruction 5000.2, the handbook states CM activities are not applicable during that phase, so there is no MSA template; the earliest phase template is TMRR.
- **The function set per phase template.** Each phase template walks the same CM functions, but their applicability shifts:
  - **TMRR (Figure C-1):** Planning, Configuration Identification, Configuration Control, and Configuration Status Accounting are active; **Configuration Audits are explicitly not applicable** in this phase. Work centers on standing up CM processes, controlling concept-study and draft-RFP material, establishing an audit trail of decisions and document iterations, and assigning unique identifiers to digital data files and document representations.
  - **EMD (Figure C-2):** the most elaborate template. Configuration Identification covers approving the system specification to establish the Functional Baseline (FBL), establishing Allocated Baselines (ABLs) per CI, and — after performance verification and product/documentation consistency — establishing Product Baselines (PBLs), with separate Government and contractor approval-authority lanes. Configuration Control introduces the CCB (charter, membership, operating procedures), Class I ECPs against FBL/ABL documentation, Requests for Variance (RFVs), Notices of Revision (NORs), and change effectivity. Configuration Status Accounting and Configuration Audits (including the verify-against-specified-and-warranted check and physical inspection) are both fully active.
  - **Production and Deployment (Figure C-3):** identification, control, status accounting, and audit continue, but the emphasis shifts to maintaining the already-established baselines (current FBL, Government ABLs, PBLs), processing engineering changes driven by approved ECPs, controlling local engineering changes so they do not impact current baselines, tracking traceable items by serial or lot number, and keeping product/documentation consistency through retrofit and replacement.
  - **Operations and Support (Figure C-4):** the leanest template. Identification, control, and status accounting are jointly run by Government and contractor based on the contractual division of responsibility, focused on re-identification after approved changes, keeping local changes from disturbing baselines, and recording configuration changes from retrofit and maintenance-action replacements. **Formal configuration audit activity is generally not applicable** in this phase. Planning here adds end-of-life concerns — planning for demilitarization and disposal.
- **Baseline and authority vocabulary appears throughout the templates.** The phase templates repeatedly invoke FBL, ABL, and PBL; the split between Government and contractor *configuration approval authority* per CI; and DCMA (or another contractually designated authority) as the decision point for minor variances to Government-approved documents, versus the Government for major variances, versus the appropriate internal contractor authority for variances to contractor-baselined documents.
- **Benefits-versus-risks framing.** Every function in every template pairs the actions with a benefits column and a risks-if-not-done column. The benefits cluster around efficient and informed change processing, consistency between product and documentation, preserved control at system interfaces, supportability, and limited/documented variances. The risks cluster around ad-hoc change management, changes approved without knowledge of impact, loss of product-to-documentation consistency, uncertainty at interfaces, and degraded supportability.

## Mental Models
- **Tailoring is subtraction from a vetted maximum, not addition from zero.** EIA-649-1 supplies the full catalog of CM "shall" statements; the worksheet and the R/T/NR matrix exist to *remove* what the phase does not need. Starting from the standard and cutting is the intended motion — importing the whole standard is the failure mode the appendix warns against.
- **The matrix tells you *whether*, the templates tell you *what*.** Figure B-1 answers the contracting question (does this requirement go on this contract in this phase?). Figures C-1 through C-4 answer the execution question (once it's on contract, what are the actual actions, and who owns them?). Use them in that order.
- **CM intensity is a curve, not a flat line.** Work ramps from process stand-up in TMRR, peaks in EMD where baselines are first established and the CCB machinery comes online, stays heavy through Production while baselines are maintained against a stream of changes, and thins in Operations & Support to maintenance-driven recording. Audits bracket the middle: absent in TMRR, central in EMD/Production, generally absent again in O&S.
- **Read the risk column as the business case.** The "risks if not done" entries are the handbook's argument for funding each function. When a stakeholder asks why a CM activity is needed in a phase, the template's risk column — chaotic ad-hoc change management, decisions made on obsolete data, replacement parts that don't fit, technical anarchy — is the prepared answer.
- **Approval authority is the routing table.** Whether a change or variance goes to the Government, to DCMA, or to an internal contractor board depends entirely on who holds configuration approval authority over the affected document and on whether the variance is major or minor. The phase templates encode this routing rather than leaving it to judgment.

## Anti-patterns
The source frames its risk columns and tailoring cautions in terms of what goes wrong when work is omitted or mis-scoped:
- **Importing EIA-649-1 wholesale instead of tailoring.** The appendix states directly that failing to tailor the "shall" statements drives unnecessary requirements and cost onto the program.
- **Ordering excessive configuration documentation.** Listed as a risk: documentation ordered that is not necessary for program management or sustainment — wasted requirements that still cost money.
- **Ad-hoc / unmanaged change.** The control templates repeatedly name chaotic, ad-hoc change management — changes approved without knowledge of their significant impacts, changes that are unnecessary or offer no benefit — culminating in the handbook's blunt phrase for the end state: essentially, technical anarchy.
- **Decisions on obsolete data.** Across the status-accounting risk columns: improper decisions about change effectivity, retrofit requirements, and deployment of items whose support assets are not in place — all driving avoidable cost.
- **Tailoring that ignores product complexity.** The handbook cautions that tailored CM practices must stay appropriate to the complexity of the product — over-tailoring a complex item is as much a hazard as over-specifying a simple one.

## Key Takeaways
1. CM requirements are *selected and scaled per phase*, not applied uniformly — Appendix B's R/T/NR matrix (Figure B-1) is the quick-reference for that decision, keyed to EIA-649-1 paragraphs and acquisition/support columns.
2. Tailoring is performed with **EIA-649-1 Annex A — Tailoring Worksheet**, which (unlike the copyright-protected principles) may be used and quoted directly in contracts.
3. The flow is fixed (Figure B-2): determine recommended requirements → tailor by picking the corresponding "shall" statements → put them in the tasking document → execute day-to-day CM (GEIA-HB-649).
4. Requirements drive cost; the central tailoring discipline is to put only the genuinely needed requirements on contract, judged against the listed tailoring factors (phase, contract type, complexity, size, use, mission criticality, logistics support).
5. Appendix C supplies one execution template per phase — TMRR, EMD, Production & Deployment, Operations & Support — each organized by CM function with Government/Contractor action lanes and a benefits-versus-risks column.
6. There is no MSA template: pre–Milestone A material solutions analysis is treated as a phase where CM activities are not applicable.
7. Audit applicability is phase-dependent: not applicable in TMRR, central in EMD and Production, and generally not applicable in Operations & Support.
8. The templates encode the baseline vocabulary (FBL/ABL/PBL), the Government/contractor approval-authority split, and the major-versus-minor variance routing (Government / DCMA / internal contractor authority).
9. For worked tailoring examples and process/resource considerations, the handbook points to **GEIA-HB-649A, Table 3**, with the standing caution to keep tailored practice matched to product complexity.
