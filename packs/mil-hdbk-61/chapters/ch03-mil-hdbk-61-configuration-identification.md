# Chapter — Configuration Identification

*Source: MIL-HDBK-61B, Section 5 (Configuration Identification). Use the current 61B revision, not the 2001 MIL-HDBK-61A(SE).*

## Core Idea

Configuration identification is the foundational activity of configuration management: it incrementally builds and sustains the authoritative, current definition of a system and its configuration items (CIs) across the whole life cycle — from development and production through deployment, operational support, and eventual demilitarization and disposal. Its job is to give every acquisition and sustainment discipline a shared, common set of documentation to work from, whether the task is developing something new, modifying an existing component, buying a product for operational use, or supporting fielded systems.

61B is explicit that this activity is a prerequisite for all the other CM activities. Configuration control, status accounting, and audit all consume the products of configuration identification. The causal chain is strict: if CIs and their documentation are not properly identified, you cannot control changes to their configuration, cannot keep accurate records or reports, and cannot validate the as-built configuration through audit. The handbook ties this directly to downstream cost: documentation that is inaccurate or incomplete tends to surface later as defective products, slipped schedules, and inflated maintenance bills after delivery.

The process itself decomposes into seven recurring tasks: select CIs at the right levels of the product structure; settle what configuration documentation each CI needs in order to pin down its physical make-up, its required behavior, and its internal and external interfaces; determine the approval authority for each document; issue identifiers for CIs and their documents; maintain identification to support fielded logistics; release configuration documentation; and establish configuration baselines for control. Good configuration control procedures are what keep the integrity of all of this intact over time.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **SAE EIA-649** — Cited by 61B as the source of the basic principles of configuration identification (Section 5.1.1) and again as the basis for document and item identification concepts (Sections 5.6.1, 5.6.2). 61B also directs that EIA-649-conformant contractor practices govern three categories — commercial products fielded inside Government systems, CIs whose performance is held under contractor-only control, and items the contractor has design cognizance over (Section 5.2).
- **GEIA-HB-649** — Referenced as a companion handbook for the range of configuration documentation a CI may carry (from a performance specification down to a commercial item description) and, at the close of the section, pointed to for activity guides, templates, and additional information on interface management (Sections 5.3.1, 5.8.1.3).
- **The major configuration baselines: FBL, ABL, PBL, and the developmental configuration** — Defined in Section 5.5.2 as the baselines tied to life-cycle milestones of a CI. The functional baseline (FBL) captures top-level performance, interoperability, and interface characteristics plus the verification needed; the allocated baseline (ABL) captures the performance-oriented functional and interface characteristics allocated down from a higher-level CI plus its verification; the product baseline (PBL) describes the complete functional and physical characteristics for production and re-procurement; the developmental configuration is the contractor's evolving, internally-controlled design solution.
- **Functional, Allocated, and Product Configuration Documentation (FCD, ACD, PCD)** — The technical documentation sets underpinning the three Government baselines, with a defined order of precedence (Section 5.5.2.10).
- **MIL-STD-961, appendix A** — Cited for the specification structure that lets a performance specification transition into a detailed specification without creating a separate document, preserving congruence across baseline levels (Section 5.5.2.10).
- **MIL-STD-31000** — Named as the standard governing engineering drawings or models delivered as a contract deliverable (Section 5.4.2.2).
- **DoD Instruction 5000.2** — Cited as the basis for the Acquisition Program Baseline (APB) established at Milestones A, B, and C (Section 5.5.1.1).
- **Document identity triad (CAGE code, document type, document identifier)** — The three elements DoD and the military components use to guarantee a document's unique identity, with revision identifier and date pinning down a specific issue (Section 5.6.1).
- **ICWG (Interface Control Working Group)** — Introduced as a specialized IPT for resolving interface issues that engineer-to-engineer interaction cannot settle (Section 5.8.1.3).

## Key Concepts

**CIs are the units of CM.** A CI is a selected item of hardware, software, or a hardware-software combination in which the Government or acquiring activity has CM concern (Section 5.3). They span an enormous range of complexity — an aircraft, ship, tank, or software program at one end; a test meter or a round of ammunition at the other. Regardless of form or size, a CI's configuration is documented and controlled, gets a unique identifier, carries associated configuration documentation, has its changes controlled, has status accounting records kept, and is subject to configuration audit (unless it already has an established PBL). The handbook is careful to note that the term CI covers both hardware and software; where a statement applies to only one, it uses HWCI or CSCI respectively, and software items are almost always designated CIs because they typically control system functionality (Section 5.3.1.3).

**Defining a system's performance does not require designating everything as a CI.** 61B explicitly decouples control from CI status: you can define and control a system's performance without designating every component as a CI, and non-CI components need not be under Government control. Lower-level requirements can be established and controlled through the contractor's own design and engineering release process. Government control of a lower-level component typically kicks in only when a change there would impact the Government-baselined performance specification for the CI above it (Section 5.3.1.1).

**CI selection is an early, high-leverage, long-lived decision.** Initial selection happens early in acquisition but its consequences are lasting, touching program management, systems engineering, acquisition logistics, and CM (Section 5.3.1.5). It sets the level of Government configuration control for the whole life cycle. Early on (for EMD), deliverable and separately installable units and other items needing significant management attention become CIs; during production, fielding, and operational support, items needed for logistics support and designated for separate procurement are added (Section 5.3.1.2). During the MSA and TMRR phases the architecture is set, the WBS is built, and major CIs are selected, feeding the supportability plan. Beyond the simple top-tier CIs (which map to contract line items and the WBS), the handbook lists reasons to break out lower-level CIs: critical/new/modified design, independent end-use functions, the need for separate configuration control or change effectivity, components common across systems, interfaces with other systems, the level at which interchangeability matters, separate delivery or installation, separate performance/test definition, and high-risk components (Section 5.3.1.4).

**Configuration documentation defines performance, functional, and physical attributes — and everything else hangs off it.** Per 61B (echoing SAE EIA-649), all other product documentation — operation and maintenance manuals, illustrated parts breakdowns, test plans and procedures — is based on and relates back to the configuration documentation (Section 5.4). For each CI, that documentation is what configuration control, logistics support, software support after deployment, and re-procurement all build on. Specification type selection depends on item maturity and operating context, and DoD's order of precedence favors existing commercial products and performance specifications over detailed ones; program-unique specifications sit at the bottom of the preference hierarchy and are used only when nothing higher applies (Section 5.4.1).

**The three Government baselines plus the developmental configuration.** A baseline is a fixed reference configuration, established by recording approved documentation at a milestone or specified time (Section 5.5.1.2). The handbook describes baselines as snapshots, commitment points, and control points. The FBL is established when its FCD is approved by the Government and is always under Government configuration control (Section 5.5.2.2). The ABL is the composite of individual ABLs, each documented in a development specification, and may be controlled by either the Government or the contractor depending on acquisition approach, life-cycle phase, support, and interface needs (Sections 5.5.2.3, 5.5.2.4). The PBL, built on the PCD, completely describes functional and physical characteristics for the production/deployment/operational-support phases, includes form-fit-function and production acceptance test requirements, and folds in the allocated documentation so the item could be re-procured without compromising performance (Section 5.5.2.8). The developmental configuration is the contractor's internally-released, contractor-controlled design and technical data that defines the evolving design solution (Section 5.5.2, item d; Section 5.5.2.7).

**Document order of precedence.** The FCD, ACD, and PCD must be mutually consistent, each a logical and more detailed extension of its predecessor. If a conflict arises between levels, precedence is always FCD first, then ACD, then PCD (Section 5.5.2.10).

**Identifiers are shorthand references that survive the whole life cycle.** The identification scheme is what lets a service person install a correct replacement part and find the right maintenance instructions years after delivery (Section 5.6). Products get unique identifiers (nomenclature, CAGE code, part/item number); individual units get a unique product unit identifier (serial number) when units must be distinguished; a modified product keeps its original serial number even when its part-identifying number changes; and a series of like units gets a product group identifier (lot number or date code) when individual serialization is impractical (Section 5.6.2). Part numbers change whenever a non-interchangeable condition is created (Section 5.6.2.1.2). For software, the identifier is a name plus a version identifier, embedded in source code, marked on media, and typically displayable to the user on command (Section 5.6.2.1.3).

**Engineering release makes documentation usable and brings it under control.** Releasing a document is the step that both opens it up for its intended use and simultaneously places it within the reach of configuration control (Section 5.7). A central constraint: the contractor's release process must prevent any engineering release tied to a Class I change to a Government-approved document until the Government has approved that Class I change (Section 5.7.1). Release records provide an audit trail of documentation status and history, verify that documentation reflects approved changes, and let engineering and manufacturing data be reconciled so changes actually reach delivered units (Section 5.7.2).

**Interface management protects the boundaries between co-functioning items.** Interfaces are the functional and physical characteristics at a common boundary that let systems, equipment, software, and data work together (Section 5.8.1). The aim is twofold: each co-functioning item must contain enough information that the items work together when independently designed and built, and any change must treat the interface attributes as design constraints. To decide how to manage an interface, 61B says to categorize it by contractual relationship, customer/acquisition-activity relationship, hierarchical level, type and complexity of the technical attributes, and developmental status (Section 5.8.1.1).

## Mental Models

- **Identification is the floor, not a wall.** Every other CM activity — control, status accounting, audit — stands on top of identification. Treat it as load-bearing infrastructure: weakness here propagates upward into defective products, schedule slips, and post-delivery maintenance cost.

- **"Know your starting point before you plan a destination."** 61B frames baselines through this everyday idea (Section 5.5.1): you cannot plan, approve, or implement a configuration change without a definition of the current configuration you are changing. A baseline is simply that defined point of reference, made formal.

- **Baselines as a sequence of incremental snapshots.** Each baseline is a point of departure for future change; incremental baselines accumulate sequentially over a CI's life as each change is approved through the control process, and the audit trail from original requirements to the current baseline lives in status accounting (Section 5.5.2.1). Think of the current approved documentation as the "current baseline," not a one-time event.

- **Same baseline, two viewpoints.** A baseline between a prime contractor and a subcontractor is an ABL from the prime's view and an FBL from the subcontractor's view, because to the subcontractor it is the top-level requirement to be met and allocated downward. 61B's stance: the FBL-versus-ABL label (or some mix of the two) does not matter, provided the documentation's change-control rules are spelled out clearly (Section 5.5.2.5).

- **Control authority is a separate variable from the baseline itself.** Under MIL-STD-973 and earlier standards, the major baselines all signaled departure points for Government configuration control. 61B redefines them for the post-acquisition-reform world: the baseline's purpose is unchanged, but who approves changes (Government or contractor) is now treated as an independent choice driven by acquisition approach, phase, support, and interface needs (Sections 5.5.2, 5.5.2.4).

- **Control to the level necessary, and no further.** The recurring discipline is to apply Government control only to the level needed for effective control and let contractor control operate below that (Section 5.5.2.4). The same minimalism appears in interface management: choose the simplest approach that meets the objective, and reserve elaborate formal interface activity for cases where simpler methods are inappropriate (Section 5.8.1.1).

- **Serial number persists; part number reflects change.** A unit keeps its identity (serial number) across its life even as its configuration — and therefore its part-identifying number — changes (Section 5.6.2). This separation is what lets effectivity be expressed as a range of serial numbers and lets maintenance and modification actions be tracked back to a specific physical unit.

- **The digital thread as one continuous chain.** In an automated environment, an item is designed at the engineer's workstation, manufacturing instructions are added at the planner's workstation, and the result feeds directly to the machinery that produces it (Section 5.4.2.2). A drawing becomes just a two-dimensional view of a model living in a database file (Section 5.4.2.1). The handbook stresses that hardware and software documentation management are fundamentally the same process — the same business rules for identification and relationships apply to both software code and documents (Section 5.4.2.3).

## Anti-patterns

61B names a few practices to avoid within this section:

- **Redundant configuration documentation.** Redundant documentation should be avoided because it creates the possibility of conflicts between levels of configuration documentation (Section 5.5.2.10).
- **Extravagant or overly complex interface management.** Complex interface management activity should be undertaken only when simpler methods are inappropriate; the simplest approach that satisfies the objective should always be chosen (Section 5.8.1.1).
- **Unnecessary issuance of Government serial numbers.** Issuing Government serial numbers should be avoided where the contractor already has an acceptable process for assigning unique serial numbers (Section 5.6.2.1.4.1).

## Key Takeaways

- Configuration identification is the prerequisite for all other CM activities; if it is weak, control, status accounting, and audit cannot function, and downstream cost and schedule suffer.
- The basic principles come from SAE EIA-649 and are independent of acquisition method; acquisition strategy influences which documents the Government controls and who approves changes, but not the necessity of applying the principles.
- A CI is the unit of CM. Defining and controlling system performance does not require designating every component as a CI — control reaches a lower level only when a change there would affect a Government-baselined CI specification.
- CI selection is an early decision with lasting consequences; it sets the level of Government configuration control for the entire life cycle and feeds supportability planning during MSA/TMRR.
- Configuration documentation defines performance, functional, and physical attributes; all other product documentation derives from it. DoD's preference favors commercial products and performance specifications over detailed and program-unique ones.
- The three Government baselines (FBL/ABL/PBL, backed by FCD/ACD/PCD) plus the contractor's developmental configuration are tied to life-cycle milestones; their control authority is now an independent variable, and their documents follow a strict FCD→ACD→PCD order of precedence.
- Identifiers (CAGE code, document type, document identifier for documents; nomenclature, CAGE code, part/serial/lot numbers for items) make the configuration traceable and supportable for the life of the fielded system; serial numbers persist while part numbers change with non-interchangeable conditions.
- Engineering release makes documentation usable and brings it under control; release tied to a Class I change must wait for Government approval, and release records form the audit trail tying documentation to delivered units.
- Interface management protects co-functioning boundaries; categorize each interface, document it, and apply the least elaborate method that works, escalating to an ICWG only when engineer-to-engineer resolution fails.

## Connects To

- **Configuration control** — Identification produces the baselines and documentation that control acts upon; 61B notes good control procedures are what preserve identification integrity, and the Class I change rule in engineering release is the explicit handoff point between the two activities.
- **Configuration status accounting (CSA)** — The audit trail from a CI's original requirements through each incremental baseline is maintained as part of CSA, and engineering release records feed status reporting.
- **Configuration audit** — Audits validate the as-built/as-documented configuration; without correct identification they are impossible, and the PBL is the documentation an audit verifies against (with the noted exception for CIs that already have an established PBL).
- **Systems engineering reviews** — Accounting for all top-level requirements in lower-level documents is described as a key function of system, preliminary, and critical design reviews, greatly aided by automated requirements allocation and traceability tools (Section 5.5.2.10).
- **Acquisition program structure** — The APB (DoDI 5000.2, Milestones A/B/C) relates closely to the configuration baselines, with performance thresholds expected to flow into the FBL specification.
- **Companion standards** — SAE EIA-649 (principles), GEIA-HB-649 (templates and guidance), MIL-STD-961 appendix A (specification structure), MIL-STD-31000 (drawings/models as deliverables), and the IPT/ICWG constructs for interface resolution.
