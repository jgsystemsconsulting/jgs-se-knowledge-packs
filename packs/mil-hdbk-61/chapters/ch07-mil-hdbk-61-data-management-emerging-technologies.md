# Chapter 7: Data Management and Emerging Technologies — Acquiring, Owning, and Controlling Data in the Digital Age

> Source: *MIL-HDBK-61B, Configuration Management Guidance* (DoD; 61B dated 7 April 2020, cataloged with Change 1, 15 Aug 2025), §9 Data Management and §10 Emerging Technologies. Use the current **61B**, not the 2001-era 61A(SE) — the digital-engineering, digital-twin, and MOSA material in this chapter exists only in the revised handbook and has no analog in 61A. The handbook now defers low-level execution detail to the industry companion SAE-GEIA-HB0649; treat 61B as the policy-and-principle layer above it.

## Core Idea
Configuration Management tells you how to control a configuration once you possess the data that describes it. Data Management (DM) is the parallel discipline that answers the questions CM assumes away: which data is actually needed, how to order it under contract, what rights the Government holds to use it, and how to store and share it across a system's life. The handbook frames DM as life-cycle stewardship of *all* product-related data — digital and paper, design and non-design — while CM is the narrower control of the subset that defines an item's functional or physical configuration. The chapter then projects both disciplines forward into the digital era: as the DoD shifts from document-based practice to model-based engineering, digital twins, and a Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA), the underlying CM principles do not weaken — if anything the need for them grows, because the artifacts under control multiply into models, viewpoints, datasets, algorithms, and "as-built"/"as-maintained" configurations that older practice never captured.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **Data Management (DM)** — the discipline covering data requirements determination, data creation (generator view), data acquisition (customer view), data/IP-rights determination, data delivery, and the storage, management, and use of data, across both digital and paper media regardless of purpose (§9.1). *When/how:* applied across the whole life cycle by the PM and functional-area IPTs; runs parallel to CM but spans more than the configuration-defining subset.
- **GEIA-859** — cited as the reference for DM principles, methods, and tools in the figure note on DM life-cycle costs (§9.3.1).
- **DoD 5010.12-M** — cited twice: as the detailed reference for data requirements determination (§9.3.1.2) and for the use of Data Item Descriptions and CDRLs (§9.3.2); section C3.3 is referenced again under data access vs. delivery (§9.3.4).
- **Data Item Descriptions (DID) and Contract Data Requirements Lists (CDRL)** — the policy-directed mechanism for ordering technical data in contracts; each data item ideally gets its own CDRL line and DID, each CDRL tied to one or more SOW work tasks that generate the deliverable (§9.3.2). Existing DIDs live in the **ASSIST** system.
- **DFARS clause 252.227-7017** — used in the RFP to require bidders to disclose, in their proposals, any restrictions on the Government's use of CDRL-ordered data (§9.3.3.b).
- **DFARS Parts 27 and 52** — define the rules for Government rights to use technical data and computer software in DoD contracts (§9.3.3 reference); **DFARS Part 227** clauses govern data-rights assertions, markings, and justifications on deliverable data (§9.3.4.f); **DFARS 215.470(b)** governs use of the DD Form 1423 CDRL (§9.3.4 reference).
- **Paperwork Reduction Act (44 U.S.C. 3512)** — cited for the rule that an enforceable right to access/copy data requires an OMB-approved DID or a FAR/DFARS clause (§9.3.4.e and reference).
- **Product Data Management (PDM) systems** — Government-operated IT systems with CM functionality used to store and manage received data; the DAG Technical Data Management section is the reference (§9.3.5, §9.3.5.1).
- **DAG (Defense Acquisition Guidebook), Technical Data Management section** — referenced for data storage, DM-and-use, and master-source topics (§9.3.5.1, §9.3.6, §9.3.7); the COTS chapters of the DAG are referenced for MOSA (§10.6.2.a).
- **DoD Digital Engineering Strategy** — the policy basis for the master-data / authoritative-source guidance and for the model-based transformation in §10 (§9.3.7, §10.2).
- **Systems Modeling Language (SysML)** — named as the example notation whose diagrams (parametric, requirements, etc.) illustrate the "viewpoint" concept in a digital environment (§10.3.2).
- **IEEE 828** — referenced for CM of digital artifacts (§10.4).
- **STAR JPSS Algorithms Integration Team Configuration Management Plan (NOAA NESDIS STAR)** — referenced as the example for CM of processes, algorithms, and computations (§10.5).
- **MOSA (Modular Open Systems Approach)** — the source describes it as a combined business-and-engineering strategy aimed at keeping the buying and supporting of a system (new or legacy — or just one component of it) competitive and cost-effective across its life, by structuring the design so its major functions and elements talk to each other through verifiable, consensus-based standard interfaces (§10.6.1).
- **DI-MGMT-82099** — cited for details on the open systems management plan (§10.6.2.e).

## Key Concepts
- **DM is the discipline CM presupposes.** Most CM guidance starts from data already in the user's hands and explains how to control item configurations with it; it is silent on how that data got there or what rights attach to it. DM fills those gaps — deciding what CM data is needed, acquiring it from OEMs through contracts, confirming the IP rights for Government use, and arranging storage and authorized use. The handbook stresses that not all data is placed under CM control, but all product-related data must still be determined, acquired, stored, and made available (§9.2).
- **The three DM cost drivers.** Within DoD, weak DM shows up three ways, each of which raises life-cycle acquisition and sustainment cost (§9.3.1): (1) failing to acquire the data and data rights needed for material life assessment or disposal; (2) over-ordering data, or mis-ordering it because the true need and intended use were never understood; and (3) being unable to reach data the program needs, especially when the OEM physically holds it.
- **Determine needs broadly, then de-duplicate.** Key DM activities affecting product CM are data requirements determination (every functional area identifies what its practitioners need across development, test, manufacture, acquisition, logistics, maintenance, operation, and disposal), the data call (soliciting those inputs), and the data requirements review (consolidating, removing duplicates, validating need). The need is almost always broader than the traditional TDP (§9.3.1.1–§9.3.1.2).
- **Form and format matter as much as content.** Most data is now digital, so the program must decide on 2D vs. 3D representations and on formats its own authoring tools can actually open. Because each 3D CAD tool emits its own usually proprietary format, the Government must hold the right authoring software (native or translated) to open, read, and manipulate models and other simulations (§9.3.1.2.c).
- **Ordering data ≠ owning the right to use it.** With DoD favoring insertion of commercial and privately developed technology, much data is the OEM's IP. Ordering it in the contract does not by itself grant usage rights; bidders must be made to disclose any use restrictions before award so gaps between needed and offered rights can be resolved (§9.3.3).
- **The standard data-rights ladder.** Two categories carry **unlimited rights** to the Government from the outset — the form/fit/function (FFF) data, and the operation, maintenance, installation, and training (OMIT) data. For other non-commercial data, the funding source sets the default: **unlimited rights** for 100% Government-funded work, **Government purpose rights** for mixed funding, and **limited or restricted rights** for solely private funding. Rights above these defaults can be negotiated with the OEM (§9.3.3.c–§9.3.3.e).
- **Access is not delivery.** A 1990s cost-saving idea — pay nothing for delivery or rights and instead get "access" to data in the contractor's systems — is rejected. Delivery means the customer takes possession; access only reaches data stored on the contractor's side. Contractually required access (formal delivery) still flows through a CDRL and DID; informal technical-liaison access is not delivery and confers no usable rights, because copyrights and proprietary markings bar DoD use absent explicit grant and legal review. Acceptance review must check content, format, *and* rights markings together (§9.3.4).
- **Hold the official copy in a Government system.** Received data should be stored in a Government-operated PDM-type system with CM functionality. The handbook rejects the "let the OEM store it cheaper" pitch on three grounds: contractor storage is not actually free and usually costs more; data and rights markings held only on the contractor's side can be altered without safeguard; and if the storage contract ends, the Government may have little time or ability to move a very large dataset to an organic system (§9.3.5).
- **One master, known and controlled.** With copies inevitably proliferating across IT systems, the program must designate which instance is the **master** / authoritative source, place it under configuration control, and notify copy-owners of any master change so they can assess impact. The same discipline applies to **derived** data (e.g., provisioning lists built from drawings) and **transformed** data (e.g., a 2D drawing generated from a 3D CAD model), which must be regenerated and kept in sync when the master changes (§9.3.7).
- **Data is a corporate asset, not a program asset.** Historically PMs and IPTs guarded data, sometimes withholding it even from downstream logisticians. The handbook insists product technical data be treated as a DoD corporate asset and shared (with approved access) across all functional areas — a single source of truth that prevents duplicate searching and recreation and enables reuse (§9.3.6).
- **The model-based paradigm shift.** The DoD is moving from document-based practice to model-based engineering, where information-rich integrated models — not separate domain documents — carry information across the life cycle. This shift brings new software, far larger data volumes, and new CM challenges, and demands a consolidated, collaborative, trusted, broadly accessible IT environment (§10.2–§10.3).
- **What CM must control in a digital environment.** Three new object classes come under CM: (a) models, viewpoints, and datasets; (b) digital artifacts; and (c) processes, algorithms, and computations (§10.3.1). A **viewpoint** is the location of data in a model used to produce an artifact (in SysML, the data in a given diagram); keeping viewpoints in the digital environment requires CM controls to preserve dataset continuity (§10.3.2).
- **Digital twins extend CM to as-built and as-maintained.** A digital twin uses best-available models, sensor data, and inputs to mirror and predict a physical instance over its life. From a CM view this means controlling not just the "as designed" baseline but the **as-built** configuration of each unit leaving the line and the **as-maintained** configuration as field modifications and approved ECPs are incorporated — voluminous data not historically ordered or captured except in cases like airworthiness and flight safety (§10.3.5).
- **Static artifacts must trace back to the baseline.** Digital artifacts (any digital item forming part of a system's digital representation) are often used at reviews and milestones. Static artifacts extracted from the digital environment raise CM difficulty; approved changes must be applied to the baseline digital environment so regenerated artifacts reflect the approved configuration, with CM ensuring traceability between baseline model and static artifact (§10.4).
- **Reuse libraries need version control too.** Processes, algorithms, and computations can be reused to boost productivity, and model libraries can become reusable catalogs that bind configuration complexity. Software tools handle version control, parallel development, workspace and build management, and process configurability so reuse improves quality without introducing errors (§10.5).
- **MOSA couples technical modularity with open business practice.** MOSA addresses emerging threats, technology change, obsolescence, sustainment, and security by separating the system into standards-based, verifiable modules. CM controls MOSA CI form, fit, function, and interface characteristics; technical baselines for modular architectures are placed under configuration control; and interface specifications, standards, and control drawings must be confirmed early and held under strict control (§10.6.1, §10.6.4.a–b, §10.6.4.1).
- **COTS in MOSA is a managed risk.** COTS/NDI offers development efficiency but introduces IP-rights, single-source, and obsolescence concerns; if a vendor stops production or changes configuration drastically, the program may have to maintain multiple configurations of one product. CM provides awareness of COTS upgrades and changes, MOSA should be a proposal evaluation criterion, and RFP language should secure the data and rights the MOSA strategy needs (§10.6.2).
- **Interfaces and dependencies are the heart of MOSA CM/DM.** Standards-based interfaces let elements evolve separately with minimal cross-impact. All external interfaces and dependencies must be documented and controlled; interface management is integral to CM and critical to integration. Unmonitored interdependencies across systems, the system of systems, and constituent systems sharing data sources create unmanaged CM/DM risk — so related activities include obtaining and securing standards, gaining data access, managing standards documentation and licenses, securing data rights, and arranging storage/control/exchange (§10.6.4–§10.6.4.3).

## Mental Models
- **DM is the supply chain; CM is the warehouse.** CM guidance describes how to run the warehouse once stock arrives; DM is everything upstream and around it — deciding what to order, getting it on contract with the right rights, receiving and inspecting it, and shelving the official copy where it can be found and reused. Skip DM and CM has nothing legitimate to control.
- **"Ordering ≠ owning ≠ holding."** Three separate gates: ordering data in a CDRL gets it generated and delivered; data *rights* (set by FFF/OMIT category or by funding source) decide whether the Government may actually use it; and physical possession of the official copy in a Government PDM system decides whether anyone can later tamper with the data or its markings. Pass only one gate and the program is exposed.
- **Access-for-delivery is a trap that looks like a saving.** Not ordering data avoids delivery and rights costs — and that is exactly why it fails: no order means no enforceable right and no guaranteed availability. The handbook's blunt instruction is to never substitute access for delivery.
- **The CM principle is constant; only the artifacts change.** Moving from paper to models, twins, and algorithms does not retire CM — it widens its scope. The same need to identify a master, control change, and trace it now applies to viewpoints, datasets, as-built/as-maintained configurations, and reuse libraries.
- **A digital twin demands the configuration of every individual unit.** Design-baseline control was enough for documents; a twin that predicts a specific physical instance needs that instance's as-built and as-maintained configuration — a data class most programs never ordered, now newly required to realize digital-thread benefits.
- **MOSA's openness lives or dies at the interface.** Modularity only delivers competition and tech-refresh if the interfaces are consensus-based, verifiable, documented, and held under strict configuration control; an uncontrolled or undocumented interface quietly recreates the lock-in MOSA was meant to break.

## Anti-patterns
- **Substituting access for delivery.** The handbook states the rule in the strongest terms — do not substitute access for delivery — because informally accessed data carries no usable Government rights and no guaranteed availability, leaving the program dependent on the contractor's discretion (§9.3.4.h, §9.3.4.g).
- **Asking for too much (or the wrong) data.** Over-ordering, driven by not understanding the true need or intended use, is named as one of the three DM failure modes that raise life-cycle cost — the data requirements review exists to strip duplicates and validate need (§9.3.1, §9.3.1.1.c).
- **Letting the OEM be the sole data store.** Relying on contractor-only storage is rejected: it is not free and usually costs more, the data and its rights markings can be altered without safeguard, and recovering a large dataset when the contract ends is a major, time-pressured undertaking (§9.3.5).
- **Treating technical data as a program asset to be hoarded.** Territorial control that denies downstream and cross-program access is called out as wasteful; data should be a shared DoD corporate asset / single source of truth (§9.3.6).
- **Redundant, non-interoperable PDM systems.** Operating multiple similar PDM-type systems — worse, letting OEMs hold data in their own systems — drives up cost, violates DoD CIO portfolio-management guidance on eliminating duplicative IT, and blocks the digital-engineering goal of integrating data across systems (§9.3.5.1).

## Key Takeaways
1. **DM and CM are parallel but distinct.** CM controls the configuration-defining subset of data; DM governs the full life cycle of *all* product-related data — what to acquire, the rights to use it, and how to store and share it. CM assumes the data; DM provides it (§9.1–§9.2).
2. **Avoid the three DM cost drivers** — under-acquiring needed data/rights, over- or mis-ordering data, and being unable to reach OEM-held data — by determining needs across every functional area and de-duplicating in a requirements review (§9.3.1).
3. **Order data through CDRLs and DIDs tied to SOW tasks**, and remember that ordering alone does not grant usage rights — make bidders disclose restrictions (DFARS 252.227-7017) before award (§9.3.2–§9.3.3).
4. **Know the rights ladder:** FFF and OMIT data carry unlimited rights automatically; otherwise the funding source sets the default — unlimited (100% Government), Government purpose (mixed), or limited/restricted (private) — with more available by negotiation (§9.3.3).
5. **Never substitute access for delivery,** and hold the official copy in a Government PDM-type system; accept deliverables only when content, format, and rights markings all comply (§9.3.4–§9.3.5).
6. **Designate and control a single master / authoritative source,** keep derived and transformed data in sync with it, and treat technical data as a shared DoD corporate asset rather than a guarded program asset (§9.3.6–§9.3.7).
7. **CM principles intensify in the digital era:** apply them to models, viewpoints, datasets, digital artifacts, and reuse libraries (IEEE 828), and extend configuration capture to as-built and as-maintained instances to enable digital twins and the digital thread (§10.3–§10.5).
8. **Implement MOSA with controlled, consensus-based interfaces** — put modular technical baselines and interface documents under strict configuration control, evaluate MOSA in proposals, secure COTS data and rights, and manage cross-system interdependencies as a shared CM/DM responsibility (§10.6).
