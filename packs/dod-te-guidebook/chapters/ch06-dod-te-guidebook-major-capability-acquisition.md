# Chapter — T&E in the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) Pathway

## Core Idea

The Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) pathway is the deliberate, full-rigor route the DoD uses for its largest and most consequential programs — Major Defense Acquisition Programs, other Acquisition Category (ACAT) I efforts, the major systems typically grouped as ACAT II, automated information systems not handled by another pathway, and any other capability routed through MCA. It is governed by DoDI 5000.85 (which DoDI 5000.02 establishes) and supported on the engineering and test sides by DoDI 5000.88 and DoDI 5000.89. Where the Urgent Capability pathway bends T&E to a two-year clock, MCA does the opposite: it gives test and evaluation a seat at every milestone and asks it to build evidence steadily across five sequential phases. The organizing argument of the chapter is that T&E earns its value by being engaged from the program's inception — at the Materiel Development Decision — and staying involved through disposal, so that each milestone decision is informed by credible data about whether the system is technically mature, operationally effective, suitable, survivable, and lethal. The recurring instruction is to integrate contractor, developmental, live fire, and operational testing into one shared body of evidence — maximizing reuse, reciprocity, and data sharing — rather than letting each test community generate its own siloed results. The Test and Evaluation Master Plan (TEMP), refreshed at each milestone, is the spine that holds this together, and the T&E Working-level Integrated Product Team (WIPT) is the standing body that keeps it coherent.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) Pathway** — the five-phase deliberate model: Materiel Solution Analysis, Technology Maturation and Risk Reduction (TMRR), Engineering and Manufacturing Development (EMD), Production and Deployment (P&D), and Operations and Support (O&S). Drawn from DoDI 5000.85; each phase is gated by a milestone or decision review.
- **Test and Evaluation Master Plan (TEMP)** — the central T&E deliverable in every phase. It is an agreement between the PM and all T&E stakeholders describing roles, responsibilities, resources, and the data-driven processes for verifying technical requirements and evaluating operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality. Generated by the PM with T&E WIPT support; produced in versions tied to Milestones A, B, C, and Full-Rate Production.
- **T&E Working-level Integrated Product Team (T&E WIPT)** — chartered by the PM during Materiel Solution Analysis; coordinates top-level T&E planning, defines data requirements and resources, ensures requirement testability, and seats representatives from systems engineering; from each of DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E; from the user and product-support communities; from the Intelligence Community; and from whichever certification authorities apply.
- **Integrated Decision Support Key (IDSK)** — required inside the TEMP by DoDI 5000.89; a table linking acquisition, technical, and program decisions to the contractor test (CT), developmental test (DT), live fire test (LFT), operational test (OT), and M&S data needed to support them. It is meant to evolve across the lifecycle and to let test events build on one another.
- **DT&E, OT&E, LFT&E, CT** — government developmental test and evaluation, operational test and evaluation, live fire test and evaluation, and contractor testing, all to be integrated and streamlined.
- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA)** — required on MDAPs (since 2017) before Milestone A, Milestone B, and any decision to enter low-rate or full-rate production. Conducted by USD(R&E) or the component; gives senior leaders an independent view of technical risk and recommended mitigations. Governed by DoDI 5000.88. T&E professionals are integral team members.
- **Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA)** — a systematic, metrics-based assessment of the maturity and risk of critical technologies, based on objective evidence from tests, demonstrations, pilots, or physics-based simulations. Where an ITRA is conducted, its report subsumes the TRA findings so a separate TRA report is not required.
- **DT&E Sufficiency Assessment (DTSA)** — conducted by USD(R&E) (per 10 U.S.C. §4252 at Milestone B and §4253 at Milestone C) when USD(A&S) is the MDA, assessing the sufficiency of DT&E plans, schedule, resources, concurrency-risk mitigation, and production entrance criteria. Findings feed the Brief Summary Report to the congressional defense committees. Appendix B provides Milestone B and C memorandum templates.
- **DT&E Program Assessment (DTA)** — provided by USD(R&E) to the MDA at the Development RFP Release decision / Milestone B and again at Milestone C for programs on DT oversight, evaluating technical performance, demonstrated capabilities, integration maturity, sustainment, and survivability.
- **Preliminary Design Review (PDR) and Critical Design Review (CDR)** — technical reviews that establish baselines and assess design maturity and risk; PDR is T&E's first close look at the contractor's design, and CDR certifies the design is mature enough to begin hardware fabrication with acceptable risk.
- **Early Operational Assessment (EOA) and Operational Assessment (OA)** — analyses and test events run by the OTA to give early operational feedback during TMRR and EMD; at least one OA is the general criterion for proceeding through Milestone C.
- **Initial Operational Test and Evaluation (IOT&E)** — the statutory test event mandated by 10 U.S.C. §4171, requiring production-representative systems, operationally realistic conditions, end-to-end testing, and an independent evaluation of operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality.
- **Full-Up System-Level (FUSL) testing and the FUSL waiver** — grounded in 10 U.S.C. §4172 for realistic survivability and lethality testing, with a defined waiver mechanism that still leaves LFT&E itself non-waivable.
- **Follow-on Operational Test and Evaluation (FOT&E)** — post-IOT&E testing in the O&S phase to confirm deficiency corrections, evaluate untested aspects, and assess modifications and new increments.
- **Validated Online Life Cycle Threat (VOLT)** — the authoritative, system-specific threat assessment built from threat modules; required as a unique report for MDAPs and oversight-list programs, and used by testers to define the threat environment and build scenarios.
- **Supporting planning documents** — Capability Development Document (CDD), Acquisition Strategy, Systems Engineering Plan (SEP), Intellectual Property (IP) Strategy, Cost Estimate, and Request for Proposal (RFP), each carrying T&E content the WIPT must shape.
- **Shared data repository / shared body of evidence** and **collaborative test data scoring boards** — mechanisms for storing test data centrally and judging whether existing data can satisfy IOT&E or LFT&E requirements.
- **Acquisition Decision Memorandum (ADM)** and **Early Fielding report** — the decision record documenting LRIP quantities and long-lead items, and the DOT&E report issued if fielding or procurement funding is released before IOT&E completes.

## Key Concepts

**Five phases, four milestone TEMPs.** The pathway moves Materiel Solution Analysis → TMRR → EMD → P&D → O&S, with the TEMP regenerated and expanded at each gate. The Milestone A TEMP plans the major T&E resources against the draft CDD (or ICD), CONOPS/OMS/MP, and VOLT threat. Each later TEMP — Milestone B, Milestone C, and Full-Rate Production — tunes three things (the IDSK, the evaluation framework, and how high-fidelity the test and M&S events are) so as to build on the prior phase's work rather than starting fresh.

**Engage T&E at inception.** The PM should pull T&E organizations in at the Materiel Development Decision and keep them involved across the whole lifecycle. Government test teams enter early in Materiel Solution Analysis to document how testing will demonstrate the required technology maturity and assess engineering, integration, and lifecycle-cost risk. The same "involve them early" instruction repeats at the entry to TMRR and EMD.

**The T&E WIPT as integrator.** The WIPT coordinates the integrated schedule — which must allow time to fix deficiencies found in test and to run the associated analysis and reporting — and ensures T&E requirements land in RFPs and contracts so the government actually gets the data it needs. It also pushes into requirements refinement (ICD/CDD) to confirm measurability, testability, achievability, and operational relevance, with the PM helping reconcile any requirement found untestable.

**Embed OT&E early.** Pulling operational testing forward requires OT&E to be aware of and participating in systems engineering and development from the start — monitoring developmental tests, understanding and trusting the pedigree of DT results, mapping which prior data can serve operational evaluation, and identifying gaps that shape future test planning. Early operational realism, introduced through integrated testing, raises the odds of catching and correcting problems while fixes are still cheap, without replacing dedicated DT&E, OT&E, or LFT&E.

**The IDSK as the integration engine.** The IDSK frames how individual test events stack to serve multiple stakeholders' evaluations at once, producing efficiencies and binding DT, CT, and OT together. It is explicitly meant to evolve through the lifecycle and to insert operational realism — mission environments, operational users — as early as feasible.

**TEMP approval authorities and timing.** For programs on T&E oversight, DOT&E is the final TEMP approver, and the TEMP is due to it at least 45 calendar days ahead of the decision point it supports. USD(R&E) approves the DT&E plan within the TEMP for all ACAT ID programs and reviews/advises the MDA on it for ACAT IB and IC programs. The TEMP is a living document, updated as data accumulate and milestones pass.

**T&E resources are planned, verified, and validated.** The TEMP documents the full resource set — test articles and threats; facilities, ranges (including cyber ranges), and software integration labs; automated test tools; M&S with verification-and-validation plans; manpower; regulatory and range requirements; data repositories; and funding. Programs identify one-of-a-kind resources and long-lead items early. Government T&E capabilities are the default; using non-government facilities requires a cost-benefit justification approved through the TEMP process. Per Section 839(b) of Public Law 115-91, the TEMP must carry a table of DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E resource estimates, refreshed at every TEMP update.

**T&E content threads through every planning document.** Beyond the TEMP, the WIPT shapes the CDD (clear, testable, traceable requirements; cyber and interoperability defined), the Acquisition Strategy (time for independent government T&E and for fixing deficiencies), the SEP (T&E as a core SE element, with TRA/PDR/CDR/ITRA feeding knowledge-based decisions), the VOLT (threat reference for plans and scenarios), the IP Strategy (data rights to build a shared body of evidence and support independent testing), the Cost Estimate (full T&E resourcing plus deficiency-mitigation funds), and the RFP (the TEMP is a source document and should be attached so contractors know what will be tested).

**The risk-assessment stack: ITRA, TRA, DTSA, DTA.** MCA layers several independent assessments. The ITRA gives the MDA a technical-risk picture at Milestones A and B and at LRIP and FRP entry, covering mission capability, technology, system development, MOSA, software, security, manufacturing, sustainment, and testing adequacy. The TRA grades critical-technology maturity on objective evidence. The DTSA (Milestone B and C) and DTA (Milestone B and C) are the DT&E-specific judgments USD(R&E) renders on the adequacy and sufficiency of planned and completed developmental testing, with results reported to Congress.

**LRIP quantities and the 10-percent rule.** The Milestone C decision authorizes entry into P&D and LRIP. The ADM documents LRIP quantities needed for T&E and any long-lead items. For oversight programs, LRIP test assets are coordinated with DOT&E; for non-oversight programs, the OTA — after consulting the PM — fixes how many test articles IOT&E needs (10 U.S.C. §4231). The LRIP quantity is stated in the first Selected Acquisition Report sent to Congress, and a rationale is required whenever that quantity tops 10 percent of the total planned production.

**IOT&E is statutory and uncompromising.** Mandated by 10 U.S.C. §4171, IOT&E demands production or production-representative articles, operationally realistic units, users, missions, threats, environments, and maintenance, adequate and realistically sized resources, and a strategy to mitigate known limitations. It must be more than modeling, simulation, or document analysis — it requires end-to-end testing with all interrelated systems, run by typical trained users under combat-stress conditions. Properly qualified integrated data from EMD may satisfy some IOT&E requirements with DOT&E (or OTA) approval. Programs are expected to finish planned testing and fix critical deficiencies before entering IOT&E.

**Realistic survivability and lethality, and the FUSL waiver.** FUSL testing satisfies 10 U.S.C. §4172's realistic-survivability and realistic-lethality requirements — adversary-representative threats fired at a combat-equipped, production-representative system, and the production-representative weapon engaged against combat-configured targets. LFT&E runs across the whole program, from component-level testing in the initial design stage up to FUSL, and tests must occur early enough to correct design deficiencies before going beyond LRIP. There is no waiver from LFT&E itself, only from the FUSL requirement: the PEO memorializes that the §4172 tests would be unreasonably expensive and impractical, the SAE forwards a similar request to USD(A&S) as the Defense Acquisition Executive (who must approve even when authority is delegated), and DOT&E certifies the TEMP's alternative live fire approach is adequate. The package sent to the congressional defense committees has two parts: a certification that the waiver is needed and an alternative LFT&E plan.

**Production-phase test variety.** The P&D phase runs First Article Testing (production-process and environmental-stress effects), Acceptance Testing (each unit functions, the point where the government takes ownership and warranty may begin), Production Qualification Tests (post-Milestone C confirmation that manufacturing processes, equipment, and procedures are effective, repeated on design or source change), IOT&E, and FUSL. FAT and AT are typically run by PMO personnel or the contractor under government-approved plans and government oversight at the contractor's facility.

**Reporting and the Early Fielding report.** For oversight programs, DOT&E delivers an IOT&E report — to the MDA, to the Secretary of Defense, and to Congress — carrying an independent assessment of how adequate the test was and of the system's operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality. If a program proceeds to operational use or releases procurement funds before IOT&E finishes, DOT&E submits an Early Fielding report instead, as soon as practicable.

**The lifecycle does not end at FRP.** O&S executes the product-support strategy and sustains the system; the PM documents performance and sustainment requirements in user agreements with measurable outcomes, and the Services review sustainment strategies against actual performance. Modifications trigger TEMP updates so their operational effect can be assessed, and FOT&E confirms deficiency corrections, evaluates previously untested aspects, and assesses modifications, new platforms, new tactics, and new threats — eventually closing with demilitarization and disposal.

## Mental Models

- **Test to the decision, milestone by milestone.** The IDSK ties every test event to a specific acquisition, technical, or program decision, and each TEMP version is built to inform the next milestone. The mental frame is a chain of evidence-fed decision gates (A → B → C → FRP), not a one-shot test campaign.
- **One shared body of evidence, many evaluators.** Stand up a shared data repository at program initiation and keep adding relevant data through every phase, so DT, OT, and LFT draw from the same well. Collaborative test data scoring boards judge whether existing integrated data can meet IOT&E or LFT&E objectives — reuse is the efficiency lever, and the OTA holds the authoritative record of integrated-test data authenticated for operational use.
- **Build forward, never restart.** Each TEMP "expands on and updates" the previous one, adapting the IDSK, evaluation framework, and M&S fidelity to leverage prior testing. The model is an accreting baseline, not a fresh plan at every gate.
- **Realism early, redesign cheap.** Inject operational realism as early as integrated testing allows, because problems caught early are far cheaper to correct than redesigns late in development — and some late fixes are simply infeasible.
- **Independent assessment is a feature, not a tax.** ITRA, TRA, DTSA, DTA, EOA/OA, and IOT&E are deliberately independent looks layered onto the program, each surfacing risk to a decision authority before commitment grows — the knowledge-based acquisition idea made concrete.
- **The contract is where data access is won or lost.** If T&E expectations are not explicit in the RFP and contract — government access to contractor events, tools, repositories, environments, M&S deliverables, plans, and data — the data may simply never arrive, raising risk to the whole T&E program. Get it into the CDRL and the deliverables list early.

## Anti-patterns

- **Leaving T&E expectations out of the RFP/contract.** The chapter names this directly: where the RFP and the acquisition contract fail to spell out the T&E expectations, the data the testers need may never be delivered — which raises risk to the test program and, potentially, to the program's cost and schedule.
- **Compressing T&E without characterizing the loss.** When schedule slips push the PM to compress the integrated test schedule in the TEMP, the chapter warns that testers must characterize the risk of not obtaining the information that the LFT&E strategy — together with the developmental and the operational evaluation frameworks — was meant to deliver. Compression is allowed, in short, only with eyes open to what evidence is being given up.
- **Treating M&S as a substitute for operational testing.** IOT&E explicitly requires more than computer modeling, simulation, or analysis of requirements, proposals, and specifications — an evaluation resting on those alone does not satisfy the statute.

## Key Takeaways

- MCA is the deliberate, five-phase pathway (Materiel Solution Analysis, TMRR, EMD, P&D, O&S) for the DoD's largest programs, governed by DoDI 5000.85 and supported by DoDI 5000.88 and 5000.89, with a milestone-gated TEMP at A, B, C, and FRP.
- T&E engages at the Materiel Development Decision and runs through disposal; the PM charters the T&E WIPT in Materiel Solution Analysis, and OT&E participation begins that early too.
- The IDSK, required in the TEMP by DoDI 5000.89, binds CT/DT/LFT/OT/M&S data to specific decisions and is the chapter's central integration mechanism, evolving across the lifecycle.
- An independent-assessment stack — ITRA (Milestones A/B, LRIP, FRP), TRA, DTSA, and DTA — feeds technical-risk and DT-sufficiency judgments to the MDA and Congress, with Appendix B providing Milestone B and C DTSA memorandum templates.
- TEMP approval rests with DOT&E for oversight programs (due 45 days before the decision point), while USD(R&E) approves or advises on the DT&E plan depending on ACAT category.
- IOT&E is statutorily mandated (10 U.S.C. §4171), demanding production-representative articles and end-to-end realistic testing; properly qualified EMD integrated data may cover some requirements with DOT&E/OTA approval.
- LFT&E is non-waivable; only FUSL/full-up testing can be waived under 10 U.S.C. §4172, via a PEO-to-SAE-to-USD(A&S) chain with DOT&E certification and a two-part congressional package (certification plus alternative LFT&E plan).
- Data access is contractual: the RFP and CDRL must specify government access to contractor test events, tools, repositories, environments, and M&S deliverables, or the data may never arrive.
- Reuse drives efficiency — a shared data repository, a shared body of evidence, and collaborative scoring boards let one dataset serve developmental, live fire, and operational evaluations.
- The lifecycle persists past FRP into O&S, where modifications drive TEMP updates and FOT&E evaluates corrections, new increments, and emerging threats before eventual disposal.

## Connects To

- **Urgent Capability Acquisition T&E** — MCA is the deliberate counterpart that UCA streamlines; the two pathways share the same building blocks (T&E WIPT, IDSK, FUSL waiver, shared body of evidence) but MCA applies them across five gated phases instead of a compressed two-year window.
- **DoDI 5000.89 and the wider T&E Enterprise Guidebook** — MCA TEMPs must align with DoDI 5000.89 and the relevant T&E focus-area chapters, so this chapter sits inside the enterprise framework rather than standing alone.
- **Live Fire Test & Evaluation and the 10 U.S.C. §4172 regime** — the FUSL waiver chain, realistic survivability/lethality definitions, and DOT&E's reporting role tie directly into the statutory LFT&E body of practice.
- **Operational Test & Evaluation and the §4171 IOT&E regime** — EOA, OA, IOT&E, the Early Fielding report, and FOT&E form the operational-evaluation arc that anchors the P&D and O&S phases.
- **Engineering of Defense Systems (DoDI 5000.88)** — the ITRA's responsibilities and criteria, and T&E's role at TRA/PDR/CDR, place this chapter alongside the systems-engineering technical-review and risk-assessment practice.
- **Integrated / Mission-Based Test Design and the Evaluation Framework** — the IDSK, end-to-end mission threads, developmental and operational evaluation frameworks, and early operational realism connect to integrated-testing and evaluation-framework concepts shared across the T&E discipline.
- **Sustainment, Reliability, and Disposition** — the logistics risk assessment, product-support strategy, reliability and maintainability requirements, and demilitarization/disposal route tie MCA into lifecycle-management and product-support practice.
