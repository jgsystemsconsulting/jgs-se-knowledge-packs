# Chapter 1: T&E Fundamentals — Types of Test and Operational Events (DT&E, OT&E, OTRR, LFT&E)

## Core Idea
Test and evaluation (T&E) exists to give the DoD the data it needs to acquire systems that let warfighters accomplish their missions. T&E supplies evidence of technical, functional, and warfighting capability, and — just as important — surfaces system deficiencies while there is still time and money to fix them, before a final acquisition or fielding decision is locked in. The Guidebook frames the discipline around a small number of distinct test types, each with its own purpose, while pushing hard for those types to be planned and executed collaboratively so that one test event can feed several independent evaluations. Two factors most strongly determine whether a T&E program succeeds: a strategy that is adequately planned and resourced, and access to the contractor's data, tools, information, and expertise. Done well, T&E lets engineers and decision makers manage programmatic risk, track technical progress, and characterize a system's operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality as it moves through acquisition.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Enterprise T&E Guidebook (OUSD R&E)** — the governing source, written to help the acquisition and T&E communities comply with the T&E policy in **DoDI 5000.89** and run a rigorous program. When/how: it is structured in three parts — a T&E Overview (foundational material spanning all six pathways of the Adaptive Acquisition Framework), Acquisition Pathway Guidance (per-pathway activities), and Focus Areas (cross-cutting topics like cybersecurity and enablers such as T&E strategies). Where guidance and policy disagree, the reader is told to defer to policy.
- **DoD Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)** — the six-pathway acquisition structure (anchored in **DoDI 5000.02**) across which the T&E Overview applies. When/how: cited as the backdrop for all T&E types. The named pathways the Overview spans are Urgent Capability Acquisition; the Middle Tier; Major Capability Acquisition; the Software Acquisition pathway; and Defense Business Systems (with an Acquisition of Services pathway noted as still under review).
- **The four test "communities" / types** — Contractor Testing (CT), government Developmental Test & Evaluation (DT&E), Operational Test & Evaluation (OT&E), and Live Fire Test & Evaluation (LFT&E). When/how: introduced as the categories of T&E the Guidebook directs to be integrated, streamlined, and automated as far as is practical so data and resources can be reused across the whole program.
- **T&E Strategy** — the primary planning and management document for the integrated test program. When/how: developed by the T&E Working Integrated Product Team (WIPT) before testing begins on any pathway, it captures DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E data requirements, the rationale for them (e.g., JCIDS and the CONOPS), and the resources needed; it is approved by DOT&E and USD(R&E) (or their designees). The Guidebook accepts several interchangeable names for this one artifact — a "test strategy," a Test and Evaluation Master Plan (TEMP), a Simplified Acquisition Master Plan (SAMP), or simply the "T&E Strategy."
- **The six OT core principles (2019)** — created by the operational test community and endorsed by **DOT&E** to field more lethal, more resilient capability at the "speed of relevance." When/how: introduced in the OT&E section as six named principles — Early OT Involvement; Tailoring to the Situation; Continuous, Cumulative Feedback; Streamlined Processes and Products; Integrated, Combined Collection and Test; and an Adaptive posture.
- **Integrated Decision Support Key (IDSK)** and evaluation framework — the mapping device inside the T&E Strategy. When/how: it correlates decisions to be informed, the capabilities to be evaluated, the measures that quantify those capabilities, and the test and modeling-and-simulation (M&S) events that generate the data.
- **Five types of operational events** — Operational Demonstration, Early Operational Assessment (EOA), Operational Assessment (OA), Initial Operational Test & Evaluation (IOT&E), and Follow-on Operational Test & Evaluation (FOT&E). When/how: introduced as the categories operational testing can take, each with its own approval path and tailoring.
- **Operational Test Readiness Review (OTRR)** — the gate held before an operational event. When/how: introduced as the check on reliability, maintainability, supportability, and on whether safety and ESOH hazards are manageable before proceeding into OT&E.
- **Full-Up System-Level (FUSL) testing and its waiver** — the live-fire requirement under **10 U.S.C. §§ 4172(c)** and the statutory process to be relieved of it. When/how: introduced at the close of the LFT&E section as the one place the law allows relief — from FUSL specifically, never from LFT&E itself.

## Key Concepts
- **Test and evaluation are two acts, not one.** Both are needed to extract value from any effort: testing produces data under identified conditions (controlled or uncontrolled, measured or not), and evaluation interprets that data against criteria. A single test can support multiple evaluations.
- **Collaboration scopes events but does not replace them.** Shared planning lets stakeholders pull data from prior events into their own analyses, which can shrink the scope of a given event — but it does not let a program skip dedicated DT&E, OT&E, or LFT&E.
- **Operational realism, early.** Injecting operational realism early raises the odds of catching and fixing problems while redesign is still affordable; deferring discovery to late development can make a fix expensive or infeasible.
- **DT&E** is the disciplined generation of substantiated knowledge about the capabilities and limitations of systems, subsystems, components, software, and materiel. It assesses technology maturity, design, production readiness, government acceptance, readiness for operational testing, and sustainment. Its evaluation criteria come from the CONOPS/OMS/MP, capability gaps, capabilities documents (ICD, CDD, COIs, COIC), technical requirements documents, and contractual performance specs. Its long task list includes verifying critical technical parameters and KPPs, enabling root-cause analysis of failures, informing cost/performance/schedule tradeoffs, reporting reliability growth, stressing the system in an operationally relevant environment to gauge OT readiness, assessing entry criteria for IOT&E and FOT&E, validating M&S parameters, and finding cyber vulnerabilities early enough for the program office to mitigate them (including support to RMF security controls).
- **OT&E** evaluates how units equipped with the system perform under realistic operational conditions and a representative threat environment, to credibly judge operational effectiveness, suitability, and survivability (per 10 U.S.C. §§ 4171 and 4172 and DoDI 5000.89). It is conducted on all programs to support development and fielding decisions; capability upgrades that could materially change performance must also be tested.
- **The DOT&E gatekeeping role.** Programs under T&E oversight cannot conduct OT until DOT&E approves the plan's adequacy in writing. Before such a program may proceed beyond low-rate initial production (BLRIP), DOT&E must report to the Secretary of Defense and the congressional defense committees, stating whether the T&E was adequate and whether a system-equipped unit was operationally effective, suitable, and survivable for combat.
- **The five operational events form a maturity ladder.** An *operational demonstration* assesses technical maturity and interoperability in a threat-realistic setting and can inform transition decisions. An *EOA* is largely an analysis of plans, early DT data, technology assessments, M&S, and reviews (including PDR), giving the OTA an early read on operational risks. An *OA* runs with more operational realism and often serves as a risk-reduction event ahead of IOT&E, sometimes on pre-production articles. *IOT&E* — required by Title 10 for major systems to reach full-rate production — uses production or production-representative articles in a dedicated field test under realistic combat conditions, including relevant threats such as cyber, EMSO, kinetic, directed energy, and CBRN. *FOT&E* follows to verify fixes or evaluate modifications, tailored as appropriate.
- **OTRR** verifies the production configuration (usually the LRIP system) is ready for OT&E and that hazards, ESOH, and software system safety are manageable. For programs on the T&E Oversight List, a Service process must certify IOT&E readiness; the review completes when the Service or Component Acquisition Executive determines material readiness, and may be run by the PM or the OTA per Service policy.
- **LFT&E** characterizes survivability (a system's ability to avoid, withstand, or recover from combat threats) and lethality (a weapon's ability to kill expected threats and targets). It tests against threats likely in combat, not just specifications. Survivability testing emphasizes vulnerability to kinetic attack and user casualties — including injury type, severity, and operational impact — and addresses personnel survivability even when the platform itself cannot survive (e.g., crash and egress); uncrewed systems and weapons can also be subject to LFT&E. Lethality testing emphasizes the weapon's lethal effects on combat-configured targets. Survivability and lethality plans live in the T&E Strategy, testing starts early, and DOT&E reports adequacy and performance to the congressional defense committees at its conclusion.
- **"Operationally representative" has a defined horizon.** For both OT&E and LFT&E, it covers the period from Initial Operational Capability plus ten years.
- **The FUSL waiver is bounded and statutory.** There is no waiver from LFT&E; the law only allows relief from FUSL testing when the required tests are unreasonably expensive and impractical. The request climbs from the PEO to the Service/Component Acquisition Executive to USD(A&S) as the Defense Acquisition Executive, who must approve it even where authority was delegated. USD(A&S) asks DOT&E to certify that an alternative approach — component, subsystem, and system-level testing plus validated M&S and combat/mishap/safety data — is adequate; DOT&E affirms in a memo with the approved TEMP, and USD(A&S) forwards the two-part package (the certification of need plus the approved Alternative LFT&E plan) to the congressional defense committees.

## Mental Models
- **One test, many evaluations.** Picture testing as data capture and evaluation as interpretation. The same captured data set can be read against several criteria, so designing events to "collect once, answer many" is the efficiency lever — provided each dedicated test type still gets its own event.
- **The "One Team" continuum.** The OT principles push CT, DT, and OT to merge into a single unified test team that shares synchronized collection rather than running sequentially. The artificial barriers between developmental and operational testing are treated as something to collapse, not manage.
- **OT as partner, not final exam.** Continuous and Cumulative Feedback reframes operational testing from an end-of-program verdict delivered to the PM into a development partner that surfaces problems early and keeps providing independent, timely assessments.
- **Characterize vs. demonstrate.** Each test design should align its purpose with the warfighter's decision — is the event meant to *characterize* a system's behavior or to *demonstrate* that a requirement is met? Knowing which the decision needs keeps tests honest and rightsized.
- **Gates before operational events.** OTRR is the readiness checkpoint: you do not enter IOT&E, OA, or FOT&E until reliability, maintainability, supportability, and hazard manageability are verified. Treat it as the operational-test equivalent of confirming the system is fit to be put in front of real users.
- **Survivability and lethality are mirror images.** Survivability asks whether the system endures the threat; lethality asks whether the weapon defeats the target. LFT&E covers both, and "operationally representative" for both reaches a decade past IOC.
- **Strategy as the spine.** The T&E Strategy — under whatever local name (SAMP/TEMP/test strategy) — is the single document that ties decisions, capabilities, measures, and events together through the IDSK. Every test type's requirements thread back into it.

## Anti-patterns
- **The "checklist mentality."** The *Adaptive* principle explicitly warns against rigid test designs and a checklist mentality that exceed the scope of the specific, evolving warfighter concerns; OTAs are meant to retain the freedom to change as the test proceeds and as they learn.
- **Early involvement reduced to symbolism.** The *Early OT Involvement* principle names the failure mode directly: treating early engagement as mere "observation" from an oversight posture, or "being seated at the table" as a token of collaboration, rather than being a genuine part of the team that shapes requirements, budgeting, contracting, and engineering.
- **Milestone reports that arrive irrelevant.** The *Continuous and Cumulative Feedback* principle flags that, in fast-paced acquisitions, reports tied only to specific milestones can be stale before they are even published — the reason feedback must be continuous and cumulative rather than milestone-bound.

## Key Takeaways
1. **T&E delivers decision-quality data** to demonstrate capability and to find and fix deficiencies before a final acquisition or fielding decision; its success hinges on an adequately planned, resourced strategy and on access to contractor data and expertise.
2. **Four test types** — CT, DT&E, OT&E, LFT&E — are meant to be integrated and reused, but collaboration only rescopes events; it never replaces dedicated DT&E, OT&E, or LFT&E.
3. **DT&E** generates substantiated knowledge about capabilities and limitations to inform technical and programmatic risk, drawing criteria from the CONOPS/OMS/MP, capabilities documents, and technical/contractual requirements.
4. **OT&E** judges operational effectiveness, suitability, and survivability under realistic conditions; for oversight programs, DOT&E must approve plans in writing and report to the Secretary of Defense and Congress before BLRIP.
5. **Operational events form a ladder** — Operational Demonstration, EOA, OA, IOT&E, FOT&E — with IOT&E using production-representative articles under realistic combat conditions and required by Title 10 for full-rate production.
6. **OTRR** gates entry into operational events by verifying reliability, maintainability, supportability, and hazard/ESOH/software-safety manageability; for oversight programs a Service process certifies IOT&E readiness.
7. **LFT&E** characterizes survivability and lethality against likely combat threats; FUSL testing is required by **10 U.S.C. §§ 4172(c)**, with a bounded, statutory waiver from FUSL only — never from LFT&E.
8. **The six OT principles (2019)** push the community toward early, tailored, continuous, streamlined, integrated, and adaptive operational testing — a "One Team," speed-of-relevance posture.
