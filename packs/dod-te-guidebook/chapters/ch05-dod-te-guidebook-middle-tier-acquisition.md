# Chapter — T&E in the Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) Pathway

## Core Idea
The Middle Tier of Acquisition pathway exists to move capabilities that are already mature enough to be prototyped or fielded into the warfighter's hands fast — the whole effort is capped at five years from MTA program start. That speed reshapes test and evaluation rather than excusing it: T&E must be planned from program inception, integrated across contractor, developmental, operational, and live fire activity, digitized and automated wherever possible, and built around a shared body of evidence that many stakeholders draw from at once. The pathway splits into **Rapid Prototyping** (innovative technology, fieldable prototype, residual operational capability) and **Rapid Fielding** (proven technology, production quantities, minimal development), and T&E supports the **outcome determination** and **transition decision** at the end of each.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) pathway** — the acquisition route governed by DoDI 5000.80, implementing Section 804 of Public Law 114-92, with supporting guidance in DoDI 5000.89 and DoDI 5000.85. When/how: used for capabilities mature enough to prototype or field within five years of program start; major-system efforts that are primarily technology development, satisfy a major interagency requirement, or carry significant international-partner involvement are steered away from it.
- **Rapid Prototyping pathway** — uses innovative technology to develop fieldable prototypes, demonstrate new capability, and meet emerging needs, with the goal of a prototype meeting defined requirements demonstrable in an operational environment and a residual operational capability inside five years. When/how: when the question is whether a new technology or a novel application of one delivers improved mission capability.
- **Rapid Fielding pathway** — uses proven technology to put new or upgraded systems into the field in production quantities, with only minimal development, beginning production within six months and completing fielding within five years of MTA start. When/how: when the technology is already proven and the need is quantity in the field, not invention.
- **Test and Evaluation Working-level Integrated Product Team (T&E WIPT)** — the team the PM charters during MTA planning to define T&E activities, data requirements, and resources, and to coordinate top-level planning for all test events and documents. When/how: stood up at program inception; the Guidebook notes that equivalent bodies (e.g., an Integrated Test Team) carry other names and are treated interchangeably here.
- **Tailored T&E Strategy** — the principal testing document for either pathway, defining how technical, functional, and operational performance will be evaluated; developed by the PM with T&E WIPT support. When/how: the Guidebook flags that the same artifact appears under several names (test strategy, TEMP, Simplified Acquisition Master Plan) and that for oversight-list programs it goes to DOT&E for approval no later than 45 days before program start.
- **Integrated Decision Support Key (IDSK)** — required by DoDI 5000.89 inside the T&E Strategy; a table linking acquisition, technical, and program decisions to the data (CT, DT, LFT, OT, M&S) needed to support them. When/how: it is the mechanism that lets test events build on one another and feed multiple stakeholders' evaluations simultaneously, integrating DT, CT, and OT; it is meant to evolve as the system matures.
- **Operational Demonstration (ops demo)** — the operational test event the lead OTA runs at the close of Rapid Prototyping to characterize technical maturity, interoperability, and risk toward operational effectiveness, suitability, and survivability in a threat-realistic environment. When/how: can be a single capstone event or a series of incremental events; uses representative units, missions, and environments.
- **Full-Up, System-Level (FUSL) live fire testing** — the live fire stage where the weapon system is powered and combat-equipped with all sub-systems operational, the culmination of LFT&E that progresses from component to sub-system to system level. When/how: required under 10 U.S.C. § 4172; a FUSL waiver (not a waiver from LFT&E itself) may be sought, with a two-part package to Congress coordinated between the acquisition executive and DOT&E.
- **Shared data repository / collaborative test data scoring boards** — a common store the PM establishes during planning so every test team can review, contribute, and reuse data, paired with boards that authenticate existing test data for potential use against IOT&E or LFT&E requirements. When/how: enables sequential testing, big-data analytics, and adaptive methods; the OTA holds the authoritative source of data certified to meet OT&E requirements.

## Key Concepts
- **Five-year cap, two flavors**: every MTA effort is bounded at five years from program start. Rapid Prototyping aims at an innovative, demonstrable prototype with residual operational capability; Rapid Fielding aims at production quantities of proven technology, with production beginning within six months.
- **T&E from inception**: the PM should bring T&E organizations into the program at its start and keep them engaged across the lifecycle, so T&E requirements land in RFPs and contracts and the government secures access to the data it needs to manage risk.
- **Integrate, streamline, automate, reuse**: contractor testing (CT), government DT&E, LFT&E, and OT&E should be combined and automated to the maximum practical extent, with maximum sharing, reciprocity, and reuse of results and artifacts across testing and certification organizations.
- **Evaluation dimensions**: T&E informs decision authorities on operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality — the recurring four-part lens applied throughout both pathways.
- **Planning documents** (Guidebook Table 2): Tailored T&E Strategy, Acquisition Strategy, Cost Estimate, IP Strategy, and RFP. The T&E Strategy is primary, but its success depends on T&E content being embedded in every other document.
- **The RFP is the gate for data rights**: if T&E expectations are not stated explicitly in the RFP and the acquisition contract, the needed data simply will not be delivered — raising risk to the T&E program and potentially to the whole program's cost and schedule. A draft T&E Strategy should be attached to the RFP.
- **DT&E purpose**: manage and mitigate development risk, verify compliance with contractual and technical requirements, prepare for OT, inform decision-makers across the lifecycle, and judge whether the integrated system delivers military utility. Government testers leverage contractor DT&E rather than duplicating it.
- **Prototype experimentation**: government developmental testing in Rapid Prototyping should go beyond confirming the capability meets its need — it should stress the technology to expose full capabilities and limits, and can inform preliminary CONOPS, tactics, techniques, and procedures.
- **Rapid Fielding production tests**: because development is minimal, DT&E here adds production-focused checks — First Article Testing (FAT), Acceptance Testing (AT), and Production Qualification Testing (PQT). The AT event is where the government takes ownership and warranty coverage may begin.
- **OT&E options in Rapid Fielding**: IOT&E (for a full-rate production decision), Operational Assessment (to reduce risk and surface issues before IOT&E), or an ops demo (for integration into an existing program of record or transition to another pathway).
- **LFT&E is not optional**: programs intending to field rapid capabilities are not exempt from the 10 U.S.C. § 4172 requirement for realistic survivability and lethality testing before outcome determination; early live fire testing matters because it catches survivability and lethality problems while the design can still change.
- **Oversight and approval**: for programs on the T&E oversight list, the T&E Strategy is approved finally by DOT&E, which must also clear the ops demo and LFT&E plans before any testing; DOT&E produces independent operational and LFT&E reports to OSD, the Joint Staff, the Services, and Congress.
- **End-of-phase decisions**: the Decision Authority makes an outcome determination or transition decision using DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E results. Rapid Prototyping outcomes include discarding the prototype and terminating, sustaining a residual capability, transitioning to Rapid Fielding, or transitioning into a program on a different pathway. Rapid Fielding outcomes include transition to operations and sustainment, integration into an existing MCA, or entry into a new or existing program of record (e.g., at Milestone C).

## Mental Models
- **Speed is a constraint on T&E, not a discount on it.** The compressed schedule is exactly why operational realism is pulled forward — embedding operational conditions and users early raises the odds of catching and fixing problems while there is still time, which the Guidebook calls especially critical on short MTA timelines.
- **The IDSK is the data-reuse engine.** Think of it as the wiring diagram between decisions and evidence: one test event can serve several evaluations at once, and stakeholders pull from prior events instead of commissioning redundant tests. It is alive — it adapts as the system matures.
- **Build one body of evidence, not many stovepipes.** The shared data repository plus scoring boards turns disparate CT/DT/OT/LFT data into a common, authenticated pool, so DT data can feed OT evaluations when its pedigree supports it — provided the test community maps prior data to evaluation areas and identifies the gaps.
- **The contract is where T&E lives or dies.** Data rights, tool access, embedded instrumentation, and deliverables all have to be written into the RFP and contract up front; collaboration over digital models, M&S, and test environments is cheaper to design in than to retrofit.
- **Same tools, shared confidence.** Letting government testers use the contractor's automated test tools (and sometimes mastering them) makes events easier to replicate and lets the government build confidence in success as early as possible — but only if the contractual provisions enable it.
- **Government capabilities by default.** Use government T&E facilities unless a cost-benefit analysis justifies an exception, which must clear the T&E Strategy approval process first.

## Anti-patterns
- **Leaving T&E expectations out of the RFP/contract.** The Guidebook is explicit: if the RFP and the acquisition contract do not state the T&E expectations, the needed data simply will not arrive — which raises risk to the test program and, potentially, to the program's overall cost and schedule.
- **Treating "rapid" as exempt from live fire.** Programs fielding rapid capabilities are not excused from the realistic survivability and lethality testing required by 10 U.S.C. § 4172 before proceeding to outcome determination; a FUSL *waiver* is narrow and still requires a two-part package to Congress, not a free pass on LFT&E.
- **Demonstrating only to spec instead of experimenting.** In Rapid Prototyping, merely showing a capability meets the need it was built for misses the point of prototype experimentation, which is to stress the technology and discover its real limits and military utility.

## Key Takeaways
1. MTA is bounded at **five years** and splits into **Rapid Prototyping** (innovative technology, fieldable prototype, residual capability) and **Rapid Fielding** (proven technology, production quantities, minimal development).
2. The PM charters a **T&E WIPT** at inception to define T&E activities, data needs, and resources, and to get T&E requirements into RFPs and contracts.
3. The **Tailored T&E Strategy** is the primary test document; for oversight-list programs DOT&E approves it no later than 45 days before program start, and it must carry an **IDSK** linking decisions to the CT/DT/LFT/OT/M&S data that supports them.
4. T&E should be **integrated, streamlined, automated, and reuse-driven**, feeding a **shared data repository** and **scoring boards** that build one authenticated body of evidence.
5. Rapid Prototyping closes with an **ops demo** by the lead OTA; Rapid Fielding offers **IOT&E, OA, or ops demo** plus production tests (**PQT, FAT, AT**).
6. **LFT&E is mandatory** under 10 U.S.C. § 4172 for both pathways; only a narrow **FUSL waiver** is available, coordinated between the acquisition executive and DOT&E.
7. T&E from DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E **informs the outcome determination and transition decision** the Decision Authority makes at the end of each phase.
