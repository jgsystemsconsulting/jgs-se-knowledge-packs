# Chapter — T&E in the Urgent Capability Acquisition Pathway

## Core Idea

The Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA) pathway exists to put a working capability into a warfighter's hands within two years of a need being validated, and the test and evaluation discipline has to bend to that clock without abandoning rigor. Under DoDI 5000.81 — which DoDI 5000.02 establishes and which DoDI 5000.89 supports on the T&E side — the pathway covers validated urgent operational needs — and other quick-reaction capabilities a program can field in less than two years. The defining tension of the chapter is speed versus evidence: the solution must resolve or substantially mitigate the underlying need within the two-year window, and an interim, less-than-full-capability fielding will not be held back just to let immature technology mature further. T&E's job is to generate enough credible evidence to inform fielding decisions while the program runs its four phases — pre-development, development, production and deployment, operations and support — often in parallel rather than in sequence. The recurring instruction across the chapter is to integrate, streamline, and tailor every test activity (contractor, developmental, live fire, operational) to share data and avoid redundant work, because there is no schedule slack to test the same thing twice.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA) Pathway** — the four-phase model (Pre-Development, Development, Production and Deployment, Operations and Support) drawn from DoDI 5000.81. The chapter stresses these phases are highly tailored and often run in parallel to expedite fielding by stripping down the documentation and reviews of the deliberate process.
- **Joint Rapid Acquisition Cell (JRAC)** — its Executive Director assigns responsibilities to the DoD component head for rapidly resolving validated urgent needs.
- **Joint Urgent Operational Need (JUON), Joint Emergent Operational Need (JEON), Urgent Operational Need (UON)** — the categories of validated need that trigger and scope the pathway. The chapter also names Warfighter Senior Integration Group (SIG)–identified urgent issues as a trigger.
- **T&E Working-level Integrated Product Team (T&E WIPT)** — chartered by the PM during pre-development; defines T&E activities, data requirements, and resources. The chapter explicitly notes that equivalent names such as Integrated Test Team are common and acceptable, and refers to all of them as the T&E WIPT.
- **Chief Developmental Tester (CDT)** — identified by the PM in pre-development to help drive the T&E Strategy.
- **Milestone Decision Authority (MDA)** and **Component Acquisition Executive (CAE)** — the CAE appoints the PM and MDA for JUONs/JEONs; the MDA approves phase entries and the production/deployment decision.
- **T&E Strategy / Acquisition Strategy** — the tailored T&E Strategy is documented inside the Acquisition Strategy; together they convey what data testing must provide and when.
- **Integrated Decision Support Key (IDSK)** — a tailored construct linking DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E information to critical acquisition, technical, and program decisions, named as a source of efficiency across the T&E lifecycle by integrating DT, CT, and OT.
- **DT&E, OT&E, LFT&E, CT** — developmental, operational, live fire test and evaluation, and contractor testing, all to be integrated.
- **Operational Test Agency (OTA)** — conducts the Operational Assessment and the post-deployment assessment; maintains the authoritative record for test data feeding operational evaluation.
- **DOT&E (Director, Operational Test and Evaluation)** — final approver of operational and live fire test plans for programs on the OSD T&E oversight list; approves post-deployment assessment plans; produces independent reports.
- **Full-Up System-Level (FUSL) testing and the FUSL Waiver Process** — grounded in the Live Fire Test Law (10 U.S.C. §§ 4172) for realistic survivability and lethality testing, with a defined waiver mechanism.
- **Validated Online Lifecycle Threat Report (VOLT)** — the threat reference informing requirements and the T&E Strategy.
- **Shared Body of Evidence / Shared Data Repository** and **collaborative test data scoring boards** — mechanisms for reuse of test data across DT, OT, and LFT evaluations.
- **Acquisition Decision Memorandum (ADM)** and **Early Fielding Report** — the production decision record and the DOT&E report on whether results confirm operational effectiveness, suitability, survivability, and lethality.

## Key Concepts

**The two-year clock and the cost ceiling.** The pathway is bounded both in time and money: the solution must be fieldable within two years of need validation, and a single solution's estimated cost is capped at $525 million for research, development, and T&E, or $3.065 billion for procurement, in FY2020 constant dollars. Urgent capability activities are not necessarily meant to become enduring programs, which shapes how much T&E investment is justified.

**Engage T&E at need identification, not at a milestone.** The PM should bring T&E organizations into the program as soon as the UON is identified. In pre-development the PM identifies a CDT and charters the T&E WIPT, and government test teams assess the testability of the requirements as early as possible so the team can document how testing will demonstrate performance consistent with the UON.

**Embed OT&E early.** Operational test awareness and participation begin in pre-development — monitoring contractor and government developmental tests, understanding the pedigree and applicability of prior results for operational use, and spotting data gaps that will shape post-deployment assessment planning.

**Tailoring is mandatory, not optional.** Operational and live fire test plans must be tailored, streamlined, feasible, and aligned to the timeline and the Acquisition Strategy. Test plans focus data collection only on activities tied directly to the theater of employment, the mission context (operation types, threats, environments, users, tactical employment), and the technical requirements in the urgent need — not on everything that could be tested.

**Mission-informed component testing under a compressed schedule.** Much developmental testing happens at the component level, but it must still be done in a mission-informed context — components tested under the technically stressing conditions operations will impose, and system-level testing in an environment resembling actual employment, in a cyber-contested setting, using service members from the user community. Early integration of both component and system testing is essential precisely because the timeline is compressed.

**Realistic survivability and lethality testing and the FUSL waiver.** Even urgent programs must satisfy 10 U.S.C. §§ 4172 before a fielding decision. Realistic survivability testing fires combat-likely munitions at a combat-configured system; realistic lethality testing fires production-representative munitions at representative targets under realistic conditions (end-to-end testing). DOT&E normally expects FUSL testing unless it grants a waiver. Critically, there is technically no waiver from LFT&E itself — only from the FUSL/end-to-end requirement. A waiver package to Congress has two parts: a certification that the waiver is needed on cost-and-practicality grounds, and an alternative LFT&E plan; the alternative plan must have a basis in testing and cannot rest solely on M&S and analysis, though design analyses, M&S, and combat-data analysis are named as permitted supplementary data sources.

**Oversight gates and the 60-day rule.** For programs on OSD T&E oversight, operational and live fire test plans go to DOT&E for approval at the Development milestone and post-deployment assessment plans at the Production and Deployment milestone; for non-oversight programs these are approved at the Service level. Plans submitted for DOT&E approval must be delivered 60 days before testing starts, and DOT&E is the final approver for oversight programs.

**Parallelism in execution.** Because of the accelerated timeline, OT&E and LFT&E execution differ from other pathways: testing can occur simultaneously across overlapping Development and Production-and-Deployment phases, testing focuses narrowly on the specific theater and mission set, the first unit equipped is the test unit, and a granted FUSL waiver can let production and deployment proceed before LFT&E is complete.

**Government test infrastructure by default.** Programs should use government T&E capabilities unless an exception is justified as cost-effective, with a cost-benefit analysis and approval through the test-plan approval process before acquiring or using non-government facilities.

**The lifecycle does not end at fielding.** O&S planning starts back in pre-development. The OTA runs a post-deployment assessment (in the field if practical, otherwise via surveys or component feedback). Capability improvements may reuse DoDI 5000.81 procedures if inside the original requirement scope, or require a new/amended requirement if outside it. No later than one year after entering O&S, a disposition official decides whether to demilitarize and dispose, sustain for the current contingency, or transition the capability to a Program of Record.

## Mental Models

- **Test to the decision, not to the spec.** Every test artifact — the IDSK, the focused test plans, the scoped LFT&E — exists to feed a specific acquisition, technical, or fielding decision within the window. If a test does not inform a decision tied to the urgent need, the tailoring logic argues against running it.
- **One body of evidence, many users.** Build a shared data repository in pre-development and a shared body of test evidence throughout, so DT, OT, and LFT each draw from the same data instead of generating their own. Collaborative test data scoring boards judge whether existing integrated data can satisfy OT&E or LFT&E objectives — reuse is the default, not the exception.
- **Embedding beats hand-off.** The chapter repeatedly favors fully embedded testers with access to all records and data over sequential information transfer, because dissemination time is time the program does not have. Increased embedding of T&E personnel on the development team is the named mechanism for both tailoring activities and ensuring key elements are covered.
- **Parallel, not pipelined.** The four phases and their T&E events are drawn as concurrent activities; the model is overlapping streams informing decisions as evidence accrues, not a relay handoff between phases.
- **Interim capability beats deferred perfection.** A less-than-full capability fielded on time is preferred to a fuller capability that waits on immature technology — the pathway's whole reason for being.

## Key Takeaways

- The UCA pathway compresses acquisition into a two-year fielding window for validated urgent needs, with cost ceilings of $525M (RDT&E) / $3.065B (procurement) in FY2020 dollars, and its phases run in parallel.
- T&E must be engaged at need identification; the PM charters a T&E WIPT and names a CDT in pre-development, and OT&E participation starts that early too.
- The tailored T&E Strategy lives inside the Acquisition Strategy and uses a tailored IDSK to bind DT&E, OT&E, and LFT&E data to specific critical decisions.
- Test plans are deliberately narrowed to the specific theater, mission context, threats, and technical requirements of the urgent need — focused data, not comprehensive coverage.
- The FUSL waiver is a waiver from full-up/end-to-end testing only, never from LFT&E itself; the alternative plan must be grounded in actual testing and approved by DOT&E, with a certification-plus-alternative-plan package to Congress.
- For oversight programs DOT&E approves operational, live fire, and post-deployment plans, with a 60-day-before-testing submission rule.
- Reuse drives efficiency: a shared data repository, a shared body of evidence, and collaborative scoring boards let one dataset serve developmental, live fire, and operational evaluations.
- Fielding is not the end — post-deployment assessment, scoped capability improvements, and a disposition analysis within one year of entering O&S close the loop, with possible transition to a Program of Record.

## Connects To

- **Deliberate / Major Capability Acquisition T&E** — UCA is explicitly the streamlined counterpart to the deliberate process, stripping documentation and reviews; the contrast clarifies what a full T&E program looks like when time is not the binding constraint.
- **DoDI 5000.89 and the broader T&E Enterprise Guidebook** — UCA test plans must align with DoDI 5000.89 policy and the relevant T&E focus-area chapters (including cyber T&E), so this chapter sits inside that enterprise framework rather than replacing it.
- **Live Fire Test & Evaluation and the 10 U.S.C. §§ 4172 regime** — the FUSL waiver process and DOT&E's survivability/lethality reporting role connect directly to the statutory LFT&E body of practice.
- **Integrated / Mission-Based Test Design** — the mission-informed component testing, end-to-end mission threads, and IDSK echo integrated-testing and evaluation-framework concepts found across the T&E discipline.
- **Cyber T&E and the Risk Management Framework** — government T&E under this pathway includes cyber testing in a cyber-contested environment and IT authorization under DoDI 8510.01 (RMF).
- **Sustainment and Disposition** — the logistics risk assessment, supportability strategy, and disposition analysis tie UCA into product-support and lifecycle-management practice, including the route from urgent capability to enduring Program of Record.
