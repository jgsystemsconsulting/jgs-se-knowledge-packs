# Chapter — T&E Organizations, Oversight, and Roles (OSD, USD(R&E), DOT&E, Components)

> Source caveat: this chapter synthesizes the DoD Test & Evaluation Enterprise Guidebook, a headline open-access T&E reference. All claims below are grounded in its Section 4 ("T&E Organizations") and the adjacent sections on program management and the oversight list. Organizational names, statutory citations, and policy references are stated as the source presents them; specifics (commanders, SharePoint locations, exact thresholds) change over time and should be re-verified against the current instruction before operational use.

## Core Idea

The DoD test-and-evaluation enterprise is not a single office — it is a layered division of labor that deliberately separates the people who *develop* a system from the people who *judge* whether it works. The guidebook organizes this enterprise along two tiers. At the top sits the **Office of the Secretary of Defense (OSD)**, where two principals split the work by *type* of testing: **USD(R&E)** — the Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering — owns developmental test and evaluation (DT&E) and the underlying test infrastructure, while the **Director, Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E)** owns operational test and evaluation (OT&E) and live-fire test and evaluation (LFT&E). Below OSD sit the **DoD Component T&E organizations** — the Army, Navy, Air Force, and a defense agency (DISA) — each with a Service T&E Executive for policy and an independent operational test agency (OTA) for execution.

The whole structure exists to preserve **independence**. The OTAs and DOT&E evaluate systems separately from the program offices that build them, so that the verdict reaching a milestone decision or Congress is not authored by the same hands that have a stake in the program proceeding. Understanding who holds which authority — who approves a TEMP, who designates a program for oversight, who reports to Congress before a system can move beyond low-rate initial production — is the practical core of navigating DoD T&E.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

The slice names a set of organizations, programs, and governing documents. Listed with how the source introduces each:

- **Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering (USD(R&E))** — introduced as the principal advisor to the Secretary and Deputy Secretary for the DoD Research and Engineering Enterprise, including DT&E. Its responsibilities are framed as flowing from **DoDI 5000.89**.
- **Director, Developmental Test, Evaluation & Assessments (D(DTE&A))** — introduced as the principal advisor to USD(R&E) for developmental test, evaluation, and assessments, bringing systems-engineering and T&E rigor to the **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)** pathways.
- **Test Resource Management Center (TRMC)** — introduced as principal advisor to USD(R&E) on T&E infrastructure strategic planning and assessment, operating under **DoDD 5105.71**.
- **Major Range and Test Facility Base (MRTFB)** — introduced as the designated core set of DoD T&E infrastructure, citing **DoDD 3200.11** and operating per **DoDI 3200.18**.
- **Central T&E Investment Program (CTEIP)**, **T&E Science and Technology (T&E/S&T) Program**, **Joint Mission Environment Test Capability (JMETC)**, **National Cyber Range Complex (NCRC)**, **Test Resource–Governance (TR-G) Division**, and **Test and Training Enabling Architecture (TENA) Software Development Activity** — introduced as the mechanisms through which TRMC executes its mission.
- **Director, Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E)** — introduced as the principal OT&E and LFT&E advisor and the senior such official in DoD, with authorities anchored in **10 U.S.C. §§ 139, 4171, 4172, and 4231** and **DoDD 5141.02** / **DoDI 5000.89**.
- **DOT&E-stewarded joint activities** — **Joint Test and Evaluation (JT&E)**, **Joint Live Fire (JLF)**, **Center for Countermeasures (CCM)**, **Joint Aircraft Survivability Program (JASP)**, **Joint Technical Coordinating Group for Munition Effectiveness (JTCG/ME)**, **Test and Evaluation Threat Resource Activity (TETRA)**, **International Test and Evaluation Program (ITEP)**, and **Cyber Assessment Program (CAP)** — each introduced as a joint activity for which DOT&E provides oversight, funding stability, and strategic guidance.
- **Service / Component T&E organizations** — the **Army T&E Executive** and **U.S. Army Test and Evaluation Command (ATEC)** (per **Army Regulation 73-1**); the **DON T&E Executive / OPNAV N94**, **OPTEVFOR (COMOPTEVFOR)**, and **MCOTEA** (per **SECNAVINST 5000.2F**); the **Air Force T&E Executive (AF/TE)**, **AFOTEC**, and **STARCOM** (per **HAF MD 1-52**); and the **DISA T&E Executive / Joint Interoperability Test Command (JITC)**.
- **T&E Oversight List** — introduced as a joint DOT&E + USD(R&E) product, published per DoDI 5000.89, designated Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI).

## Key Concepts

**The USD(R&E) / DOT&E split is by test type, not by hierarchy.** Both are OSD-level principals, but they own different halves of the T&E picture. USD(R&E) leads developmental testing, prototyping, experimentation, and the engineering side — and crucially, it approves the DT&E plan inside a program's Test and Evaluation Master Plan (TEMP) and prepares the Milestone B and Milestone C DT&E sufficiency assessments for the largest programs (Major Defense Acquisition Programs, MDAPs). DOT&E prescribes OT&E and LFT&E policy and approves the operational and live-fire portions of test planning for programs it has placed under oversight.

**The Test Resource Management Center is the enterprise's infrastructure landlord.** TRMC looks across the *entire* T&E infrastructure — ranges, facilities, instrumentation — to keep it aligned with DoD modernization and ready to test emerging capabilities. It oversees the MRTFB (the core, designated test infrastructure) and runs a portfolio of programs to keep that infrastructure current: CTEIP funds new multi-Service test capabilities too large for any single Service to build; the T&E/S&T Program matures test technologies from roughly TRL 3 to TRL 6 so DoD can test tomorrow's weapons; JMETC links distributed facilities into joint, live-virtual-constructive test events; the NCRC delivers cyber-contested environments for cyber T&E and training; and TENA supplies the architecture and software that make range systems interoperable and reusable.

**DOT&E's authority is statutory and culminates at Congress.** Several of DOT&E's responsibilities are not merely policy — they are tied to specific sections of Title 10. DOT&E designates programs for oversight under 10 U.S.C. §§ 139, 4171, 4172, and 4231; approves the adequacy of operational and live-fire test plans *in writing before testing begins*; determines how many test articles must be procured; and submits independent reports to OSD, the Joint Staff, the Components, and the congressional defense committees. A defining gate: before a system under oversight can proceed *beyond low-rate initial production (LRIP)*, DOT&E must submit a report on the completed OT&E and LFT&E. DOT&E also files an annual report to Congress summarizing the year's operational and live-fire activity.

**The T&E Oversight List is the trigger that pulls a program into OSD scrutiny.** DOT&E and USD(R&E) *jointly* publish and maintain this list, which names every program under oversight for DT, OT, or LFT&E. Membership matters because it changes who approves your test plans. For programs on the list, DOT&E becomes the approval authority for the TEMP and overarching test planning for OT and LF. The two principals apply different inclusion criteria for their respective domains — DOT&E weighs dollar thresholds, congressional/DoD interest, and warfighting significance; USD(R&E) weighs ACAT category, missile-defense integration, national-security-system status, and unresolved high technical risk flagged by an Independent Technical Risk Assessment.

**Each Service mirrors the same two-role pattern: an Executive for policy, an OTA for independent judgment.** The Service T&E Executive is the senior T&E advisor and the policy/oversight/interface authority — for example, the Army T&E Executive coordinates directly with USD(R&E) and DOT&E and approves test documentation before it goes to OSD; the Air Force T&E Executive (AF/TE) is the final Service-level review authority and signatory for TEMPs. The OTA does the independent evaluating: ATEC for the Army (containing the evaluation center AEC, the operational test command OTC, and developmental Test Centers), OPTEVFOR for the Navy, MCOTEA for the Marine Corps, AFOTEC for the Air Force, and JITC for IT and national security systems. The source notes JITC is the *only non-Service OTA* for IT/NSS, and that AFOTEC's testing of Space Force capabilities is transitioning to STARCOM.

**At the program level, T&E roles are deliberately layered too.** The Program Manager (PM) builds and resources the T&E program; the PM charters a T&E Working Integrated Product Team (T&E WIPT) to translate strategy into test documentation, and designates a Chief Developmental Tester (CDT), a Lead DT&E Organization, and a Lead OTA. This mirrors the OSD-level pattern one tier down: the people planning and running developmental tests are organizationally distinct from the operational test agency that supports DOT&E's independent assessment.

## Mental Models

**The "two hands" model of independence.** Picture two hands: one builds (the PM, the Lead DT&E Organization, the contractor), one judges (the OTA, DOT&E). The enterprise is engineered so the judging hand never belongs to the building hand. Every organization in this slice falls into one role or the other, and the higher you go, the more formal the separation becomes — culminating in DOT&E's independent reports to Congress.

**Type-of-test as the org chart's primary axis.** When you meet a new T&E organization, the first question is not "what Service?" but "developmental or operational/live-fire?" USD(R&E) and its TRMC machinery sit on the developmental/infrastructure side; DOT&E and the OTAs sit on the operational/live-fire side. The Service Executives bridge both, but the OTAs are firmly operational.

**The oversight list as a tripwire.** A program is not under OSD T&E oversight by default — it crosses a tripwire (dollar value, ACAT category, congressional interest, technical risk, mission criticality) and lands on the joint list. Crossing it changes your approval chain: now OSD principals sign your TEMP and your operational/live-fire plans. Coming *off* the list is also defined — testing complete, reporting done, development stabilized.

**TRMC as a shared utility.** Think of TRMC's programs (MRTFB, CTEIP, JMETC, NCRC, TENA, T&E/S&T) as a public-utility layer: no single program office could afford to build national ranges, cyber ranges, or distributed-test interoperability software, so the enterprise pools the investment and shares the capability across all Services. CTEIP exists precisely to solve "too large for a single Service" modernization.

**DOT&E's joint activities as a standing toolkit.** JT&E, JLF, CCM, JASP, JTCG/ME, TETRA, ITEP, and CAP are persistent capabilities DOT&E keeps funded and stable so that any program needing joint testing, threat-representative simulation, countermeasure evaluation, munitions-effectiveness data, allied test access, or cyber assessment can draw on a ready resource rather than standing one up from scratch.

## Key Takeaways

- DoD T&E is a two-tier enterprise: OSD principals (USD(R&E) for DT&E and infrastructure; DOT&E for OT&E and LFT&E) over Component organizations (a Service T&E Executive plus an independent OTA in each Service/agency).
- The architecture's purpose is independence: those who evaluate are separated from those who develop, all the way up to DOT&E's independent reports to Congress.
- USD(R&E) approves the DT&E plan in the TEMP and prepares MS B/MS C DT&E sufficiency assessments for MDAPs; DOT&E approves OT and LF planning for programs on oversight and approves plan adequacy in writing before testing starts.
- TRMC is the infrastructure authority, overseeing the MRTFB and running CTEIP, the T&E/S&T Program, JMETC, NCRC, TENA, and the TR-G Division to keep test capability ahead of weapons technology.
- The joint T&E Oversight List (DOT&E + USD(R&E), CUI) determines which programs fall under OSD T&E approval authority; entry and exit are governed by explicit criteria.
- DOT&E's authority is statutory (Title 10) and gated: a system under oversight cannot move beyond LRIP until DOT&E reports on its completed OT&E/LFT&E.
- Each Service repeats the Executive-plus-OTA pattern: ATEC (Army), OPTEVFOR and MCOTEA (Navy/USMC), AFOTEC and STARCOM (Air Force/Space Force), and JITC (the only non-Service OTA, for IT/NSS).
