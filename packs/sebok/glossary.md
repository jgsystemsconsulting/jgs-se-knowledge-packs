# SEBoK Glossary

Key terms from the Guide to the Systems Engineering Body of Knowledge (SEBoK) v2.14, alphabetical, with chapter references. Definitions are synthesized for reference use, not reproduced; usage follows the SEBoK Glossary of Terms. Content under CC BY-NC-SA 3.0; attribution to the BKCASE Project required.

**Agile (in SE)** — a strategic capability to sense change, respond well, and evolve the solution across the whole life cycle; treated by SEBoK as "being agile," not merely "doing agile." (Ch 11, Ch 10)

**Architecture Definition** — the SE process that establishes a system's behavioural and structural characteristics (functional, logical, physical views) so elements work together to meet stakeholder needs; SEBoK's reading of the ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 process. (Ch 17)

**Authoritative Source of Truth (ASoT)** — the governed, single, consistent body of system data and models from which views are generated in a model-based / digital-engineering practice. (Ch 17, Ch 36)

**Business or Mission Analysis** — the Concept Definition activity that frames the problem space and solution classes, establishing why a solution is needed before any system-of-interest exists. (Ch 16)

**Capability (organizational)** — what a business or team can actually do to perform SE, distinct from an individual's competency; capability can fall short even when competency is high. (Ch 26, Ch 27, Ch 28)

**Competency (KSAA)** — an individual's knowledge, skills, abilities, and attitudes for an SE role; necessary but not sufficient for performance. (Ch 28)

**Concept Definition** — the set of SE activities (Business/Mission Analysis + Stakeholder Needs Definition) that examine the problem space before the system-of-interest is formally defined. (Ch 16)

**Concurrency, iteration, recursion** — the three reasons life-cycle processes are applied non-linearly rather than in a fixed serial sequence. (Ch 13, Ch 17)

**Constituent system** — an operationally and managerially independent system that participates in a system of systems while remaining useful on its own. (Ch 24)

**Decision gate** — a criteria-based control point (review/milestone) at which independent stakeholders judge readiness to proceed. (Ch 9, Ch 21)

**Development approach** — how the work inside a life cycle is done; SEBoK's families are sequential, incremental, and evolutionary, with agile overlapping them. (Ch 10, Ch 12)

**Digital Engineering (DE)** — an integrated digital approach using authoritative data and models as a continuum across the life cycle; framed as a Why/What/Who/How transformation, with MBSE one component of it. (Ch 1, Ch 36)

**Emergence** — system-level properties or behaviours that arise from the whole and are not present in the parts; with complexity, the root reason systems thinking is needed. (Ch 2, Ch 4, Ch 34)

**Enabling system** — a system that supports the life cycle (development, delivery, operation, sustainment, disposal) of the system-of-interest without being part of the delivered product. (Ch 7, Ch 21)

**Engineered system (ES)** — a technical or socio-technical system that is the subject of an SE life cycle, designed or adapted to interact with an operational environment to achieve a purpose; the SEBoK's primary scope. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Engineered system context** — the engineered system together with the related engineered, social, and natural systems and environments it interacts with; what "system architecture" properly addresses. (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 7)

**Enterprise Systems Engineering (ESE)** — applying SE to the planning, design, improvement, and operation of an enterprise viewed "as a system," where people are components, not just users. (Ch 23, Ch 33)

**Heuristic** — a rule learned from practice that works reliably but whose underlying cause is not yet scientifically explained; one of the three grades of SE knowledge (concept, principle, heuristic). (Ch 2)

**Holism / reductionism** — the balanced pair at the heart of any systems approach: treating the system as a whole versus the focused study of its parts. (Ch 5)

**Industrial DevOps** — the practical realization of agile SE principles through integrated practices, toolchains, and value-stream-oriented workflows. (Ch 11)

**ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288** — the canonical system life-cycle processes standard; the trunk from which most SE standards branch and against which they are harmonized. (Ch 14, Ch 19, Ch 13)

**ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748** — the life-cycle management guidance family (e.g. 24748-1 terms/concepts, -2 applying 15288, -10 agile). (Ch 9, Ch 11, Ch 12, Ch 14)

**ISO/IEC/IEEE 24774** — the specification for how a process description must be written (name, purpose, outcomes, activities, tasks), giving processes a uniform form. (Ch 13)

**Life cycle** — the evolution of an entity (system, product, service, project) from conception through retirement (per ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1). (Ch 9)

**Life cycle model** — a staged framework representing a life cycle that an organization selects, adapts per project, and continuously improves; not a rigid construct. (Ch 9, Ch 12)

**Loss-Driven Systems Engineering (LDSE)** — a unifying, loss-centred view of the safety/security/reliability/resilience "-ilities," engineering against types of loss rather than each attribute in isolation. (Ch 34)

**Maier's characteristics of SoS** — operational independence and managerial independence of constituents (plus evolutionary development, emergent behaviour, geographic distribution) used to recognize a true system of systems. (Ch 24, Ch 23)

**Measure of Effectiveness (MOE)** — a stakeholder-facing measure of how well a solution meets a need, set during Concept Definition. (Ch 16)

**Model** — a simplified representation of a system that abstracts selected aspects (function, structure, behaviour, performance, cost) to promote understanding. (Ch 6)

**Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)** — the formalized application of models to support SE activities across the life cycle; a subset of digital engineering and the medium through which Part 3 processes are increasingly executed. (Ch 6, Ch 8, Ch 36)

**New Product Development Process (NPDP)** — the integrated running-together of systems engineering and market development through which an enterprise develops and fields a product. (Ch 21)

**Principle (in systems thinking)** — a basic generalization accepted as true and usable as a basis for reasoning or conduct; sits between concept and heuristic. (Ch 5, Ch 2)

**Product system** — the end product plus the enabling products needed to develop, deliver, operate, and dispose of it; the unit of work in Product Systems Engineering. (Ch 21)

**Quality attribute / -ility / non-functional requirement / specialty** — an emergent whole-system property (security, reliability, safety, usability …); the collective engineering of these is specialty engineering. (Ch 34)

**Sense–respond–evolve** — the abstract definition of agility in SE: sense change, respond well, evolve the solution over time. (Ch 11)

**Service system** — a configuration of people, technology/infrastructure, organizations, and shared information that delivers a service through value co-creation with the customer; cannot be inventoried. (Ch 22)

**Service Systems Engineering (SSE)** — applying SE to design and deliver services, optimizing a value co-creation chain in which the customer is an active participant. (Ch 22)

**Seven Samurai of Systems Engineering** — Hitchins' set of seven interacting systems (context, intervention, realization, deployed, collaborating, sustainment, competing) framing any SE problem. (Ch 7)

**Specialty engineering** — the collective engineering of a system's quality attributes, harmonizing their conflicting constraints with functionality. (Ch 34)

**Stage** — a bounded period within a life cycle tied to the state of the entity's description or realization, anchored to milestones or decision points. (Ch 9)

**Stakeholder Needs Definition** — the Concept Definition activity that transforms stakeholder needs into an integrated set of needs (correct, consistent, complete, feasible). (Ch 16)

**Systems Engineering (SE)** — a transdisciplinary, integrative approach that lets an engineered system be realized, operated, and retired successfully, drawing on systems principles together with scientific, technological, and management methods (INCOSE 2023). (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Systems Engineering Management (SEM)** — managing the technical/engineering resources and assets of an SE effort, distinguished from general project management by its technical focus. (Ch 15, Ch 31)

**Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP)** — the plan governing how SE will be conducted on a project; paired with the project manager's PMP/SEP. (Ch 15, Ch 31)

**Systems of Systems (SoS)** — a set of systems that interact to deliver a capability none can produce alone, while each constituent stays independently useful. (Ch 24, Ch 23)

**Systems science** — the study of general principles, recurring patterns (isomorphies), and processes common to systems of every kind; the theoretical grounding for systems thinking. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Systems thinking** — the integrating paradigm linking systems science to systems approaches, captured as concepts, principles, and patterns and balancing holism with reductionism. (Ch 5)

**System-of-interest (SoI)** — the system whose life cycle is under consideration; framed within a narrower/wider system context. (Ch 7, Ch 10)

**Tailoring** — adapting a selected process to a project's context, risk, and constraints, with each decision documented and traceable. (Ch 14)

**Technical review / audit** — the mechanism by which independent, knowledgeable stakeholders examine system state and work products against pre-established criteria. (Ch 9)

**Traditional Systems Engineering (TSE)** — conventional project-scoped SE engineering one large complex system; the contrast point for ESE, SSE, and SoSE. (Ch 23, Ch 22)

**T-shaped systems engineer** — broad working knowledge of wider-system concerns (the bar) resting on real domain depth (the stem). (Ch 1, Ch 22)

**Vee model** — the SE realization model in which design definition descends the left side, realization climbs the right, and V&V is performed concurrently and recursively level by level. (Ch 10, Ch 17, Ch 32)
