# SEBoK Glossary

**Abstraction** — Hiding unimportant details to focus on essential characteristics, enabling management of structural and behavioral complexity (Ch 6).
**Acquisition Strategy** — High-level business and technical approach to achieve program objectives within resource constraints across the lifecycle (Ch 35).
**Allocation** — Assignment of logical-architecture functions to physical system elements, creating traceability and surfacing derived requirements (Ch 18).
**Analytical Model** — Representation of mathematical relationships, subdivided into dynamic (time-varying) and static models (Ch 6).
**Architecture** — The fundamental organization of a system's elements, their relationships, and governing design principles, embodied in the system and its environment (Ch 22).
**Audit** — Independent examination of work products to assess compliance with specifications, standards, or contractual criteria (Ch 10).
**Baseline** — A formally approved set of requirements, design, or specifications anchoring cost/schedule/technical commitments and requiring change control (Ch 16).
**Behavior** — The effects or outcomes a system produces when it interacts with its environment, distinct from its structure (Ch 2).
**Brown-field System** — Evolution of an existing system where legacy data and current state serve as inputs to analysis (Ch 17).
**Business or Mission Analysis** — Strategic analysis identifying the problem/opportunity and establishing mission, goals, objectives, and measures of success (Ch 17).
**Capability** — Ability to meet operational, mission, or organizational needs; gaps between baseline and desired states drive strategic planning (Ch 24).
**Certification** — Independent audit and verification by an external body that a system or management system conforms to a standard (Ch 20).
**Complex Adaptive System (CAS)** — A system of autonomous agents that self-organize, learn from feedback, and produce emergent behavior (Ch 40).
**Complexity** — Variability in the number of elements and dimensions of their relations; a key phenomenon (with emergence) driving the need for systems thinking (Ch 1).
**Concurrent Engineering** — Integrated, parallel design of products and their related lifecycle processes from conception through disposal (Ch 22).
**Configuration Management (CM)** — Disciplined identification, control, auditing, and tracking of changes to system and project artifacts to maintain consistency (Ch 16).
**Conformance** — Voluntary adherence to consensus standards; full conformance satisfies all requirements, tailored conformance applies a formal tailoring process (Ch 20).
**Conformance vs. Compliance** — Conformance is voluntary adherence to standards; compliance is mandatory adherence to legal, regulatory, or contractual requirements (Ch 20).
**Cost Driver** — A physical, performance, operational, or programmatic attribute with the most influence on total estimated cost (Ch 34).
**Cost Estimating Relationship (CER)** — A statistically derived equation expressing cost or effort as a function of one or more cost drivers (Ch 34).
**Cybernetics** — The study of communication, regulation, and control in systems through information flow and feedback mechanisms (Ch 4).
**Decision Gate** — A staged lifecycle checkpoint where review results inform decisions to proceed, pause, restart, or terminate work (Ch 10).
**Defense-in-Depth** — Prevention or minimization of loss through multiple coordinated controls rather than a single mechanism (Ch 37).
**Descriptive Model** — Representation of logical relationships such as structure, functions, and interconnections (Ch 6).
**Design Structure Matrix (DSM)** — A matrix visualizing dependencies between system components and project activities (Ch 6).
**Digital Engineering (DE)** — A transformation framework establishing a computer-readable digital thread linking all system models as the authoritative source of truth (Ch 41).
**Digital Thread** — A unified, continuous connection of machine-readable model data across all disciplines and lifecycle phases (Ch 41).
**Digital Twin** — A high-fidelity, often physics-based model of a physical system enabling simulation and analysis across its lifecycle (Ch 41).
**Emergence** — Properties or behaviors arising from system interactions that cannot be predicted from individual elements alone (Ch 1).
**Enabling Systems** — Systems or services that facilitate the SoI across lifecycle stages (development, utilization, support) but are distinct from it (Ch 8).
**Engineered System** — A combination of technology and people created, used, and sustained for an identified purpose across a managed lifecycle (Ch 1).
**Enterprise** — A collection of people, processes, and technology serving combined objectives, intentionally establishing operational dependence to maximize efficiency (Ch 24).
**Enterprise Architecture Framework** — A structure organizing architectural artifacts by stakeholder perspective and dimension (e.g., Zachman, DoDAF, FEAF) (Ch 24).
**Entry and Exit Criteria** — Pre-defined conditions that gate the start (entry) and close (exit) of a review or lifecycle-stage transition (Ch 10).
**Equifinality** — In open systems, the property that the same final state may be reached from different initial conditions via different paths (Ch 5).
**Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** — A top-down graphical method decomposing system failure into combinations of component failures using logic gates (Ch 39).
**Federation of Systems (FoS)** — Highly autonomous, distributed systems united by choice rather than hierarchical control, each retaining its own destiny (Ch 24).
**Feedback Loop** — A cycle in which a system's output or state influences its own future behavior; central to cybernetics and dynamics (Ch 4).
**Function** — An action transforming inputs into outputs (data, materials, energy), organized hierarchically from mission down to leaf-functions (Ch 18).
**Functional Architecture Model** — A set of functions and sub-functions defining the transformations a system must perform to complete its mission (Ch 18).
**General Systems Theory (GST)** — Principles relevant to all open systems, exploiting correspondence relationships (homologies) across disciplines (Ch 4).
**Green-field System** — Development from a blank slate, requiring as-is/to-be characterization of the problem space (Ch 17).
**Hardware Assurance** — Quantifying and improving confidence that hardware weaknesses do not become exploitable vulnerabilities across the lifecycle (Ch 39).
**Hazard Control** — Minimizing hazards, controlling unavoidable hazards, and ensuring safe transitions between acceptable states (Ch 37).
**Holism vs. Reductionism** — Balancing decomposition into realizable elements against assessment of the complete system's emergent properties in context (Ch 8).
**Homologies** — Structural or behavioral similarities between systems in different disciplines, allowing knowledge to transfer across them (Ch 4).
**Human Systems Integration (HSI)** — A discipline coordinating all human-related considerations across seven domains to balance performance, safety, and affordability (Ch 38).
**Incremental Commitment Spiral Model (ICSM)** — A risk-driven, phase-gate process that buys information before committing resources at periodic commitment reviews (Ch 11).
**Interface Control Document (ICD)** — A baseline specification documenting interfaces, interactions, and information-exchange requirements between components (Ch 22).
**Iteration** — Repeated application of processes to accommodate stakeholder feedback, evolving requirements, and design tradeoffs (Ch 13).
**Knowledge Area (KA)** — A thematic grouping of SE topics related to a specific application context, each subdividing into focused articles (Ch 21).
**Life Cycle Cost (LCC)** — Total cost of acquisition and ownership over the full lifecycle, including development, operations, support, and disposal (Ch 34).
**Life Cycle Model** — A framework of processes organized into stages with decision gates based on entry/exit criteria (Ch 12).
**Loss-Driven Systems Engineering (LDSE)** — An overarching process unifying loss-related specialty areas (safety, security, resilience, reliability) typically treated in isolation (Ch 37).
**Maintainability** — Probability that a maintenance action restores service within a stated time under stated conditions (Ch 39).
**Measure of Effectiveness (MOE)** — A mission-outcome metric used to validate the system against objectives, spanning hardware, software, and human performance (Ch 17).
**Mission Engineering** — An interdisciplinary process decomposing missions to assess relationships and impacts across the end-to-end mission (Ch 25).
**Model** — A representation used to capture, analyze, and communicate information about a system, varying in scope, formality, abstraction, and fidelity (Ch 9).
**Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)** — A paradigm using formalized system models (e.g., SysML) as the primary SE artefact, augmenting document-based approaches (Ch 9).
**Modular Architecture** — A design of loosely coupled, reusable components with well-defined interfaces enabling rapid extension and adaptation (Ch 11).
**Multiple Objective Decision Analysis (MODA)** — A framework using value functions and swing weights to compare alternatives across disparate measures (Ch 16).
**Obsolescence** — Diminishing manufacturing sources and material shortages (DMSMS), addressed early via long-life materials and availability planning (Ch 19).
**Open System** — A system whose elements interact with, and exchange matter/energy/information across a boundary with, its environment (Ch 1).
**Operational Independence** — The property that SoS constituent systems operate under their own control with their own users and management (Ch 25).
**Partitioning** — Decomposition or grouping of functions using affinity criteria to identify feasible system elements (Ch 18).
**Physical Architecture Model** — Models of concrete physical elements (hardware, software, humans, processes) that realize the logical architecture (Ch 18).
**Problematic Situation** — A fuzzy, ill-defined state in which stakeholders perceive different problems; the soft-systems alternative to a single problem definition (Ch 4).
**Product System** — An integrated set of product elements composed to deliver functionality unachievable by any element alone (Ch 22).
**Recursion** — Application of technical and management processes at each hierarchical level of decomposition, with each level's outputs feeding the next (Ch 13).
**Regression Testing** — Verification that upgrades do not unintentionally affect the functions of unmodified system portions (Ch 19).
**Reliability** — Probability that a product performs its intended function under stated conditions without failure for a given time period (Ch 39).
**Requirements Management (RM)** — A cross-cutting discipline managing needs and requirements from elicitation through allocation, traceability, and change control (Ch 16).
**Requisite Variety (Ashby's Law)** — A control system must possess at least as much variety as the system it controls (Ch 5).
**Service** — Activities that cause transformation of state by mutually agreed terms between a provider and a customer (Ch 7).
**Service Level Agreement (SLA)** — Negotiated technical and non-functional parameters establishing reliability and performance baselines for a service system (Ch 23).
**Service System** — A collection of interacting entities (people, processes, organizations, technologies) that co-create value between provider and consumer (Ch 23).
**Set-Based Design (SBD)** — A method analyzing multiple alternatives simultaneously, progressively narrowing feasible tradespaces (Ch 41).
**Simulation** — Implementation of an analytical model in executable code with input/output infrastructure (Ch 6).
**Single Source of Truth** — A centralized, authoritative repository (typically the system model) for all technical information across disciplines (Ch 9).
**Socio-technical System** — A system whose behavior depends on the integration of technological, social, and natural elements (Ch 2).
**Specialty Engineering** — Analysis of features requiring special skills (reliability, safety, logistics, human factors) to identify requirements and lifecycle impact (Ch 22).
**Stakeholder Needs Definition** — Identifying and documenting an integrated set of stakeholder needs grounded in lifecycle concepts, drivers, and constraints (Ch 17).
**Stakeholder Value** — The benefit realized by stakeholders when system outcomes are delivered in their operational context, within constraints (Ch 8).
**Standards Alignment** — Collaborative effort to ensure SE standards share vocabulary, process sets, and structures, enabling concurrent use (Ch 20).
**Statement of Work (SOW)** — A document defining technical scope, deliverables, acceptance criteria, and schedule (Ch 35).
**System** — A set of elements in interaction whose "togetherness" produces a whole with properties no individual part exhibits alone (Ch 2).
**System Boundary** — The cognitive and physical distinction between a system, its elements, and its environment, defining allowable interactions (Ch 2).
**System Context** — The collection of system elements and relationships needed to position a problem within the wider system and understand consequences (Ch 1).
**System Element** — A discrete part (hardware, software, data, human, process, facility) that can be implemented to fulfill design properties (Ch 18).
**System of Interest (SoI)** — The specific engineered system that is the focus of a systems engineering lifecycle, distinguished from its context (Ch 2).
**System-of-Systems (SoS)** — A composition of operationally independent systems interacting to achieve mission objectives no single constituent can achieve alone (Ch 25).
**Systems Engineering Plan (SEP)** — A governance document recording the SE strategy and defining how a project is organized, conducted, and controlled (Ch 35).
**Systems Praxis** — The combined intellectual and practical effort to create holistic solutions, integrating theory with effective practice (Ch 1).
**Systems Thinking** — The integrative capability connecting systems-science foundations, theories, and representations with practice across the lifecycle (Ch 5).
**SysML** — A graphical language extending UML for systems engineering, providing standardized diagram types to represent system information (Ch 9).
**Tailoring** — Adjusting standard lifecycle processes to match project-specific context, complexity, and risk, with documented rationale (Ch 14).
**Temporal Architecture Model** — Classification of functions by execution frequency (synchronous cyclic vs. asynchronous event-driven) and decisional hierarchy (Ch 18).
**Traceability** — Documented bidirectional linkage between requirements, their sources, and their verification artifacts (Ch 16).
**Trade Space** — The multidimensional set of alternatives bounded by objectives and constraints, explored by trade studies (Ch 16).
**Trade Study** — A decision-management method decomposing a decision into objectives, measures, alternatives, and value functions to compare options (Ch 16).
**Tradespace** — The feasible and infeasible design space bounded by requirements and physics-based performance models (Ch 41).
**Transition Points** — Logical moments in the lifecycle, aligned to stage boundaries or milestones, where technical reviews and audits occur (Ch 10).
**Validation** — Confirmation, often via reviews involving stakeholders and end users, that requirements, design, and work products meet real needs (Ch 10).
**Value Co-creation** — The collaborative process through which service provider and consumer jointly create value (Ch 23).
**Value Stream** — The end-to-end sequence of linked tasks and information flows necessary to deliver customer value, mapping where waste occurs (Ch 11).
**Value-Focused Thinking** — A generative approach establishing value criteria first, then designing alternatives to maximize them (Ch 16).
**Vee Model** — A systems engineering process model where change entry points reflect scope; system-level changes enter at the top, component-level at the bottom (Ch 19).
**View and Viewpoint** — A view represents a system from related concerns; a viewpoint specifies the conventions for constructing views (Ch 6).
**Wicked Problem** — A problem that cannot be fully solved or even fully defined; attempting solutions yields incremental understanding of both problem and solution (Ch 8).
**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** — A deliverable-oriented hierarchical decomposition of project scope mapped to components and capabilities (Ch 6).
