# Chapter — Systems of Systems (SoS)

*Source: SEBoK v2.14, Part 4 Knowledge Area: Systems of Systems (SoS) (Lead Authors: Mike Henshaw and Judith Dahmann; with constituent articles by Judith Dahmann, Duncan Kemp, Bud Lawson, Heidi Davidz, Alan Faisandier, Ron Giachetti, Andy Hernandez, and Rhys Kissell).*

## Core Idea

A **system of systems (SoS)** is a set of systems or system elements that interact to deliver a unique capability none of the constituent systems can produce alone — and crucially, each constituent remains a useful system in its own right, with its own development, management goals, and resources. **System of systems engineering (SoSE)** is presented here not as a brand-new discipline but as the systems engineering community's response to the complex, interconnected systems of the twenty-first century. What makes it hard is what makes it distinctive: because the constituent systems are operationally and managerially independent, the comfortable assumption of a single authority owning the whole — the bedrock of most traditional SE — simply does not hold. The systems engineer's job shifts from designing a monolith to **composing** independent systems, mostly by managing interfaces and leveraging existing functionality, while accepting that control is partial, change is continual, and behavior at the SoS level can surprise you.

The KA repeatedly stresses that SoSE pulls the engineer beyond pure technical work into **socio-technical and sometimes socio-economic** territory. An SoS is rarely a permanent artifact built once and fielded; it is an evolving, often-discovered arrangement of systems networked together for a purpose, and as connectivity between modern systems keeps climbing, more and more capability is being delivered this way — converging with cyber-physical systems and the internet of things.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)

- **Maier's five key characteristics of SoS** (Maier, 1998) — component systems that are operationally independent; component systems that are managerially independent; geographical distribution; emergent behavior; and evolutionary development. Maier flags **operational independence** and **managerial independence** as the two *principal distinguishing* characteristics: lacking both, a system is not an SoS regardless of complexity or geographic spread. Used as the entry definition of what counts as an SoS.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 21839 definition** (ISO, 2019) — the formal definition of SoS and of **Constituent Systems**, where each constituent is "a useful system by itself" that interacts within the SoS to provide its unique capability. Anchors the standards-based vocabulary.
- **US DoD definition of SoSE** (DoD, 2008) — planning, analyzing, organizing, and integrating existing and new systems blended into an SoS capability exceeding the sum of the constituent parts. Used to frame technical-management activity.
- **Four types of SoS taxonomy** (Maier 1998; Dahmann and Baldwin 2008; ISO 21841, 2019) — **Directed, Acknowledged, Collaborative, Virtual**, ordered by diminishing central control. Codified in ISO 21841 "Taxonomy of Systems of systems." Used throughout the KA as the organizing axis for how much an architect or manager can actually steer the SoS.
- **Seven SoS pain points** (Dahmann, 2014/2015; INCOSE SE Handbook 2023) — SoS Authorities; Leadership; Constituent Systems' Perspectives; Capabilities and Requirements; Autonomy, Interdependencies and Emergence; Testing, Validation, and Learning; SoS Principles. The catalog of recurring challenges facing SoSE.
- **Maier's four architecting principles for SoS** (Maier, 1998) — attend to intermediate steps in a planned evolution; apply standards/protocols through **policy triage**; leverage interfaces to realize capability; and **ensure cooperation** by incentivizing independent owners to collaborate.
- **The Wave Model** (DoD 2008; Dahmann et al. 2011) — an evolutionary, five-process-element meta-methodology using stable intermediate steps, originally designed for Acknowledged SoS with a small SoSE team, also applied to Collaborative challenges and to Program-level SoS test and evaluation.
- **Interoperability frameworks** — the **NCOIC Interoperability Framework** (Osvalds, 2009) with its three broad levels (Network Transport; Information Services; People, Processes and Applications) and the **European Interoperability Framework** (European Commission, 2017) with its six levels in a political context. Used to argue interoperability must be coherent vertically, not just at the data/network layer.
- **Open Systems Architecture (OSA)** and **MOSA (Modular Open Systems Approach)** — an approach to the *use of standards* in acquisition/procurement (not a class of architecture) to enable competition and rapid module replacement within an SoS.
- **SoSE approach catalog** (Cook and Unewisse, 2017) — named alternative approaches: **Enhanced Traditional Systems Engineering (ETSE)**, **Complex Systems Engineering (CSE)**, **Dynamic Optimization of SoS using Value Measurement (DOSVM)**, **SoS Governance (SoSG)**, the **US DoD Wave Model**, **US Navy Mission Engineering Approach**, **British Systems Thinking Approach (BSTA)**, **Systemic Strategic Planning and Execution (SSPE)**, and a **Hybrid SoSE Approach Based on the Wave Model**.
- **Dimensions of complexity applied to SoS** (Watson, 2019) — Diversity, Connectivity, Adaptability, Multi-perspective, Complex system behavior, Dynamics, Representations, Evolution, System emergence, Indeterminate boundaries — each mapped onto why SoS are inherently complex.
- **INCOSE Complexity Primer guiding principles** (INCOSE, 2016/2021) — including "Think like a gardener, not a watchmaker," "Take an adaptive stance," "Zoom in and zoom out," "Achieve Balance," and "Understand what motivates autonomous agents," applied to SoSE.
- **Cynefin framework** (Kurtz and Snowden, 2003) — four domains (Known, Knowable, Complex, Chaotic) used to explain why human/social elements make SoS behavior hard to predict.
- **Capability Systems Engineering** (Kemp and Daw; INCOSE UK Capability SE Guide) — delivering enduring outcomes (not outputs) through people, processes, information, and equipment.
- **Mission Engineering** (US DoD Mission Engineering Guide / MEG 2.0, 2023) — making the **mission itself the system of interest**, with the methodology of Mission Problem Definition, Mission Characterization, Mission Architecture, Mission Engineering Analysis, and Results and Recommendations.

## Key Concepts

- **Independence is the root cause, not complexity alone.** It is the *operational and managerial independence* of constituents — each with its own owner, users, business processes, funding, and development cycle — that drives nearly every technical and organizational challenge in the KA. A constituent may keep changing on its own schedule, for its own users, indifferent to SoS-level effects.
- **The four types form a control gradient.** Directed (constituents subordinated to the SoS) → Acknowledged (recognized objectives and a manager, but constituents keep independent ownership) → Collaborative (voluntary interaction toward agreed purposes) → Virtual (no central authority, no agreed purpose; large-scale behavior emerges via relatively invisible mechanisms). Real SoS usually blend types and shift over time. While early attention focused on Acknowledged SoS where current SE adapts, the predominance of SoS is now seen as Collaborative and Virtual.
- **Process tailoring, not replacement.** The KA's table maps standard SE processes onto SoS reality: with often no top-level SoS authority, **agreement processes** may run without explicit agreements; **technical processes** define cross-cutting capability while development, integration, V&V are executed by the constituent systems with SoSE monitoring and review. SoSE-level integration/verification/validation applies when constituents are brought together.
- **Requirements become a negotiation.** Traditionally SE starts from a clear, complete requirement set. In an SoS, constituents arrive with their own requirements pointed at other purposes; the SoS often acts as a *new "user"* asking systems to take on new requirements they were never built for. Where SoS needs and constituent requirements conflict, the SoSE must find alternatives — changing constituents or adding new systems.
- **Testing the whole is often impossible.** Asynchronous, independently funded constituent development cycles, plus the sheer scale and diversity of many SoS, make traditional end-to-end testing on every change prohibitively costly or unfeasible. Often the only realistic measure of SoS performance comes from operational data or from modeling, simulation, and analysis.
- **Architecting centers on interfaces.** The architecture is the technical framework specifying how systems are employed operationally (the CONOPS), the internal/external dependencies among constituents, and end-to-end functionality, data flow, and communications. Because the architect often lacks knowledge of systems owned by others (even competitors), a mixed **black-box / white-box** approach is needed, and hidden functionality behind well-defined interfaces can still produce undesired emergence.
- **Forward vs. Reverse Architecting** (Wilkinson et al., 2010) — architecting to *build better systems* (Forward) versus architecting to *understand existing parts of the environment as systems* (Reverse). SoS work leans on the reverse posture far more than monolithic system design does.
- **Open standards as the lever of influence.** Since opportunities to change constituents are limited, agreement on interface definitions and the use of open systems standards is how competent composition and flexible evolution are achieved — including for COTS and MOTS components.
- **Interoperability is multi-layered and vertical.** It is not enough at the data/network level; the NCOIC and European frameworks show that agreement at, say, the data/object-model layer actually requires alignment all the way up to business or political objectives. Data exchange itself is an architectural pattern, plus a data format, plus a communication protocol (Charest et al., 2020), with XML a common choice.
- **Governance replaces management; trust replaces control.** Sauser et al. (2009) describe the **"control paradox of SoS"**: as types move toward Virtual, central control diminishes, so management gives way to governance and command. Henshaw et al. (2013) frame an SoS as a set of **trust and contract relationships** (formal and informal) between systems, making interface trust an engineering-governance concern. The three governance questions (Siemieniuch and Sinclair, 2014): is the right work being chosen (leadership); is the work being them right (management), and how do we know (metrics)?
- **Situational awareness is both a failure mode and a payoff.** Many SoS failures trace to one constituent's operator deciding on inadequate knowledge of the bigger picture; yet improving information sharing across the SoS (e.g., Network Enabled Capability) is itself a primary motivation for building SoS. Increasingly autonomous constituents (cyber-physical systems) can *degrade* human situational awareness while themselves lacking competent models of humans.
- **Scale and scope range from purely technical to enterprise-wide.** From technical integration (e.g., a consumer audio-visual "system") to socio-technical disaster-response SoS to enterprise-scale concerns like counterfeiting as a whole ecosystem of systems, organizations, and policies.
- **Analytic methods are overwhelmingly model-based.** A 2021–2023 IEEE review found ~91% of domain papers use some model-based approach — network models, agent-based models, mathematical/optimization models, MBSE/SysML, simulation, machine learning, and conceptual models — with ~22% using graph analysis (including DSMs and hypergraph theory) and ~26% applying AI.

## Mental Models

- **Compose, don't construct.** The SoS engineer assembles a capability from independently owned parts by managing interfaces and leveraging existing functionality — sometimes adding capability whose only purpose is to facilitate SoS operation. The mindset is composition under constraint, not clean-sheet design.
- **Gardener, not watchmaker.** The INCOSE Complexity Primer's image, adopted directly as the SoS architect's required mindset: evolve a living solution through influence and intervention rather than construct a fixed mechanism through control and design. Design is defined by continual change and adaptation, not by an endpoint.
- **Middle-out, zoom-in/zoom-out.** Effective SoSE is described as a *middle-out* process: honor the top-down drivers for the SoS while respecting the bottom-up needs and capabilities of the constituents, iteratively shifting scales between the two.
- **Stable intermediate steps along a wave.** The Wave Model's insight: plan evolution as a sequence of stable intermediate configurations and start each iteration by assessing what changed since the last wave — institutionalizing "learn from problems."
- **Don't optimize the whole by optimizing a part.** The KA repeatedly warns that optimizing a constituent can sub-optimize the SoS, and an over-optimized whole turns rigid. The architect may even need a constituent to run sub-optimally at its own level to achieve SoS effectiveness — Rebovich's point that participation in an SoS rarely looks like a net gain to a single-system stakeholder.
- **Influence and incentives over command.** With control resting largely with the constituents, the levers that matter are motivation, incentives, trust, and an appreciation of non-technical factors (commercial benefit, IP protection) that make independent owners *choose* to collaborate.
- **The mission as the system of interest.** In Mission Engineering, flip the frame: instead of asking whether a system meets its technical performance metrics, decompose the mission end-to-end and ask whether the SoS produces the effects that contribute to mission success in a realistic scenario — identifying the right things to do ("doing the right things") to feed the SE process that builds things right.

## Anti-patterns (only if named)

- **The control paradox of SoS** (Sauser et al., 2009) — treating an SoS as if traditional management/control applies, when diminishing central authority (especially toward Collaborative and Virtual types) means control as a function of rules, time, and bandwidth must give way to governance, trust, influence, and agility.
- **Optimizing a constituent at the expense of the SoS** — Rebovich's articulated fundamental problem: single-system stakeholders see SoS participation as added obligations and constraints, rarely a net gain, so local optimization quietly degrades overall SoS performance.
- **"Technology first and technology only"** — the narrow perspective SoS Governance (SoSG) explicitly seeks to expand beyond, since context, feasibility, and long-term evolution matter as much as technical integration.
- **Trying to optimize when you don't understand the situation** — the KA states bluntly that within SoSE, an attempt to "optimize" usually signals you have not grasped the situation; seek balance among competing tensions instead of binary either/or trade-offs.

## Key Takeaways

- An SoS exists only when constituents are **operationally and managerially independent** yet interact to deliver a capability none could alone; these two characteristics, not complexity or geographic spread, are the test (Maier 1998; ISO/IEC/IEEE 21839).
- The **four types — Directed, Acknowledged, Collaborative, Virtual** — form a gradient of diminishing central control (ISO 21841); most real SoS blend types and shift over time, and the bulk of real-world SoS are Collaborative or Virtual.
- The **seven pain points** (authorities, leadership, constituent perspectives, capabilities/requirements, autonomy-interdependencies-emergence, testing/validation/learning, SoS principles) frame the recurring difficulties; nearly all stem from constituent independence.
- SE processes are **tailored, not replaced**: technical processes define cross-cutting capability while constituents execute development and V&V under SoSE monitoring; SoS-level requirements often become negotiations with the SoS acting as a new "user."
- **Architecting is interface-centric** under partial knowledge, using black-box/white-box approaches, open standards, and Maier's four principles (planned evolution, policy triage, leveraging interfaces, ensuring cooperation); interoperability must cohere vertically up to business and political objectives.
- SoS are inherently **complex** across Watson's complexity dimensions; the response is complexity thinking ("gardener not watchmaker," influence over control, middle-out, learn from problems), and **management gives way to governance** built on trust and contract relationships.
- A family of **named SoSE approaches** (ETSE, CSE, DOSVM, SoSG, Wave Model, US Navy Mission Engineering, BSTA, SSPE, and Wave-Model hybrids) lets practitioners match method to the degree of central control and stability of objectives.
- **Mission Engineering** extends SoSE by making the mission the system of interest, evaluating whether the SoS produces mission-contributing effects in realistic scenarios rather than just meeting isolated technical metrics.
