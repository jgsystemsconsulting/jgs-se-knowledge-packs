# Chapter — Representing Systems with Models

## Core Idea
A model is a simplified representation of a system at some point in time or space, built to promote understanding of the real system by abstracting one or more of its aspects — function, structure, properties, performance, behavior, or cost. This SEBoK Knowledge Area, *Representing Systems with Models* (BKCASE authorship), treats the modeling of systems as holistic, value-providing entities as a central process of systems engineering. Applied early, modeling and simulation can document functions and requirements, assess mission performance, estimate cost, evaluate trade-offs, and surface insights that improve performance while reducing risk and cost. Modeling occurs at every level — component, subsystem, system, and systems-of-systems level — and across the whole life cycle. The KA frames the practice through six topics: *What is a Model?*, *Why Model?*, *Types of Models*, *System Modeling Concepts*, *Integrating Supporting Aspects into System Models*, and *Modeling Standards*.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)
- **Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)** — INCOSE's *Systems Engineering Vision 2020* definition: the formalized application of modeling to support requirements, design, analysis, verification, and validation, beginning in conceptual design and continuing through later life-cycle phases. When/how: adopt it when system models become the primary artifacts of the SE process — managed, controlled, and integrated into the technical baseline — in contrast to the traditional document-centric approach where text specifications are the controlled artifacts.
- **Modeling language (abstract syntax / concrete syntax / semantics)** — the means by which abstract (non-physical) models express meaning. When/how: use the three-part decomposition to evaluate or select a language. *Abstract syntax* gives the constructs and construction rules (analogous to parts of speech and grammar); *concrete syntax* gives the symbols (graphical nodes/arcs, or programming-language text); *semantics* give the constructs their meaning, defined in natural language or by mapping to a formalism.
- **Model taxonomy: physical vs. abstract; descriptive vs. analytical** — derived from the US DoD M&S Glossary's "physical, mathematical, or otherwise logical" classification. When/how: classify a needed model up front. *Physical models* are concrete (a model airplane mockup); *abstract models* split into *descriptive* (logical relationships — parts trees, interconnections, functions, test cases) and *analytical* (mathematical relationships). Analytical models further divide into *dynamic* (time-varying state, e.g., position/velocity/fuel over time) and *static* (e.g., mass-properties or reliability prediction).
- **System model** — a hybrid descriptive-and-analytical model that spans several modeling domains and must provide both general-purpose and domain-specific constructs, comprising multiple views (planning, requirements, design, analysis, verification). When/how: use it as the integrating spine across domain models. Wayne Wymore is credited with the early formal definition of a system model on a mathematical framework (*A Mathematical Theory of Systems Engineering: The Elements*, 1967).
- **View and Viewpoint (IEEE 1471)** — *view*: a representation of the whole system as seen through a related grouping of concerns; a *viewpoint*: the specification of conventions for constructing and using a view (the pattern/template, including purpose and audience). When/how: use viewpoints to address stakeholder concerns and generate the corresponding views (requirements, functional, structural, parametric, and discipline-specific views such as reliability, safety, security).
- **Black-box vs. white-box models** — a *black-box* exposes only externally visible features and behavior, hiding internal design; a *white-box* shows internal structure and behavior. When/how: apply recursively to each level of decomposition to build paired black-box/white-box models per component.
- **Conceptual model / concept model (meta-model)** — the set of concepts in a system and the relationships among them, accompanied by definitions; when used to specify a modeling language's abstract syntax it is a meta-model, domain meta-model, or schema. When/how: SEBoK cites the *Systems Engineering Concept Model* developed to support OMG SysML and ISO AP233 integration, augmented by a *semantic dictionary* (glossary of terms).
- **Live, virtual, and constructive simulation** — the US defense classification: *live* = live operators / real systems; *virtual* = live operators / simulated systems; *constructive* = simulated operators / simulated systems. When/how: use to position a simulation's mix of real vs. simulated operators and systems, including hardware/software-in-the-loop.
- **Integrated supporting-aspect frameworks** — methods for folding supporting SE aspects into the *core system model* under a system-centric paradigm: **WBS** and **Design Structure Matrix (DSM)** for project-product integration (OPM-based and SysML-based), **SysML-based** and **OPM-based requirements engineering**, and model-based risk approaches **CORAS**, **STAMP** (System-Theoretic Accident Model and Processes), and **ROSE** (Risk-Oriented Systems Engineering). When/how: use these to capture schedule, work-packages, requirements traces, and risk layers in sync with the system model rather than in disparate, hardly-interfacing models.

## Key Concepts
- **A model has a clearly defined purpose first.** One of the first principles of modeling is to define why the model exists. The *Why Model?* topic enumerates purposes across the life cycle: characterizing an existing (often poorly documented) system; mission and system concept formulation and evaluation; system design synthesis and requirements flowdown; support for system integration and verification (including hardware-in-the-loop and software-in-the-loop testing); support for training; and knowledge capture and system design evolution (including families of products).
- **Modeling tools enforce the language.** Models are built by a modeler using tools — drills and lathes for physical models, software for abstract ones. A tool typically offers a palette and content area, checks conformance to the language's rules, and helps produce a well-formed model, much as a word processor checks grammar. Standard languages enable model interchange between tools.
- **Indicators of an effective model.** *Model scope* must match the intended purpose, sized by **breadth** (requirements coverage — functional, interface, performance, physical, and non-functional), **depth** (decomposition coverage from context down to components), and **fidelity** (level of detail for any given part). *Model quality* is distinct from design quality — a high-quality model can faithfully represent a flawed design; quality is judged by adherence to modeling guidelines (naming conventions, annotations, proper construct use, reuse) and by how well the model serves its purpose. *Model-based metrics* let teams assess progress, estimate effort and cost, and assess technical quality, risk, and model quality — potentially with more precision than document-based metrics.
- **Simulation is the execution of a model over time.** A computer simulation comprises the analytical model in executable code, input conditions/data, and the computing infrastructure. Designer choices include stochastic vs. deterministic, steady-state vs. dynamic, continuous vs. discrete, and local vs. distributed. *Agent-based simulation* predicts complex emergent behavior from interacting autonomous agents. *Visualization* (plots, response surfaces, probability distributions, animation) processes results into meaningful form.
- **Integration is what makes a model-based approach pay off.** Descriptive, analytical, and executable system models represent different facets of the same system and must be integrated, as must the electrical/mechanical/software discipline models and component design models (which must trace to the requirements model). Integration requires **semantic interoperability** — a construct in one model meaning the same as the corresponding construct in another — achieved via **model transformations** that establish correspondence, plus data exchange by file, API, or shared repository.
- **Supporting aspects belong inside the system model.** The core system model is more than a description; it is the common basis for capturing and integrating supporting aspects: project/engineering management, requirements, risk, V&V, integration, and the "-ilities" (RAMSS, manufacturability, robustness, resilience, flexibility, evolvability). Project-Product Integration favors a *system-centric* approach — folding project concepts (schedule, budget, deliverables, work-packages) into the system model — over the more common project-centric approach. Requirements gain rationale, contradiction-detection, and ownership when modeled internally; risk is integrated through MBRA approaches, with STAMP recasting safety as a control problem rather than a reliability problem.
- **Modeling standards enable adoption.** Standards define agreed system-modeling concepts and enable integration across domains, plus cross-discipline, cross-project, and cross-organization communication and artifact reuse. SEBoK's representative lists include descriptive languages (FFBD, IDEF0, OPM, SysML, UPDM for DoDAF/MODAF, OWL), analytical/simulation standards (DIS, HLA, Modelica, fUML), data-exchange standards (AP-233, ReqIF, XMI, RDF), transformations (QVT, SysML-Modelica, OPM-to-SysML), general frameworks (MDA, IEEE 1471), and domain-specific standards (AADL, MARTE, UML, VHDL, BPMN).

## Mental Models
- **Conceptual models are the SE counterpart of the mechanical blueprint** — they are how systems are conceived, architected, designed, and built. The difference SEBoK stresses: blueprints are exact, agreed-syntax representations with a long communication tradition, whereas conceptual models are only beginning to reach complete, unambiguous representation of a system under development.
- **Abstraction is the most fundamental modeling concept** — hiding unimportant detail to focus on essential characteristics, because systems worth modeling have too many details to capture and may be behaviorally complex (emergent properties, non-deterministic behavior). View/viewpoint and black-box/white-box are the two most widely recognized ways to manage levels of abstraction.
- **An assumption that "the differences don't matter" is the silent failure mode of abstraction** — a model may treat many individual components as identical and ignore small differences; if that assumption is wrong, the model can produce false confidence (e.g., in structural integrity).
- **Many models, one system-of-interest** — physical, mathematical, and logical forms each capture different facets (the airplane mockup, the trajectory equations, the failure logic), so a single SoI generally needs several models that must later be made consistent.
- **The system model is the gravitational center** — in a model-based approach, supporting aspects (project, requirements, risk) orbit and synchronize with the core system model rather than living as separate, barely-interfacing artifacts.

## Anti-patterns
- **Document-centric systems engineering** — the traditional approach SEBoK contrasts with MBSE, where text-based documentation and specifications (rather than models) are the managed and controlled artifacts; MBSE is positioned to reduce the risk and cost this incurs by surfacing issues early.
- **Confusing model quality with design quality** — judging a model good because the design is good (or bad because the design is bad). SEBoK's chair example: a high-quality CAD model can accurately represent a chair design that nonetheless collapses when used.

## Key Takeaways
1. A **model** is a purpose-driven, simplified representation of a system; define the **purpose first**, then scope it by **breadth, depth, and fidelity**.
2. **MBSE** makes system models the primary, controlled SE artifacts (per INCOSE *SE Vision 2020*), displacing the document-centric baseline.
3. Models classify as **physical vs. abstract**, and abstract as **descriptive vs. analytical** (analytical further as **dynamic vs. static**); a **system model** is a hybrid spanning domains and views.
4. **Abstraction** is the core concept; manage it with **view/viewpoint** (IEEE 1471) and **black-box/white-box** decomposition.
5. **Simulation** is the execution of a model over time; the defense community frames it as **live, virtual, and constructive**.
6. The payoff requires **integration** across descriptive, analytical, executable, discipline, and component models — demanding **semantic interoperability**, **model transformations**, and data exchange.
7. **Supporting aspects** (project, requirements, risk) integrate best system-centrically, into the core model — via WBS/DSM, SysML- and OPM-based requirements engineering, and CORAS/STAMP/ROSE.
8. **Modeling standards** for languages, data exchange, and transformation are the key enablers of broad MBSE adoption and cross-domain integration.
