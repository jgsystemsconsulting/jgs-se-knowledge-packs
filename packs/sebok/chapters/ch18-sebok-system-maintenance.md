# Chapter 18 — System Maintenance

## Core Idea
Engineering does not stop when a system is fielded. The **System Maintenance** knowledge area treats the post-deployment life of a system as a first-class systems engineering concern: how a system is *sustained*, *extended*, *modernized*, and finally *disposed of* across the whole life cycle. Its central claim is that planning for these activities cannot wait until the system is operational — it begins early in acquisition with a **maintenance concept**, and the initial maintenance requirements must be set during stakeholder needs definition (Clause 6.4.1 of ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2015) and kept evolving thereafter. The KA frames maintenance as a process executed *concurrently* with the operations process (per Clause 6.4.9), whose purpose is to sustain a system's capability to deliver a service by monitoring it, recording problems, taking corrective/adaptive/perfective/preventive action, and confirming that capability is restored. Around that core maintenance process the KA wraps a broader notion of **system sustainment** (also called *product and service life management*) that spans the entire span from initial procurement to disposal — a period far longer than the time spent designing and building the system.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Maintenance concept / maintenance process (ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2015, Clause 6.4.9)** — the process whose purpose is to sustain a system's capability to provide a service; it monitors capability, logs problems for analysis, applies corrective, adaptive, perfective, and preventive action, then confirms capability has been restored.
  - When/how: begins at acquisition with a maintenance concept and runs concurrently with the operations process for the life of the system. Its successful implementation yields a maintenance strategy, maintenance constraints fed back as inputs to requirements, available replacement system elements, sustained services, reported needs for corrective design changes, and recorded failure and lifetime data.
- **Maintenance plan** — the artifact that specifies scheduled servicing tasks and intervals (preventive maintenance) and unscheduled servicing tasks (adaptive or corrective maintenance), and that establishes the requirements for support resources.
  - When/how: developed during planning; its tasks are then allocated to the various maintenance agencies.
- **Maintenance allocation chart** — the device used to tag each maintenance task to the appropriate maintenance agency (in-service/in-house work centers, approved contractors, affiliated repair facilities, original equipment manufacturers, etc.).
- **Level of repair analysis (LORA)** — the analysis on which maintenance planning relies, performed as a function of the system acquisition process; initial planning addresses the actions and support needed to ensure a minimum **life cycle cost (LCC)**.
- **Reliability-centered maintenance (RCM)** — the strategy that incorporates preventive maintenance to preempt failures, thereby extending the mean time between corrective maintenance and enhancing system availability.
- **Affordable System Operational Effectiveness (ASOE)** — the DAU model (DAU 2010) that organizes the SE aspects of logistics and logistics planning, balancing system performance, availability, process efficiency, and life cycle cost.
- **Product Support Package (twelve integrated product support elements)** — the documented output of supportability analysis. The KA names the elements: integrated life-cycle sustainment planning (product/IT/medical system support management); **Design Interface**; **Sustainment Engineering**; **Supply Support**; **Maintenance Planning**; **Support Equipment**; **Technical Data**; **Manpower & Personnel**; **Training & Training Support**; **Facilities & Infrastructure**; **Packaging, Handling, Storage, & Transportation (PHS&T)**; and **Computer Resources**.
  - When/how: planned during sustainment planning and then implemented once the system becomes operational; changes to a fielded system should adjust the affected elements and respect their interrelationships.
- **Service Life Extension (SLE) / Service Life Extension Program (SLEP)** — the practice of continued use of a product or service beyond its original design life, emphasizing reliability upgrades and component replacement or rebuilding to delay entry into wear-out. For military systems SLE is treated as a subset of modification or modernization.
- **Engineering Change Proposal (ECP) / change control boards** — the engineering change management mechanisms used to initiate and govern updates and modifications to the original system.
- **Form, fit, function, and interface (F3I)** — the principle applied to upgrades where backward compatibility is required, so that a low-level change does not disturb higher-level requirements.
- **The Vee Model for Modifications** — a SEBoK-original adaptation showing the standard Vee entered at one of three levels (points A, B, C) depending on where in the hierarchy a modification originates; the Vee may be entered multiple times during the life of the system.
- **Failure Modes, Effects, and Criticality Analysis (FMECA)** — emphasized (per the INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook) for understanding root causes of product failures and grounding product changes.
- **Disposal process (per INCOSE SEH)** — the process whose purpose is to remove a system element from the operation environment to permanently terminate its use and to deal with hazardous or toxic materials/waste in accordance with applicable guidance, policy, regulation, and statutes.
- **"Cradle-to-grave system"** — the named regime of stringent tracking, bookkeeping, and reporting imposed (under RCRA, 40 C.F.R. parts 239–282) on those who generate, transport, or operate the treatment, storage, and disposal sites handling hazardous waste.
- **Green engineering** — per the EPA, designing, commercializing, and using processes and products that remain technically and economically feasible while cutting pollutant generation at the source and risks to human health and the environment.

## Key Concepts

### Maintenance starts in acquisition, not in operations
Maintenance planning is conducted to evolve and establish the requirements and tasks to achieve, restore, and then hold operational capability across the system's life. Because the maintenance process must run concurrently with operations, the initial maintenance requirements are defined during stakeholder needs definition and then keep evolving through development and operation. The recurring considerations the KA lists are: maximizing system availability (which depends on designed-in reliability and maintainability and on available resources); preserving operating potential through scheduled maintenance under an RCM strategy; segmenting non-critical maintenance for potential outsourcing to approved subcontractors to optimize scarce technical manpower and turn-around times; and harnessing IT for maintenance management by systematically capturing and tracking operating and maintenance activity to support analysis and planning.

### Scheduled vs. unscheduled servicing
The KA distinguishes **preventive maintenance** (scheduled servicing tasks and intervals — daily inspections/checks, servicing, cleaning, higher-level scheduled servicing below depot level, major scheduled servicing such as overhaul and corrosion treatment) from **adaptive or corrective maintenance** (unscheduled servicing — detecting and isolating a fault down to the faulty replaceable unit, swapping out the failed unit, plus minor modifications and small damage repairs, and major repairs beyond normal removal/replace). Both classes of task are captured in the maintenance plan and allocated to maintenance agencies via the maintenance allocation chart.

### Support resources and the maintenance-change threshold
Even a maintenance-free design depends on the availability of support resources — personnel; spares; facilities; technical data and documentation; plus support and test equipment (STE) — which must be factored into the acquisition agreement. Adequate **training and certification** of technical personnel is part of this: training materials and trained staff need timely updates as systems are upgraded and as the fielded fleet grows or staff turn over, and certification standards should be defined and contracted as part of the supply agreement. A key practical decision is whether a requested change is within scope as a maintenance change or large enough to require a full SE life-cycle project; the maintaining organization should establish clear thresholds using criteria such as cost, schedule, risk, or criticality.

### Logistics: supportability is a function of design
In the SEBoK, **logistics** is the science of planning and carrying out the acquisition and use of whatever resources are needed to keep a system operating. Crucially, the ability to sustain a system is determined both by how inherently **supportable** the system is (which design drives) and by the processes used to sustain it in the user's context. Sustainment planning therefore has two thrusts: *influence inherent supportability* (operational suitability) and *plan the sustainment processes*. The KA stresses that sustainment considerations are highly inter-related — there is no single canonical list — and groups the key design considerations into **architecture**, **reliability**, **maintainability**, and **support** considerations:
- **Architecture considerations** — openness, modularity, scalability, and upgradeability enable an incremental acquisition strategy and pay dividends when obsolescence and end-of-life issues are later resolved through a technology refreshment strategy.
- **Reliability considerations** — reliability drives both effectiveness and the logistics burden/cost to fix failures; it is one of the most critical determinants of the logistics infrastructure and footprint, traded off against availability, cost, schedule, weight, power, and volume.
- **Maintainability considerations** — a *designed-in* capability that reduces the time, staff, tools, test equipment, training, facilities, and cost needed to maintain the system. Good maintenance procedures cannot overcome poor maintainability design. Intrinsic contributing factors include **modularity** (remove-and-replace vs. on-board repair, without over-modularizing), **interoperability** (standard interface protocols, with physical interfaces designed so mating can only happen correctly), **physical accessibility**, minimal preventive-maintenance designs, embedded training/testing, and **Human Systems Integration (HSI)**.
- **Support considerations** — support features cannot easily be added after the design is set, so supportability must be a high priority early; typical supportability features include diagnostics, prognostics, calibration, documentation, maintenance data collection, compatibility, interoperability, transportability, handling, packing, facilities, and accessibility.

Process efficiency — how well the system can be produced, operated, serviced, and maintained — reflects how well the logistics processes, infrastructure, and footprint have been balanced, and can keep improving even after the design window has passed through techniques such as lean-six-sigma, supply chain optimization, and other continuous process improvement (CPI).

### Sustainment implementation and Sustaining Engineering
Once a system is operational, the sustainment plan is implemented through the twelve integrated product support elements. When a fielded system needs correction or new capability, those SE activities correspond to the IPS element **Sustaining Engineering**, and any change should adjust the affected IPS elements while respecting their interrelationships so the support strategy stays effective. The degree of change scales with severity: minor problems may need only a tweaked maintenance procedure, a supplier change, a training-course update, or a manual change, whereas redesign-level problems may require engineering change proposals, trade studies on the IPS elements, business-case analysis, and revisions to the product support strategy. **Evolutionary systems** deliver capability incrementally and plan for future enhancements; a **system of systems (SoS)** perspective is needed to synchronize the primary and sustainment systems.

### Service life management: the SE process does not end at deployment
**Product and service life management** (a.k.a. system sustainment) plans and supports the system over a life that spans far longer than design and development. It applies equally to commercial systems (energy, information, the Internet, health) and government systems (defense, transportation, water-handling, government services), and the *type* of system (commercial or government) selects which body of knowledge and best practices apply. The KA organizes the post-deployment body of knowledge into three areas — **service life extension**, **modernization and upgrades**, and **disposal and retirement** — and insists that planning for them occur during the requirements phase, including analysis of life-cycle-cost alternatives.

### Service Life Extension (SLE)
SLE is continued use beyond original design life, weighing the risk and LCC of continued use against the cost of replacement. It is typically required when the system no longer meets performance/reliability requirements, when operation/maintenance cost exceeds the cost of SLE (or available budgets), when parts are no longer available, when operation would violate rules/regulations, or when parts are about to reach their life limits. A *proactive*, predictive approach is preferred so SLE happens before the system fails to meet requirements and before O&S costs rise unacceptably. **System design life** is the major consideration — its safety limits and material-aging assumptions, set early in design, define what extension is possible — and aging systems (aircraft, bridges, nuclear plants) need periodic inspection to gauge aging and fatigue. **Software maintenance** is a critical aspect because legacy systems carry computer resources whose essential functions must not be disrupted and which are too costly to replace or redesign. **Diminishing manufacturing sources and material shortages (DMSMS)** must be addressed early. Worked examples in the KA include the Boeing B-52 (fielded 1955) and Boeing 737 (fielded 1967) still in use; nuclear reactors, where safety is the precondition for extension; water-handling systems as "systems that live forever"; and enterprise SLE (e.g., NASA's space transportation system) requiring a holistic, balanced cost view.

### Capability updates, upgrades, and modernization
Modernization and upgrades change a product or service to add functions/interfaces, improve performance, or improve supportability — driven by reduced performance/safety/reliability, stakeholder desire for new capability, component obsolescence (including lack of spare parts), or new uses requiring new capabilities. Modernization uses the same SE processes as original development, but tailored to the constraints of the existing architecture, design, and components, and it demands a detailed understanding of the legacy system before any change. Where documentation is missing, **reverse engineering** is required to capture the true "as-is configuration," and Seacord, Plakosh, and Lewis's **horseshoe model** (functional, code, and architecture transformation) frames the software transformation. After changes, **regression testing** confirms unmodified portions still behave correctly, aided by a **requirements verification traceability matrix**. The **Vee Model for Modifications** shows that a change must be entered at the hierarchy level where it originates: a subsystem requirement/design change enters at point B and must be flowed up through parent requirements; an obsolescence-driven lowest-level change enters at point C and should be **F3I**-compatible so higher-level requirements are unaffected. Concurrent modifications can be managed by the **"block" method** (a group of systems modified simultaneously and deployed together) and by **continuous integration** running concurrently with it. **Commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS)** components bring technological advancement, smaller size, and lower cost, but carry the risk of rapid obsolescence (sometimes within two years) and interface change, requiring careful attention to form factor and physical/electrical interface.

### Disposal and retirement
Every deployed system eventually becomes uneconomical to maintain, obsolete, or unrepairable, so a comprehensive SE process anticipates an equipment phase-out period and accounts for disposal in design and life-cycle-cost assessment. Ecological considerations dominate, with the principal waste-management concerns being air, water, and noise pollution and control, radiation, and solid waste. Regulation is heavy: in the US the EPA and OSHA govern disposal; RCRA (40 C.F.R. parts 239–282) underlies the **cradle-to-grave** tracking of hazardous waste; the EU's **REACH** regulation (managed by ECHA) requires registration/disclosure of substances, and **RoHS** restricts hazardous substances in electrical/electronic equipment. Some component disposal happens *during* operational life as parts fail and are replaced, so disposal tasks and resources must be planned before demand arises. Disposal is described as the least efficient and least desirable way to process waste material, so **green engineering**, recycling, and reuse are preferred. Service and enterprise systems generally require **parallel operation** of a replacement alongside the existing system to avoid loss of functionality (examples: phasing in new ATC systems, the analog-to-digital television transition, and the move to IPv6).

## Mental Models
- **Plan post-deployment up front.** The maintenance concept, sustainment plan, and even the disposal plan are written *during requirements*, not improvised once the system is in the field. Supportability and disposability cannot be bolted on afterward.
- **Supportability is a design property, not just a process.** Reliability, maintainability, and support features are designed in; logistics processes then sustain what the design made sustainable. Good procedures cannot rescue poor maintainability design.
- **Scheduled preempts unscheduled.** RCM trades planned preventive maintenance now to extend mean time between corrective maintenance and lift availability — pay maintenance attention early to avoid failure later.
- **Enter the Vee where the change lives.** A modification is introduced at the hierarchy level it originates (A/B/C) and the impact is traced *up* through parent requirements; a well-designed low-level change is F3I-compatible so it never reaches the top.
- **Extend vs. replace is an LCC decision.** Whether to run SLE, modernize, or retire is fundamentally a comparison of continued-use LCC against replacement LCC plus the user's functional utility — and the assumptions behind earlier LCC analyses must be re-challenged.
- **Cradle-to-grave.** A system's life is closed-loop: the same SE discipline that fields it must track its hazardous materials to final disposition, with disposal considered from the design phase onward.
- **Never break the service.** For service and enterprise systems, extension, modernization, and retirement all run on a fielded system that must keep serving users — hence phased change, backward compatibility, and parallel operation.

## Anti-patterns
*(Stated as cautions, pitfalls, or risks in the source rather than as formally named patterns.)*
- **Assuming the SE process stops at operational handover** — the explicitly listed pitfall; certain life-management functions and organizations in the post-delivery phase are part of the SE process, and not addressing these areas early and throughout the life cycle "can have dire consequences."
- **Over-modularizing** — packaging components for remove-and-replace without trade-offs on replacement, transportation, and repair cost, rather than finding the most cost-effective approach.
- **Treating disposability as low priority** — disposal is commonly viewed as external to whoever currently holds the system (no direct revenue, hidden cost, someone outside SE performs it), which the KA flags as a recurring failing.
- **Modifying without understanding the legacy baseline** — making changes before reverse-engineering the true as-is configuration and documenting the existing (including software) architecture.
- **Skipping regression testing on unmodified functions** — failing to confirm that an upgrade did not disturb existing functions, behaviors, or safety-related interfaces.
- **Letting documentation, configuration management, or O&M budget lapse** — named (for ITS) as factors that shorten useful life and force early retirement.
- **Ignoring COTS obsolescence** — adopting COTS for its cost/technology advantages without planning for component obsolescence (sometimes within two years) and interface change.

## Key Takeaways
1. **System Maintenance is post-deployment systems engineering.** The KA spans the maintenance process plus logistics, service life management, service life extension, capability updates/upgrades/modernization, and disposal and retirement.
2. **Maintenance planning begins in acquisition** with a maintenance concept; maintenance requirements are defined during stakeholder needs definition (15288 Clause 6.4.1) and the maintenance process runs concurrently with operations (Clause 6.4.9), aiming at minimum **life cycle cost** via **LORA** and an **RCM** strategy.
3. **The maintenance plan + maintenance allocation chart** organize preventive (scheduled) and corrective/adaptive (unscheduled) tasks and assign them to maintenance agencies, all dependent on support resources (STE, technical data, personnel, spares, facilities) and trained, certified people.
4. **Supportability is a function of design**: architecture, reliability, maintainability, and support considerations are designed in, then sustained through the **twelve integrated product support elements** and **Sustaining Engineering**, ideally within an **ASOE**-style balance of effectiveness, suitability, and cost.
5. **Extend, modernize, or retire are LCC-driven choices.** **SLE** weighs continued-use cost against replacement; **modernization** reuses tailored SE processes (ECPs, FMECA, F3I, the Vee Model for Modifications, regression testing, COTS trade-offs); **disposal/retirement** demands early planning, cradle-to-grave hazardous-material control, green engineering, and parallel operation to protect service continuity.
