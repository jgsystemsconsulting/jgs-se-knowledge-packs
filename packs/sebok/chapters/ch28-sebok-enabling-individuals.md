## Core Idea
Systems engineering ultimately gets done by people, and this Knowledge Area is about turning a person into someone who can perform SE well and ethically. It belongs to Part 5, which explains how SE gets enabled at three organizational levels — the business or enterprise; the team; and the individual — and this KA is the individual level. The throughline across its four articles is a single loop: an organization defines SE *roles* (sets of tasks) and the *competencies* those roles demand, expressed as KSAA — knowledge, skills, abilities, and attitudes; it *assesses* what competency an individual needs and possesses; it *develops* that competency to close gaps; and it expects the individual to act within an *ethical* framework so the engineer ultimately does good and not harm. The KA is careful about a distinction that matters: competency is not the same as performance. An individual can be highly proficient in an SE competency and still perform poorly because the surrounding capability, capacity, or organizational dynamics get in the way. Developing individual competency is necessary but not sufficient. The closing observation — that the word "attitudes" inside KSAA carries an ethical dimension — is what ties the ethics article back to the competency model rather than leaving it as an afterthought.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **KSAA (knowledge, skills, abilities, and attitudes)** — the foundational building blocks from which SE competency is constructed. KSAAs belong to the individual; competencies belong to the job/role. What is inherent in a person can be developed through education, training, and on-the-job experience.
  - When/how to use: as the unit of analysis for both defining what a role requires and assessing what a person has. Defining KSAAs against a standard taxonomy lets them serve as learning objectives for competency development.
- **Competency, Capability, Capacity, and Performance (the four-term distinction)** — the KA separates these deliberately. *Competency* concerns disagreement in the literature over whether it applies only at the individual level or also at team/project/enterprise levels; *capability* takes in not only human capital but also processes, machines, tools, and equipment; *capacity* accounts for limits such as a constrained timeframe degrading the results of even an outstanding individual; final execution and performance is a function of all three.
  - When/how to use: to diagnose *why* SE performance is or isn't happening — competency alone never explains it.
- **Bloom's Taxonomy (cognitive and affective domains)** — the structure used to write KSAA statements consistently and to define proficiency. The cognitive domain runs (least to most complex): *Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create*. The affective domain runs: *Receive, Respond, Value, Organize, Characterize*.
  - When/how to use: cognitive levels label the kind of intellectual action a KSAA demands; affective levels capture willingness/interest and the degree to which a systems engineer is *characterized* by taking inherently systemic approaches. Both domains should be included in SE competency models — every piece of information passes through the affective (emotional) processors before cognitive integration.
- **Published SE Competency Models (Table 1 — the reference set)** — INCOSE UK WG (2010), the DAU ENG Competency Model (2013), NASA APPEL (2009), MITRE Competency Model (2007), and CMMI for Development (SEI, 2007). Each was built for a unique purpose, in a specific context, validated in a particular way.
  - When/how to use: as published references when an organization decides not to build proprietary models; users must understand each model's development method and context, because primary competencies for one organization may differ from another's.
- **INCOSE SE Competency Model (three theme areas)** — *systems thinking*, *holistic life cycle view*, and *systems engineering management*, each with a set of competencies; developed by a UK working group (Cowper et al. 2005) and later adopted by the broader INCOSE organization.
  - When/how to use: as a general (non-domain-specific) framework organized around the systems thinking + full life cycle + technical-and-managerial-methods consensus.
- **DoD ENG Competency Model (four top-level groupings, 41 competency areas)** — *Analytical, Technical Management, Professional,* and *Business Acumen*. Technical Management is the DoD equivalent of INCOSE's SE management but adds software engineering and acquisition; professional and business-acumen competencies were added for the leadership, contract, and defense-industry-engagement needs of acquisition engineering professionals.
  - When/how to use: when SE is practiced inside an acquisition/contracting environment that demands leadership and business competencies beyond pure SE.
- **NASA APPEL SE Competency Model (System Design, Product Realization, Technical Management)** — the SE portion of a model that spans both project engineering and systems engineering, with a third part common to both disciplines (covering NASA environments, human capital, security, safety and mission assurance, professional/leadership development, and knowledge management).
  - When/how to use: when distinguishing project-engineering from systems-engineering competencies and the overlap between them.
- **Multi-dimensional competency framework (independent axes)** — a complete engineering competency model framework treats competencies as independent dimensions, each with unique KSAAs. Jansma and Derro (2007) proposed three axes (technical knowledge, personal behaviors, process); a large complex-systems organization typically needs four: *Discipline, Life Cycle, Domain,* and *Mission*.
  - When/how to use: to position SE competencies relative to other engineering and non-engineering competencies; an organization or individual marks which areas apply and rates proficiency per sub-area (the layered/multi-dimensional "cube" of Figure 1).
- **Proficiency Level scales** — the KA presents several: INCOSE's *awareness, supervised practitioner, practitioner, expert*; APPEL's *participate, apply, manage, guide*; NASA's role-based levels — technical engineer or project team member; subsystem lead or manager; project manager or project systems engineer, program manager/program systems engineer); and the DCPAS 5-tier *awareness, basic, intermediate, advanced, expert*.
  - When/how to use: to assign KSAAs to proficiency categories within each competency. The Bloom level is not used exclusively to set proficiency — the same SE method applied to a simple subsystem may sit at supervised-practitioner level, while applying it to complex enterprise or systems-of-systems may sit at practitioner or expert.
- **SE Competency Development Framework (Table 1 of *Developing Individuals*)** — organizes development by *Goal → Objective → Method*, with the PRIMARY GOAL being delivery of excellent systems and the SECONDARY GOAL being the competency to deliver them; methods include training courses, job rotation, mentoring, hands-on experience, hand-picking individuals, university/customized education, certificate and certification programs, ISO 9000, process improvement, concept maps, standardized policies, web portals, knowledge repositories, on-call experts, and rotating professors (Davidz and Martin 2011).
  - When/how to use: as the menu of interventions an organization selects from once it knows which competency gaps it must close.

## Key Concepts
- **Roles allocate responsibility; KSAAs fulfill competencies.** Within a business, SE responsibilities are allocated to individuals by defining SE roles, each associated with a set of tasks. A set of KSAAs enables an individual to fulfill the competencies needed for those tasks. Competency can be thought of as being able to apply the right KSAAs in order to complete particular job-related tasks (Whitcomb, Khan, White 2014), and competencies align with the tasks expected for a job position (Holt and Perry 2011).
- **No one is proficient in everything; the organization must cover the set.** An expert systems engineer holds many competencies at high proficiency, but no individual can be highly proficient in all of them. Taken together, a team and its business may hold every needed competency at high proficiency. Organizational capability is *not* a direct summation of individuals' competencies — organizational dynamics can raise or lower overall proficiency and performance.
- **Most models converge on three things, diverge on domain.** SE competency models generally agree that systems thinking, a holistic full-life-cycle view, and knowledge of both technical and managerial SE methods are required for a fully capable systems engineer, and that an accomplished SE has expertise in at least one domain of practice. General models recognize the need for domain knowledge but usually don't specify domain competencies; most organizations tailor models to add domain KSAAs and organizational peculiarities.
- **No community consensus on a single model.** No consensus exists on one model or a small related set. Many models are context- or organization-specific (e.g., the Aerospace Industry Competency Model from ETA/AIA/NDIA; the NASA CMS Dictionary of domain-specific expertise). Users must know a model's development method and context before adopting it.
- **Certification is a positive indicator, not a guarantee.** INCOSE certification provides formal recognition of competency in specific areas (demonstrated through education, experience, knowledge), requiring a test, SE experience, and recommendations. Like all such credentials, it does not guarantee competence or suitability for a particular role, but is a positive indicator and an acknowledged common baseline.
- **Determining context before competencies.** Before knowing what SE competencies it needs, an organization should examine its situation — environment, history, strategy. The MITRE framework characterizing levels of systems complexity (from the traditional program domain to the messy frontier) helps identify which competencies are needed; an organization should weigh both current situation and forward strategy (e.g., a planned transition into enterprise systems engineering may require building a competency model for both the old and the future context).
- **Quality of the competency assessment.** Where *application* serves as the measure of competency, a measure of goodness is essential: applying a competency in an exceptionally complex situation is not necessarily successful, and managing or guiding only helps if done well. An individual may be fully proficient yet never given the opportunity to use a competency — context must be understood when assessing.
- **Closing competency gaps (two steps).** To identify gaps, an organization lists desired competencies (per *Roles and Competencies*) and assesses individuals' competencies (per *Assessing Individuals*), then selects development methods. Systems thinking, in particular, is developed primarily through experiential learning (Davidz 2006; Davidz and Nightingale 2008) — for example, working in an integration-and-test environment that deals with the full system.
- **Filters — selecting people for systems roles.** An organization may select employees for systems roles based on characteristics ("filters"). Before filtering it should examine how the list of characteristics was determined and how each enables a systems job; a characteristic that is *necessary* is much stronger than one that merely *enables*, and findings from one organization may not generalize to another.
- **Repositioning the product life cycle.** Because individual systems competencies are primarily developed through experiential learning, shortening the product life cycle is one way to ensure individuals acquire the full range of desired competency sooner (e.g., NASA's APPEL approach).
- **Maintaining the competency plan.** A competency plan must be maintained — re-examined and updated periodically — to keep pace with the continual evolution of global contexts, of business strategies, and of the SEBoK itself; assessing and improving competencies should be part of normal operations.
- **Ethics versus morals.** *Morals* are guiding personal thoughts and feelings about right and wrong — part of a person's character, formed by upbringing and culture, and usually considered immutable. *Ethics* are standards shared within a social system (professional, business, academic, and others) that say what conduct is appropriate; ethical judgement may have to address novel situations as a person's profession or role changes (Whitbeck 2007). Tensions among responsibilities to society, customer, employer, or family create ethical dilemmas.
- **The systems engineer's two extra ethical responsibilities.** Beyond most engineering professions, SE (1) helps *determine* customer needs and desires while defining and managing requirements, creating an obligation to ensure problem/program definition is influenced solely by the customer's or user's interests — not the engineer's or the firm's; and (2) integrates and oversees the work of others whose expertise differs, making the obligation to widen one's understanding and seek competent advice more acute.
- **Where ethics figures in modern systems.** The article samples areas: *data confidentiality and security, surveillance, and privacy* (systems touching Personally Identifiable Information, PII); *laws and regulations* (which impose system requirements whether or not stated, including safeguarding others' intellectual property — trade secrets, copyrights, trademarks, patents); and *cultural issues* (awareness of societal diversity, acting without prejudice or discrimination for products used by humans globally).
- **Codes of ethics and the INCOSE Code of Ethics.** Codes are promulgated by the IEEE, NSPE, INCOSE, and others. The INCOSE Code of Ethics states principles (honesty, impartiality, integrity, keeping abreast of knowledge, increasing competence, supporting professional organizations) and the systems engineer's duties: guard the public interest and protect environment, safety, and welfare; accept responsibility and be open to ethical scrutiny; proactively mitigate unsafe practice; manage risk using a whole-system viewpoint and understanding of systemic interfaces; and promote understanding and acceptance of prudent SE measures.
- **Enforcing ethics.** Organizations enforce ethics through policies — typical rules forbid exchange of favors with suppliers/customers/regulators, require accurate and complete reporting of product information (e.g., test data) to the contracting agency, and forbid conflicts of interest. Because transgressions can be hard to define, policies must be designed to be *observable and enforceable*; internal audit or external regulators may enforce at the individual, team, organizational, or enterprise level, with punishments up to termination.

## Mental Models
- **Roles pull, KSAAs push.** A role is a set of tasks that *pulls* certain competencies into existence; KSAAs are what an individual *pushes* up against that demand. Competency is the match between the two. To staff a role, ask what tasks it owns, what competencies those tasks need, and which KSAAs deliver them.
- **Competency ≠ capability ≠ capacity ≠ performance.** When SE isn't getting done well, don't stop at "the engineer isn't competent." Performance is a function of competency *and* capability (processes, tools, equipment) *and* capacity (time and other limits). A brilliant risk manager in a team that ignores the talent still produces poor performance.
- **The organization is the unit that must be complete — not the person.** No individual is proficient in everything, so the test is whether the team/business collectively covers the needed competency set at sufficient proficiency. And because of organizational dynamics, the whole is not the sum of the parts.
- **Read both Bloom domains.** Cognitive levels (Remember → Create) tell you the intellectual difficulty of a KSAA; affective levels (Receive → Characterize) tell you whether the person *cares* and is *characterized* by systemic thinking. A competency model that skips the affective dimension misses willingness and disposition — which gate everything cognitive.
- **Proficiency depends on the situation, not just the verb.** "Apply" a method on a simple subsystem is supervised-practitioner work; "apply" the same method to a system-of-systems may be expert work. Set proficiency by KSAA-in-context, not by the Bloom verb alone.
- **Experience grows systems thinking; classrooms alone don't.** Since systems thinking develops primarily through experiential learning, the highest-leverage interventions put people on the full system (integration and test, job rotation, hands-on assignments). Training has a role, but experience is the engine — which is why shortening the product life cycle can accelerate competency.
- **Necessary beats enabling when filtering.** Before selecting people by traits, ask whether each trait is *necessary* for the systems job or merely *enables* it — and whether the finding even generalizes from the organization that produced it. A list that predicts success in one company may not in another.
- **"Attitudes" is the ethical hinge of KSAA.** The "A" for attitudes inside KSAA already carries an ethical dimension, so ethics is not bolted onto competency — it is part of it. The ethical framework is what ensures the systems engineer ultimately does good and not harm.
- **In SE, defining the problem is itself an ethical act.** Because SE shapes what the customer's needs even *are*, the systems engineer can quietly bias the program toward the firm's interests. The discipline of letting problem definition be driven solely by the customer/user is an SE-specific ethical guardrail.

## Anti-patterns
The KA does not present a labeled anti-pattern table, but it explicitly names several failure modes to avoid:
- **Treating individual competency as sufficient for performance.** "Developing individual competencies is not enough to ensure exemplary SE performance" — context (capability, capacity, flawed procedures, an organization that ignores the talent) can preclude exemplary performance even of a high competency.
- **Using a competency model out of its context.** Adopting a model without understanding its development method and context risks applying competencies primary for one organization to another where they don't fit; users should be aware of the context surrounding each model.
- **Using Bloom levels alone to set proficiency.** Bloom's domain levels ought not be the sole basis for setting proficiency levels," because higher-level cognitive capabilities belong across proficiency levels and depend on the KSAA and its context.
- **Filtering on traits without validating them.** Using a list of individual characteristics as a filter without examining how the list was derived, whether each trait is necessary versus merely enabling, and whether it generalizes.
- **Unobservable, unenforceable ethics policies.** Because conflict of interest and other transgressions can be hard to define, policies that are not designed to be observable and enforceable fail in practice.
- **Letting SE self-interest shape requirements.** Allowing the systems engineer's or the firm's interests — rather than the customer's or user's — to influence problem or program definition.

## Key Takeaways
1. Part 5 enables SE at three organizational levels — business/enterprise, team, and individual — and this KA is the individual level: how individuals are given SE roles, assessed, developed, and held to ethical standards.
2. SE responsibility is allocated through *roles* (sets of tasks); *competencies* are the abilities those roles need, and *KSAA* (knowledge, skills, abilities, attitudes) are what the individual brings to fulfill them.
3. Final SE performance is a function of *competency, capability, and capacity* — never competency alone; capability includes processes, tools, and equipment, and capacity accounts for limits like time.
4. Bloom's Taxonomy (cognitive: Remember→Create; affective: Receive→Characterize) gives a standard structure for writing KSAA statements and defining proficiency, and both domains belong in an SE competency model.
5. No single SE competency model has community consensus; published references include the INCOSE UK model (systems thinking / holistic life cycle view / SE management), the DAU ENG model (Analytical / Technical Management / Professional / Business Acumen, 41 areas), and NASA APPEL (System Design / Product Realization / Technical Management) — each tied to its own context.
6. Most models converge on systems thinking, a holistic full-life-cycle view, and technical-plus-managerial SE methods, with at least one domain of expertise; INCOSE certification is a positive indicator and common baseline, not a guarantee of competence.
7. Assessing individuals starts with determining organizational *context* (e.g., the MITRE complexity framework) and required *roles and competencies*, then rates competency via proficiency-level scales — but competency must be distinguished from performance, which context can suppress.
8. To develop individuals, close competency gaps by listing desired competencies, assessing individuals, then selecting methods (training, job rotation, mentoring, hands-on experience, hand-selection, education/certificate programs, certification); systems thinking is grown primarily through experiential learning.
9. The "attitudes" in KSAA carries an ethical dimension; ethics (shared social-system standards) differ from morals (immutable personal character), and SE carries two extra ethical duties — keeping problem definition driven solely by the customer/user, and seeking competent advice across disciplines it integrates.
10. The INCOSE Code of Ethics and codes from IEEE and NSPE define duties to guard the public interest and safety; organizations enforce ethics through observable, enforceable policies covering favors, accurate reporting, and conflicts of interest.
