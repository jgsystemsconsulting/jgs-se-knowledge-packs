# Chapter 14: Process Selection and Tailoring

> Source note: synthesized from SEBoK v2.14, Knowledge Area "Process Selection and Tailoring" (lead author David Endler; contributing authors Mike Yokell and Garry Roedler), which contains three articles — *Guidelines for the Selection and Tailoring of Processes*, *Using ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2*, and *Tailoring*. SEBoK is licensed CC BY-NC-SA; this chapter paraphrases and restructures the source rather than reproducing it. Exact SEBoK article names, standard designators, and term naming are preserved.

## Core Idea
Choosing which life cycle processes to apply across a system's life — and at what level of rigor — is treated here as a *strategic* organizational challenge, not a clerical one. No standard process set fits every project off the shelf, so two complementary moves are needed: **selecting** appropriate processes from a recognized framework, and **tailoring** them to a project's specific context, risks, and constraints. The Knowledge Area anchors both moves to ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (the system life cycle processes standard) and to ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2 (its application guideline). The recurring theme is balance: enough rigor to manage risk, enough agility to avoid waste, with every tailoring decision documented and traceable so later stakeholders understand *why* the process looks the way it does.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023 (system life cycle processes)** — the foundational standard for life cycle processes, organized into four process groups — Agreement; Organizational Project-Enabling; Technical Management; and Technical Processes.
  - When/how to use: as the shared reference framework a project or organization starts from. The source stresses 15288 is deliberately short and concise, and explicitly does *not* prescribe a particular life cycle model, development methodology, method, modelling approach, or technique (Clause 1) — its processes can be applied alongside agile and other techniques, whether or not they are tailored.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2:2024 (guidelines for applying 15288)** — the practical companion that elaborates how to *apply* the concise 15288. Where 15288 states the processes, 24748-2 develops seven application aspects: application strategy; application of system concepts; of life cycle concepts; of organizational concepts; of project concepts; of process concepts; and of conformance and adaptation concepts.
  - When/how to use: as the go-to guidance when an organization or project needs to operationalize 15288 — defining a strategy, scoping the system-of-interest, working the life cycle stages and decision gates, and adapting processes for conformance.
- **The 24748-2 Application Strategy (five steps)** — a defined sequence for putting 15288 into use, whether for a single project, for improving an organization's processes, or for embedding life cycle processes inside a larger process such as acquisition: (1) plan the application, (2) adapt 15288 if applicable, (3) conduct pilot project(s), (4) formalize the approach, and (5) institutionalize the approach.
  - When/how to use: as the rollout playbook when introducing or revising a process framework — pilot before mandating, then formalize and institutionalize what works.
- **Domain-overlay model of standards** — 15288 positioned as a foundation that domain-specific standards extend or sit on top of. Named overlays include ASPICE, ISO 26262, RTCA DO-178C, RTCA DO-254, IEC 62304, and ISO 13485; named framework-style companions with specific focuses include ISO/IEC 27000, NIST 800-160, and SAE 1001.
  - When/how to use: start from 15288 as the base, then overlay the domain-specific standard(s) required to meet regulatory or certification obligations in a given sector (automotive, avionics, medical, security).
- **PMI Scaling Factors (project context factors)** — the source cites seven factors that shape tailoring decisions: team size; geographic spread; organizational spread; availability of skills; compliance; domain complexity; and solution complexity.
  - When/how to use: as a checklist of dials when deciding how much process a project needs. The source adds further factors worth weighing — stakeholder landscape, budget and schedule, risk tolerance, and project criticality (safety, mission, etc.).
- **The rigor-balance model (Figure 1 in the source)** — tailoring framed as steering between two failure modes: *too little rigor*, which drives up the risk of technical problems, schedule slippage, and cost overruns; and *too much rigor*, which piles on process overhead, cuts flexibility, and drives cost up. (Figure attributed to an INCOSE SEH figure by Krueger, adapted from Salter 2003.)
  - When/how to use: as the orienting mental picture for every tailoring decision — the goal is the appropriate point between the two extremes for the project's risk profile.

## Key Concepts
- **Core principles of selection**: the source lists five — *risk and context* (rigor should match complexity, criticality, and regulatory environment; a safety-critical avionics system warrants far more than a consumer TV screen); *tailoring* (expect it, plan for it upfront, document the rationale — a framework with tailoring guidelines beats a single uniform process); *traceability* (most systems demand end-to-end links among requirements, design, implementation, and verification results); *tool and data integration* (processes connecting to PLM/ALM/CM tools enable automation, audit trails, and reporting); and *governance and accountability* (clear ownership, change control boards, and role definitions prevent fragmentation).
- **Human and organizational factors**: process quality is bounded by the people executing it. The KA highlights *competence management* (skills matrices, role training, staff certification), *culture and change management* (adoption tracks company culture; new methods need structured change management), and *communication interfaces* (multi-discipline systems need defined communication rules and cross-disciplinary forums).
- **Knowledge and lessons learned**: processes should build in *organizational learning* (capturing lessons learned, good practices, and failure analyses) and govern *reuse and reference architectures* (reusable components, design patterns, product and process reference architectures cut risk and cost — but only when processes explicitly encourage and govern reuse).
- **Good practices for effective processes**: among those named — *lightweight process frameworks* (build around core processes such as requirements, architecture, integration, verification, validation, configuration, and risk management; overly detailed definitions get ignored; the right process "weight" depends on mission criticality and risk); *risk-based verification* (scale test rigor and independence to risk); *supplier governance*; *training and coaching* with process champions; *measurement and feedback loops* (data on defects, requirements churn, lead times); *management support* (when leadership demands compliance, debates about necessity evaporate); *pilot projects*; *process assurance* (life cycle processes guard against unintended omission, unnecessary activity, and lack of discipline).
- **Effectiveness before efficiency**: a stated good practice and recurring principle — a process must be *effective* before it is made *efficient*. Getting the wrong answer faster or cheaper is no win.
- **Tailoring is dynamic, not one-time**: tailoring is revisited throughout the life cycle as risks and circumstances evolve. As trust grows or risk and complexity fall, activities can be simplified; when risks rise, rigor can be reintroduced.
- **Fact-based tailoring**: decisions should rest on facts, evidence, and risk assessment rather than intuition, ideally with independent review to counter bias, and recorded through structured Decision Management so alternatives, trade-offs, and rationale leave a traceable record.
- **Documenting assumptions and rationale**: every tailoring choice should be justified and recorded — e.g., reducing the number of formal design reviews should carry a documented reason (high team cohesion, proven element maturity) so audits and project transitions can reconstruct the reasoning.
- **Integration with project planning**: tailoring runs in parallel with planning because scope, schedule, budget, milestones, and resourcing are tightly coupled to which processes are chosen and how they are applied (aggressive schedules may demand automated testing; budget limits may cap prototypes).
- **Concurrency, iteration, and recursion**: life cycle processes are applied concurrently, iteratively, and recursively — requirements definition, architecture definition, and verification may proceed simultaneously and in multiple cycles, with processes applied recursively across the system structure to maintain coherence and traceability.
- **Methods and tools serve the process**: 15288 prescribes no specific tools; the KA's guidance is process-first — purpose-driven selection (the tool supports the tailored process, not the reverse), connectivity with other tools, adequate training, and engineer judgment (tools are aids, not substitutes for critical thinking). Tool choice should also account for whether the needed data can be obtained and verified.

## Mental Models
- **Match the rigor to the risk.** Process weight is a dial, not a default. Set it by the system's complexity, criticality, and regulatory environment — avionics gets more, a TV screen gets less — and revisit the setting as risk changes.
- **Tailoring will happen; design for it.** Since no project adopts the standard process set off the shelf, treat tailoring as an expected, governed activity with documented rationale, not an exception to be discovered late.
- **Start from the base standard, overlay the domain.** Use 15288 as the concise foundation and 24748-2 as the application guide, then lay domain-specific standards (ISO 26262, DO-178C, IEC 62304, and the like) on top to satisfy certification needs.
- **Pilot, formalize, institutionalize.** Don't mandate a new process framework by decree — run it through representative pilot projects first, then formalize and embed what proves out (the 24748-2 strategy sequence).
- **Effective first, efficient second.** Optimize a process only once it produces the right result. Speed and cost savings on a process that gives wrong answers are negative value.
- **Write down why.** A tailoring decision without recorded rationale is a liability at the next audit or handover — the documented reason is what makes the choice defensible and reversible.
- **Process before tool; judgment before automation.** Select tools to serve the tailored process and keep engineer judgment in the loop; a tool that dictates the process, or that substitutes for thinking, inverts the relationship.

## Anti-patterns
The *Tailoring* article names four **common tailoring traps** explicitly:
- **Reusing baselines blindly** — lifting a tailored process from a different project without revisiting its assumptions.
- **Over-engineering** — applying every process "just to be safe," producing needless cost and delay.
- **Assuming uniformity** — believing the same risks or controls apply across all projects.
- **Failing to engage stakeholders** — excluding key voices, which erodes buy-in and breeds conflict.

The KA also names tool-and-method pitfalls (lack of integration creating silos and rework; security and data-management complications; heavy training burdens; over-reliance on tools eroding independent judgment) and the data-quality failure captured as "garbage in, garbage out." Under "myths about processes," it rebuts the misconception that 15288 prescribes a methodology — it does not, and its processes can be used with agile and other techniques.

## Key Takeaways
1. Process selection across the life cycle is a strategic challenge; ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2 is the source's recommended resource for meeting it, applied on top of ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288.
2. Five core principles govern selection: risk and context, tailoring, traceability, tool and data integration, and governance and accountability.
3. 15288 is deliberately concise and prescribes no life cycle model, methodology, method, or technique; 24748-2 elaborates the seven application aspects (strategy, system, life cycle, organizational, project, process, conformance and adaptation).
4. The 24748-2 application strategy is a five-step rollout: plan, adapt if applicable, pilot, formalize, institutionalize.
5. Treat 15288 as a foundation and overlay domain-specific standards (ASPICE, ISO 26262, DO-178C, DO-254, IEC 62304, ISO 13485) or framework companions (ISO/IEC 27000, NIST 800-160, SAE 1001) to meet regulatory and certification needs.
6. Tailoring scales rigor to project risk, complexity, and constraints; it is dynamic and revisited across the life cycle, balancing the twin dangers of too little and too much rigor.
7. PMI Scaling Factors (team size, geographic and organizational distribution, skill availability, compliance, domain complexity, solution complexity) — plus stakeholder landscape, budget/schedule, risk tolerance, and criticality — drive tailoring decisions.
8. Good tailoring is fact-based, documented (assumptions and rationale recorded via Decision Management), stakeholder-collaborative, integrated with planning, and dynamically monitored via Project Assessment and Control.
9. Processes must be effective before efficient; lightweight frameworks built around core processes, backed by management support and pilots, outperform exhaustive uniform process definitions.
10. Avoid the four named traps — blind baseline reuse, over-engineering, assuming uniformity, and failing to engage stakeholders — and keep tools subordinate to the process and to engineer judgment.
