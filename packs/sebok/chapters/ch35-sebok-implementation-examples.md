# Chapter — Systems Engineering Implementation Examples

*Source: SEBoK v2.14, Part 7: Systems Engineering Implementation Examples (Lead Authors: Heidi Davidz and Richard Turner; with constituent examples and the Matrix of Implementation Examples by Heidi Davidz, Alice Squires, Tom Hilburn, Alex Lee, John Brackett, Richard Turner, Rick Adcock, Brian White, Art Pyster, Tom Hilburn, Chuck Calvano, Phyllis Marbach, W. Clifton Baldwin, Anthony Long, and others).*

## Core Idea

Part 7 is not a body of new theory — it is the SEBoK's evidence locker. Where Parts 1–6 lay out the principles, processes, and disciplines of systems engineering, Part 7 collects concrete instances where those principles were applied (or neglected) in real programs, so that students, educators, and practitioners can see both the payoff of good practice and the cost of poor practice. The pedagogical bet, borrowed explicitly from how medicine, law, and business have taught for decades, is that contextual knowledge — knowledge bound to a specific situation — is the most usable form of knowledge, and that case-study teaching is how an organization shares it so lessons can be re-applied. NASA's own rationale, quoted in the KA, is that organizational learning happens when knowledge is shared in usable, contextual ways.

Two things distinguish a Part 7 example from a war story. First, each example is deliberately *mapped back* into the rest of the SEBoK: a published case study is summarized by the BKCASE team, linked to its original source, and then tied to the specific SEBoK topics it illustrates via the **Matrix of Implementation Examples**. Second, the examples are chosen to span sources, domains, and geographies, and to show *both* effective and ineffective use of SE — the FBI Virtual Case File failure sits in the same collection as the GPS triumph on purpose. The intent is typicality, not heroics: ordinary instances of SE done well or badly, each anchored to the canon so a reader can follow the thread from "what happened here" to "which principle this teaches."

A recurring meta-theme runs through the modern examples: traditional, document-centric, reductionist SE is necessary but increasingly insufficient as systems become complex, interconnected, evolving, and socio-technical. Several examples (GPS II, the Russian taxi and project-management schedulers, the ICCPM complex-projects case, NextGen) are included precisely to argue that system of systems engineering (SoSE), enterprise systems engineering (ESE), complex (adaptive) systems engineering (CSE), and model-based approaches are where the field is heading.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)

- **Friedman-Sage framework** (Friedman and Sage 2003; 2004) — the structuring device behind the US Air Force Center for Systems Engineering (AF CSE) case studies. It decomposes a case into contractor, government, and shared responsibilities across nine concept areas: Requirements Definition and Management; Systems Architecture Development; System/Subsystem Design; Verification/Validation; Risk Management; Systems Integration and Interfaces; Life Cycle Support; Deployment and Post Deployment; and System and Program Management. Used to analyze the **Hubble Space Telescope** and **Global Positioning System** cases. GPS II critiques the framework as too narrow — only two contractual stakeholders and a largely linear life cycle, with only risk (not opportunity) considered.
- **Learning Principles (LPs)** (Friedman and Sage 2005) — the device by which each AF CSE/AFIT case distills broadly applicable SE knowledge into a handful of numbered principles that point back to specific SEBoK areas. SWFTS derives seven (requirements traceability, communications, productivity, quality, managing change, managing variants, life cycle); GPS derives four (enabling individuals, configuration management, enabling the organization, risk management); HST derives five (stakeholder requirements definition, planning via pre-program trade studies, system integration, life cycle model management, risk management).
- **Case Study Profiler** (SEBoK Original) — a 2×2 quad chart placing each case study by *complexity* (x-axis: complicated → complex) and *engineering difficulty* (y-axis: Conventional SE → Complex SE, where the upper range covers SoSE, ESE, and CSE). An admitted oversimplification, used as a first-order characterization of each case.
- **Incremental Commitment Spiral Model (ICSM)** — the risk-driven, evolutionary process model used in the **Next Generation Medical Infusion Pump (SymbiqTM)** case, progressing through Exploration, Valuation, Foundations, and Development phases, each closed by a Commitment Review. The case notes terminology evolution (earlier "ICM," "architecting phase" rather than "foundations phase").
- **The "V model" / Vee (V) model** — invoked in two contrasting ways: as the SE-process spine of the Russian IBS business transformation (Stages 1–7, deliberately run partly in parallel rather than strictly linearly), and as the "conventional SE V-process enriched by a model-based paradigm" (the JPL Model-based V Process) in the **Thirty-Meter Telescope (TMT)** MBSE case.
- **Multi-agent technology / Virtual World + Real World architecture** (Rzevski and Skobelev 2014) — the complex-adaptive design pattern shared by the **London Taxi Service Scheduler** and the **Russian Space Agency Project Management** cases: a Knowledge Base (Ontology + Values), a Multi-agent Virtual World modeling the Real World, and communication channels linking them, with autonomous agents (Order/Driver, Task/Human Resource/Project/Enterprise) negotiating allocations.
- **Executable Systems Engineering Method (ESEM)** and **OpenMBEE** (TMT case) — ESEM augments the **Object-Oriented Systems Engineering Method (OOSEM)** by injecting formal, executable SysML modeling patterns; OpenMBEE's Model Management System (MMS) provides the open tooling backbone for TMT's Alignment and Phasing System (APS) and NFIRAOS.
- **DBSE → MBSE transition** (SWFTS case) — the explicit framing of moving from document-based systems engineering (DBSE) to model-based systems engineering (MBSE), validated by running both in parallel and tracing every difference to root cause.
- **Six systemic frameworks for complex project management** (Findlay and Straus 2015) — systems thinking; the features of complex adaptive systems (CASs); complexity theory itself; and the Complexity Model of Change; polarity thinking; and cognitive complexity / adult development theory. Supported by three theorems of complexity theory — the **robust model axiom** (Conant and Ashby 1970), the **law of requisite variety** (Ashby 1956), and the **scale condition** (Bar-Yam 2004) — plus triple-loop learning and the **Zing complex adaptive meeting process** with its "talk-type-read-review" rule of interaction.
- **Four provisioning paradigms of SEBoK Part 4** — the engineering of product systems, of enterprise systems, of service systems, and of systems of systems — applied as four analytic lenses on the **Northwest Hydro System**.
- **Capability-based development / capability-based architecting** and **agile program management** — the recommended best practices distilled from the **Russian IBS business transformation** for creating or transforming an enterprise system or SoS.
- **Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)**, guided by **ISO 14971:2000** for medical device design — the safety-risk-analysis technique elaborated phase-by-phase in the SymbiqTM case and named (by its absence on the ground) in the Apollo 1 lessons.
- **DoD Architecture Framework (DoDAF)** and the **Federal Enterprise Architecture (FEA)** — used to describe the NextGen enterprise architecture (Operational, Systems, and Technical Standards Views; AV-1, AV-2, OV-1/2/3/5/6c, SV-4/5).
- **Reliability, Availability, Maintainability, and Safety (RAMS)** with **FRACAS** (Failure Reporting and Corrective Action System) — the engineering focus integrated into all phases of the **Standard Korean Light Transit System (SKLTS)**.
- **Agile Practices mapped to LEAN Disciplines** (Marbach) — the product-backlog/user-story/sprint structure used to reverse-engineer artifacts for a UAV prototype, explicitly mapped to seven LEAN disciplines.

## Key Concepts

- **Examples are categorized by who performs the SE.** Because organizational character shapes the engineering, examples are grouped as government, commercial, and "combined," and within the SEBoK they are also organized by domain: Defense, Information, Management, Medical, Space, Transportation, and Utilities (with Construction, Cyber-Physical, and Geospatial marked "Coming Soon").
- **The Matrix maps examples to topics both ways.** It lets a reader either find which examples illustrate a given SEBoK topic, or find which topics a given example illustrates — a deliberate two-way index across topics like Systems Thinking, Requirements, Architecture, V&V, Risk Management, Configuration Management, and Enabling SE.
- **Good practice has a recognizable signature.** Across the success cases, the same moves recur: staff key positions with domain experts (GPS LP1); rigorously maintain functional/program baselines (GPS LP2); secure consistent high-level advocacy for funding stability (GPS LP3); apply disciplined risk management across the life cycle (GPS LP4); engage the customer early and institutionalize their role (HST LP1); use pre-program trade studies to explore alternatives (HST LP2); treat life-cycle support as a day-one design driver (HST LP4); and buy information to reduce risk via incremental commitment rather than rushing requirements (SymbiqTM/ICSM).
- **Failure has an equally recognizable signature.** The FBI VCF, FAA AAS, and Denver baggage cases share a cluster of named pathologies: poorly defined and continuously changing requirements; schedule driven by what was *desired* rather than what was *possible*; inadequate enterprise architecture; weak or discontinuous management oversight; insufficient integration of work threads developed in parallel; and absent or untested back-out paths (the FBI's proposed "flash cut-over" with no return to ACS being the sharpest illustration).
- **The rising cost of late defect correction is made literal.** The HST case quantifies it: a test-fixture error fixable for ~$1,000 at design, or detectable for ~$10 million via an end-to-end ground test of the telescope, instead cost ~$1 billion to correct on orbit. SWFTS reinforces the same economics with measured data — an 18% shift of defect discovery from platform integration to laboratory integration, where eradication is estimated 1.6×–4× cheaper.
- **MBSE delivers measurable ROI, not just elegance.** The SWFTS retrospective compared a 2010 DOORS-managed baseline to a 2014 MBSE baseline of similar complexity: the MBSE baseline handled 42% more interface-requirement changes while consuming only 16% more hours — SE hours per requirement falling from 12.1 to 9.9, an 18% efficiency gain — alongside a 9% reduction in interface defects. The model's captured relationships also enable automated integrity checking ("model concordance") and on-demand generation of tailored artifacts.
- **Complex adaptive systems demand a different toolkit entirely.** The taxi and Russian-space-agency schedulers state outright that the complexity ruled out *all* conventional SE/PM practices; multi-agent negotiation, short scheduling cycles, dynamic commitment times, forecasting, and even cheat-detection were required. The taxi system's results — 98.5% of orders allocated automatically, idle runs down 22.5%, investment repaid in two months — are presented as the payoff of treating the problem as complex rather than complicated.
- **Single points of failure (SPOFs) and interface management dominate the space-mission cautionary tales.** Cassini/Huygens nearly lost the Titan survey to an unmanaged orbiter-lander radio interface — a poorly-characterized SPOF whose Doppler-shift flaw went undetected because NASA was treated as a competitor and details were cloaked behind propriety. The lesson generalizes: every partition introduces friction and surface energy at the interface (Vinarcik 2014, citing Clausewitz), and "rainy day" scenarios deserve as much early analysis as "sunny day" ones.
- **Standardization and openness pay off architecturally.** The **Virginia Class Submarine** sonar case shows the shift from proprietary to commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) interfaces cutting historically procured parts by 60% on the lead ship, and yields a concrete set of **architecture evaluation metrics** spanning commonality, modularity, interfaces, standards-based openness, and RMT.
- **Software-centric safety decisions are systems decisions.** The **Therac-25** (Medical Radiation) accidents — five deaths — trace to a systems-engineering decision to expand software responsibility for safety while removing hardware interlocks, leaving software a single point of failure that race conditions could trigger; the 1983 fault tree omitted software failures entirely.
- **Systems thinking can simplify, not just complicate.** The **Design for Maintainability** (NeoNurture) example flips the usual framing: designing an incubator to *break in a non-catastrophic way*, built from locally abundant automobile parts and locally available repair knowledge, was a more practical solution than a more capable but unmaintainable device — addressing the finding that ~95% of donated medical technology breaks within five years in developing-country contexts.

## Mental Models

- **Case as mirror, not monument.** Read each example to recognize a pattern you can re-apply, not to admire a program. The KA's framing is that learning comes from emphasizing the long-term consequences of SE and programmatic decisions on cost, schedule, and operational effectiveness — both the good consequences and the bad.
- **Trace the decision, then trace the cost forward.** HST, SWFTS, and Cassini/Huygens all reward the same habit: locate the pivotal decision (test-fixture spec, removing interlocks, partitioning the radio link) and follow its cost as it compounds across later life-cycle phases. Defects get exponentially more expensive to fix the later they are found.
- **Optimize the whole system, accept "good" over "optimal" parts.** The MSTI spacecraft built itself in one year by optimizing across the whole project rather than allowing subsystem-level sub-optimizations ("building Porsches, not Formula 1s"), reusing hardware architectures, using a hardware-in-the-loop test bed, and empowering Responsible Engineering Authorities to make more *good* decisions rather than chase optimal ones.
- **Architecture is stable; designs instantiate it.** GPS II argues a good architecture, once set, should guide development and change little, while the designs instantiating it evolve as understanding grows — and warns against confusing the two. It also contrasts reductionism (attention to subsystems and their interfaces) with holism (concentrating on whole-system behavior and emergence).
- **Capabilities are emergent, not assigned to a box.** The Russian IBS transformation found that capabilities could not be mapped onto any single business-agent (not BUs, not projects, not employees); they emerged from integrated end-to-end processes and from intangibles — governing media, culture, relationships, competences — supported by new capability-support systems.
- **Manage polarities, don't "solve" wicked problems.** In complex projects, treat interdependent tensions (centralize for efficiency vs. decentralize for adaptability) as polarities to be balanced over time rather than problems with a single optimal answer; pursuing one pole at the expense of the other yields sub-optimal results.
- **Influence a living system; you cannot control it.** The ICCPM complex-projects case reframes large projects as CASs you exert influence over by continually updating your model and learning new ways to act — not machines you control. The vivid image: flying a plane while it is being rebuilt into a different vehicle, mid-air, with passengers boarding who demand better service at lower cost.
- **Buy information to reduce risk.** SymbiqTM's incremental-commitment posture treats early money as the purchase of risk reduction — prototyping, usability testing, and FMEAs at each phase before committing — rather than rushing requirements to capture market share. Many organizations do the opposite and pay later.
- **Apply SE late if you must — it still helps.** The UK West Coast Route Modernisation and the Reverse-Engineering-a-UAV cases both show value from introducing SE practices *after* problems emerge or *after* a prototype already flies: fixing scope, instituting governance, and reverse-engineering requirements/hazard analyses/trace matrices iteratively.

## Anti-patterns (only if named)

- **Flash cut-over with no fallback** (FBI VCF) — proposing to replace the legacy Automated Case Support system with the untested Virtual Case File over a single weekend, with no mechanism to return to ACS even if VCF failed.
- **"Clone and own" variant management** (SWFTS LP6) — the common DBSE practice of forking the current baseline for each new product-family member, which makes synchronizing core-baseline changes across the family painfully labor-intensive; the model-based alternative treats variants as deviations from a core baseline so changes propagate automatically.
- **Schedule focused on what is desired, not what is possible** (FBI VCF) — accelerating to a politically acceptable timeline, skipping formal requirements definition and validation under a cost-plus contract whose scope was undefined, with ~80% scope growth and ~400 change requests once software became demonstrable.
- **Testing milestones skipped or shortcutted** (FAA AAS) — building new software on the assumption that previously developed (but untested) software had been tested and performed, atop an extraordinary volume of requirements changes (over 500 ISSS changes in 1990 forcing 150,000 lines rewritten).
- **Overconfidence in software and removal of hardware interlocks** (Therac-25) — making software the safety arbiter while eliminating the mechanical interlocks of the predecessor Therac-20, then trusting unrealistic risk assessments that excluded software failure modes; compounded by no mechanism to follow up reported incidents.
- **Politically-driven partitioning and information hoarding** (Cassini/Huygens) — introducing an unnecessary organizational interface for diplomatic reasons, then withholding full communications specifications from a partner treated as a competitor, preventing detection of a trivially-fixable-before-launch firmware flaw.
- **Sunny-day-only hazard analysis** (Apollo 1) — analyzing hazards only for known in-flight phases (liftoff, max-q, staging) while ranking the ground "plugs-out" test as low risk; no FMEA was conducted for the prelaunch/on-the-ground situation, and no one seriously considered an in-capsule fire.

## Key Takeaways

- Part 7 exists to **illustrate, link, and teach** — it summarizes real (often externally published) case studies, ties each to the specific SEBoK topics it demonstrates via the **Matrix of Implementation Examples**, and deliberately includes both effective and ineffective SE so the canon is grounded in evidence.
- The **AF CSE/AFIT cases** (HST, GPS) are structured by the **Friedman-Sage framework**'s nine concept areas and distilled into numbered **Learning Principles**; GPS II then critiques that framework as too narrow for SoSE/ESE/CSE realities.
- **Defect-correction cost rises steeply with life-cycle phase** — HST's $1K→$10M→$1B progression is the canonical illustration, and SWFTS quantifies the inverse payoff of shifting defect discovery earlier (to laboratory integration).
- **MBSE is justified by measured ROI** (SWFTS: 18% efficiency gain, 9% fewer interface defects, model concordance, on-demand tailored artifacts) and by precision and cross-domain integration (TMT via ESEM/OpenMBEE/SysML), not by elegance alone.
- **Failure cases converge on the same root causes** — unstable/ill-defined requirements, schedule driven by desire over feasibility, missing enterprise architecture, weak management continuity and oversight, poor integration of parallel work, and absent fallback/testing (FBI VCF, FAA AAS, Denver baggage).
- **Complex, adaptive, and socio-technical systems need non-traditional SE** — multi-agent Virtual/Real-World schedulers (taxi, Russian space agency), complexity-theory-grounded project management (ICCPM), and enterprise transformation via capability-based development and agile program management (IBS) all declare conventional SE/PM insufficient.
- **Interfaces, single points of failure, and information sharing** make or break programs — Cassini/Huygens and Apollo 1 show how unmanaged interfaces, uncharacterized SPOFs, withheld information, and sunny-day-only analysis nearly (or did) cause disaster.
- **Standardization, openness, and maintainability** are first-class architectural levers — Virginia Class (COTS, 60% parts reduction, architecture evaluation metrics) and Design for Maintainability (NeoNurture's break-gracefully, build-from-local-parts ethos) show systems thinking simplifying as well as scaling.
- **The four Part-4 provisioning paradigms** (product, service, enterprise, SoS) together give a comprehensive view of a very large complex system (Northwest Hydro System) that no single paradigm could capture.
