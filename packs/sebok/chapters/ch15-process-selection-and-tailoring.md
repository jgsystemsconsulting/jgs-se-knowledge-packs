# Chapter 15: Process Selection and Tailoring

## Core Idea
Tailoring is the disciplined adaptation of ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 life cycle processes to a project's specific context, risks, and stakeholder environment—balancing rigor against overhead to ensure processes remain fit for purpose throughout the project lifecycle.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **PMI Scaling Factors**: A structured method for deciding how to tailor processes based on project context.
  - When to use: During project planning and risk assessment to identify which contextual variables should drive tailoring decisions.

- **Process Concurrency, Iteration, and Recursion model**: Describes how life cycle processes are applied simultaneously at multiple levels and cycles rather than strictly sequential.
  - When to use: When designing project workflows to ensure requirements definition, architecture definition, and verification can overlap and evolve together while maintaining coherence.

- **Four Practical Application Modes (ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2)**: Categorizes how ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 processes are deployed: agreement processes, organizational project-enabling processes, technical management processes, and technical processes.
  - When to use: When aligning tailoring strategy with the legal, organizational, and technical boundaries of the project.

## Key Concepts
- **Tailoring**: The dynamic adaptation of standard processes to project-specific context, complexity, and risk—not a one-time activity but a continuous reassessment throughout the lifecycle.

- **Project Context Factors**: Seven primary drivers identified by PMI Scaling Factors (team size, geographic distribution, organizational distribution, skill availability, compliance, domain complexity, solution complexity) plus secondary factors (stakeholder landscape, budget, schedule, risk tolerance, project criticality).

- **Fit for Purpose**: The principle that tailored processes must directly support project goals and manage risk at an acceptable level without unnecessary overhead.

- **Fact-Based Tailoring**: Decision-making grounded in evidence, risk assessment, and documented rationale rather than intuition or habit.

- **Stakeholder Collaboration**: Alignment among acquirers, suppliers, and regulators on tailoring approach, which builds trust and reduces downstream conflict.

- **Dynamic Assessment and Control**: Continuous evaluation of tailoring effectiveness during execution, with process adjustments in response to changing risks or feedback.

## Mental Models
- **The Tailoring Balance**: Think of tailoring as avoiding two opposing risks—too little rigor (technical issues, delays, cost overruns) and too much rigor (overhead, reduced flexibility, inflated costs). Effective tailoring sits in the middle.

- **Process as a Living Artifact**: Treat the tailored process baseline as dynamic, not static; revisit and adjust it whenever project circumstances shift (new risks, stakeholder feedback, complexity changes).

- **Layered Integration**: Tailor processes in parallel with project planning (scope, schedule, budget, resources), not after. The two inform each other continuously.

## Anti-patterns
- **Reusing Baselines Blindly**: Borrowing a tailored process from another project without revisiting assumptions, leading to mismatch with actual project conditions.

- **Over-Engineering**: Applying all standard processes "just to be safe," incurring unnecessary cost and schedule penalties.

- **Assuming Uniformity**: Believing that the same set of controls or risks applies across all projects within an organization or domain.

- **Failing to Engage Stakeholders**: Excluding key voices (acquirers, suppliers, regulators) from tailoring decisions, resulting in lack of buy-in and increased conflict risk.

- **Tool-Driven Tailoring**: Letting tool capabilities or limitations dictate process rather than letting process requirements drive tool selection.

- **Garbage In, Garbage Out (GIGO)**: Selecting tools or data sources without verifying data availability and quality, compromising the validity of process outputs.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Tailor as a discipline, not an art form**: Base tailoring decisions on documented facts, risk assessments, and stakeholder collaboration, with clear rationale recorded for future reference and audits.

2. **Revisit tailoring dynamically**: As project risks, complexity, and stakeholder context evolve, reassess whether the current tailored process still fits. Good tailoring adapts continuously, not once at project start.

3. **Integrate tailoring with project planning**: Tailoring and project planning must run in parallel; decisions on scope, schedule, budget, and resources are interdependent with process selection.

4. **Involve all relevant stakeholders**: Early and sustained engagement of acquirers, suppliers, and regulators builds trust, prevents surprises, and ensures buy-in when processes are tight.

5. **Balance rigor against flexibility**: Too much process overhead kills morale and inflates costs; too little invites technical and schedule risk. Effective tailoring sits at the intersection of acceptable risk and efficient execution.

6. **Document assumptions and rationale explicitly**: Every tailoring choice (e.g., streamlined reviews, reduced formality) must justify why (e.g., high team cohesion, low solution complexity, proven supplier capability). This documentation supports audits and future project knowledge.

7. **Use tools to support process, not vice versa**: Select methods and tools only after defining the tailored process; tools are aids to engineering judgment, not replacements for it.

## Connects To
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (Systems and software engineering — System life cycle processes)**: The foundational standard that provides the comprehensive process framework to be tailored.

- **Project Planning**: Tailoring must run concurrently with project planning to ensure that scope, schedule, budget, and resource allocation align with process choices.

- **Risk Management**: Tailoring decisions are informed by risk assessment; as risks evolve, the tailored process baseline should be revisited and adjusted.

- **Decision Management**: Structured decision processes help document and justify tailoring trade-offs, creating accountability and traceability.

- **Assessment and Control**: Continuous monitoring of whether the tailored processes remain effective and aligned with project execution, with feedback loops to adjust tailoring as needed.

- **Configuration Management**: Tailored processes are themselves configuration items—changes to the process baseline should be tracked and approved like any other baseline change.

- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2 (Life cycle management — Guidelines for the application of ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288)**: Provides detailed guidance on how to apply standard processes in four modes (agreement, organizational project-enabling, technical management, and technical processes).
