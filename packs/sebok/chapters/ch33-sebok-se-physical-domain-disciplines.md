# Chapter — SE and Aerospace, Electrical, Mechanical, Civil, Economics and Enterprise IT

*Source: SEBoK v2.14, knowledge area "Systems Engineering and Physical/Domain Engineering Disciplines." This KA collects six articles pairing SE with a partner discipline. In this version, four are placeholder stubs (Aerospace, Electrical, Civil, Economics — each marked "Intro Paragraph" with no Works Cited, Primary, or Additional References). Two are fully developed: "Systems Engineering and Mechanical Engineering" (BKCASE authorship) and "Systems Engineering and Enterprise IT" (BKCASE authorship). This chapter synthesizes the developed articles and flags the stubs honestly.*

## Core Idea

This KA examines the boundary between systems engineering and the established physical and domain engineering disciplines it must integrate. Its through-line is a recurring observation: as engineers in any specific discipline gain seniority, they stop working only inside their subdiscipline and start taking on the integrative, cross-disciplinary, full-life-cycle concerns that are the substance of systems engineering. The relationship is not "SE versus ME" or "SE versus EIT" — it is a continuum where the discipline supplies the deep technical substance and SE supplies the holistic discipline for integrating and managing the contributions of every role.

The **Mechanical Engineering (ME)** article makes this concrete from the bottom up. It surveys ME's fundamentals — rigid-body and deformable-body mechanics, dynamics, fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, controls, and design — then shows how those fundamentals feed multidisciplinary subdisciplines (aerospace, naval) where the products become systems of systems. A junior mechanical engineer runs a structural analysis on a single assembly; a senior one reviews mechanical documents for conflict across all disciplines, resolves employer-versus-customer requirement discrepancies, ensures interoperability with external components, and owns a part's full life cycle — which is to say, performs systems engineering with a mechanical focus.

The **Enterprise IT (EIT)** article makes the same point from the top down. EIT organizations realize, use, and retire engineered systems that are largely information-technology based — ERP, supply chain management, customer relationship management, accounting, inventory, and the like. The article reads these organizations through the lens of the **Enterprise Information Technology Body of Knowledge (EITBOK)**, jointly developed by the IEEE Computer Society and ACM and maintained as a public wiki by the IEEE. Its historical motivation is the chronic failure rate of IT projects documented by the Standish Group's 1994 *Chaos Report*: 31.1% of projects were cancelled before completion and 52.7% cost 189% of their original estimate. The fix the article advances is SE principles plus cross-functional **Integrated Product Teams (IPTs)** — exactly the balancing act that prevents single-perspective domination of outcomes.

A note on scope: four of the six partner-discipline articles in this KA are unwritten stubs in SEBoK v2.14. The substantive content lives entirely in the ME and EIT articles, so this chapter grounds its claims there while preserving the KA's full naming.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)

- **Enterprise Information Technology Body of Knowledge (EITBOK)** — the IEEE Computer Society / ACM body of knowledge, maintained as a public IEEE wiki, that the EIT article uses as its primary lens for explaining EIT organizations in SE terms.
- **Integrated Product Teams (IPTs)** — cross-functional teams (sometimes the article's preferred response to IT project failure) in which each product is shepherded from concept through delivery, support, and retirement by representatives from every area of responsibility: developing customer requirements; design and development; test; configuration management; release engineering; delivery; and support.
- **Enterprise Systems Engineering (ESE)** — named as the activity set that can support business-management activities — mission and strategic planning; managing business processes and information; managing performance; managing the portfolio; budgeting and allocating resources; plus program and project management.
- **Enterprise Architecture (EA)** — defined (per the EITBOK) as carrying out enterprise analysis, design, planning, and implementation through a holistic approach for successful strategy development and execution; the article notes many alternative definitions exist and that EA builds on Systems Architecture and software architecture.
- **Principles of Systems Thinking applied to EA** — the article works through EA using named systems-thinking principles (rendered in bold italics in the source): *separation of concerns (SoC)*, the *boundary principle*, the *holism principle*, the principle of *views*, *dualism* (e.g. process/data, people/technology), *abstraction*, *parsimony*, and the *principle of viewpoints* per ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010.
- **Enterprise architecture frameworks** — a pre-defined, integrated set of viewpoints framing recurring concerns. Named exemplars and their lineage: Hammer et al. (1986, the earliest recorded), the NIST Enterprise Architecture Model (a 5-layer model still influential), Zachman's information systems architecture, the OASIS SOA Reference Model, and the defense frameworks **DODAF**, **MODAF**, NATO's **NAF**, and their successor **OMG's UAF**.
- **EA structuring principles** — modularity, network, encapsulation, layer hierarchy, regularity, and similarity/difference.
- **Socio-technical systems** — EIT organizations are responsible for complex socio-technical systems containing technical elements (software, hardware, networks, storage, databases) plus essential human components and business processes; characterized by *openness* (strong interaction with environments) and being *unfinished* (evolving over time).
- **The five major concerns of the EIT Organization** — Enterprise Architecture; Strategy and Governance; Change; Interoperability of Component Systems; and Security of Systems and Data (the article also develops Quality, Disaster Preparedness, Operations and Support, Ethics, and Roles).
- **Confidentiality, integrity, and availability** — the three dimensions of security that EIT security exists to preserve for information and information systems.
- **External capability/maturity models for EIT** — five named frameworks: **IT-CMF** (IT-Capability Maturity Framework, originating at Intel Labs Europe; 35 EIT management capabilities across four macro capabilities), **CMMI** (Capability Maturity Model Integration, originating at the Software Engineering Institute in 1987 from a DoD request; five maturity levels), **SFIA** (Skills Framework for the Information Age; 97 professional skills, seven responsibility levels), **e-CF** (European e-Competence Framework / EN 16234-1; 41 competences), and the **I Competency Dictionary (iCD)** underlying METI/IPA's **ITSS** in Japan (11 job categories, 35 specialty fields, seven competence levels).
- **ABET definition of engineering design** — used in the ME article as a discipline-agnostic anchor: design as an iterative, creative, decision-making process devising a system, component, or process to meet needs and specifications within constraints, including identifying opportunities, developing requirements, analysis and synthesis, generating and evaluating multiple solutions, considering risks, and making trade-offs.
- **ASME codes and standards** — invoked in the ME article as the discipline's standards backbone, from its first standard (the 1884 "Code for the Conduct of Trials of Steam Boilers") to present-day standards used in more than 100 countries.
- **Set-based design** — named in the ME design discussion as an increasingly common approach that gains flexibility by pursuing a broad design space and eliminating options over time as data and validation warrant.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 20000** — the International Standard for Service Management, usable to develop and manage EIT operations, complemented by **ITIL** (the Information Technology Infrastructure Library).

## Key Concepts

- **The seniority continuum.** In both articles, the central mechanism connecting a domain discipline to SE is career progression. Junior mechanical engineers focus on individual subdisciplines (e.g. structural analysis of one assembly); senior ones take on the larger context in which mechanical components fit — reviewing for cross-discipline conflict, resolving requirement discrepancies, ensuring interoperability, owning full part life cycles. The article supports this with an empirical observation: a May 2022 listing of 20 senior mechanical-engineer positions described responsibilities that were, in substance, SE activities.
- **ME's fundamental knowledge areas as building blocks.** Mechanics divides (per Hibbeler) into rigid-body mechanics, deformable-body mechanics, and fluid mechanics. Statics writes Newton's second law as F=0; dynamics uses F=ma with conservation of energy and momentum. Fluid mechanics models forces via the Navier-Stokes equations (simplifying to the Euler equations for inviscid flow, and to Bernoulli's equation for steady, incompressible, irrotational streamline flow), characterized by dimensionless parameters — Reynolds, Froude, and Mach numbers. Thermodynamics is captured by its zeroth through third laws (the first and second together making perpetual motion impossible). Controls causes a system variable to conform to a reference value via feedback.
- **Mechanical products as the substrate of systems of systems.** ME products form the building blocks for systems of systems: a materials scientist's fan blade goes into a turbine engine, on a fighter jet flying from an aircraft carrier that naval and systems engineers designed. A Boeing 747 has roughly 6 million parts from over 550 suppliers (relying on supply chain management); an airline fleet is a system of aircraft, and the air traffic network a system of systems. Naval engineers, designing an aircraft carrier across hull/mechanical/electrical design, hydrodynamics, integrated warfare, aircraft operations, and crew hotel services, function in many ways as systems engineers.
- **Human systems integration as the clearest ME–SE bridge.** The article calls human systems integration one of the best examples of the ME–SE relationship: multidisciplinary teams assess ergonomics, health, safety, user interface design, and human performance, and systems engineers often lead these assessments.
- **The enterprise as system or system of systems.** An EIT enterprise can be viewed as a *system-like* enterprise (tight coupling, single-executive function) or a *system-of-systems-like* enterprise (loosely coupled organizations, blurred boundaries, partly shared outcomes). The choice of perspective varies with the enterprise, its mission, and the engineering task. An extended enterprise may include a company plus its suppliers and customers, with no single point of control.
- **Product life cycle vs. project life cycle.** The article insists on distinguishing a product's life cycle (concept approval → development → distribution → retirement, managed within the EIT organization) from project life cycles (the projects through which each stage is managed). Most software/systems discussions conflate the two by focusing on project life cycles.
- **EA proceeds by separating, then unifying.** SoC takes the enterprise apart (picking out subjects); abstraction puts like things together through generalization (forgetting inessential detail). The two are described as the most central principles to architecture and as working together. The boundary and holism principles are applied recursively as the internal structure becomes known, with typical relations including causality, dependency, client-server, and input-output.
- **Interoperability is the hard, continuous problem.** Defined (via the EITBOK, grounded in an ISO definition) as the ability of systems and organizations to exchange and use information without knowledge of each other's inner workings. Because technology and organizations change so rapidly, achieving and maintaining interoperability is a primary reason IT services are difficult — and is how delivering services differs from delivering finished products.
- **Security is assumed-breach.** The article frames security with the Mueller/Comey "two kinds of companies" quotations to set an assumed-breach posture, then defines the goal as preserving confidentiality, integrity, and availability, with EIT security practices striving to understand current risk tolerance, understand threats and potential damages, keep cyberattack risk and damage below a tolerable level, and detect and handle incidents efficiently.
- **SE prevents the failures.** The article gives three worked examples of SE interventions that rescued or correctly shut down EIT projects: a document-processing upgrade where a schedule ignored testing, documentation, and tech support until an IPT produced a realistic 9-month plan met within 3 weeks; an offshore start-up project with no SE or IPT, shut down once a review showed delivery six months beyond what the CEO was told; and an HR-management product saved by an IPT plus a newly established Project Management Office (PMO) that integrated geographically disparate efforts. The recurring lesson: almost all failures trace to a missing SE balancing act.

## Mental Models

- **Discipline depth, SE breadth.** Picture the domain engineer's expertise as a deep, narrow column and SE as the horizontal beam tying many columns together. Aerospace and naval engineering sit where many ME columns meet; the senior engineer is the one who stops digging the column and starts laying the beam. This is the KA's organizing image and applies equally to ME and EIT.
- **The standards-and-codes backbone.** ASME for mechanical, the long ISO/IEC/IEEE service-management and architecture-description list for EIT — read each discipline's standards corpus as the accumulated, codified consequence of past system failures (ASME's first standard governed steam-boiler trials). Standards are where discipline knowledge becomes enforceable system constraint.
- **Two engines of the EIT organization.** Run the EIT organization as a product engine (build/acquire systems, integrate into the mega-SoS, maintain interoperability and integrity) and a governance engine (translate enterprise goals into EIT goals, drive change while holding agreed operation levels). Parsimony keeps both simple enough to be used and not corrupted; dualism shows up as the perpetual trade-off among budget, project prioritization, and features.
- **Architecture as a map with legends.** Each EA view is a map of the enterprise; each must carry a documented *viewpoint* — a prescribed set of conventions, functioning as a contract between architect and stakeholders on how a chosen set of concerns is addressed. Pick the viewpoint to fit the concerned stakeholder, the way you pick a map projection to fit a journey.
- **Normal operations as the default to defend.** Operations and support exist to maintain a normal state where systems execute as expected with no surprises. Disruption comes either from a planned asset transition or an unexpected event; the model is to defend "normal," execute the disaster-recovery plan when a disaster hits, and trigger incident management (system halts) or problem management (system misbehaving) otherwise.
- **Maturity measured by an external yardstick.** When asking how good an EIT organization or workforce is, reach for an external model rather than self-assessment: IT-CMF and CMMI for organizational/process maturity, SFIA / e-CF / iCD-ITSS for individual skills and responsibility levels. Each gives a shared, comparable scale across organizations and countries.
- **Design as constrained, iterative decision-making.** Borrow the ABET frame across any discipline: design devises a system/component/process to meet needs within constraints, iterating through requirements, analysis and synthesis, multiple solutions, risk consideration, and trade-offs. Whether the design process is run waterfall (design-spiral to a single-point design) or agile (iterative, set-based), the decision-theoretic core is the same.

## Key Takeaways

- This KA pairs SE with six partner disciplines, but only **Mechanical Engineering** and **Enterprise IT** are developed in v2.14; Aerospace, Electrical, Civil, and Economics remain "Intro Paragraph" stubs with no references.
- The unifying thesis is the **seniority continuum**: domain engineers increasingly perform SE work — integration, cross-discipline conflict resolution, requirement reconciliation, full-life-cycle ownership — as they advance, with the senior-mechanical-engineer job listings as evidence.
- ME's fundamentals — rigid/deformable-body mechanics, dynamics, fluid mechanics (Navier-Stokes → Euler → Bernoulli; Reynolds/Froude/Mach), thermodynamics (zeroth–third laws), controls, and design — are the building blocks from which multidisciplinary, SoS-scale products (aerospace, naval) are assembled.
- **Human systems integration** is named as one of the best examples of the ME–SE relationship, and systems engineers often lead it.
- EIT is read through the **EITBOK**; its motivating evidence is the Standish 1994 *Chaos Report* (31.1% cancelled, 52.7% at 189% of estimate), and its prescribed remedy is SE principles plus **Integrated Product Teams**.
- An enterprise is modeled as a **system-like** or **system-of-systems-like** entity; EIT organizations own complex, open, unfinished **socio-technical systems**.
- The **five major EIT concerns** are Enterprise Architecture, Strategy and Governance, Change, Interoperability, and Security — with security framed around **confidentiality, integrity, and availability**.
- **Enterprise Architecture** is conducted through systems-thinking principles (separation of concerns, boundary, holism, views, dualism, abstraction, parsimony) and realized via frameworks (Zachman, NIST, DODAF/MODAF/NAF/UAF) and structuring principles (modularity, network, encapsulation, layer hierarchy, regularity, similarity/difference).
- Five external models — **IT-CMF, CMMI, SFIA, e-CF, iCD/ITSS** — benchmark EIT organizational maturity and individual skill, and "how effective systems engineering is largely decides how effective the EIT organization is."
- Across both articles, SE is the holistic discipline that integrates every role and ensures a single perspective does not dominate outcomes.
