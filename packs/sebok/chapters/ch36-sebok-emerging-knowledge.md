## Core Idea
Part 8 exists because systems engineering is not a finished discipline. The classically trained systems engineers of the 1970s and 1980s, the Part argues, face a fundamental shift in thinking driven by the software-centricity of modern systems, cybersecurity, agent-based and object-oriented practices, and model-based methods — each of which brings its own methods and tools. The Part's purpose is to introduce the more significant emerging changes before they have settled into mainstream practice, with the explicit promise that as a topic matures it is migrated out of Part 8 and into the appropriate Part of the SEBoK. The Part offers System of Systems Engineering (SoSE) as the worked example of this lifecycle: an emerging topic that generated emerging research, became a foundational body of knowledge, and now lives in Part 4 — Systems of Systems. Part 8 therefore has a deliberately temporary, staging-area character: it is where the discipline collects what it does not yet fully know how to do, organized into two complementary halves — *Emerging Topics* (what is changing in practice) and *Emerging Research* (where the formal investigation of those changes is being gathered). The connective thread running through every topic is transformation: the move from a document-based, heuristic-based, aerospace-and-defense-rooted discipline toward one that is model-based, theory-grounded, socio-technically aware, and increasingly entangled with artificial intelligence.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Emerging Topics and Emerging Research (the two KAs of Part 8)** — *Emerging Topics* informs the reader of significant and rapidly emerging needs and trends in practicing SE, curated by the SEBoK editorial board by probability of significant impact; *Emerging Research* is a single repository gathering references to disparate research so systems engineers can find related work. The two are sequential by design: as topics gain visibility, researchers investigate them, with corporate R&D doing early work and academia and government formalizing it.
  - When/how to use: treat *Emerging Topics* as the early-warning radar for practice changes and *Emerging Research* as the bibliography for going deeper, drawing on PhD dissertations, INCOSE and IEEE publications and events, and NSF-EDSE and SERC funded research.
- **INCOSE Systems Engineering Vision 2035 and Vision 2025 (INCOSE 2021; INCOSE 2014)** — the Part's anchoring statements of where SE is heading. Vision 2035 defines the attributes of a transformed SE discipline: the future of SE is model-based and enabled by enterprise digital transformation; practices advance to handle complexity and enable enterprise agility; SE leverages other disciplines such as data science to manage data growth; formal SE theoretical foundations get codified; AI both changes SE practice and the systems SE produces; and there is a step change in SE education centered on lifelong learning.
  - When/how to use: as the strategic frame for why each Part 8 topic matters and as the bridge to *Systems Engineering: Historic and Future Challenges* and *Foundations of Systems Engineering*.
- **SE4AI and AI4SE (the dual transformation)** — terms first used during an early-2019 Future of Systems Engineering (FuSE) workshop that was hosted by INCOSE to name a two-directional evolution. *AI4SE* brings AI and ML techniques to bear on improving human-driven engineering practice (the "augmented intelligence" goal — e.g., scale in model construction, efficiency in design-space exploration). *SE4AI* turns SE methods onto the design and operation of intelligent systems, yielding outcomes such as better safety, security, and ethics.
  - When/how to use: as the organizing lens for any AI-and-SE question — first decide whether AI is the tool improving your SE work (AI4SE) or the thing being engineered (SE4AI), because the challenges and methods differ sharply.
- **Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)** — defined (per INCOSE 2007) as the formalized application of modeling to support requirements, design, analysis, verification, and validation activities beginning in conceptual design and continuing through development and later lifecycle phases. Its distinguishing characteristic is that the model becomes a primary artifact of the SE process, displacing the document-based approach whose emphasis is producing and controlling documentation about the system.
  - When/how to use: as the transitional framework — introduced in Part 8 because it is mid-transition — for shifting an organization from document-based to model-based practice; the article traces its lineage from Wymore (1993) through IDEF0, enhanced functional flow block diagrams, OMG's Model Driven Architecture (MDA), OMG SysML (adopted 2006), and UPDM.
- **Digital Engineering and the digital thread** — Digital Engineering centers on creating computer-readable models representing all aspects of a system and supporting all lifecycle activities (design, development, manufacture, operation), built on shared data schemata so that a *digital thread* integrates the diverse stakeholders. The DoD Digital Engineering Strategy (June 2018) framed five goals to streamline acquisition. The Part states the precise relationship: **MBSE is a subset of digital engineering** — MBSE covers requirements, architecture, design, V&V, while digital engineering also encompasses the physics-based models of other disciplines and the digital thread that links them, serving as the authoritative source of truth for system data.
  - When/how to use: when scoping a digital transformation, position MBSE as one component inside the broader digital engineering environment, and treat the integration of MBSE with physics-based models (and a representation such as SysML 2.0 plus an ontology) as the open integration challenge.
- **Set-Based Design (SBD)** — a complex design method (Singer, Doerry, and Buckley 2009) that enables robust design by (1) weighing a great many alternatives; (2) proving feasibility ahead of any decision; and (3) drawing on experts who each design from their own perspective and then take the intersection of their individual sets to optimize. SBD analyzes *sets* of alternatives rather than single solutions, where a set is two or more design points sharing at least one design option.
  - When/how to use: best in early-stage design when there are many design variables, tight coupling among them, conflicting requirements, flexibility in requirements allowing trades, or poorly-understood technologies requiring learning; pair it with a model-based engineering (MBE/MBSE) environment to explore the feasible tradespace in near-real time. The article supplies an eight-step *System Analyst SBD Tradespace Exploration Process* and a five-characteristic *SBD Conceptual Framework*.
- **General Machine Learning Life Cycle / Workflow (Pullum article, after Kuwajima et al. 2020)** — an AI-based system runs through a different lifecycle than traditional systems, with V&V activities occurring throughout the lifecycle and, beyond requirements allocated to the AI subsystem, additional *data requirements* that propagate up to the system out of the AI subsystem.
  - When/how to use: as the structural map for engineering an AI-containing system — the AI element is handled as a subsystem, with V&V performed on that subsystem together with its interfaces, just as for any other subsystem, so the high-level definitions of V&V do not change even though the methods must.
- **RE4AI taxonomy (Belani et al. 2019; Table 1 of the Pullum article)** — Requirements Engineering for AI, a taxonomy mapping requirements-engineering activities (Elicitation, Analysis, Specification, Validation, Management, Documentation) against AI-related entities (Data, Model, System), surfacing challenges such as imbalanced datasets, costly data labelling, the entanglement/CACE problem, and hidden feedback loops.
  - When/how to use: as a checklist for the distinctive requirements-engineering challenges that arise when an AI element is in the system, including two named coinages — *CACE: change anything, change everything* and *GORE: goal-oriented requirements engineering*.

## Key Concepts
- **The staging-area lifecycle of knowledge**: Part 8 is explicitly transient. Topics are introduced here and then expanded into other SEBoK KAs over time; once mainstream, they leave. SoSE is the canonical demonstration of the full arc from emerging topic, to emerging research, to foundational body of knowledge now resident in Part 4.
- **SE remains heuristic, with theory under construction**: SE evolved from practices in related industries (particularly aerospace and defense) used as the basis for a standardized lifecycle approach, so its practices are still largely heuristic; efforts are underway to evolve a theoretical foundation (see *Foundations of Systems Engineering*). Some practices are now standard (e.g., risk analysis), while MBSE, agile, and systems-of-systems are in transition.
- **Socio-technical systems — multiple definitions, one working definition**: the term is used differently across domains, but the SEBoK adopts a single working definition for the SE context: *systems sitting where social and technical systems meet* (Kroes et al. 2006). The concept describes the interrelationship between humans and machines; as a design approach it appears as *socio-technical systems design (STSD)*, bringing human, social, organizational, and technical elements into the design of organizational systems (Baxter and Sommerville 2011). Some argue that every system is, in the end, socio-technical (Palmer et al. 2019), yet the topic is not well defined in the SE context. There is no "state of the practice" for modeling them; the article catalogs adaptable approaches — qualitative (soft-systems), agent-based modeling (ABM), economic modeling, and system dynamics — and notes a pivotal shift in mindset: when modeling socio-technical versus traditional engineering systems, focus less on "control" and more on "influence" (Pennock and Rouse 2016).
- **AI is a capability, not a specific technology**: AI is best understood as the ability of a system to exhibit behavior that would be considered intelligent if exhibited by a human; crucially, the label "AI" points to no single technology — intelligent behavior may be realized via conventional software or hardware logic. Modern AI splits into systems whose decision logic is explicitly pre-programmed and systems where decision-making capability is *learned from data* — the latter, Machine Learning (ML), is the predominant driver of the current paradigm shift.
- **The three ML categories and the digital-transformation context**: ML algorithms fall into *Supervised* (learning input-output mappings from labelled data), *Unsupervised* (learning grouping or clustering when labels are absent), and *Reinforcement* learning (an agent training itself via a reward function). AI is best understood within *digital transformation* — the confluence of big data, cloud computing, AI, and the Internet of Things (IoT). The systems engineer's role is not to mirror the data scientist but to develop working familiarity with the supporting technologies (data integration, databases, cloud storage, enterprise architecture and APIs, ML frameworks, batch/online processing, executable environments, programming languages, and visualization tools) and to differentiate SE roles from those of data scientists, data engineers, application developers, and AI engineers.
- **Three key SE challenges of intelligent systems (Brown et al. AI article)**: (1) *New failure modes* not previously experienced — the AI community recognizes five: reward hacking; negative side effects; unsafe exploration; scalable oversight; and distributional shift; (2) *unpredictability of performance* due to non-deterministic and evolving behavior, since ML systems may change performance as they learn during operations, which challenges validating compliance before operations; (3) *lack of trust and robustness* in future performance, because acceptability rests on expert judgement, expert judgement requires explainable results, and explainable behavior of AI systems is problematic.
- **Why AI V&V is genuinely different (Pullum article)**: the high-level definitions of V&V do not change for AI-containing systems, but the methods must. Drawing on a survey of engineers (Ishikawa and Yoshioka 2019), the article identifies attributes that make ML hard to engineer — *lack of an oracle* (difficulty defining correctness criteria), *imperfection* (no AI system can be 100% accurate), *uncertain behavior for untested data* (radical behavior change from slight input changes, e.g., adversarial examples), and *high dependency of behavior on training data* — generalized as erosion of determinism, unpredictability and unexplainability of individual outputs, unanticipated emergent behavior, complex algorithmic decision-making, and weakness against slight input changes.
- **Data as the life-blood of AI**: data quality attributes for AI span correctness; accuracy; currency and timeliness; consistency; usability; accessibility; security and privacy; accountability; scalability; freedom from bias; and coverage of the operational space. There are no strong guarantees on when a dataset is "large enough," and "large" alone is insufficient — the data must sufficiently cover the operational space. Adversarial inputs (Szegedy et al. 2014) are a distinct data challenge, demonstrated across image classification, natural language processing, and reinforcement learning (e.g., reward hacking).
- **AI system properties needed for trust**: a non-comprehensive set of properties whose assurance enables trust — accountability, controllability, explainability, interpretability, reliability, resilience, robustness, safety, and transparency — each defined precisely (e.g., interpretability as how far a human is able to grasp what caused a given decision, per Miller 2017).
- **AI V&V approaches and the emerging standards landscape**: approaches include formal methods (model checking; formal proofs; probabilistic verification); software testing; simulation-based testing; and live experiments — with specific named techniques such as *metamorphic testing* (addressing the oracle problem), an *ML test score*, *corroborative verification*, testing against strong adversarial attacks, formal verification, and *assurance cases*. Standards development organizations are actively working: ISO/IEC JTC 1 SC 42 (the first international SDO to set up an AI expert group), the IEEE P7000 series (including IEEE P7009 on Fail-Safe Design of Autonomous and Semi-Autonomous Systems), ANSI/UL 4600 (the safety standard for evaluating autonomous products), and the SAE G-34 committee on Artificial Intelligence in Aviation.
- **MBSE adoption is real, uneven, and front-loaded (Cloutier survey article)**: surveys in 2009, 2012, 2014, 2018, and a 2019 JPL workshop trace adoption trends. Key findings — MBSE is spreading beyond defense and space into civil-engineering domains (energy, infrastructure, transportation); it is applied more in *early* lifecycle phases (architecting, requirements analysis, conceptualization) than later ones; a JPL question found 76% saw system/subsystem architecting as where MBSE holds the most promise; a persistent skills shortage coexists with organizations providing *less* training; and inhibitors include the availability of MBSE skills, cultural and general resistance to change, and lack of perceived value (with software engineers' perceived value of MBSE notably declining survey to survey).
- **A model is an abstraction of reality**: broadly, one can regard a model as a facsimile, or an abstraction, of reality — even a requirements document is, in this sense, a model. MBSE represents SE's migration to computer-based graphical user interfaces for analysis and design, paralleling how aerospace engineers moved from drafting boards to CAD and software engineers from text editors to integrated GUIs.
- **Digital twin is related but distinct**: a digital twin is a high-fidelity model of the system used to emulate the actual system, enabling an organization to analyze design changes before incorporating them into the real system — a distinct concept from digital engineering itself.
- **Set vocabulary in SBD**: a *design factor* is some solution parameter, characteristic, or relationship bearing on the design at system level; *set drivers* are fundamental design decisions defining system traits that make current and future missions possible, whereas *set modifiers* are "added-on" decisions that can be modified to adapt for new missions. SBD's named advantages include reliable efficient communication, greater parallelism with effective early use of subteams, data-based early decisions, and promotion of institutional learning (Ward et al. 1995).
- **Emerging Research repositories**: the Part curates recently defended doctoral dissertations (e.g., early-lifecycle prediction of system reliability using eight factors in the style of COCOMO/COSYSMO; the evolution of information digital ecosystems applying the TRIZ method; cybersecurity decision patterns as adaptive knowledge encoding), and points to INCOSE periodicals (the *Systems Engineering* journal, *INSIGHT*, the members newsletter), IEEE Systems Council conferences (e.g., SysCon), and the NSF-EDSE program and SERC University-Affiliated Research Center as the formal channels where emerging research accumulates.

## Mental Models
- **Part 8 is a waiting room, not a wing.** Knowledge enters Part 8 unsettled and leaves once it matures, migrating into the Part where it permanently belongs. When you read a Part 8 topic, ask "where will this live when it grows up?" — SoSE already graduated to Part 4.
- **AI4SE vs. SE4AI: tool or product?** Before reasoning about AI and SE, decide which direction you are in. If AI is augmenting your engineering work, you are in AI4SE and chasing scale and efficiency. If AI is inside the system you are building, you are in SE4AI and chasing safety, assurance, and trust. The two share a foundation but pose different problems.
- **The AI element is just another subsystem — until V&V.** Treat the AI capability as a subsystem with interfaces, and conventional SE framing still applies. The break comes at verification and validation: the oracle is missing, the system is non-deterministic and may keep learning, and behavior is hostage to training data. Same definitions, radically different methods.
- **Influence, not control.** Socio-technical and intelligent systems resist the command-and-control instinct of classical SE. Pennock and Rouse's reframing — model for influence rather than control — is the posture shift these emerging systems demand.
- **MBSE is a subset of digital engineering; the digital thread is the whole.** Don't conflate the two. MBSE gives you the SE models; digital engineering connects those to physics-based models and every other discipline through a shared, authoritative digital thread. Scoping a transformation around MBSE alone under-reaches.
- **Establish feasibility before you decide.** SBD's discipline is to hold many alternatives open, prove what is feasible first, and let the intersection of expert sets converge slowly to a point solution — the opposite of committing to a single concept early and defending it.
- **Adoption lags capability, and front-loads.** MBSE works best where it is used least later: it concentrates in early lifecycle phases, spreads unevenly across industries, and stalls on skills and culture rather than on tooling. Plan transformation around the human and organizational change, not just the technology.

## Key Takeaways
- Part 8 is the SEBoK's deliberately temporary staging area for SE knowledge that is emerging and transitioning; its two KAs — *Emerging Topics* and *Emerging Research* — separate "what is changing in practice" from "where that change is being formally investigated."
- INCOSE Vision 2035 supplies the strategic narrative: SE's future is model-based and digitally-enabled, complexity- and agility-driven, theory-grounded, AI-shaped, and dependent on a step change in lifelong education.
- AI enters SE in two directions — AI4SE (AI improving SE practice) and SE4AI (SE methods applied to engineering intelligent systems) — first named at the 2019 FuSE workshop; the directions share a foundation but diverge in challenges.
- Engineering AI-containing systems keeps the high-level definitions of V&V but demands new methods because of the absence of an oracle, intrinsic imperfection, non-deterministic and evolving behavior, dependency on training data, and the difficulty of explainability that underpins trust.
- MBSE — model as primary artifact — is mid-transition, is a subset of the broader digital engineering effort (with the digital thread as authoritative source of truth and the digital twin as a distinct high-fidelity emulator), and adoption surveys show it spreading beyond defense/space, concentrating in early lifecycle phases, and constrained by a persistent skills shortage and cultural resistance.
- Set-based design and socio-technical systems modeling represent emerging method-level shifts — keeping many feasible alternatives open before committing, and modeling for influence rather than control.
