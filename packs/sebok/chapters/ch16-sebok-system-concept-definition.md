# Chapter 16 — System Concept Definition

## Core Idea
**Concept Definition** is the set of SE activities that examine the *problem space* together with the needs and requirements of the business (or enterprise) and the stakeholders — and it deliberately begins **before** any formal definition of the system-of-interest (SoI) exists. Its job is to answer *why* a solution is needed and *what* it should accomplish, before anyone commits to *how* the problem will be solved or how that solution will be defined and developed. The knowledge area decomposes into two primary activities: **Business or Mission Analysis** and **Stakeholder Needs Definition**. Their products and artifacts feed forward into the **System Definition** process. In short: nail down the mission and the integrated set of needs first; let architecture, requirements, and design follow from a problem you actually understand.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms)
- **Business or Mission Analysis** — the *first* process of Concept Definition. Establishes the overall strategic problem, threat, or opportunity and identifies candidate **solution classes** to address it (per ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288). Use it at the very start of engineering any SoI, typically at the enterprise level, to decide whether a project is even worth authorizing.
- **Stakeholder Needs Definition** — the *second* process. Takes the outputs of Business or Mission Analysis and explores what capabilities the SoI must provide for stakeholders to accomplish the mission. Use it after (and iteratively *with*) Business or Mission Analysis to build the **integrated set of needs**.
- **Mission, Goals, Objectives (MGOs) and Measures** — the cascade SEBoK uses to make the mission actionable. *Mission* = the why; *Goals* break the mission into manageable, shared pieces; *Objectives* detail what the team and the SoI must do to hit the goals; *Measures* are quantitative metrics — expressed as **Measures of Effectiveness (MOE)** — used to validate the SoI against the objectives. Use the MGOs/measures chain to convert a problem statement into success criteria.
- **The push and pull paradigms** — the two paradigms that drive how concept definition is done. *Pull* responds to an identified problem or capability gap (e.g., a missing defense or infrastructure capability). *Push* creates a solution for a perceived opportunity (e.g., an anticipated product or service), whether or not a market currently exists. Use this lens to recognize why a project exists and how it will be validated (push domains often rely on alpha/beta testing).
- **Solution class** — the *means* of achieving a solution: a new system; modifying or upgrading one that exists; combining several existing systems; or driving operational changes. Use solution classes as the unit of feasibility assessment during Business or Mission Analysis, evaluated through the **Decision Management** process.
- **Green-field vs. brown-field** — the framing for new versus modified systems. *Green-field* starts from a blank page; analysis characterizes the **as-is / present-state** problem and the **to-be / future-state** resolution. *Brown-field* evolves or transforms an existing system, reusing its established data as analysis input. Use this distinction to choose the right concept-definition entry point and data sources.
- **Integrated set of needs** — the central output of Stakeholder Needs Definition: a well-formed set that is **correct, consistent, complete, and feasible**, fusing stakeholder needs with risks, drivers, constraints, and life cycle concepts analysis. Use it to scope the project, resource it, and serve as the basis against which requirements, design, and the realized SoI are validated.
- **Stakeholder register** — the recording device for stakeholders, capturing each one's involvement with the SoI and a ranking. Started in Business or Mission Analysis, expanded in Stakeholder Needs Definition. Use it to maintain traceability of elicited needs back to their source.

## Key Concepts
- **Why → what → how ordering.** Both the *why* (mission/purpose) and the *what* (acceptable solution) must be settled before *how* (solution type, then definition and development) is considered. Concept Definition owns the *why* and *what*.
- **Iteration between the two activities.** Business or Mission Analysis and Stakeholder Needs Definition are performed iteratively to refine understanding of both the problem space and the options in the solution space.
- **Four perspectives the project team must understand:** the purpose of the SoI (*why?*), the acceptability/desirability of a solution (*what?*), the measures (*how well?*), and the operating environment (*in what conditions?*).
- **Defining the problem, threat, or opportunity** is a four-step move: identify the impacted strategic/business-operations stakeholders, collaborate to understand the impact, state the problem/threat/opportunity clearly, and secure stakeholder concurrence on that statement.
- **Business vs. user perspectives differ — and both must be met.** A user cares whether the SoI meets their needs, not about the developer's profit, time-to-market, or market share. This produces multiple, sometimes conflicting, sets of MGOs and measures (e.g., product price vs. profitability vs. market share) that must be prioritized against enterprise strategy.
- **Preliminary life cycle concepts** (operational, acquisition, development, manufacturing/coding, verification, validation, deployment, operations, support, retirement) define what the SoI must do and how well, across modes/states, operational scenarios, use cases, **misuse cases**, and **loss scenarios**. They enable feasibility analysis and evaluation of solution options, and are later matured in Stakeholder Needs Definition.
- **Uncertainty breeds risk early.** Preliminary life cycle concepts carry uncertainty; the resulting risks must be identified and handled via the **Risk Management** process, and they can themselves spawn additional life cycle concepts (e.g., for cyber security or hazards).
- **Assessment of continuation.** Business or Mission Analysis ends with an organizational go/no-go decision keyed to **alignment with the enterprise strategy**; a "go" authorizes the project (and budget/acquisition concept) and hands off to Stakeholder Needs Definition.
- **Stakeholders are the primary source of needs.** Omitting a relevant stakeholder typically produces missing needs and a failure to pass **System Validation**. SEBoK organizes stakeholder identification by **life cycle stage** (Concept, Development, Production/Integration/V&V, Logistics & Maintenance, Operation, Disposal).
- **Approving authorities** are a distinct, named subset of stakeholders responsible for certifying, qualifying, and approving the system for operational use — and they are *not* assumed to be just "the customer." They may be internal or external, especially for highly-regulated systems.
- **Elicitation is multi-method.** Recommended techniques include structured brainstorming workshops, interviews and questionnaires, focus groups, visual/descriptive content, documentation review, and feedback from System Verification and System Validation.
- **Rationale and priority are part of the need, not extras.** Capturing *why* for every need — and asking stakeholders to prioritize — often reveals the *real* need, especially when a stakeholder states a need as an implementation. Some needs are critical; others are "nice-to-haves" a stakeholder could live without under budget/schedule pressure.
- **Drivers and constraints** are things outside the project's control that constrain or drive the solution space: design constraints/standards, production constraints, human factors (HMI), regulations, operating environment (natural/induced), social/cultural environment, existing-system interactions/interfaces/dependencies, technology maturity, cost, and schedule.
- **Life cycle concepts analysis and maturation** transforms the preliminary concepts into a mature set that is **consistent, correct, complete, and feasible**, producing architectural and analytical/behavioral models. The **logical architecture** defines the system boundary and functions; functions are grouped logically and their interactions captured.
- **Need statements have their own grammar.** Needs are written in structured natural language from the stakeholder's perspective and deliberately **avoid "shall"** (to distinguish them from requirements) — e.g., "The stakeholders need the system to…". They are recorded in the SoI's integrated dataset with traceability for stakeholder review and confirmation.

## Mental Models
- **Concept Definition is the problem-framing phase; System Definition is the solution phase.** Everything in this KA is about understanding and bounding the problem so the downstream solution work has a sound footing. Cross the *why/what* boundary before the *how* work begins.
- **Two nested funnels.** Business or Mission Analysis funnels a strategic problem/threat/opportunity down to MGOs, measures, and candidate solution classes; Stakeholder Needs Definition funnels stakeholder inputs plus risks/drivers/constraints/life-cycle-concepts into one integrated set of needs. The second funnel's inputs are the first funnel's outputs.
- **Needs vs. requirements is a perspective shift, not a rename.** A *need* expresses the stakeholder's external (black-box) view of *what they need the system to do*; a requirement (produced downstream) expresses the developer's view of *what the SoI must do to meet it*. Keeping the vocabulary separate (no "shall" in needs) prevents the "expectation vs. need vs. requirement" confusion SEBoK explicitly flags.
- **Problem and solution co-define each other.** Solutions should respond to well-defined problems, and problem definitions should be bounded by what's feasible in the solution space — **System Analysis** provides the link between the two. Don't over-specify a problem you can't solve, or chase a solution to a problem you haven't framed.
- **Validation's anchor is set here.** The integrated set of needs is the baseline the realized system is *validated against*. A gap in the needs is a latent validation failure; that's why stakeholder completeness and approving-authority identification matter so early.
- **MGOs as a translation ladder.** Mission (why) → Goals (manageable pieces) → Objectives (what to do) → Measures/MOE (how well, quantified). Each rung makes the one above it actionable and the one below it traceable.
- **Bottom-up meets top-down.** Because real systems mix legacy and new elements in a blend of push and pull, combine a bottom-up approach (to honor legacy elements and existing interfaces) with a top-down approach (to identify required services and capabilities) — elaborated under *Applying Life Cycle Processes*.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Concept Definition answers *why* and *what* before *how*** — and starts before any formal SoI definition exists.
2. It has exactly **two activities**: **Business or Mission Analysis** (define the problem/threat/opportunity, MGOs, measures, candidate solution classes) and **Stakeholder Needs Definition** (build the integrated set of needs).
3. **MGOs and measures (as MOEs)** convert the mission into actionable, quantitatively validatable success criteria — and there may be *separate* business-perspective and user-perspective sets that must be prioritized against enterprise strategy.
4. **Push vs. pull** explains a project's origin and shapes its validation approach; **green-field vs. brown-field** drives the analysis entry point and data sources.
5. A **solution class** (new system, modify/upgrade existing, leverage multiple existing, operational change) is the unit of feasibility assessment, evaluated via **Decision Management** and tied to **enterprise strategy alignment** at the go/no-go gate.
6. **Stakeholders are the primary source of needs**; missing one risks failing **System Validation**. Identify them by **life cycle stage** and explicitly find the **approving authorities** (not just the customer).
7. Elicitation captures **rationale and priority** for every need; the **integrated set of needs** must be **correct, consistent, complete, and feasible**, and needs are written **without "shall"** to keep them distinct from requirements.
8. Concept Definition runs **concurrently with System Architecture Design Definition** and feeds the **System Definition** process; **Risk Management** is engaged from the preliminary-life-cycle-concepts stage onward.

## Anti-patterns
*(Issues SEBoK names explicitly in this knowledge area.)*
- **Leaving out a relevant stakeholder** — typically ends in missing needs and requirements, and ultimately a failure at System Validation.
- **Assuming "the customer" is the only approving authority** — approving authorities (those who certify/qualify/approve the system for operational use) may be internal or external and must be identified deliberately, especially for highly-regulated systems.
- **Stating a need as an implementation** — collapses *what is needed* into *how to do it*; SEBoK counters this by requiring rationale, which "often reveals the real need."
- **Failing to address risk during elicitation** — yields an incomplete set of needs (and downstream requirements), producing an SoI that will fail System Validation.

## Connects To
- **System Definition** (the next KA) — receives the products/artifacts of Concept Definition; **System Requirements Definition** transforms the integrated set of needs (stakeholder/black-box view) into design-input requirements (developer/white-box view), feeding System Architecture Definition, System Design Definition, and System Verification.
- **System Architecture Design Definition** — runs *concurrently* with Concept Definition; candidate **solution classes** and logical/functional architecture exploration begin here.
- **System Analysis** — provides the link between problem definitions and feasible solutions during Concept Definition.
- **Decision Management** — used to evaluate alternative solution classes and guide selection (modeling, simulation, analytical techniques, expert judgment).
- **Risk Management** — engaged from the preliminary life cycle concepts onward; identified risks carry forward as inputs to Stakeholder Needs Definition.
- **System Validation** — the integrated set of needs is the baseline against which the realized SoI is validated; incomplete needs surface here as failures.
- **Measurement** — MGO measures are realized as **Measures of Effectiveness (MOE)** for validating the SoI against objectives across the life cycle.
- **Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)** — life cycle concepts, elicited needs, and the logical architecture can be captured in the system model and traced to their sources (stakeholder, MGO, risk, life cycle stage).
- **Applying Life Cycle Processes** — home of the combined bottom-up/top-down treatment of legacy plus new elements under the push/pull mix.
- **Standards & references:** **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023** (System Life Cycle Processes), **ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148** (Requirements Engineering), **INCOSE SE Handbook v5.0**, the **INCOSE Needs and Requirements Manual (NRM)**, **Guide to Needs and Requirements (GtNR)**, and **Guide to Writing Requirements (GtWR)** — the cited backbone for this KA.
