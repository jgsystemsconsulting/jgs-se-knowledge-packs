# Chapter 10: Development Approaches

## Core Idea
The **Development Approaches** Knowledge Area (Lead Author David Endler; Contributing Authors Mike Yokell and Garry Roedler) addresses how a systems engineer chooses and manages the path a system of interest (SoI) takes through the concept and development stages of its life cycle. Although these methods touch both the concept stage and the development stage, they are conventionally grouped under the label "development approaches" because adjectives like waterfall, incremental, and evolutionary attach to the development stage rather than to the whole of the life cycle. SEBoK sorts the field into three broad families — sequential, incremental, and evolutionary — with agile treated as a further type or a subset that overlaps the others. The choice among them turns on three criteria: how completely the SoI requirements are known up front, whether the work is broken into increments, and what purpose those increments serve. A recurring caution runs through the whole KA: every real development effort is concurrent and iterative to some non-zero degree, so these families are reference points rather than rigid boxes with clear-cut boundaries.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Sequential Development Approach** — a linear, phase-based progression with limited, mostly-early iteration. When/how: use for projects with well-defined, stable requirements in familiar domains where risk is manageable and traceability matters (aerospace, healthcare, defense, civil engineering).
- **Waterfall model** — the earliest influential sequential model, introduced by Winston W. Royce (1970). When/how: SEBoK notes Royce offered it as a cautionary example, not a recommendation; its phases (requirements analysis, system design, implementation/construction, integration and testing, deployment, maintenance) run with each completed before the next begins.
- **Vee model** (presented as the **Dual Vee Model**, Forsberg, Mooz, Cotterman 2005) — a sequential model that makes verification and validation explicit by mapping each development step to a corresponding verification step. When/how: use to visualize the relationship between left-side system definition and right-side system realization, and to plan verification while requirements are still being developed.
- **Incremental Development Approach** — develops a sequence of increasing-capability increments, some delivered and some used only for learning, until the final increment meets all requirements. When/how: use in complex, high-uncertainty projects, or when early market entry with limited capability is advantageous.
- **Incremental Commitment Spiral Model (ICSM)** (Boehm; Pew and Mavor 2007) — a modern incremental example extending Boehm's classic Spiral Model (1987) for systems engineering. When/how: use where formal reviews, traceability, and accountability are required (defense, transportation, critical infrastructure); commitments are made incrementally at decision/commitment-review points only as far as evidence justifies.
- **Evolutionary Development Approach** — starts from a limited requirement set and grows the system through working iterations, each released to real users to gather feedback. When/how: use in innovative or dynamic markets where requirements will change and speed of delivery matters.
- **DevOps / DevSecOps** (D'Souza, derived from Banach 2019 and Anx 2021) — cited as the representative evolutionary example, built on continuous integration (CI) and continuous delivery (CD). When/how: use in cloud services, IoT, and large-scale software platforms; SEBoK notes DevOps can also be read as a life cycle model because of its infinite development–operations repetition.
- **Agile Development Approach** — iterative development with frequent inspection and adaptation, incremental deliveries, and requirements that evolve through cross-functional collaboration and continual stakeholder feedback (definition per ISO/IEC/IEEE 26515). When/how: use in dynamic environments with rapidly evolving customer needs; adapt carefully for large-scale, multi-disciplinary systems.
- **Agile Manifesto** (Beck et al. 2001) — the four-value statement underpinning agile, supported by twelve principles. When/how: SEBoK stresses these were written for software and may not transfer cleanly to hardware, human, and process elements of systems engineering.
- **Scrum, SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework), LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum)** — non-exhaustive agile framework examples (no endorsement implied). When/how: Scrum for time-boxed sprints with defined roles; SAFe to scale agile across many teams using PI (Program Increment) Planning; LeSS as a lighter-weight, simplification-focused alternative to SAFe.
- **Lean Systems Engineering (LSE)** — application of lean thinking (Womack 2003) to SE and related enterprise/project management, applicable across the whole life cycle. When/how: use to deliver best life-cycle value with minimal waste; LSE means more and better SE, not less.
- **Lean Enablers for Systems Engineering (LEfSE)** (INCOSE LSE Working Group 2009; Oppenheim 2011) — a checklist of "dos" and "don'ts" grouped under the six lean principles. When/how: use as a non-mandatory good-practice checklist that amends, rather than replaces, traditional SE.

## Key Concepts
- **Life cycle vs. stage vs. life cycle model**: A life cycle is an entity's evolution from conception to retirement (per ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1); a stage denotes a period tied to the state of the entity's description or realization and marks major milestones or decision points; a life cycle model is a framework of processes and activities covering the life cycle.
- **Concept stage vs. development stage**: Both are where SE attention concentrates; the chief difference SEBoK draws is that the concept stage typically involves a narrower or simply different stakeholder set than the development stage does.
- **Classification criteria**: Approaches are grouped mainly by how well SoI requirements are known, whether increments exist, and what those increments are used for.
- **Concurrency and iteration are never zero**: Real development always has some concurrency and iteration; Figure 1 of the Development Approach Concepts article depicts this with a round symbol along the development stage. Different system elements of one SoI may warrant different approaches (e.g., hardware vs. software, or research vs. in-service support).
- **Sequential approach in practice**: Fully sequential approaches are essentially theoretical; real sequential work performs tasks in order with some iteration, and information must flow between stages to manage risk. The Vee separates the "left" side (system definition) from the "bottom/right" (system realization); all life cycle processes are applied concurrently, iteratively, and recursively at each level of the system hierarchy. Because the Vee covers only the development stage, it is explicitly not a life cycle model.
- **Increments vs. learning iterations**: In incremental development, not every iteration yields a tangible increment — some iterations exist purely to reduce uncertainty by exploring feasibility, user needs, or integration challenges. V&V is distributed across iterations rather than concentrated at the end.
- **Incremental commitment**: ICSM divides development into cycles, each ending at a commitment review; stakeholders commit only as far as gathered evidence and confidence justify, which lets a project pivot, refine goals, or stop early.
- **Evolutionary release-and-feedback loop**: Each evolutionary iteration is released for actual use; user feedback, performance data, and market response shape the next version. Key practical needs are modular/scalable architecture, loosely coupled well-defined interfaces, continuous integration and verification, and disciplined traceability and configuration management to assess upgradeability of deployed systems.
- **Agile vocabulary**: Iteration is repeating the same process(es) at one and the same level within the system structure (ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023). Agile breaks work into sprints (typically 1–4 weeks), each ending in a potentially shippable product increment. System-level user stories follow the "As a <role>, I want <activity> so that <business value>" pattern and group into user journeys or epics; sprint planning estimates complexity (often story points); retrospectives drive continuous improvement. Agile tends to avoid the terms "milestones" and "decision gates," favoring more frequent, smaller, less formal stakeholder interaction (ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1).
- **Lean principles (Oppenheim 2011)**: Six principles for product development — capture customer-defined value; map the value stream and eliminate waste; flow the work without stopping or backflow; let customers pull value; pursue perfection of all processes; respect for people. LSE raises responsibility, authority, and accountability (RAA), and treats mission assurance as non-negotiable while still demanding minimal waste.

## Mental Models
- **The three families as a requirements-knowledge spectrum**: Sequential assumes requirements are largely known and stable; incremental assumes they are partly known and will be refined through increments; evolutionary assumes only part is known up front and the rest emerges from real-world use. Pick the family that matches how much you actually know.
- **Iteration purpose, not just iteration presence**: What separates incremental from evolutionary is less "do you iterate" and more "what is each iteration for" — capability build-up and risk burn-down (incremental) versus fielded use and market feedback (evolutionary).
- **Concurrency dial, never off**: Treat any chosen approach as sitting on a dial of concurrency/iteration that never reads zero; the named families are idealized positions, and a single SoI can run different elements at different dial settings.
- **Vee as a verification-planning lens**: Read the Vee not as a timeline but as a left-to-right pairing that forces you to define how each part will be verified while you are still defining it, reducing the risk of late, expensive failures.
- **Commit only as far as the evidence reaches (ICSM)**: Make the smallest commitment the current evidence supports, review, then decide whether to advance, refine, or stop — turning uncertainty into a series of bounded bets.
- **Fail early, fail often within lean flow**: Use rapid architecting and discovery to surface problems early; pull value to the moment the customer needs it, avoiding both shelf-life obsolescence from finishing too early and schedule slips from finishing too late.

## Anti-patterns
- **Misreading waterfall as a pure linear method**: SEBoK explicitly records that Royce presented the waterfall as a cautionary example of what goes wrong without feedback and iteration, yet it was widely adopted in its rigid linear form — the iteration Royce advocated was often overlooked in practice.
- **Treating "shelf-life obsolescence" as harmless**: Under the lean pull principle, completing a task excessively early is a defect, risking lost human memory or changed requirements, just as late completion causes schedule slips.
- **Confusing LSE with doing less SE**: SEBoK is explicit that Lean Systems Engineering implies not less SE but rather more of it, and better, with any task legitimately required for success retained but executed with minimal waste.
- **Transplanting agile software values unchanged into systems engineering**: The Agile Manifesto's values and twelve principles were formulated for software and may not transfer seamlessly to hardware, human elements, and processes; direct application to large-scale, multi-disciplinary projects requires deliberate adaptation.

## Key Takeaways
1. Development approaches are classified mainly by three criteria — extent to which requirements are known, presence of increments, and the purpose of those increments — into sequential, incremental, and evolutionary families, with agile as a cross-cutting type/subset.
2. The labels (waterfall, incremental, evolutionary) describe the development stage, not the whole life cycle; the Vee, covering only development, is therefore not a life cycle model.
3. All real development is concurrent and iterative to a non-zero degree, and one SoI often needs different approaches for different system elements (e.g., hardware vs. software).
4. Sequential/Vee gives predictability, repeatability, documentation, and traceability for stable-requirement, safety-critical, regulated work, but late changes are costly once early decisions lock in.
5. Incremental (exemplified by ICSM) burns down technical risk and admits emerging technology by delivering capability in chunks and committing only as far as evidence justifies at each commitment review.
6. Evolutionary (exemplified by DevOps/DevSecOps with CI/CD) thrives under uncertainty by fielding working versions early and steering with real user feedback, demanding modular architecture, automation, and strong configuration management.
7. Agile structures work into short sprints producing shippable increments, using user stories, epics, retrospectives, and frameworks like Scrum, SAFe, and LeSS — adapted carefully for systems beyond software.
8. Lean Systems Engineering applies six lean principles across the whole life cycle to maximize life-cycle value and minimize waste, with LEfSE serving as a non-mandatory good-practice checklist that augments traditional SE.

## Connects To
- **Life Cycle Models (SEBoK KA)**: Development approaches sit inside the broader life cycle model discussion; the distinction that these approaches govern the development (and concept) stage rather than the full life cycle is the explicit bridge.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1 (Life cycle management — Guidelines)** and **ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-2 (application of 15288)**: Source standards for life cycle/stage definitions and for the agile guidance on less-formal stakeholder interaction.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023 (System life cycle processes)**: Supplies the formal definition of iteration used in the agile article and the process framework recursively applied across the Vee's hierarchy.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 26515 (Developing information for users in an agile environment)**: Source of the SEBoK agile definition.
- **INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook, 5th Edition (2023)**: Cited chapters (2.1.2, 2.2, 2.2.1–2.2.3, 4.2) are the primary alignment for each development-approach article.
- **System Verification and Validation (SEBoK)**: The Vee model and evolutionary CI/verification practices connect directly to V&V planning and continuous verification.
- **Risk Management (SEBoK)**: Incremental approaches and ICSM are framed around technical-risk reduction, recurring trade studies, and risk reassessment at iteration/commitment boundaries.
- **Configuration Management and Traceability (SEBoK)**: Incremental and evolutionary approaches both elevate version control, baselines, and decision/assumption documentation as iterations evolve requirements and interfaces.
- **System Architecture (SEBoK)**: Evolutionary development depends on modular, scalable, loosely coupled architecture to absorb change without major rework.
- **Lean / specialty engineering (SEBoK Part 6)**: Lean Engineering interacts with the traditional SE technical processes (concept definition, system definition, realization, deployment and use) and many specialty disciplines.
