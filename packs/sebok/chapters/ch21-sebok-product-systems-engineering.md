# Chapter — Product Systems Engineering

*Source: SEBoK v2.14, Part 4 (Applications of Systems Engineering), Product Systems Engineering knowledge area. Lead Authors: Bud Lawson and Ricardo Pineda; Contributing Author: Rick Adcock. KA sponsored by PPI.*

## Core Idea

Product systems engineering (PSE) sits at the heart of the **new product development process (NPDP)** — the work an enterprise does to develop a product and successfully push it into a market segment, whether that market is consumer-based (private enterprises, general consumers) or public/not-for-profit (a region's military, healthcare, education, transportation, or energy needs). The KA's defining move is to insist that the engineered thing is never just the end product. It is a **product system**: the end product *plus* the enabling products needed to develop, deliver, operate, and dispose of it. PSE takes a full life cycle view — "cradle to grave" — and only becomes necessary when those enabling products are themselves complex, or when their relationship to the end product is complex; if not a traditional product development process suffices.

The second defining move is that NPDP is two overlapping, integrated activities running together: **systems engineering** (concept generation, engineering design/development, deployment) and **market development** (market research, market analysis, product acceptance, market growth/diffusion, rate of adoption). Around these sit manufacturability/producibility, logistics, quality, disposal, standards conformance, stakeholder value, and customer expectations, plus the enterprise's own competence — sales, support, maintenance, training. PSE's job is to align a product with the enterprise's business objectives, its internal capabilities, and the competition, and to engineer the product *together with* the systems that realize and sustain it.

A third move broadens what counts as a "product." PSE deliberately rejects the textbook habit of treating products as only hardware and software. The author is explicit: systems engineers must not allocate required system behavior to hardware and software elements alone. Products also include personnel, facilities, data, materials, media, techniques, and processes — and even an organization or a process can be a product.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)

- **Product system** — an end product taken together with the enabling products that support it. In **ANSI/EIA 632-2003** ("Processes for Engineering a System") the bare term *system* is used, but the standard's scope is restricted to product systems; SEBoK uses ANSI/EIA 632 figures to illustrate end products and enabling products.
- **Product realization system** — the related system that enables realization of the product system; it holds all the resources applied to conceive, develop, produce, test, and deploy the **intervention system** (the product system being brought into being to address a perceived problem). Drawn from Martin (2004); Lawson (2010) calls it the *respondent* system in the **system coupling diagram**.
- **Product sustainment system** — when the realization system delivers the product into its environment, a separate system of enabling products and operational services keeps it operational; the realization system may have to develop or modify the sustainment system for the particular intervention.
- **Wider system-of-interest (WSOI)** — the delivered product plus its enabling systems considered collectively; also used to describe the outsourcing holism (the full supply chain) that must be managed for a successful product or service.
- **Integrated Product Development Process (IPDP)** — the continuously evolving process that turns market needs into profitable products. Per Magrab et al. (2010) the SEBoK lays it out in four stages: **Stage I Product Identification**, **Stage II Concept Development**, **Stage III Design and Manufacturing**, **Stage IV Product Launch**, with a **continuous improvement (CI)** loop feeding every stage and a **decision gate** at each stage boundary.
- **Integrated product teams (IPTs)** / **integrated product development teams (IPDTs)** — cross-functional teams of all relevant stakeholders that tailor and execute the IPDP; the evolution runs concept → **concurrent engineering** → **integrated product development (IPD)** → **integrated product and process development (IPPD)** (INCOSE 2012).
- **Concept of operations (ConOps)** / **Market Service Description** — the two ways a product's intended operational use is captured (military vs. commercial), so product characteristics can be aligned with operational intent.
- **Architecture frameworks** — named exemplars for defining product architecture: commercial — **Zachman framework**, **TOGAF**, **e-TOM (Enhanced-Telecom Operations Map)**; public — **DODAF 2.0**, **FEAF (Federal Enterprise Architecture Framework)**, **MODAF**.
- **Architecture Tradeoff Analysis Method (ATAM)** — the SEI method (Kazman et al. 2000) for evaluating software architectures against quality-attribute goals and exposing architectural risks to business goals.
- **Specialty engineering** — per the INCOSE SE Handbook, analysis of specific system features needing special skills (logistics support, EMC, environmental impact, human factors, safety/health, training, reliability/maintainability, certification, etc.); Eisner (2008) lists it among the "thirty elements" of SE.
- **Readiness levels** — **TRL** (technology readiness levels, the NASA 9-level scale from Mankins 1995, adapted by DoD/AFRL/DoE), **MRL** (manufacturing readiness levels — adds producibility/affordability concerns TRLs miss), **IRL** (integration readiness levels — interface maturity), and **SRL** (system readiness levels — a function of individual TRLs and their IRL integration points, per Sauser 2006).
- **Configuration management (CM)** with **configuration control board (CCB)** / change control board, **engineering change request (ECR)** / **configuration change request (CCR)**, **configuration item (CI)** (HWCI, SWCI, firmware, COTS, etc.), **interface control document (ICD)** / **interface design description (IDD)**, and **interface control working group (ICWG)**.

## Key Concepts

- **End products vs. enabling products.** An end product is itself a system of subsystems, and each can have its own enabling products. The ordinary product development process tends to engineer only the end product; PSE earns its keep precisely when the enabling products (or their coupling to the end product) are complex.
- **Three kinds of related system.** A product is engineered alongside a realization system (which creates it) and a sustainment system (which keeps it running) — and trade-offs sometimes have to be struck *between* the features and functions of the product, the realization system, and the sustainment system.
- **Connections are more than interfaces.** Product systems consist of elements and two kinds of connection — element-to-element and element-to-environment ("context"). A connection carries both **interactions** (exchanges of data, material, force, energy across interfaces) and **relationships** (spatial, motion-related, temporal, and *social*). Social relationships — ownership, understanding, how a human "feels" about the system — are often ignored in PSE yet can decide a product's success or failure.
- **Architect with a whole-systems approach.** The architect starts at the highest level of abstraction (needs, functions, characteristics, constraints) before naming components, capturing the product in functional ("what"), behavioral (intended use), logical (interactions/relationships), and physical views. Architecture is captured as a set of models; an **architecture framework** describes a "view" — a collection of models representing the whole system from a set of related stakeholder concerns (per ISO/IEC 42010, IEEE 1471).
- **Acquired vs. offered products.** For **acquired products** the acquirer specifies needs/requirements, selects a supplier, then owns/operates/maintains the result — and government acquirers tend to impose long, detailed *design* specifications that constrain PSE. For **offered products** the supplier perceives a market opportunity and determines the product's properties (and its own life cycle model) via market surveying and forecasting. This distinction drives how product system requirements are defined.
- **Value must exceed cost in the customer's eyes.** Ring's (1998) **system value cycle** has three levels — stakeholder value, customer need, system solution — and value is only fully realized within the context of time, cost, and resources appropriate to key stakeholders.
- **Trade-offs at three levels.** Evaluation of alternatives must trade off conflicting properties (e.g., quality/efficiency against schedule/cost) at the product/system level, the subsystem/architecture level, and the technology level (Blanchard and Fabrycky 2011). Methods named: utility theory, analytic hierarchy process; the Pugh selection method; multi-objective decision analysis; multi-attribute utility analysis, multivariate analysis, and ATAM for software.
- **Product adoption drives timing.** Both the product and the product-model life cycles are paced by the **product adoption rate** (Rogers' diffusion/innovation adoption curve); as a product nears market saturation an upgraded version should be ready, and PSE is critical to timing that release and choosing its highest-value features.
- **Readiness levels gate deployment.** Decisions to introduce a product depend on TRL, IRL, MRL, SRL and the enterprise's operational readiness. TRLs reveal lab/test performance but *not* whether a thing can be produced affordably — hence MRL. COTS maturity assessments break down if the operational environment exceeds the COTS product's design limits.
- **Certification is a fifth verification method.** Beyond inspection, analysis, demonstration, and testing, the INCOSE SE Handbook treats **certification** as verification against legal/industrial standards by an outside authority (e.g., FAA airworthiness, FDA medical, CE, UL, ASME). Enabling-product certifications (pilot/operator certification, NERC system-operator certification, PQT, security C&A, HEMP, TEMPEST) matter too.

## Mental Models

- **The product is an iceberg.** The end product is the visible tip; the enabling products, the realization system, and the sustainment system are the submerged mass. Engineering only the tip works for simple products — PSE exists for the cases where the mass below the waterline is what makes or breaks success.
- **Two-engine NPDP.** Picture new product development as two engines that must stay synchronized: the SE engine (concept → design → deployment) and the market-development engine (research → analysis → acceptance → diffusion). Spin one without the other and the product either lacks a market or lacks a buildable solution.
- **Product as an enabling service system.** Flip the frame: once deployed, a product system *is* a service to its acquirer's operations. Hardware/software enable service provisioning; enterprises absorb products as assets to enable operations; products integrate into systems of systems. A product is never an end in itself — it is bought to enable a capability ("the ability to do something useful").
- **Value flows down three levels.** Run Ring's cycle top-down — stakeholder value sets the frame, customer need scopes it, system solution realizes it — and check that each architecture alternative's contribution traces back up to stakeholder and user value.
- **Readiness as a maturity stack.** Don't read TRL alone. Stack it: TRL (does the technology work?), MRL (can we afford to make it?), IRL (will the pieces integrate?), SRL (is the whole system mature?). A high TRL with a low MRL is a lab success that can't ship.
- **Concurrency over the serial baton-pass.** The old input-output serial development (design → throw over the wall → manufacture) is the failure mode IPD/IPPD and IPTs exist to kill. Bring manufacturing, test, support, finance, and marketing into the team from the outset so life-cycle concerns shape the design instead of ambushing it later.
- **CCB as the single source of truth.** A product may take hundreds of changes across its life. Funnel every baseline change through the CCB via ECR/CCR so the "as-is" configuration is always known — because the operating system is often significantly different from the "as-originally-intended" specification.

## Key Takeaways

- PSE engineers the **product system** — end product *plus* enabling products — over the full life cycle, not just the end product; it is warranted only when enabling products or their couplings are complex.
- New product development fuses **systems engineering** and **market development**; aligning the product with business objectives, internal capabilities, and competition is part of the engineering job.
- A product is engineered together with its **realization system** and **sustainment system**, and trade-offs may be needed across all three.
- "Product" is broader than hardware/software — it spans personnel, facilities, data, materials, media, techniques, and processes; never allocate system behavior to hardware and software alone.
- The **IPDP** runs in four stages (Product Identification, Concept Development, Design and Manufacturing, Product Launch) with **decision gates** and a **continuous improvement** loop, executed by **IPTs/IPDTs** that displaced serial development with **concurrent engineering → IPD → IPPD**.
- **Acquired vs. offered** products demand different requirement and life-cycle strategies; government acquisition tends to impose constraining design specifications.
- Value, trade-offs, and architecture decisions interlock: evaluate alternative architectures and technologies against producibility, quality, performance, cost, schedule, and coverage of the customer need — using methods up to and including **ATAM** for software.
- Deployment hinges on a maturity stack — **TRL, MRL, IRL, SRL** plus operational readiness — and on **certification** as a distinct verification method, both planned in early.
- Disciplined **configuration and interface control** (CCB, ICD/IDD, ICWG, CIs) and integrated **risk management** are non-negotiable for sustained product success.

## Connects To (other SEBoK KAs / standards)

- **Service Systems Engineering (SSE)** — PSE's sibling context in Part 4; the realization system can *be* a service system, and SSE often depends on products produced by PSE (each product in a service infrastructure may need its own PSE life cycle). Product systems are themselves framed as enabling service systems.
- **Enterprise Systems Engineering (ESE)** — one ESE responsibility is managing PSE considerations across multiple product lines while maximizing profit and customer satisfaction; the realization system can be an enterprise system; education/training systems may be needed as a personnel system.
- **Systems of Systems (SoS)** — provided products are integrated into the system of systems; PSE is one of the four Part 4 application contexts that combine in real projects.
- **Capability Engineering** — product/service requirements are derived from **capability management**, which selects coherent sets of products across capability components to meet enterprise goals.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288** — source of the **enabling systems** concept, the **agreement processes** governing acquired products, and the life-cycle stakeholder set (owners, conceivers, developers, producers, users, maintainers via Lawson 2010).
- **ANSI/EIA 632-2003** — the product-system / end-product / enabling-product structure illustrated throughout the KA.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 (and IEEE 1471-2000)** — the architecture-description and "view"/stakeholder-concern definitions underpinning product architecture and the stakeholder-driven architectural properties.
- **Quality Management / ISO 9000, ISO 9001, AS9100, QS-16949, CMMI** — the quality and process-improvement schemes PSE interfaces with (per the Business Activities article).
- **Systems Engineering and Project Management / SEMP** — PSE's concurrency demands tight coupling with project management, coordinated through the systems engineering management plan.
- **Measurement (Part 3)** — referenced for evaluating trade-offs between conflicting properties.
- **System Integration / System Verification** — the assembly, integration, and test (AIT) and verification activities PSE drives; certification is treated as a fifth verification method beyond inspection, analysis, demonstration, and testing.
- **Procurement and Acquisition** — referenced for the market/acquisition linkage across the product life cycle.
- **Traditional Systems Engineering (TSE)** — PSE is aligned with TSE (Wasson; Sage and Rouse; Blanchard and Fabrycky) but extends it beyond hardware/software to the full breadth of product types.
