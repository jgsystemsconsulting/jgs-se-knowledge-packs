# Chapter 30: Enabling Individuals

## Core Idea
Enabling individuals to perform systems engineering requires understanding SE competencies, roles, and the knowledge-skills-abilities-attitudes (KSAA) that individuals develop through education, training, and on-the-job experience. Individual capability directly shapes organizational SE performance.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Systems Engineering Competency Models**: Structured frameworks defining competencies required for SE roles, with proficiency levels assessed using Bloom's Taxonomy (cognitive and affective domains).
  - When to use: When establishing role expectations, assessing individual proficiency, and designing development programs tailored to organizational context.

- **INCOSE SE Competency Model (UK Advisory Board)**: Divides competencies into three theme areas—systems thinking, holistic lifecycle view, and systems management—with specific competencies in each.
  - When to use: As a published reference model for organizations seeking industry-standard SE competency structure.

- **DAU ENG Competency Model**: Defines 41 competency areas grouped into analytical, technical management, professional, and business acumen units, designed for US DoD acquisition engineering professionals.
  - When to use: In defense and government acquisition contexts requiring explicit alignment with acquisition engineering practices.

- **NASA APPEL Competency Model**: Covers system design, product realization, and technical management competencies; includes project management and common competency areas shared across engineering disciplines.
  - When to use: In space systems, aerospace, and mission-driven environments where SE integrates with program management.

- **Bloom's Taxonomy for KSAA Assessment**: Cognitive domain (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create) and affective domain (Receive, Respond, Value, Organize, Characterize) structure proficiency levels and learning objectives.
  - When to use: When defining observable, assessable learning outcomes for competency development and certification.

- **Multi-dimensional Engineering Competency Framework**: Uses discipline, lifecycle, domain, and (optionally) mission as independent axes to assess proficiency across multiple competency dimensions simultaneously.
  - When to use: In large engineering organizations with diverse product domains and specialist engineering roles.

## Key Concepts
- **Competency**: The ability to use appropriate KSAA to successfully complete job-related tasks at an acceptable level of proficiency.
- **KSAA (Knowledge, Skills, Abilities, Attitudes)**: The constituent elements of competency, developed through education, training, and experiential learning.
- **Systems Thinking**: Foundational competency reflecting the ability to adopt a holistic, lifecycle-spanning view of the system and its context.
- **Proficiency Level**: A scale (e.g., awareness, supervised practitioner, practitioner, expert) indicating the depth of demonstrated competency in a specific KSAA.
- **Competency Gap**: The difference between desired competency in a role and the current competency level of an individual or organization.
- **Affective Learning**: Growth in attitude, interest, judgment, and the willingness to apply knowledge in a systems-oriented manner, complementing cognitive domain learning.

## Mental Models
- **Think of competency as multi-dimensional, not linear.** An expert in requirements analysis may be novice-level in systems architecture; organizational capability depends on the collective proficiency across all required dimensions, not on individual mastery of everything.
- **Experiential learning is the primary developer of SE competency.** Formal education and training are enablers, but hands-on engagement with full system lifecycle tasks is what builds systems thinking; shorten product cycles or use rotation assignments to accelerate this.
- **Separate individual competency from organizational performance.** A highly competent individual in risk management may underperform if embedded in a team or organization that ignores risk discipline; competency, capability (processes/tools/equipment), and capacity (time/resources) must all align.
- **Use proficiency levels to target development interventions.** Bloom's Taxonomy levels (Remember through Create, cognitive; Receive through Characterize, affective) allow precise definition of what "competent" means and what learning objective to aim for in training.

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming certification guarantees competence.** INCOSE certification and similar credentials are positive indicators of knowledge and experience, but they do not guarantee suitability for a particular role or readiness to perform in a specific organizational context.
- **Building competency models in isolation from organizational context.** Competency models developed without understanding the organization's domain, complexity level (traditional vs. frontier), strategy, and system lifecycle will misalign training and selection with actual needs.
- **Treating competency development as purely individual training.** The SEBoK emphasizes that organizational capability depends on processes, tools, team dynamics, and organizational support systems, not just individual skill accumulation.
- **Overrelying on general models without domain tailoring.** Published SE competency models (INCOSE, NASA, DAU, MITRE) are valuable references but require domain- and organization-specific customization to reflect actual role requirements and specialist knowledge.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Define roles and competencies explicitly.** Identify which SE roles exist in the organization, what KSAA each role requires, and at what proficiency level; this is prerequisite to assessment and development.
2. **Use Bloom's Taxonomy to specify observable learning outcomes.** Cognitive and affective domains together ensure competency definitions are measurable, learnable, and aligned to both knowledge and attitude development.
3. **Develop individual competency through multiple methods in combination.** Training courses, job rotation, mentoring, hands-on experience, and formal education each contribute differently; combine them based on organizational goals and the specific competency gap.
4. **Recognize that experiential learning dominates SE competency development.** Systems thinking, in particular, develops through working on full-lifecycle system tasks; organizations should structure job assignments and product cycles to provide these opportunities.
5. **Assess context before selecting a competency model.** Organizations working in traditional program environments have different competency emphases than those in messy frontier or enterprise systems engineering; choose or tailor models accordingly.
6. **Distinguish between necessary, important, and enabling characteristics** when using filters (e.g., hiring criteria for systems roles) to avoid false assumptions about which individual traits truly enable SE performance.
7. **Complement individual certification with organizational process improvement.** Certifications provide baseline credentials, but organizational capability grows through standardized processes, knowledge management, and collective discipline—not from individual certifications alone.
