# Chapter 31 — Systems Engineering and Project Management

> Synthesized reference notes from SEBoK v2.14, Knowledge Area **Systems Engineering and Project Management** (Part 6). Content is transformed for study use under CC BY-NC-SA 3.0; attribution to the BKCASE Project. No long verbatim passages are reproduced.

## Core Idea

This Knowledge Area exists to acquaint the systems engineer with project management and to make the relationship between the two disciplines explicit. SEBoK's framing of project management is simple: coordinate and plan the work so that an acceptable product, service, or enterprise endeavor is delivered within the constraints of schedule, budget, resources, infrastructure, staffing, and technology. The KA's lead author (Dick Fairley) deliberately splits the territory in two — there is a **project manager (PM)** who owns the programmatic attributes (plans, estimates, schedule, budget, project structure, staffing, resources, infrastructure, risk factors) and a **systems engineer (SE)** who owns the product/technical attributes (requirements allocation and flow-down, system architecture, technical-team structure and interactions, specialty engineering, integration, verification, validation). Both worry about the same management activities — planning, measuring and controlling, leading and directing, and managing risk — but over different objects.

The central, recurring message is that there is **no standard relationship** between SE and PM. The two roles can be disjoint, partially overlapping, or one nested inside the other, and the exact split depends on customer and stakeholder interactions, the parent organization's structure, and relationships with affiliate contractors and subcontractors. What the KA insists on is that, between them, the PM and SE hold the managerial and technical leadership of the whole project — so each project's people must work out their own boundary explicitly, document it (in the **PMP** and the **SEMP**), and communicate frequently across it. The KA then drills into the operational disciplines where the two roles meet hardest: project structure and governance, cost estimating, procurement and acquisition, and portfolio management.

## Frameworks Introduced

(Exact SEBoK terms; what each is and when/how it is used.)

- **PMBOK® Guide (8th Edition)** — PMI's primary reference consolidating recognized PM terminology and proven practice; PMI's definition of a *project* is a one-time, time-bounded effort that produces a unique product, service, or result. SEBoK treats it as a baseline for technical-management processes inside SE projects and as the basis for the **Project Management Professional (PMP)** certification. The 8th edition fuses the 6th and 7th editions and is organized around **five domain areas**, **six principles**, and **seven performance domains**.
- **Project Management Focus Areas (five domain areas)** — the 8th edition's streamlined re-branding of the 6th edition's process groups: **Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring and Controlling, Closing**. The aim is to keep a recognizable workflow while dropping the memorization-heavy ITTO (inputs, tools, techniques, outputs) descriptions.
- **The six PMBOK® principles** — keeping a holistic view; staying value-focused; building quality into processes and deliverables; leading accountably; weaving sustainability in; and fostering a culture of empowerment.
- **The seven performance domains (8th ed.)** — Governance, Scope, Schedule, Finance, Stakeholders, Resources, Risk — applicable regardless of approach (predictive, incremental, iterative, or adaptive). SEBoK explicitly contrasts these with the 7th edition's *eight* domains — the Team; Stakeholders; Planning; Development Approach and Life Cycle; Project Work; Measurement; and Delivery, Uncertainty).
- **Four-element characterization of PM** (Fairley 2009 / Forsberg 2005) — the KA's own lens on what matters in project management, used in parallel with the PMBOK®: **Planning and Estimating, Measuring and Controlling, Leading and Directing, Managing Risk**.
- **Project Management Plan (PMP)** and **Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP)** — the two key documents that define roles, processes, and methodologies. The PMP is the master planning document covering *all* activities including technical ones; the SEMP is the master plan for the SE technical elements and is required to stay consistent with — and to evolve alongside — the PMP. In U.S. DoD work, the government's **Systems Engineering Plan (SEP)** sits alongside, and the contractor's SEMP is expected to integrate with and stay consistent with it.
- **The organizational structure continuum** — projects are organized as **functional**, **project**, or **matrix** structures (with a fourth treated under Systems Engineering Organizational Strategy). The continuum determines how much control the PM has over SE functions and who owns budget and resources. In a **weak matrix** functional managers assign workers; in a **strong matrix** the PM controls budget and can reject or hire workers.
- **Project Management Office (PMO)** — provides centralized control over a set of projects, focused on business objectives across the portfolio; manages shared resources, oversight, interdependencies, policies, standards, training, and compliance.
- **Integrated Master Plan (IMP) / Integrated Master Schedule (IMS)** — the event-driven plan and time-driven schedule used as planning products (prominent in defense work; DAG Chapter 11). The IMS coordinates schedule-driven projects; the IMP coordinates event-driven projects.
- **Integrated Product Team (IPT), Change Control Board (CCB), Engineering Review Board (ERB)** — the related governance structures that force PM–SE coordination. The top-level IPT is sometimes the **Systems Engineering and Integration Team (SEIT)**. The CCB receives an **Engineering Change Proposal (ECP)** and decides feasibility as part of configuration management; the ERB performs technical impact analysis, adjudicates trade studies, and reviews baseline changes (e.g., the **Management Safety Review Board (MSRB)**).
- **Parametric cost models** — named exemplars: **PRICE** for hardware, **COCOMO** for software, **COSYSMO** for systems-engineering effort.
- **The three primary cost-estimating methods** — **Analogy** ("it's like one of these"), **Parametric** ("this pattern holds," built on **cost estimating relationships (CERs)**), and **Build-up** ("it's made up of these," bottom-up roll-up over a WBS). Two lesser methods: **expert opinion / engineering judgment** (incl. the **Delphi technique**) and **extrapolation from actual** (a subset of other methods, e.g. EVM).
- **The seven-step cost estimating process** — (1) develop the **WBS**; (2) establish a **technical baseline**; (3) collect data; (4) analyze data; (5) apply methodology and develop **CERs**; (6) roll up and validate; (7) generate cost reports / **Basis of Estimate (BOE)**. Iterative, with feedback loops.
- **Acquisition Process Model** — a phased model (DAU 2010) in which technology is defined and matured into concepts, then developed, readied for production, and supported in the field; a given acquisition may enter at any development phase depending on technology maturity.
- **Systems Engineering Plan (SEP)** in acquisition — the blueprint documenting the SE strategy for an acquisition program; defines SE structure, government/contractor boundaries, summarizes the acquisition strategy, and links to program risks.
- **Portfolio Management (PfM) as an organizational project-enabling process** — per **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288**, with inputs (strategic plan, portfolio direction/constraints, supply strategy, project status) and outputs (portfolio management plan, project portfolio, lessons learned, reports/records); structured into PMI's portfolio life cycle of **optimization, initiation, planning, execution** and PMI's **six performance management domains**.

## Key Concepts

- **PM and SE manage the same activities over different objects.** Both plan, measure and control, lead and direct, and manage risk — but the PM does it for the overall project (programmatic risk) and the SE does it for the technical solution (technical risk). The danger is each confronting their own risks without adequate communication, so that the right trade-offs across requirements, schedule, budget, infrastructure, and technology never get made.
- **Planning answers who/what/where/when/why.** Who (staffing — competencies, numbers, communication, coordination), what (scope of activities), where (locale — local or geographically distributed), when (scheduling), why (rationale). SEBoK observes that communication and coordination among stakeholders during planning can matter *more* than the documented plan that results.
- **An estimate projects the past forward, adjusted for what differs.** The named estimation techniques are analogy, rule of thumb, expert judgment, and parametric models. SE contributes by ensuring the life cycle is understood, dependencies (on other systems/organizations and logical development dependencies) are identified, and key skills/resources are planned. The high-level architecture and risk assessment seed both the **work breakdown structure (WBS)** and the **organizational breakdown structure**.
- **Measuring and controlling is the heart of execution.** Measurement collects measures of work products and processes (e.g., requirement coverage via review, analysis, prototyping, traceability; effort/schedule via earned value). Controlling analyzes that data and triggers corrective action when actual diverges from planned.
- **SE projects are inherently high-risk, so risk management is continuous.** Many unknowns and uncertainties are inherent; new risk factors emerge during the project. The last decade has added growing interest in **opportunity management** — risk management's converse (Hillson; Olsson; Chapman & Ward).
- **The PMBOK® is industry-agnostic and must be tailored.** It is not a rigid methodology; principles, domains, and processes are tools to be adapted to each project's complexity, customer, and goals. Because it lacks technical-domain specificity for complex systems, the PM–SE relationship must be clarified so management and engineering life cycles align. SE artifacts (technical risk assessments, **interface control documents (ICDs)**, SEMP updates) feed back into the project risk domain; project artifacts (WBS elements, milestones) map to technical work packages, reviews, and decision gates in the SDLC.
- **Roles are best made explicit to reduce confusion.** Because overlap breeds tension between PM and SE, the cleanest defense is to describe roles and responsibilities explicitly (in PMP and SEMP) — and an effective systems engineer needs strong management skills and experience alongside technical depth, becoming a core member of the team that manages and leads the project.
- **Structure determines governance authority.** In a functional organization the PM is a coordinator with limited control and the functional manager owns budget/resources; in a project organization the PM has full authority and the SE is subject to the PM's direction; a matrix blends both. PM–SE relationships must be clarified and communicated to all stakeholders regardless of structure.
- **Schedule-driven vs. requirements-driven projects pull governance in opposite directions.** When meeting the schedule outranks satisfying all requirements (contractual delivery dates, market-driven product releases), the PM tends to play the central role and an IMS coordinates the work. When satisfying requirements outranks the schedule (exploratory threat-mitigation development, regulated aviation/medical systems), the SE tends to play the central role and an IMP coordinates event-driven work.
- **Cost estimating is "art and science" at the SE–PM intersection.** SE *defines* the technical scope, cost estimating *quantifies* it, and PM *manages* it. Historically owned by specialist **cost analysts**, estimating increasingly needs systems engineers for product and design knowledge. Estimates evolve as design matures and must be refreshed at major milestones.
- **Historical data is the foundation of credible estimates.** Without it there is no basis for methodology and estimates are mere guesses; collecting it is akin to forensic analysis of development history, often a project in its own right, and depends on stakeholder interviews to understand what the data means.
- **Acquisition is broader than procurement.** Procurement is buying goods and services; acquisition spans the whole life cycle — initiation; conceptualization; design; then development and testing; contracting; production; deployment; logistics support; modification; and finally disposal. SE is an overarching, integrating mechanism across every acquisition phase, balancing capability needs against design considerations and constraints of technology, budget, and schedule.
- **Contract type allocates risk.** **Fixed price** transfers risk to the supplier (cost overruns absorbed by the supplier; bids inflated to cover risk); **cost-reimbursement (cost plus)** keeps risk primarily with the offeror and is used under high uncertainty. Other forms include subcontracts, outsourcing, and a COTS spectrum from **exclusively COTS** through **integrated COTS** to **COTS modification** (the last requiring the most time and cost).
- **Portfolio management selects the "right" work.** Program/project management does projects the right *way*; portfolio management selects the right programs/projects; systems engineering supplies the right *systems thinking*. Skipping PfM raises organizational risk and can erode stakeholder value. A project should continue only if it still contributes to strategy, makes progress on its goals, obeys project directives, follows its plan, and adds value at acceptable returns.

## Mental Models

**The producer and the director.** SEBoK's governing analogy: the project manager is the *movie producer* balancing schedule, budget, and resources for customer satisfaction; the systems engineer is the *movie director* responsible for product content. Use this to predict who should lead — producer-led when schedule dominates, director-led when requirements dominate.

**The PM–SE overlap is a Venn diagram you must draw yourself.** There is no standard split. On some projects responsibilities don't overlap at all; on others planning and managing are shared; on small projects one person is both. Don't assume a default — explicitly carve the boundary for *this* project and write it into the PMP and SEMP.

**Two plans that move together.** The SEMP is subordinate to the PMP but is not a static appendix — it has to remain consistent with the PMP and change in step with it (and, in defense work, integrate with the customer's SEP). Treat a SEMP that has drifted out of step with the PMP as a defect.

**"SE defines it, cost estimating quantifies it, PM manages it."** When a number is disputed, trace it back through this chain: is the disagreement about technical scope (SE), about how the scope was costed (estimating), or about how the budget is being run (PM)? The fix lives wherever the chain broke.

**Pick the estimating method by where you are in the life cycle.** Analogy early (little data, sanity checks); parametric across the life cycle (sensitivity analyses, trade studies, proposals); build-up late, in operational settings with established processes and detailed actuals. Use a second method as a cross-check rather than trusting one in isolation.

**Choose the contract that puts the risk where it belongs.** Map uncertainty to contract type: stable, well-defined scope → fixed price (risk to supplier); high uncertainty or likely stakeholder change → cost-reimbursement (risk to offeror). The contract is a risk-allocation instrument, not just a price.

**Right way vs. right thing.** Project management and good engineering can execute a project flawlessly that should never have been funded. Portfolio management is the layer that asks whether this is the *right* project at all — and it feeds termination decisions back up from project progress, so a well-run-but-no-longer-valuable project still gets cancelled.

## Key Takeaways

1. **PM and SE share four management activities over two different object sets.** Planning/estimating, measuring/controlling, leading/directing, and managing risk — programmatic for the PM, technical for the SE. Force communication across the boundary so trade-offs actually get made.
2. **There is no standard PM–SE relationship — define it per project.** Disjoint, overlapping, or nested are all valid; the people on each project must work out and document their own split.
3. **The PMP and SEMP are the boundary contract.** The PMP covers all activities; the SEMP covers the SE technical elements and must stay consistent with and evolve alongside the PMP (and the customer's SEP in defense work).
4. **Treat the PMBOK® as a tailorable toolkit, not a methodology.** Use the 8th edition's five domains, six principles, and seven performance domains as tools applied with the SDLC; following process for its own sake produces failed, value-poor projects.
5. **Plan against who/what/where/when/why and estimate from the past.** Let SE seed the WBS and OBS from architecture and risk; remember coordination during planning can outweigh the plan document itself.
6. **Match governance to whether the project is schedule- or requirements-driven.** Schedule-driven → PM-centric, coordinated by IMS; requirements-driven → SE-centric, coordinated by IMP. Use IPTs, CCBs, and ERBs as the shared governance machinery.
7. **Estimate cost iteratively with the right method and real historical data.** Run the seven-step process, choose analogy/parametric/build-up by life-cycle stage and data availability, document a defensible BOE, and refresh at every major milestone.
8. **Distinguish acquisition from procurement and allocate risk through contract type.** SE integrates across every acquisition phase; fixed price moves risk to the supplier, cost-reimbursement keeps it with the offeror, and the COTS spectrum trades integration cost for modification.
9. **Use portfolio management to select and continually re-justify the work.** Qualify and prioritize opportunities, allocate resources, and terminate or redirect projects that stop contributing to strategy — feeding project progress back into reprioritization.

## Connects To

- **PMBOK® Guide / PMI Standards** (8th ed. 2025; 6th ed. 2017; 5th ed. 2013) and **The Standard for Portfolio Management** — the project- and portfolio-management canon underpinning the whole KA, including the **PMP** certification baseline.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288** (System Life Cycle Processes) — defines portfolio management as an *organizational project-enabling process* and grounds the cost-estimating purpose (ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023); the backbone standard linking PM and SE.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 16326** (Life Cycle Processes — Project Management) — guidance for developing project plans, cited alongside INCOSE and NASA handbooks.
- **SEBoK Part 3 — Systems Engineering and Management** — explicit cross-references to **Measurement**, **Project Assessment and Control**, **Risk Management**, **Technical Management Processes**, **Stakeholder Needs Definition**, and **Configuration Management** (the change-control flow).
- **SEBoK Part 5 — Enabling Systems Engineering** — **Team Capability** (organizing the team; PM-central vs. SE-central structures), **Systems Engineering Organizational Strategy** (the fourth organizational structure and the SEIT), and the broader organizational considerations for PM–SE relationships.
- **INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook** (3.2.2 / 4th ed. 2015 / 5.0) — portfolio-management best practices, the SE definition (per INCOSE 2021), and life-cycle process guidance.
- **CMMI for Development** — cited (with the PMBOK® and SEBoK) as describing the overlapping scope of SE and PM.
- **DoD / DAU acquisition framework** — the **Defense Acquisition Guidebook (DAG)** (IMP/IMS, acquisition process model), the **Guide for Integrating Systems Engineering into DoD Acquisition Contracts**, the **SEP Outline**, and **MIL-STD-881** (WBS framework).
- **Cost-estimating bodies and guides** — **ICEAA / CEBoK®** (with predecessors SCEA and ISPA), the **GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (GAO-20-195G)**, the **DoD Cost Estimating Guide**, and the **NASA Cost Estimating Handbook** — the external standards behind the cost-estimating article.
- **Decision analysis** (Parnell et al., *Handbook of Decision Analysis*) — the basis for the project-prioritization value models portfolio managers use to select and fund work.
