## Core Idea
Every system has properties that emerge from the whole rather than from any single part — security, reliability, safety, resilience, resistance to electromagnetic interference, usability, and dozens more. This Knowledge Area is about those properties and how they are realized. The vocabulary is famously non-standard: the same things are called **quality attributes**, **non-functional requirements**, **-ilities**, and **specialties**. The KA adopts the term **specialty engineering** for the collective engineering of all of a system's quality attributes. The defining difficulty is that these attributes are interdependent rather than orthogonal — making a system more secure can make it harder to use but also safer; raising reliability often demands costlier parts and redundancy, which raises delivery cost but may lower maintenance cost. The systems engineer's job is therefore one of continuous balancing: specifying which attributes matter for a given system, stating how they will be measured, trading off conflicting ones, and verifying the system actually has them. Specialty requirements typically show up as **constraints on the overall system design space**, and the role of systems engineering is to harmonize those constraints with functionality to produce a system that delivers utility and effectiveness at an affordable price. A recurring meta-theme runs across the articles — several of these attributes (resilience, safety, security, reliability, availability, environmental impact, quality) share a common concern with **loss**, and engineering them in isolation wastes effort and creates conflicts that a unified, loss-driven view can eliminate.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Specialty engineering** — the KA's umbrella term for the collective engineering of all of a system's quality attributes. The KA notes a genuine split of opinion (left unresolved on purpose, since articles are written by many authors): some treat disciplines like security, safety, and resilience engineering as **sub-disciplines of SE itself**; others treat them as **stand-alone disciplines** closely tied to SE.
  - When/how to use: as the organizing concept for the whole KA — the lens through which security, safety, RAM, resilience, HSI, affordability, hardware assurance, and EMI/EMC are all viewed as facets of one balancing act led by systems engineering.
- **The three-filter Integration Process for Specialty Engineering (Figure 1, after USAF 2000)** — a model of how SE integrates traditional, specialty, and product demands into a design. The **first filter** is conceptual analysis using traditional design considerations (structural, electronics, aerodynamics, mechanical, thermodynamics); the **second filter** evaluates the concept through specialty disciplines (affordability; safety; quality assurance; reliability and maintainability; human factors; producibility; test; packaging; and logistics); the **third filter** folds in facility design, equipment design, procedural data, computer programs, and personnel so as to set final design-selection requirements.
  - When/how to use: as the mental picture for *where* specialty engineering enters — design alternatives must survive all three filters, and a design could in principle be done without the specialty filter, which is exactly why special emphasis on integration is mandatory.
- **Loss-Driven Systems Engineering (LDSE)** (Brtis) — defined as an overarching SE process that holistically addresses the quality characteristics concerned with loss (resilience, safety, security, and others). It is the value-adding unification of the SE specialty areas that address potential losses. The framework rests on shared attributes across the loss-driven areas: which **asset types** are considered; which **loss types** are addressed; which **adversity types** are addressed; and which **coping strategies** are weighed, and the **aspects of the system and its environment** under consideration.
  - When/how to use: when multiple loss-related specialties touch the same system — LDSE says to elicit loss-driven requirements together, make architectural and design decisions holistically across the specialties, and integrate the management of all loss-related risks, rather than letting each specialty operate in a silo.
- **The Core Principles for LDSE (Winstead 2020)** — eight candidate principles unified across the loss-driven specialties: SE minimizes hazards; SE seeks to control hazards that cannot be avoided (including assuring transitions between known acceptable modes/states); SE uses proven and accepted processes/solutions/methods/materials when they achieve the intended trustworthiness; the person inside the system should be equipped to prevent loss, limit it, and recover from it; SE should strive for the simplest solutions; SE produces evolvable systems that maintain or improve loss-driven properties through change; actions should trace to the entity responsible; every critical task ought to be achievable by more than a single means.
  - When/how to use: as a starting, cross-specialty principle set during early life cycle activities; Winstead also adapts Watson's (2019) SE principles to make them well-suited to LDSE.
- **The LDSE "loss control" design principles (Winstead 2021)** — a set for cyber-physical systems, independent of any one specialty: Anomaly Detection, Commensurate Protection, Commensurate Response, Continuous Protection, Defense-in-depth, Distributed Privilege, Diversity (Dynamicity), Domain Separation, Least Functionality, Least Persistence, Least Privilege, Least Sharing, Loss Margins, Mediated Access, Protective Defaults, Protective Failure, Protective Recovery, and Redundancy.
  - When/how to use: as reusable architecture/design heuristics for controlling loss in cyber-physical systems — note these recur, near-verbatim, in the System Resilience taxonomy and the System Security article, which is the point: shared design solutions across loss-driven areas.
- **Human Systems Integration (HSI) and its seven domains** — HSI is the managerial and technical discipline that plans, enables, coordinates, and optimizes every human-related consideration across the system life cycle (SAE 2019). The seven domains (per the DoD Instruction set) are **Manpower, Personnel, Training, Human Factors Engineering, Safety & Occupational Health, Force Protection & Survivability, and Habitability**. Organizations vary the count and names (the UK Def Stan 00-251 set, the original US Army set), but the technical work of the domains is always present.
  - When/how to use: when humans must be integrated as first-class system elements — initiated early (during stakeholder requirements generation), documented in the SEMP or a stand-alone HSI Plan (HSIP), and led by a **human systems integrator** who is a knowledgeable systems engineer (not merely a human factors engineer) coordinating the domain experts.
- **The Reliability, Availability, and Maintainability (RAM) process model (GEIA-STD-0009)** — four processes — **grasping user requirements and constraints; designing for reliability; producing for reliability; and monitoring through operation and use**. RAM are inherent attributes considered across the whole development life cycle.
  - When/how to use: as the spine for RAM engineering, supported by FRACAS/FRACAS-class failure-tracking systems, FMECA, fault trees, reliability block diagrams, and the reliability test families.
- **The three-layer objectives-based Resilience taxonomy (RSWG, drawing on Brtis 2016; Jackson and Ferris 2013; Winstead 2020)** — Layer 1 the **fundamental objectives** (avoid, withstand, recover from adversity); Layer 2 the **means objectives** (adapt, anticipate, constrain, continue, degrade gracefully, disaggregate, evade, evolve, fortify, manage complexity, minimize adversity, minimize faults, monitor, preserve integrity, prevent, reduce vulnerability, repair, replace, tolerate, understand); Layer 3 the **architecture, design, and operational techniques** (absorption, anomaly detection, defense-in-depth, diversification, domain separation, fault tolerance, least privilege, modularity, redundancy, safe state, segmentation, shielding, virtualization, and many more). The three layers relate by many-to-many relationships.
  - When/how to use: to move from *why* resilience (Layer 1) to *what* the stakeholder needs (Layer 2, treated as high-level functional requirements during stakeholder needs definition) to *how* the system achieves it (Layer 3, considered during requirements, architecture, design, and operation).
- **The RSWG resilience definition and the proactive/reactive distinction** — INCOSE's Resilient Systems Working Group treats resilience as the capacity to keep delivering required capability in the face of adversity," and asserts it must address both the **reactive perspective** (reactions after encountering adversity) and the **proactive perspective** (actions taken before adversity). The high-level means are **avoiding, withstanding, and recovering** from adversity.
  - When/how to use: as the working definition that resolves the proliferation of competing resilience definitions; "avoiding" adversity is included as a legitimate means for engineered systems, alongside the classic withstand/recover.
- **The System Security essentials and Loss Control Objectives (Ross, Winstead, & McEvilley 2022, i.e., NIST SP 800-160 Vol. 1 Rev. 1)** — security is freedom from conditions that may lead to loss of assets with undesired consequences. A secure system ideally has three essential characteristics: **enable required capability delivery despite adversity; enforce constraints so only desired behaviors/outcomes occur; enforce constraints based on rules defining only authorized interactions**. The two **Loss Control Objectives** are **Prevent the Loss from Occurring** and **Limit the Extent of Loss** (the latter via limiting dispersion, duration, capacity, or volume, and via Loss Recovery and Loss Delay).
  - When/how to use: as the foundational security model — security's scope matches SE's scope (every SE process and activity has security considerations), so security must be a foundational perspective from concept exploration; design for **as secure as reasonably practical (ASARP)**, informed by the principle of **commensurate trustworthiness**.

## Key Concepts
- **Specialty requirements as design-space constraints**: requirements about quality attributes (also called specialty requirements) must be reviewed for impact on life cycle costs, schedule, technical performance, and operational utility — e.g., security requirements affect operator workstations, EMI requirements affect inter-subsystem interface signals, mass-volume requirements can preclude certain materials. Including specialty engineers within each SE team ensures all specialty requirements are identified and balanced through development, and engineering specialists audit the evolving design and configuration items so overall performance still satisfies the specialty requirements. Barry Boehm's SQOTA project (SERC, 2016) introduced an ontology of system quality attributes to enable smarter trade-offs and understand their impact on cost; de Weck et al. (2012) provide an extended treatment of the -ilities.
- **The loss-driven commonality argument**: capability-driven methodologies (the INCOSE SE Handbook, ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288) integrate the full life cycle around *delivering* capability but treat loss-driven specialties largely in isolation. The loss-driven areas — availability; environmental impact; maintainability; resilience engineering; reliability; risk management; safety engineering; security engineering; and quality management — share loss scenarios, requirements, modeling and analysis techniques, architecture and design solutions, and risk management. Benefits of unifying them include reducing redundant effort, ensuring comprehensive loss consideration, eliminating conflicts among loss-driven solutions, and mutual learning across specialties. Brtis found six SE practice areas in 15288/the Handbook need augmentation for LDSE: Business or Mission Analysis, Stakeholder Needs and Requirements Definition, System Requirements Definition, Architecture Definition, Design Definition, and Risk Management.
- **Siloism**: Jackson (2020) names **siloism** — the unwillingness of team members to share information — as a central management threat to LDSE, recommending integrated product teams (IPTs) with organizational structure and rigorous management to encourage sharing across specialties. Endler (2021) finds existing SE standards poorly describe loss-driven activities and fail to integrate them with traditional engineering.
- **HSI is not human factors**: HSI should not be confused with **Human Factors Engineering (HFE)**, which is one *domain* of HSI focused on designing human interfaces. HSI is the mutual integration of technology, organizations, and people. The discipline traces to the US Army's **MANPRINT** (Manpower and Personnel Integration) program (1986); the UK MoD uses **Human Factors Integration (HFI)**. The human systems integrator is typically senior engineering staff reporting to the SE lead or chief engineer, makes trade-offs among domains, and need not be an expert in any single domain.
- **HSI metrics**: a **measure of effectiveness (MOE)** corresponds to mission objective accomplishment and is typically achieved by hardware+software+human together, so there are not usually HSI-specific MOEs. MOEs decompose into **measures of performance (MOP)** and **measures of suitability (MOS)**, and there *can* be HSI-specific MOPs and MOSs (e.g., an air-defense radar MOE of detection probability with an MOP for resolution, plus an MOP capturing how well the operator can identify the target).
- **Manufacturability vs. producibility**: both are key attributes determining the ease of building a system. **Manufacturability** is just how easily something can be manufactured; **producibility** reaches further, taking in other parts of the production task such as packaging and shipping. Manufacturing equipment and processes can cost more than the system being built (Maier and Rechtin 2009), so manufacturability/producibility is a discriminator between competing solution concepts and must be considered early.
- **Affordability and the Parnas modularization principle**: a system is affordable to the degree performance, cost, and schedule constraints are kept in balance across the system's life while the mission's needs are met (INCOSE 2015). Historically cost/schedule estimation has been decoupled from technical trade-off analyses, drawing organizations into easiest-first approaches that leave them with brittle, expensive-to-change architectures (technical debt). **Modularizing the architecture around the system's most frequent sources of change (Parnas 1979)** is a key affordability principle, because change side effects are then contained in one element rather than rippling across the system. This extends to product-line commonality/variability, service-oriented elements, and smart/autonomous systems — and shifts the focus from life cycle cost to **total ownership cost** (including failure costs). Aggregating stakeholder preferences is itself hard, hitting multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) problems and paradoxes such as **Arrow's impossibility theorem**.
- **Hardware assurance: "function as intended and only as intended"**: system hardware assurance is a set of system security engineering activities to increase confidence that electronics function as intended and *only* as intended across their life cycle. **Weaknesses** are flaws/bugs/errors in design, architecture, code, or implementation; **vulnerabilities** are weaknesses exploitable in the context of use (Martin 2014). Checking for *unintended* functions is the hard part — a state-of-the-art component may have billions of transistors hiding exploitable functions (the webcam-that-records-when-off example). Assurance must be "baked in" early because risks created during architecture, design, and manufacturing are costly to fix later; both component value and mitigation cost rise at each life cycle stage. Operational risk areas: failure to meet quality standards, maliciously tainted goods, and counterfeit hardware. Quantification methods are adapted from quality/reliability engineering (FMEA), supplemented by **game-theoretic analysis** of attacker/defender interactions, the **SAE AS6171** confidence-interval approach for counterfeit detection, testing against catalogs like the MITRE **CVE** list, and **distributed ledger technology (DLT)** for data integrity, immutability, and traceability. The discipline relies on a **dynamic risk profile** and **mitigation-in-depth**.
- **RAM definitions and the failure vocabulary**: **Reliability** is the probability of performing the intended function under stated conditions without failure for a given time (ASQ); **Maintainability** is the probability a maintenance action can be performed within a stated time under stated conditions, with two categories — **serviceability** (ease of scheduled inspection/servicing) and **repairability** (ease of restoring service after failure); **Availability** is the probability a repairable system is operational at a given point in time, and depends on reliability and maintainability. A **failure** is the event/inoperable state where an item does not perform as specified; the **failure mechanism** is the physical/chemical/electrical/thermal process; the **failure mode** is the way/consequence of the mechanism; **severity** is the magnitude of impact (GEIA 2008; Laprie 1992).
- **RAM models and metrics**: system models include **reliability block diagrams (RBDs)** (which depict paths to *success*), **fault trees** (which depict paths to *failure*, pioneered by Bell Labs in the 1960s, using AND/OR/NOT/K-of-N gates), **FMEA/FMECA**, Markov models, and Petri nets. Key metrics: **MTTF** (mean time to failure, non-restorable) and **MTBF** (mean time between failures, restorable); **MTTR** (mean time to repair). Availability has three flavors — **inherent availability** (corrective maintenance downtime only), **achieved availability** (corrective + preventive), and **operational availability** (all downtime including logistical/administrative). **Reliability importance** is the partial derivative of system reliability w.r.t. a component's reliability; **criticality** combines a component's reliability, failure consequences, and failure-to-system-failure frequency to prioritize improvement. The persistent data problem: reliability data is censored, biased, observational, and missing covariates, with small samples — so estimates from limited data are usually very imprecise ("GIGO" applies acutely to system models), and **censored units** must be analyzed, not omitted.
- **Resilience: stress, strain, and the scope of adversity**: an **adversity** is any condition that could degrade delivered capability; it may come from inside or outside the system, be expected or unexpected, and (if from humans) malicious or accidental. The proximate adversities directly affecting the system are **stresses**; their effects on the system are **strains**. Future risks can cause present strain, so systems should be designed resilient to future risks too. **Affordable resilience** means an effective balance between cost and resilience over the life cycle, prioritized via standard techniques such as Multi-attribute Utility Theory (MAUT) or the Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP). **Resilience requirements** often take the form of **resilience scenarios** (found in ConOps/OpsCons) and come in three equivalent forms — natural language, entity-relationship diagram, and a SysML extension.
- **Resilience modeling is emerging, with no accepted single method**: a system resilience model is a selective abstraction of a system providing required capability when facing adversity. Approaches include **Resilience Contracts (RCs)** (Madni, Erwin, & Sievers 2020), which extend Contract-Based Design to handle unpredictability and are represented as a **Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP)**; and **system dynamics** (causal loop diagrams, archetypes, exploratory vs. calibrated quantitative models). Caveats: use a model only for its intended purpose; check assumptions and constraints; and watch **Constraint Theory** issues (Friedman & Phan 2017) such as over-/under-constrained computational sets and Basic Nodal Squares (BNS) forming the "kernel of intrinsic constraint."
- **EMI/EMC and spectrum governance**: **Electromagnetic Interference (EMI)** is the disruption of an electronic device's operation in the vicinity of an RF-spectrum electromagnetic field; **Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC)** is the ability to operate in the intended environment without unacceptable degradation or causing it. EMI is categorized as **narrowband** and **broadband** emissions. Spectrum is governed internationally by the **ITU** (193 member countries) via four organizations (World Radio-communication Conference, Radio Regulations Board, Radio-communications Bureau, Radio-communication Study Groups); the US uses **dual management** — the **FCC** (non-government) and the **NTIA** (federal government), established by the Communications Act of 1934. Military operations abroad require **Host Nation Coordination/Approval (HNC/HNA)**. **TEMPEST** is the codename for the field of emission security (compromising emanations). **Radiation Hardening** protects electronics against ionizing radiation (Van Allen belts, cosmic radiation, reactor gamma/neutron, nuclear EMP), countering **lattice displacement** (permanent) and **ionization effects** (temporary, e.g., bit flips).
- **System Safety per MIL-STD-882E**: system safety is "applying engineering and management principles, criteria, and techniques so as to reach acceptable risk... throughout all phases of the system life cycle" (DoD 2012); a **hazard** is a real or potential condition that could lead to a mishap (death, injury, illness, equipment/property loss, or environmental damage); **risk** combines the severity of the mishap and the probability it occurs. AFI 91-202 names eight generic activities — documenting the approach, hazard identification/analysis, risk assessment (severity and probability), identifying/assessing mitigations, implementing measures to reduce risk to acceptable levels, verifying risk reduction, risk acceptance by appropriate authorities, and tracking hazards/risks across the life cycle. The usual mechanisms here are a **Safety Working Group (SWG)** together with a **Management Safety Review Board (MSRB)**. The KA's Safety Ontology (Table 1) ties failure modes, severity, criticality, hazard identification, risk, and mitigation together and shows safety's close tie to RAM.
- **System Security: assets, loss, loss scenarios, and trustworthiness**: an **asset** is an item of value to a stakeholder; the asset classes are Material Resources and Infrastructure, System Capability, Human Resources, Intellectual Property, Data and Information, and Derivative Non-Tangible (image, reputation, trust). **Loss** is having an asset taken/destroyed or failing to keep it in a desired state — and it provides language understood by all stakeholders, letting needs be expressed as loss, loss scenarios, loss tolerance, and acceptable loss. **Loss scenarios** capture internal system conditions (states, faults) and external environmental conditions that lead to unacceptable outcomes, without necessarily tracing to "root causes." Security calls for focusing on a system's **trustworthiness** rather than singularly on risk; absent assurance, security functionality is mere **veneer security** (Saydjari 2018). Security is one of 10 areas of growing stakeholder expectation in the INCOSE SE Vision 2035, which aims for security to become "as foundational to systems design as performance and safety already are today."

## Mental Models
- **Quality attributes are properties of the whole, and they fight each other.** A quality attribute is largely a function of the system as a whole, not its parts, and the attributes are interdependent, not orthogonal. Treat every -ility decision as a trade — more security can cost usability, more reliability can cost money but save maintenance. The systems engineer's value is in balancing, measuring, and verifying these, not maximizing any one.
- **Specialty requirements are constraints on the design space; SE harmonizes them.** Specialty engineering shows up as constraints, and a design *could* be completed without ever passing the specialty filter — which is precisely why integrating specialists into the team and auditing the evolving design is mandatory, not optional.
- **Look at loss across the specialties, not one silo at a time.** Resilience, safety, security, reliability, availability, environmental impact, and quality all care about loss — shared assets, losses, adversities, requirements, and design solutions. Engineering them separately duplicates effort and breeds conflicts; a loss-driven view (LDSE) unifies them. Siloism is the management failure that keeps this from happening.
- **Resilience reads top-down: avoid/withstand/recover → means → techniques.** When designing for resilience, descend the three-layer taxonomy. Start with the fundamental objectives (avoid, withstand, recover), translate them into stakeholder means objectives (adapt, anticipate, fortify, degrade gracefully...), then select concrete architecture/design/operational techniques (redundancy, diversification, segmentation, safe state...). The layers map many-to-many, so one technique can serve several objectives.
- **Stress is what hits the system; strain is what the system feels.** Distinguish the proximate adversity (stress) from its effect (strain), and remember a *future* risk can impose a *present* strain — so design for resilience to anticipated future adversity, not just today's.
- **"Function as intended and only as intended."** For hardware (and arguably all elements), specifying and verifying intended function is the easy half; checking that nothing *extra* was built in is the hard half. Bake assurance in early — fixing a tainted or weak component late is exponentially more expensive and disruptive.
- **HSI is the integrator's job, not the human factors engineer's.** Putting an HFE in the integrator seat is a mistake unless they are a strong systems engineer. HSI integrates technology, organizations, and people across seven domains; HFE is just one of those domains.
- **Security's scope is SE's scope — so engineer it in from concept.** "Where security is not engineered in from the very start, a system has little chance of being made secure later by retrofit (Anderson 1972). Express needs in terms of loss, design ASARP with commensurate trustworthiness, and build assurance — anything less is veneer security.
- **Read the model's limits before trusting its answer.** Resilience and RAM models are abstractions; reliability data is censored and sparse, and complex models can be over- or under-constrained. Use a model only for its intended purpose, validate assumptions, and treat strong extrapolations as unquantified risk to the systems engineer.

## Anti-patterns
The KA names several pitfalls and hazardous patterns explicitly:
- **Siloism (Jackson 2020)** — the unwillingness of team members to share information across loss-driven specialties; if unmitigated it reduces the effectiveness of the entire team. Mitigation: integrated product teams (IPTs) with structure and rigorous management.
- **Relegating HSI to a "specialty engineering" team / mis-staffing the integrator (HSI Pitfalls)** — assigning a human factors engineer who is not well versed in the SE process to the integrator role, and relegating HSI to a specialty team, deprives the integrator of sufficient scope and authority to accomplish the mission.
- **Easiest-first acquisition for apparent affordability** — focusing on minimizing acquisition cost draws organizations into easiest-first approaches that yield early successes but leave them left holding brittle, costly-to-change architectures that grow technical debt and life-cycle cost.
- **Autonomous-system hazardous failure modes (System Affordability)** — explicitly named failure modes for autonomous systems: instability from positive feedback (agents over-correcting against control limits), self-modifying agents that fail after several self-modifications and are hard to debug, agents reasoning weakly about commonsense control decisions, and multiple agents making contradictory control decisions without the ability to recognize or negotiate the contradiction.
- **Veneer security (Saydjari 2018)** — security functionality presented without the assurance evidence (from V&V) that would merit trustworthiness, providing an unmerited sense of trustworthiness.
- **Omitting censored units in reliability analysis** — inexperienced analysts who do not know how to analyze censored data omit the censored units, which biases the analysis; failure to plan the chain running backward from decision, to analysis, to model, to the data required yields inadequate data collection and missing essential information.

## Key Takeaways
1. Quality attributes (a.k.a. -ilities, non-functional requirements, specialties) are properties of the whole system; the KA calls their collective engineering **specialty engineering**, and the SE job is to specify, measure, trade off, and verify them while balancing their interdependence.
2. Specialty requirements present as **constraints on the design space**; SE integrates traditional, specialty, and product demands (the three-filter model after USAF 2000) and embeds specialists in the team so requirements are identified, balanced, and audited.
3. **Loss-Driven Systems Engineering (LDSE)** unifies the loss-related specialties (resilience, safety, security, reliability, availability, environmental impact, quality, risk management) around shared assets, losses, adversities, and coping strategies — eliminating redundant effort and conflicting solutions; **siloism** is the main obstacle.
4. **Human Systems Integration (HSI)** integrates technology, organizations, and people across seven domains (Manpower, Personnel, Training, Human Factors Engineering, Safety & Occupational Health, Force Protection & Survivability, Habitability); it is led by a human-systems-integrator who is a systems engineer, and it is distinct from Human Factors Engineering.
5. **RAM** — reliability, availability (inherent/achieved/operational), and maintainability (serviceability + repairability) — is engineered across the four-process GEIA model using RBDs (paths to success), fault trees (paths to failure), FMEA/FMECA, and metrics like MTTF/MTBF/MTTR, while contending with censored, sparse data.
6. **Resilience** is "the ability to provide required capability when facing adversity" (RSWG), addressed proactively and reactively, via a three-layer taxonomy: fundamental objectives (avoid/withstand/recover) → means objectives → architecture/design/operational techniques; design for **affordable resilience** balanced over the life cycle.
7. **System hardware assurance** seeks confidence that electronics function "as intended and only as intended," distinguishing **weaknesses** from **vulnerabilities**, baking assurance in early, and managing a **dynamic risk profile** with mitigation-in-depth.
8. **System safety** (MIL-STD-882E) minimizes hazards to achieve acceptable risk (severity × probability) across the life cycle via eight generic activities, SWG/MSRB mechanisms, and a safety ontology tightly coupled to RAM.
9. **System security** (NIST SP 800-160 Vol. 1 Rev. 1) is freedom from conditions leading to asset loss; it spans the whole SE scope, expresses needs in terms of **loss / loss scenarios / loss tolerance**, pursues **as-secure-as-reasonably-practical (ASARP)** with **commensurate trustworthiness**, and targets the two **Loss Control Objectives** — prevent the loss, and limit its extent.
10. EMI/EMC, radiation hardening, TEMPEST, and spectrum governance (ITU, FCC/NTIA dual management, HNC/HNA) are the electromagnetic-domain quality attributes that constrain world-wide systems, often at large retrofit cost.
