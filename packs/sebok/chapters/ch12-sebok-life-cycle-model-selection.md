## Core Idea

A life cycle model is the organizing framework that guides how a system or service is developed and managed across its whole existence. SEBoK's central message in this Knowledge Area is that such models are **not rigid constructs**: they should be organization-specific, selected for the situation, adapted per project, and then continuously evaluated and improved. The KA covers two linked decisions — choosing/adapting the *life cycle model* (the staged framework) and choosing/adapting the *development approach* (how the work inside it gets done) — and closes with how the technical and management *life cycle processes* run across those stages rather than being trapped inside any single stage.

The KA explicitly aligns its concepts to **ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1, clause 6.2.6**. The four selection/adaptation topics share the same author team, and "Applying Life Cycle Processes" extends them with the through-life view of process behavior.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact SEBoK terms, when/how)

- **Life cycle model** — defined (citing ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1:2024) as a framework of processes and activities for the life cycle that may be organized into stages and acts as a common reference for communication and understanding. Decision-making reviews or gates based on entry/exit criteria are typically part of it. Used as the top-level object you select, adapt, and maintain.
- **Development approach** — the way work is carried out within a selected (and adapted) life cycle model. SEBoK names four **candidate development approaches: Sequential, Incremental, Evolutionary, and Agile**. Selected per system of interest, per system element, and per enabling system; chosen to reduce risk given what is and isn't known.
- **PMI Situation Context Framework (SCF)** — published by the Project Management Institute as a tool for selecting and tailoring a situation-dependent strategy for software-based solution delivery. SEBoK presents it as defining **seven dimensions** (team size; geographic spread; organizational spread; availability of skills; compliance; domain complexity; and solution complexity), each carrying scaling factors. Offered alongside ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1 as a good practice for choosing an approach.
- **Risk analysis** — named as the tool used to compare candidate development approaches and guide the selection.
- **Generic Life Cycle Model** — the SEBoK reference model of life cycle stages and their relationships; used as the backbone for the "Applying Life Cycle Processes" discussion of concurrency, iteration, and recursion.
- **"Hump diagram" principle** — Figure 1's depiction (modified from Lawson 2010, building on *Systems Approach Applied to Engineered Systems*) of how much activity each process carries across the generic life cycle; the peaks ("humps") mark when a process becomes a stage's main focus. SEBoK frames implementing this principle affordably and appropriately as a critical driver for all life cycle models.
- **Value propositions** of company-wide processes — an explicit list sourced from **INCOSE SEHv5, 7.1.1.1** (see Key Concepts).
- **System breakdown structure / hierarchical decomposition of a System-of-Interest** — the schema (Faisandier 2012) used to explain recursion of life cycle processes across decomposition layers.

## Key Concepts

**Selecting the Life Cycle Model.** Organizations build and keep life cycle models in order to develop systems/services that meet their strategic plans, policies, goals, and objectives. Models should *always* be organization-specific — even where an industry imposes regulatory life cycle requirements — because every organization is unique in industry, history, and culture, and models that work in one organization rarely transfer cleanly to another. Because a single organization may run many very different projects (varying in size, complexity, proportion of new technology, customer requirements, or approval regulations), it may maintain several alternative life cycle models plus instructions on which applies when and what adaptation is allowed.

*Value proposition (from INCOSE SEHv5, 7.1.1.1).* Company-wide processes: provide repeatable/predictable performance across projects (aiding planning, estimating, and demonstrating reliability to stakeholders); leverage practices proven on some projects and spread them; enable process improvement across the organization; ease transferring staff because roles are defined and performed consistently; let lessons from one project feed future projects; and improve new-project startup (less reinventing the wheel). Shared resources — tool support, document templates, a knowledge-management database — add further benefit.

*Establish and maintain.* Models are built from three input classes: (1) legislative, regulatory, and other government requirements; (2) industry SE and management standards, training, and capability maturity models; (3) academic education, research results, future concepts/perspectives, and requests for financial support. Building the "right" model is a balancing act between regulatory requirements, appropriate best practices, and corporate culture/history; involving all relevant stakeholders raises commitment and prevents the not-invented-here syndrome. Because the inputs keep changing, the organization should continually monitor them, develop **key performance indicators** to measure efficiency and effectiveness, and use those KPIs to drive continuous improvement.

**Adapting the Life Cycle Model.** After selection, the model is tailored to the project and system. Needs driving adaptation come from inside (product-line considerations, follow-on business) and outside (regulations, customer requirements/preferences). When adapting, stages can be **added, deleted, combined, or modified**, and entry/exit criteria of reviews or audits may change to ease stage transitions. **Adaptation guidelines** developed alongside the model set how far adaptation may go; exceeding those limits requires organizational approval, and *all adaptations must be documented*. Adaptation must respect overall scope — ignoring sibling projects in the same organization can forfeit the value propositions above.

- *Multi-party scope example:* organization X buys a concept from A, develops the system, outsources production to B, sells to C for operation and maintenance, then itself disposes of the system. X owns the development and retirement stages — but the *transitions* to the other stages must also be managed.
- *Adaptation for domains:* a software-dominated system may warrant a different model than a hardware-dominated one; neither domain must follow the other's life cycle. A hardware/software system can combine models, **synchronized at certain points**, with each domain's perspective considered.
- *All stages, not just development:* every stage — production, utilization, support, and retirement — needs an approach selected and adapted to the system and project context, with planning documented. The development approach can differ across system elements.

**Selecting the Development Approach.** Determined not only for the system of interest but for *each* system element and *each* enabling system; the chosen approaches will likely differ. New technologies or a new business area make previously used approaches likely obsolete. Selection among Sequential / Incremental / Evolutionary / Agile uses risk analysis, with guidance available from ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1 and PMI's SCF. SEBoK lists selection **considerations**: how well requirements are understood; system size; pace of technology change; staff constraints; budget constraints; whether the user has preferences on first-delivery capabilities; and whether the user must phase out an old system all at once. **Acquirer and supplier should agree** on the development approaches.

**Adapting the Development Approach.** Because every project is unique, the approach (not just the model) usually needs project-specific tailoring — driven by unfamiliar technologies, very different system-element domains (software-intensive vs. purely mechanical), or a different make-or-buy stance than past projects.

- *Adaptation for domains:* large, complex systems may use different, separately adapted approaches per element; their results must be **regularly aligned**. Sync intervals shouldn't be *too short* (a two-week software release won't be matched by major hardware change) nor *too long* (design decisions made without other domains create hard-to-resolve interface inconsistencies). Adaptation is typically the job of experienced systems engineers, who should involve those responsible for individual system elements to build buy-in and surface concerns.
- *Adaptation for quality characteristics:* some developments are dominated by a particular quality. A military submarine demands minimizing its signature, so elements must be well coordinated because small effects compound during integration; the approach must keep inter-department exchange smooth. Healthcare projects have authorities scrutinize both product *and* process, prioritizing predictability, stability, repeatability, and high assurance — characteristics of **sequential** development approaches.

**Applying Life Cycle Processes (Adcock).** SE technical and management activities are grouped into **life cycle processes** that define activities performed across one or more stages to supply information for life cycle decisions and to support the realization, use, and sustainment of a System-of-Interest (SoI). Activities are applied per three overlapping principles — **concurrency, iteration, and recursion** — different SE authors' related views of the same need to take a holistic systems approach while still structuring and sequencing work.

- *Terminology grounded here:* **process** (a series of actions transforming inputs into outputs — interpreted in SEBoK as technical, life cycle, business, or manufacturing-flow processes); **requirement** (something needed/wanted, not always compulsory; exists at multiple enterprise/system levels and abstractions, with interpretations that can shift over time); **architecture** (a system's organizational structure, applicable to product, enterprise, or service; *architecting* is the practice of designing structures; *frameworks* represent architectures). Other named processes — system analysis, integration, verification, validation, deployment, operation, maintenance, disposal — are detailed in the System Realization and System Deployment and Use KAs.
- *Concurrency:* process execution is **not compartmentalized** to single stages (Lawson 2010); the hump diagram shows through-life activity, with peaks marking a process's focal stage and the surrounding activity representing through-life issues and forward planning (e.g., ensuring verification resources are in place).
- *Iteration:* applies to stages *and* processes — e.g., the close coupling between exploring a problem/opportunity and defining feasible solutions makes Concept Definition and System Definition processes naturally iterative (Faisandier 2012).
- *Recursion:* the System Definition processes apply recursively across decomposition layers because "system" is itself recursive — SoI, system, and system element rest on the same concepts.

**Systems Approach to Solution Synthesis.** Synthesis mixes **top-down** (problem → solution: understand the problem/operational needs, bound the solution space, then iterate Concept Definition with System Definition and System Analysis) and **bottom-up** (evolving or adding capabilities to an existing system, often requiring **reverse engineering** to (re)characterize the SoI before modifying it). Most real problems combine the two — the **"middle-out" approach** — keeping focus on whole-system issues while still exploring the detail needed for realizable solutions.

## Mental Models

- **Model vs. approach are two distinct dials.** The life cycle *model* sets the staged frame (and its gates/criteria); the development *approach* sets how work flows inside it. You select and adapt each independently, and the approach can vary by system element and by stage.
- **Organization-specific by default.** Treat a borrowed life cycle model as a hypothesis, not a template — fit to industry, history, and culture decides whether it survives the transfer.
- **Adaptation has a fence and a logbook.** Stay inside the adaptation guidelines and you may tailor freely; cross the fence and you need organizational approval — and either way you write it down.
- **Sync cadence is a Goldilocks problem.** When domains run different approaches, align results often enough to avoid interface drift but not so often that the slower domain has nothing new to align against.
- **Quality characteristic pulls the approach.** Dominant qualities (signature, safety, high assurance) bias element coordination and can push the whole approach toward sequential, process-auditable execution.
- **Processes flow through life; stages don't own them.** Read the hump diagram as "where the focus is," not "where the activity is confined" — maintenance, verification, and integration concerns live in early stages too.
- **Recursion of "system."** Apply System Definition processes layer by layer because SoI/system/system element are the same idea at different scales.
- **Middle-out as the pragmatic default.** Pure top-down or pure bottom-up are endpoints; combine need-driven invention with constraint-driven reuse.

## Anti-patterns (only if named)

- **Not-invented-here syndrome** — failing to involve relevant stakeholders when establishing a life cycle model erodes commitment to the rules; SEBoK names broad stakeholder involvement as the counter.
- **Ignoring sibling-project scope when adapting** — adapting a model while disregarding other projects in the same organization can void the value propositions (repeatability, staff transfer, shared lessons).
- **Mis-set domain sync intervals** — aligning multi-domain development results too short wastes effort on an unchanged slower domain; too long lets domains design in isolation and produces interface inconsistencies that are difficult to resolve.

## Key Takeaways

- Life cycle models are essential, organization-specific frameworks — select, adapt, and continuously improve them against changing inputs using KPIs; never treat them as static.
- Company-wide processes earn their keep through repeatability, process improvement, staff mobility, reusable lessons, faster startup, and shared resources (per INCOSE SEHv5, 7.1.1.1).
- Adaptation can add/delete/combine/modify stages and change review entry/exit criteria — within adaptation guidelines, with approval beyond them, and always documented.
- Choose a development approach (Sequential, Incremental, Evolutionary, Agile) per system of interest, system element, and enabling system, using risk analysis plus guidance from ISO/IEC/IEEE 24748-1 and PMI's seven-dimension Situation Context Framework — and get acquirer/supplier agreement.
- Multi-domain and quality-dominated projects need coordinated, well-timed alignment; high-assurance contexts (e.g., healthcare) favor sequential approaches because process is scrutinized alongside product.
- Across the Generic Life Cycle Model, processes run with concurrency, iteration, and recursion; solution synthesis is a top-down/bottom-up "middle-out" blend that keeps the whole-system view.
