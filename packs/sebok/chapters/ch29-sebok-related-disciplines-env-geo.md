# Chapter 29: Related Disciplines — Environmental Engineering and Geospatial/Geodetic Engineering

## Core Idea
Part 6 reframes systems engineering itself as a system — one that interfaces and crosses into adjoining and related disciplines, into communities of practice, and into areas of study that together form the *systems context* in which SE exists. Because SE is presented as a fundamentally transdisciplinary endeavor whose principles apply to any system regardless of application area, the well-prepared systems engineer is expected to carry technical knowledge that reaches beyond "pure" SE and into the disciplines relevant to the system of interest. Part 6 does not attempt a full census of adjacent knowledge areas; it offers a curated selection of related disciplines most likely to matter to a multi-disciplinary audience.

This chapter covers two of those Knowledge Areas. The first, **Environmental Engineering**, addresses four issues that arise across system design and operation — designing for a given operating environment, environmental impact, green design, and regulatory compliance — and frames much of that work as requirements writing, standards conformance, and the resource-heavy environmental impact statement (EIS) process. The second, **Geospatial/Geodetic Engineering (GGE)**, surfaces how deeply systems depend on knowing *where* and *when* things are: GIS, satellite positioning, spatial reference systems, and the portrayal of geographic data are woven into so many systems and systems of systems that the GGE KA concludes geospatial and geodetic engineering should be regarded as a Specialty Engineering discipline in its own right.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The four issues of Environmental Engineering** — the KA opens by naming the four matters environmental engineering addresses in system design and operation: (1) green design; (2) environmental impact; (3) designing for a given operating environment; and (4) meeting environmental regulations.
  - When/how to use: as the scoping checklist for the environmental dimension of any system. Operating-environment design feeds requirements (temperature, humidity, UV exposure, radiation, magnetic forces, vibration); impact and compliance feed the EIS and regulatory work; green design feeds life-cycle thinking.
- **The EIS decision flow (Figure 1 of the Environmental Engineering article)** — the US process for deciding whether an environmental impact statement is required: a proposal is examined by the regulator before funding; if it falls into an excluded category, no further action follows; otherwise an environmental assessment is performed; if that assessment yields a *finding of no significant impact (FONSI)*, no further action follows; in all other cases an EIS is required.
  - When/how to use: as the gating model for project approval where environmental review applies. The KA stresses that an EIS is a resource-significant task — for a complex US project it can add years, cost millions, and become a vector for opponents to delay the project (e.g., the Honolulu light rail EIS, cited as four years and $156M).
- **The EPA principles of green engineering** — the KA reproduces the EPA's definition of green engineering (design, commercialization, and use of feasible, economical processes and products that minimize pollution at the source and risk to human health and the environment) and its associated principles: engineer holistically and use systems analysis with environmental-impact-assessment tools; preserving and improving natural ecosystems while safeguarding human health; apply life-cycle thinking throughout; keep material and energy inputs/outputs inherently safe and benign; minimize natural-resource depletion; strive to prevent waste; respect local geography, aspirations, and cultures; innovate beyond dominant technologies toward sustainability; and actively engage communities and stakeholders.
  - When/how to use: as a design heuristic set that maps environmental responsibility onto the SE habits of holistic analysis and early-phase decision-making, where the EPA notes protective decisions carry the greatest impact and cost-effectiveness when applied early in design and development.
- **The INCOSE Specialty Engineering activities as a lens on GGE** — the GGE *Relationship* article works through several Specialty Engineering disciplines listed in the INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook (2015) and shows where geospatial/geodetic subject-matter expertise contributes: *Environmental Engineering/Impact Analysis*, *Interoperability Analysis*, *Logistics Engineering*, *Reliability, Availability, and Maintainability*, *Resilience Engineering*, *System Safety Engineering*, and *Usability Analysis/Human Systems Integration* (placed within Human Factors Engineering, HFE).
  - When/how to use: as the bridge that justifies pulling GGE expertise into SE teams. Most of these disciplines are likewise treated by SEBoK articles in the *SE and Quality Attributes* KA, so the lens doubles as a cross-reference map.
- **Positioning, Navigation and Timing (PNT)** — the GGE *Overview* names the three combined services delivered by Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS): positioning, navigation, and timing. Because GNSS range measurements rely on extremely accurate one-way signal travel times, the satellites carry extremely accurate clocks and broadcast time, enabling time synchronization of other systems and frequency normals derived from the signal.
  - When/how to use: as the reminder that GNSS is not only "where" but also "when" — many systems depend on GNSS-derived timing, and the majority of national critical infrastructures now depend on GNSS, making open access to GNSS signals itself a piece of critical infrastructure.
- **The continuous fields vs. discrete objects model of geographic data** — the GGE articles repeatedly use the two fundamental concepts of geographic data: *continuous fields* (spatially distributed phenomena with no clear boundaries, representing the world as variables defined at every position — e.g., temperature; commonly *raster data* when a repeated/equidistant pattern is used) and *discrete objects or features* (delimited by boundaries and optionally carrying attribute data — termed *vector data* in a GIS context).
  - When/how to use: as the first decision a modeler makes about geographic representation; it governs data structure, storage, and the kind of analysis possible. Vector models are built from *feature catalogues* (e.g., the DGIWG Feature Data Dictionary, DFDD) that define which real-world objects and domain values are represented.
- **The geospatial Bodies of Knowledge (GISTBoK, GEOINT EBK, GI-N2K, EO4GEO)** — the *Further Insights* article maps the discipline's own BOK landscape: the US-rooted *Geographic Information Science and Technology Body of Knowledge (GISTBoK)* by UCGIS; the USGIF *GEOINT Essential Body of Knowledge* for the Geospatial Intelligence (GEOINT) discipline; and the European workstream *GI-N2K (Geographic Information – Need to Know)* and its successor *EO4GEO (Earth Observation for Geoinformation)*.
  - When/how to use: as the entry points for a systems engineer building competency in the geospatial domain. The current GISTBoK is organized into 10 Knowledge Areas (Foundational Concepts; Knowledge Economy; Computing Platforms; Programming and Development; Data Capture; Data Management; Analytics and Modeling; Cartography and Visualization; Domain Applications; and GIS&T and Society).
- **The United Nations 14 Global Fundamental Geospatial Data Themes (UN-GGIM 2019)** — a reference list of geospatial data themes: Global Geodetic Reference Frame; Addresses; Buildings and Settlements; Elevation and Depth; Functional Areas; Geographical Names; Geology and Soils; Land Cover and Land Use; Land Parcels; Physical Infrastructure; Population Distribution; Orthoimagery; Transport Networks; and Water.
  - When/how to use: as a first-pass indicator of what geographic data a system might need, recognizing that the actual set always depends on the system's purpose and goals.

## Key Concepts
- **Operating-environment requirements**: a system is designed for a particular operating environment, and the allowable range of conditions (temperature, humidity, and where relevant UV, radiation, magnetic forces, vibration, and others) must be specified in the system's requirements. The general principles for writing requirements apply, and requirements are often written to demand compliance with a set of standards.
- **The environmental impact statement (EIS) as a cost/schedule/risk driver**: many countries require an assessment of environmental impact for large projects before regulatory approval, documented in an EIS. The EIS can greatly add to a project's cost, schedule, and risk; a common tactic by opponents is to claim the EIS was incomplete because it omitted some environmental impacts.
- **Compliance, standards, and the regulated operating environment**: depending on the product, operating-condition standards may exist — e.g., *ISO 9241-6* for the office environment of a video display terminal, and for military equipment the US *MIL-STD-810G* or the UK *DEF STAN 00-35*. Organizational compliance with environmental regulation is anchored by *ISO 14001*, which sets standards for an organization to comply with environmental regulations. The article notes compliance can be a significant cost (one cited firm spends 17% of every sales dollar on compliance) but can also be turned into competitive advantage.
- **Life-cycle thinking and energy efficiency**: increased energy efficiency can significantly reduce total life cycle cost — the article's Toyota Prius example had the lowest life cycle cost over a 60,000-mile / three-year window despite a higher purchase price. *Carbon footprint* (e.g., the BSI *PAS 2050* greenhouse-gas specification) and *sustainability* (discussed against the UN *Our Common Future* report and the *Rio Declaration*) round out green design.
- **GIS is everywhere data has spatial context**: GIS technology is involved wherever event data is portrayed, processed, or analyzed in a geospatial context, regardless of interface (web, desktop, mobile). The KA cites research that ~60% of all data has a spatial reference (against the often-claimed 80%), and that almost everything that happens happens *somewhere*, so knowing where can be critically important.
- **Interoperability as a first-order geospatial concern**: for decades interoperability has been a leading problem in geospatial infrastructures. The *Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC)* (founded 1994; first standard the OpenGIS Simple Features Specification in 1997; most successful standards the *Web Map Service, WMS* and *Web Feature Service, WFS*) leads here, working closely with *ISO TC 211 "Geographic information/Geomatics"*, alongside the *International Hydrographic Organization (IHO)* and the *World Meteorological Organization (WMO)*. A NASA-funded Booz Allen Hamilton (2005) study found a project adopting geospatial interoperability standards realized a risk-adjusted ROI of 119.0% over five years (163.0% over ten) and saved 26.2% versus a proprietary-standard project — tying interoperability directly to the INCOSE *Affordability/Cost-Effectiveness/Life Cycle Cost Analysis* activity.
- **Spatial reference systems and the geoid**: every coordinate description must be referenced to a spatial reference system — local engineering, object-fixed, national, regional, or global (e.g., *WGS84*, the reference system in which GPS works). Horizontal coordinates (latitude/longitude on an ellipsoid fixed to the Earth via a geodetic datum/reference frame) are largely mathematical; the third dimension (height) is treated differently and is commonly referenced to a mean sea level (MSL) surface approximated by the *geoid*, which depends on the irregular distribution of the Earth's masses.
- **Map projections and cartography as portrayal**: reducing the Earth's 3D surface to a 2D map or screen cannot avoid distortion; mathematical geodesy and map projections (built on differential geometry) provide the basis. Projection choice emphasizes different characteristics — the classical *Mercator projection* (a straight line is a rhumb line of constant bearing, easing magnetic-compass steering) versus the *Stereographic projection* used in aeronautics (where shortest distance is a straight line). Cartography supplies the rules for *what* to display and *how* — symbols, lines, areas, color, generalization.
- **Resilient PNT and GNSS vulnerability**: awareness has grown of the vulnerability of systems depending on GPS/GNSS. The Royal Academy of Engineering (2013) recommends that critical-infrastructure and safety-critical systems requiring GNSS-derived time/timing be specified so it runs on holdover technology for as long as three days; alternatives like *eLoran* and *Satelles* are discussed. Jamming and disruptions do occur (e.g., a 2017 warning during the G20 in Hamburg), so prudent engineers ensure systems are resilient and fault-tolerant in the absence of GNSS signals.
- **Geographic data design considerations**: implementing a GIS database forces decisions on what data is needed; visualization scale and level of detail; dimensionality (2D, 2.5D, 3D, and time/4D); standards and feature catalogues; positional accuracy (tied to acquisition method and cost); semantic detail (attributes and domain values); topological relations (connectivity for routing or utility networks); update frequency and method (favoring a *single source of information* via central services); data volume (geographic data can reach terabytes and petabytes); and data sources (authoritative, e.g. USGS or Ordnance Survey; commercial; open, e.g. OpenStreetMap), with their copyright, classification, export-control, and liability constraints — the latter especially acute for disputed national borders.
- **Metadata, geocoding, and geographic identifiers**: geographic data must be discoverable via metadata — the *Dublin Core* set for general items, and the dedicated *ISO 19115* family for geographic data (identification, extent, quality, spatial/temporal aspects, content, reference, portrayal, distribution). Beyond coordinates (standardized by *ISO 6709*), location can be expressed by geographic identifiers managed in a *gazetteer* (names, addresses, country/subdivision codes per ISO 3166-1/-2) or by geocodes — proprietary (*What3words*) or open (*Geohash*, *Mapcode*), hierarchical or non-hierarchical.

## Mental Models
- **Environmental engineering is mostly requirements, standards, and a gate.** Three of its four issues resolve into familiar SE moves: specify the operating environment as requirements, conform to the right standards (ISO 14001, MIL-STD-810G/DEF STAN 00-35, product-specific standards), and pass the EIS gate. Treat the EIS as a schedule-and-risk item, not a formality — it can run years and millions and is a known target for project opponents.
- **Green design pays back over the life cycle, and earlier is cheaper.** Energy efficiency lowers total life cycle cost even at higher purchase price; the EPA's own framing is that protective decisions have the greatest impact and cost-effectiveness when applied early in design. Push environmental decisions upstream.
- **Almost everything happens somewhere — and often somewhen.** If a system observes, moves, references, or coordinates objects, it likely needs to know *where* (positioning, GIS, spatial reference systems) and frequently *when* (GNSS-derived timing). PNT bundles both; design for both.
- **Pick the projection for the job.** There is no distortion-free map. Mercator for constant-bearing navigation by compass; Stereographic for straight-line shortest paths in aeronautics. The projection is a design choice that encodes what you want to preserve.
- **Continuous field or discrete object — decide first.** The representation choice for geographic data (raster/field vs. vector/feature) is upstream of storage, analysis, and tooling. Get it right before building the database.
- **Single source of information for geographic data.** Geographic data is costly to update and can be enormous (terabytes to petabytes). Provisioning via central services so one authoritative dataset is maintained and accessed over services beats distributing and re-updating many copies — and it bakes availability and IT-security constraints into the infrastructure design.
- **GNSS is a dependency, so plan for its absence.** Because so many critical and safety-critical systems lean on GNSS for position and timing, prudent engineers treat GNSS loss as a design case: holdover timing, resilient/alternative PNT, and fault tolerance rather than termination of safe operation.
- **GGE is Specialty Engineering.** Read the geospatial/geodetic domain the way you read reliability or safety — as a specialty discipline whose subject-matter experts belong on the SE team whenever the system positions, navigates, references, or portrays anything spatial.

## Anti-patterns
The slice does not present a named, tabulated anti-pattern list for either KA (unlike, for example, the *Standards* KA's "Pitfalls" table). The closest explicitly named failure modes are:
- **The "80% of all data is geospatial" overclaim** — the GGE *Overview* explicitly corrects the often-repeated claim that 80% of all data is geospatially referenced, citing research that the figure is closer to 60%. Treat the inflated number as a known misstatement.
- **EIS-incompleteness as a delay tactic** — the Environmental Engineering article names the recurring tactic of opponents claiming an EIS omitted some environmental impacts in order to delay a project; the implied good practice is a defensibly complete EIS.
- **Treating GNSS availability as guaranteed** — the *Resilience Engineering* discussion frames unhedged dependence on GPS/GNSS as a vulnerability that prudent engineers must design against (holdover, resilient PNT, fault tolerance).

## Key Takeaways
1. Part 6 treats systems engineering as a system embedded in a *systems context* of related disciplines; the transdisciplinary systems engineer is expected to carry knowledge beyond "pure" SE into the disciplines of the system of interest.
2. Environmental engineering addresses four issues: design for a given operating environment, environmental impact, green design, and compliance with environmental regulations.
3. Operating-environment conditions (temperature, humidity, and as relevant UV, radiation, magnetic forces, vibration) must be captured in requirements, often as compliance with standards (ISO 9241-6; MIL-STD-810G; DEF STAN 00-35).
4. The environmental impact statement (EIS) is a resource-significant gate — the US flow runs proposal → exclusion check → environmental assessment → FONSI-or-EIS — and can dominate cost, schedule, and risk (e.g., Honolulu light rail: four years, $156M).
5. Green engineering (per the EPA) and ISO 14001 anchor green design and regulatory compliance; life-cycle thinking and energy efficiency can lower total life cycle cost despite higher upfront cost.
6. Geographic Information Systems and geospatial/geodetic technologies are pervasive: roughly 60% of all data has a spatial reference, and most national critical infrastructures now depend on GNSS, making open GNSS signal access itself critical infrastructure.
7. GNSS delivers combined Positioning, Navigation and Timing (PNT); its accurate clocks make GNSS a timing dependency, not just a positioning one — and a vulnerability that resilient design (holdover, eLoran/Satelles, fault tolerance) must address.
8. All spatial descriptions reference a spatial reference system (e.g., WGS84 for GPS); horizontal coordinates rest on an ellipsoid and geodetic datum, while heights are commonly referenced to MSL/the geoid, which reflects the Earth's irregular mass distribution.
9. Geographic data divides into continuous fields (raster) and discrete objects (vector/features); design choices span dimensionality, accuracy, semantic detail, topology, updates, data volume, and source/legal constraints, with metadata (ISO 19115) and geocoding (gazetteers, ISO 6709, What3words/Geohash/Mapcode) making data discoverable and addressable.
10. Geospatial/geodetic engineering maps onto numerous INCOSE Specialty Engineering activities and has its own mature Bodies of Knowledge (GISTBoK, GEOINT EBK, GI-N2K, EO4GEO); the KA concludes GGE itself should be regarded as a Specialty Engineering discipline for systems and systems of systems.
