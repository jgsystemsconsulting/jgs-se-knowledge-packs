# Chapter — The MRL Scale, Threads, and the MRL-TRL Relationship

## Core Idea

Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs) are a ten-step ordinal scale that says, at any point in a program's acquisition life cycle, how mature the manufacturing capability *should* be and where the open manufacturing risks lie. The scale runs from pre-systems-acquisition studies (MRL 1) up through the SETR (systems-engineering technical review) chain, the acquisition decision points, and the milestones, ending with mature full-rate production plus continuous improvement (MRL 10). Crucially, the deskbook insists the *number itself is only a convenience*: it is a target that focuses the team on risk, not a grade to be stamped on a part. What actually matters is whether the program element has reached the appropriate maturity and, if not, what remains to be done — a determination made against the MRL Criteria Matrix, not by picking a number.

To make that maturity assessable rather than impressionistic, the scale is cut two ways. Horizontally, maturity is demonstrated in progressively more realistic *environments* (laboratory → production-relevant → production-representative → pilot line → production line). Vertically, manufacturing risk is decomposed into nine *threads* (and their sub-threads) so each dimension of "can we actually build this" can be traced independently from MRL 1 to MRL 10. Sitting alongside MRLs is the parallel nine-level Technology Readiness Level (TRL) scale: the two are related but deliberately *not* one-to-one, and the discipline of reading them together is what the deskbook calls "moving manufacturing left."

> Caveat / scope: this chapter deepens the GAO TRA pack's treatment of the MRL 1-10 scale. Where the GAO guide introduces MRLs as a companion to TRLs at a programmatic level, this chapter draws the detail directly from the DoD MRL Deskbook — the per-level definitions, the environment lexicon, the nine threads, and the explicit caveats on how the TRL/MRL relationship should be read.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs), 10 levels** — introduced in Section 2.1 ("Overview of Manufacturing Readiness Levels") and defined level-by-level in Section 2.2 ("Manufacturing Readiness Levels Defined"). The deskbook groups them: MRLs 1-4 cover manufacturing maturity and risk from pre-systems acquisition through solution selection; MRLs 5-6 mature the needed technologies through early component/subsystem prototypes up to a preliminary design; MRL 7 measures the capability to produce in a production-representative environment ahead of the Critical Design Review (CDR); MRL 8 proves processes on the designated pilot line; MRL 9 turns on quality, throughput, and rate to enable the transition to full-rate production; MRL 10 measures lean practices and continuous improvement in production.
- **MRL Criteria Matrix (Appendix A, available at DoDMRL.org)** — named in Section 2.2 and again in Section 2.4. This is the authoritative artifact: detailed criteria for each of the 10 MRLs, broken out by thread and sub-thread across the acquisition life cycle. The matrix is what lets a user trace each thread's maturation separately and is explicitly described as tailorable to a particular technology or application.
- **Environment lexicon** — defined in Section 2.3 ("Definition of Environments and Other Terms"), developed by the MRL Working Group to give assessors a common vocabulary: Laboratory Environment, Production-Relevant Environment, Production-Representative Environment, Pilot Line, and Production Line, plus the supporting terms Manufacturability and Producibility.
- **Nine MRL threads (and sub-threads)** — introduced in Section 2.4 ("MRL Threads and Sub-Threads") as the categorization of the primary manufacturing risks.
- **Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs), 9 levels** — presented in Section 2.5 ("The MRL and TRL Relationship"), with the nine hardware TRLs tabulated as adapted from the DoD Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA) Guidance, revised May 2011.
- **Supporting policy and reference anchors cited in the text** — DoDI 5000.02 (Operation of the Adaptive Acquisition Framework), which sets the role and frequency of technical and manufacturing risk evaluation; 10 USC 2366B, the Milestone B certification requirement; Independent Technical Risk Assessments (ITRA), which feed evidence into the TRL determination; and the GAO TRA Guide (GAO-20-48G), which supplies the critical-technology definition used here.

## Key Concepts

**The number is a target, not a grade.** Section 2.2 is blunt that the MRLs are a *non-linear ordinal scale*. Non-linear means the effort, time, and resources to climb from one level to the next are not equal — moving up one MRL can cost far more than the previous step. The footnote on this point is explicit, and the deskbook reinforces it in Section 2.4: an assessment's job is to identify risk and develop Manufacturing Maturity Plans (MMPs), not to assign a number or render a go/no-go verdict.

**The two questions every assessment must answer.** Section 2.4 reduces the whole exercise to two: (1) for the program element under review, has it reached the appropriate level of manufacturing maturity, and (2) if it has not, what work remains to meet the metric. The answer comes from working the Criteria Matrix, not from labeling the element.

**Per-level shape of the scale.** The level definitions in Section 2.2 each tie a maturity state to a place in the life cycle:
- *MRL 1* — basic manufacturing implications identified; the work is studies of industrial/supply base trends, manufacturing science, material availability, supply chain, and metrology.
- *MRL 2* — manufacturing concepts identified; broad studies of material and process approaches, supply chains, workforce skillsets, and feasibility/risk begin to emerge.
- *MRL 3* — manufacturing proof of concept developed; producibility and manufacturability are evaluated through modeling, simulation, or laboratory experiments; experimental hardware exists in the lab with limited functionality.
- *MRL 4* — an exit criterion for the Materiel Solution Analysis (MSA) Phase approaching Milestone A; risks are folded into the Analysis of Alternatives (AoA), cost drivers identified and their uncertainty quantified, and initial Key Performance Parameters (KPPs) identified.
- *MRL 5* — typical of the TMRR Phase midpoint; the industrial base assessment has started, enabling/critical technologies are identified, and prototype materials are demonstrated on components in a production-relevant environment, though many processes are still in development.
- *MRL 6* — associated with the Milestone B decision to enter Engineering and Manufacturing Development (EMD); denotes acceptance of a preliminary system design, with most manufacturing processes defined and characterized and producibility trade studies complete.
- *MRL 7* — typical of detailed-design completion in EMD leading to CDR; processes demonstrated in a production-representative environment, the cost model updated and tracked against allocated targets.
- *MRL 8* — associated with Milestone C and entry into Low-Rate Initial Production (LRIP); materials, tooling, test equipment, and facilities are proven on the pilot line, with First Article Inspections completed.
- *MRL 9* — readiness for entry into Full-Rate Production (FRP); the system has achieved LRIP, major design features are stable and proven in operational test and evaluation, and learning curves have been analyzed against actual data.
- *MRL 10* — the highest level, tied to Production & Deployment or Operations & Support; FRP is demonstrated, lean practices are in place, and changes are limited to continuous improvement or obsolescence.

**Environments encode realism, not just stage.** Section 2.3 makes maturity demonstrable by escalating the *setting* in which capability is shown:
- *Laboratory Environment* — where the engineering and production disciplines develop and test the processes, procedures, and equipment for making a product; used before Milestone A to assess producibility, new equipment, processes, and initial quality/rate data.
- *Production-Relevant Environment* — has some shop-floor realism (facilities, personnel, tooling, materials) with minimum reliance on lab resources; the contractor must show it can meet EMD cost, schedule, and performance targets through prototype production, but a production line is not required.
- *Production-Representative Environment* — as much realism as the design maturity allows, using the personnel, equipment, processes, and materials that will be on the pilot line, with high-quality work instructions and tooling and no reliance on the lab.
- *Pilot Line* — incorporates all key realism elements (equipment, skill levels, facilities, materials, work instructions, tooling, even temperature, cleanliness, and lighting) needed to build production-configuration items meeting design requirements in LRIP, using FRP processes to the maximum extent practical.
- *Production Line* — has every capability needed to manufacture production-configuration items, with processes that are in control and able to hit the rates and quantities the program requires.

The deskbook stresses these definitions describe a *natural progression* and that the responsible organization and its contractors must agree on the specific production-realism content for each, tied to the actual manufacturing risk.

**Manufacturability vs. producibility.** Section 2.3 separates these deliberately. Manufacturability concerns design-cycle characteristics — process capability, machine/facility flexibility, the ability to consistently produce at the required cost and quality — with activities like designing for commonality, modularity, and robust design. Producibility is how readily an item can be produced while still satisfying its engineering, quality, and affordability requirements, with activities like material characterization, variable-reduction analysis (e.g., Taguchi, design of experiments), and closed-loop process control on critical items.

**The nine threads.** Section 2.4 categorizes the primary manufacturing risks into: Technology and Industrial Base; Design; Cost and Funding; Materials; Process Capability and Control; Quality; Manufacturing Workforce (engineering and production); Facilities; and Manufacturing Management. Many decompose into sub-threads — for example, Materials breaks into Maturity, Availability, Supply Chain Management, and Special Handling; the Manufacturing Management thread covers planning and scheduling for manufacturing, materials planning, operational-technology cybersecurity on the floor, and SIE/STE. The sub-thread structure exists so maturity can be traced continuously from one level to the next without gaps.

**MRLs and TRLs measure different things.** Section 2.5 frames TRLs as the long-standing measure of *technology* maturation and transition, whereas MRLs gauge *manufacturing* maturity and its risk relative to wherever the technology currently stands in its own development. Both are key program-risk metrics — TRLs for performance objectives, MRLs for production objectives. TRL assessments help flag new/unique/critical processes that need further manufacturing development, and ITRAs supply evidence for the TRL determination inside an MRL Assessment. The MRL criteria were intentionally built to carry an *advised* level of technology readiness so that manufacturing personnel are pushed to work closely with the technologist.

**Critical technologies (CTs).** Drawn from the GAO TRA Guide and repeated here, a CT is a technology element that is new or novel (or applied in a novel way) and that the system needs to meet its operational performance requirements inside the defined cost and schedule. Because early system developers historically pay the least attention to manufacturing, technology development up to MRL 4 is flagged as a prime concern for MRL practitioners.

## Mental Models

**The grid: levels on one axis, threads on the other.** The cleanest way to hold the scale is as a matrix, not a thermometer. The Criteria Matrix runs MRL 1-10 across the top and the nine threads (with sub-threads) down the side. A program is not "at MRL 6" the way water is at 60 degrees; it is at some maturity *per thread*, and the lowest, riskiest threads are what an assessment is hunting for. The number people quote is shorthand for the worst-supported corner of that grid.

**Climbing a staircase of realism.** Each environment is a more demanding stage on which to prove the same claim — "we can build this to requirements." The lab proves the idea; the production-relevant environment proves prototypes outside the lab; the production-representative environment dresses the rehearsal in nearly full costume; the pilot line is the dress rehearsal under LRIP conditions; the production line is opening night at rate. Skipping a step on the staircase means you never earned the confidence that the next one assumed.

**Pace manufacturing maturity by technology maturity.** The Section 2.5.1 best practice is a balance beam. Lean too far toward technological advancement and you get a high-TRL design whose manufacturability was never considered; lean too far the other way and you get a later-stage program with high MRLs sitting on an unstable design. Either imbalance raises overall programmatic risk. The healthiest path lets manufacturing maturity be *paced by* technology maturity, with manufacturing and producibility concerns pulled into technology development early — the literal meaning of "moving manufacturing left."

**Look forward without being there yet.** A recurring move in Section 2.5.1: assess against the current target while reviewing the *next* level's criteria to spot gaps. During Milestone B preparation a program is assessed against MRL 6 but should already review MRL 7 to prepare for CDR; for the faster UCA/MTA pathways, PMs should evaluate CTs against MRL 4 and analyze whether the technology can reach TRL 6 by Milestone B. The deskbook is explicit that this "look forward" does not require *being* at the higher level — it requires confirming the resources, time, and funding exist to get there.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes directly:

- **Treating the MRL Assessment as a go/no-go gate.** Section 2.4 states the goal is to identify risk and develop MMPs, *not* to make go/no-go program decisions off a target MRL number. When a target criterion is not met, decision-makers evaluate the risk to program success — the number does not decide for them.
- **Assigning a number instead of working the criteria.** Section 2.4 warns that maturity is determined through the assessment process using the Criteria Matrix — not by simply pinning a level onto whatever element is under review. Quoting a level without the criteria behind it hollows out the assessment.
- **Reading the MRL-TRL relationship as one-to-one.** Section 2.5 is explicit: there is a general relationship but no direct one-to-one requirement. Forcing a fixed mapping misrepresents both scales.
- **High TRL, ignored manufacturability (and its mirror).** Section 2.5.1 names both: programs with higher TRLs that never considered manufacturability, and later-stage programs with higher MRLs but unstable designs, both carry higher programmatic risk. Letting design instability persist risks manufacturing inferior products that fail to meet warfighter needs and can drive unstable processes, cost growth, and schedule delays.
- **Deferring manufacturing concerns to late development.** Section 2.5.1 notes early system developers are historically the least likely to consider manufacturing concerns, which is exactly why technology development up to MRL 4 needs forward-looking, design-for-manufacturability attention.

## Key Takeaways

- MRLs are a 10-level, non-linear ordinal scale describing the manufacturing maturity expected at each point in the acquisition life cycle; the number is a risk-focusing target, not a grade.
- The real assessment output is an answer to two questions — has the element met the appropriate maturity, and if not what remains — worked through the MRL Criteria Matrix, not assigned as a number.
- Maturity is demonstrated in progressively realistic environments: laboratory, production-relevant, production-representative, pilot line, and production line, with agreed production-realism content per phase.
- Manufacturing risk is decomposed into nine threads (and sub-threads) so each dimension can be traced independently from MRL 1 to MRL 10.
- MRLs (production risk) and TRLs (performance/technology maturity) are complementary but explicitly *not* one-to-one; reading them together is the practice of moving manufacturing left.
- The governing best practice is to pace manufacturing maturity by technology maturity and to "look forward" to the next level's criteria — assessing against the current target while preparing for the gaps in the next.
- An MRL Assessment identifies risk and produces Manufacturing Maturity Plans; it is not a go/no-go decision instrument.
