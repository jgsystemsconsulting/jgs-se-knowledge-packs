# Chapter — Applying MRLs in Contract Language

## Core Idea

An MRL assessment that lives only in a program office briefing has no teeth. The leverage comes from writing manufacturing and quality maturity requirements *into the contract* — so that an offeror's manufacturing risk posture shapes who wins the award, and the winner is then bound to keep maturing the right things. This chapter treats manufacturing- and quality-management industry standards as contractual instruments. Embedding them in development plans, acquisition strategies, and the contract itself is presented as a best practice for both government acquirers and commercial enterprises, with the consistent qualifier that every standard must be tailored to the specific program rather than dropped in wholesale.

A second, equally important idea: the MRL Criteria Matrix is descriptive, not directive. The criteria describe what manufacturing maturity *looks like* at each level; they do not, by themselves, order a contractor to perform any task. The tasking has to come from somewhere else — chiefly the SAE AS6500A manufacturing-management standard and the language the government writes into the RFP and Statement of Work (SOW). The chapter's whole purpose is to bridge that gap: how to convert MRL expectations into enforceable obligations.

This deepens the GAO TRA Guide's treatment of MRL 1-10. Where the GAO material defines the maturity scale and how to read it, this chapter is about the contractual machinery — Section L, Section M, the SOW, DIDs, and CDRLs — that makes the scale *operative* in a real acquisition.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **SAE AS6500A, Manufacturing Management Program** — introduced as the central tasking document. Its Section 4.3 requires manufacturing risks to be identified, mitigated, tracked to closure, and folded into program risk management across the full life cycle. It also drives manufacturing feasibility assessments, MRL Assessments, and Production Readiness Reviews (PRRs). Used in the SOW as a conformance requirement, often with a cross-reference matrix demonstrating how the contractor's policies and procedures map to the standard.

- **MRL Criteria Matrix** — the collection of criteria against which manufacturing maturity is graded. Framed here as *non-directive*: it states expectations (e.g., MRL 6, Sub-thread B.2, Design Maturity expects preliminary Key Characteristics to be identified before PDR) but does not itself compel action. Used as the assessment yardstick referenced throughout RFP and SOW language.

- **DoD Manufacturing Readiness Level Deskbook (DoDMRL.org)** — the cited authority for MRL definitions, criteria, and process. RFP and SOW examples point offerors and contractors to the latest Deskbook version as the guide for conducting assessments and selecting which suppliers to review (via the Deskbook's Section 4.3).

- **RFP Section L (Instructions, Conditions, and Notices to Offerors)** — specifies *what* the offeror must submit and in *what format* to substantiate use of assessments or best practices, reducing later misunderstandings about manufacturing/quality risk.

- **RFP Section M (Evaluation Factors for Award)** — specifies *how* the Section L submission will be scored. Together, L and M ensure manufacturing readiness is actually exercised during source selection.

- **AS9100D, Quality Management Systems** — the IAQG quality standard for aviation, space, and defense organizations, used to satisfy the Quality thread. Cited as applicable across all acquisition and life-cycle phases.

- **IAQG (International Aerospace Quality Group) standard family** — owns 9100 (organizations), 9110 (maintenance organizations), and 9120 (distributors), plus guidance standards 9101 (audit), 9102 (first article inspection), and 9103 (variation management of Key Characteristics).

- **ISO 9001 and IATF 16949** — named as additional quality-management standards beyond the AS9100 family.

- **MIL-HDBK-896A, Manufacturing Management Program Guide** — cited as the reference for contractually implementing AS6500A; usable by industry for their own suppliers as well.

- **Data Item Descriptions (DIDs) and CDRLs** — named instruments: **DI-MGMT-81889B** (Manufacturing Plan), **DI-SESS-81974** (Assessment of Manufacturing Risk and Readiness), and **DI-QCIC-81794A** (Quality Assurance Program Plan). Tailored onto a contract via the **DD Form 1423** Contract Data Requirements List.

- **DFARS Subpart 215.304** — requires that offeror manufacturing readiness be weighed in source selection for ACAT I programs. **FAR Part 46 / Subpart 46.2** (contract quality requirements) and **DFARS Subpart 246.4** (government contract quality assurance) are cited as the floor that any contract quality language must satisfy.

- **NDAA FY2017 (P.L. 114-328, §2448b)** — cited as the statutory basis requiring technical and manufacturing risk assessment for Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAPs).

## Key Concepts

**Tailoring over prescription.** The chapter opens and closes on the same warning: these are best-practice patterns, not mandates, and they do not override DoD policy, statute, the FAR, or DFARS. Every example clause is meant to be adapted to program needs.

**Maturity as a source-selection discriminator.** Strategy for any RFP or solicitation should call for MRL-based assessment of manufacturing risk, maturity, and quality. That input can be used to distinguish between offerors and, at minimum, should shape the contract's requirements. Offeror responses should report manufacturing maturity, recognized risks, and quality level for the proposed effort — ideally from a self-assessment or independent assessment against the MRL criteria.

**Phase-anchored expectations.** Acquisition planning sets manufacturing/quality requirements to the relevant milestone (pre-Milestone A, A, B, C, FRP, or O&S). Assessments are run before major milestone and technical reviews (PDR, CDR, PRR) and their results presented there so decision-makers have factual maturity knowledge. The chapter gives phase-specific patterns: a manufacturing **feasibility assessment** of each competing design alternative pre-Milestone A; a **gap analysis** against MRL criteria post-Milestone A (TMRR) to find what must be demonstrated by Milestone B; and **PRRs** feeding a Milestone-C production decision.

**The SEP carries entry and exit MRL targets.** The Systems Engineering Plan should state both entry and exit MRL levels appropriate to the development phase — a concrete handoff point between planning artifacts and the maturity scale.

**Flow-down.** Requirements do not stop at the prime. Industry contracts to the supply chain should carry MRL Assessment flow-down clauses, and the contractor must state its approach for flowing requirements down to key and critical suppliers.

**Section L / Section M as a matched pair.** L defines the deliverable content and format; M defines the scoring rubric for that same content. Worked examples are given for each of five topics — Manufacturing Management System (against AS6500/AS6500A), MRL Demonstration (with target MRLs and MMPs), Manufacturing Plan (assembly sequence, process flow, build schedule, tooling, suppliers, manpower/facilities/equipment), Quality Management System (with QMS certifications like ISO 9000 or AS9100), and Supplier Management.

**SOW obligations are the binding layer.** Where L/M govern selection, the SOW binds performance. Example "shall" statements cover conducting MRL Assessments and MMP-driven maturation, planning on-site supplier assessments, specifying assessment locations and frequencies in the Integrated Master Schedule, integrating risk mitigation into the Program Risk Management System, and reporting MMP status at all program and technical reviews. A typical division of labor: the government leads assessments at the prime's facilities; the prime leads them at suppliers with government participation.

**Minimize deliverable DIDs.** Deliverables should be kept to what is genuinely needed. Where possible, MMPs should live inside the program's normal risk-management process (ideally a Risk Management Plan) rather than as a separate CDRL item, and a Manufacturing Plan may already be satisfied through the contractor's command media.

**AS6500A and the MRL criteria are complementary, not equivalent.** AS6500A tasks many of the same activities the MRL threads assess, so effective AS6500A implementation makes it likely the target MRL is achieved — but neither AS6500A nor AS9100D satisfies *every* MRL criterion. ## Mental Models

**The MRL criteria are a thermometer; AS6500A is the thermostat.** The criteria *measure* maturity but do not *cause* anything to happen. AS6500A (plus SOW tasking) is what actually drives the heat. Using the Key Characteristics example: the MRL 6 criterion *expects* preliminary KCs before PDR, but AS6500A *requires* KCs to be identified in the Technical Data Package — so satisfying the standard tends to satisfy the criterion.

**L answers "what do you have to show me?"; M answers "how do I grade it?"** Holding these two in sync is the single most reliable way to prevent disputes about what manufacturing maturity the government was actually buying.

**Selection-time leverage vs. performance-time leverage.** Sections L and M act once, at award, to pick the offeror with the better manufacturing posture. The SOW, DIDs, and CDRLs act continuously, after award, to keep that posture maturing. A program needs both halves; one without the other leaks risk.

**Standards as a conformance ledger, not a checkbox.** Conformance to AS6500A is demonstrated through a cross-reference matrix tying each requirement to documentation, artifacts, objective evidence, and rationale — maturity claims must be backed by substantiating artifacts, not assertions. The same evidence discipline shows up in the MRL Assessment clauses, which require objective evidence supporting every target criterion assessed.

## Anti-patterns

The source does not name explicit anti-patterns. It does, however, flag two recurring cautions worth carrying forward as guardrails: (1) treating any of these example clauses as prescriptive or as superseding the FAR/DFARS/policy — the text repeatedly warns against this; and (2) over-generating deliverable DIDs when the same evidence already exists in the contractor's normal risk-management process or command media.

## Key Takeaways

- The MRL Criteria Matrix grades maturity but does not direct work; tasking must come from AS6500A and from L/M/SOW contract language.
- Use MRL-based assessment as a discriminator in source selection, and at minimum let it shape contract requirements.
- Anchor required entry and exit MRL levels in the SEP and to the relevant milestone; run assessments ahead of PDR/CDR/PRR and present results there.
- Section L defines submission content and format; Section M defines the matching evaluation rubric — keep them paired across all five topic areas.
- Bind performance through SOW "shall" statements: conduct MRL Assessments, develop and track MMPs, schedule assessments in the IMS, integrate risk into the program risk system, and flow requirements down to key and critical suppliers.
- Reference AS6500A (with MIL-HDBK-896A for implementation) and AS9100D/ISO 9001/IATF 16949 for quality; demonstrate conformance via a cross-reference matrix backed by objective evidence.
- Keep deliverable DIDs minimal; tailor DI-MGMT-81889B, DI-SESS-81974, and DI-QCIC-81794A onto the contract through the DD Form 1423 CDRL, and prefer housing MMPs in the program RMP.
- Statute and regulation set the floor: DFARS 215.304 (ACAT I source selection), FAR Part 46 / DFARS 246.4 (quality), and NDAA FY2017 §2448b (MDAP risk assessment).

## Connects To

- **gao-tra (MRL 1-10):** This chapter is the contractual operationalization of the maturity scale that the GAO TRA material defines. GAO tells you what an MRL *means*; this chapter tells you how to *buy* and *enforce* one.
- **Risk management:** MRL Assessments feed the Program Risk Management System and Integrated Master Schedule; MMPs are the maturation vehicle and ideally live inside the program's RMP.
- **Technical reviews (PDR, CDR, PRR):** assessment timing and result presentation are tied directly to these gates, linking manufacturing maturity to the broader systems-engineering review cadence.
- **Quality management:** the AS9100/IAQG family, ISO 9001, and IATF 16949 connect this material to the quality-engineering domain and to the Quality thread of the MRL criteria.
- **Acquisition policy:** FAR Part 46, DFARS Subparts 215.304 and 246.4, and NDAA FY2017 §2448b tie the practice into the formal DoD acquisition and statutory framework.
