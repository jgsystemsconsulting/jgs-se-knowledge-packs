# Chapter — Tailoring MRL Criteria and the MRL Users Guide

## Core Idea

The MRL criteria are a fixed measurement scale, but the situations they are applied to are not. A basic-research lab, a one-of-a-kind satellite, a class of ships, a depot absorbing support for an orphaned product, and a commercial firm running its own gated product development all carry manufacturing risk — yet each demands a different reading of the same criteria. This chapter pairs two things the Deskbook treats as inseparable: the practical instrument for running an assessment (the spreadsheet-based MRL Users Guide) and the discipline of *tailoring* — adapting which criteria apply, and how strictly, to the specific technology, product, or program in front of you.

The governing principle is that tailoring is allowed, even expected, but it is never a free pass to ignore risk. Every criterion in the matrix represents its own distinct risk area. If a criterion is dropped without thorough assessment, the risk it covered does not vanish — it becomes a "known-unknown" that can stay buried until a later, more expensive phase. So adaptation must always preserve traceability back to the underlying MRL criteria, and any criterion judged not feasible to assess must be documented, justified, and handed to the receiving party for transparency. Trained subject-matter experts (SMEs) are what make this judgment trustworthy: they decide what a criterion really requires in a given context and when a near-equivalent criterion can stand in for one that cannot be assessed.

> Caveat honored: This chapter deepens the gao-tra pack's treatment of MRL 1–10. Where that pack presents the ten levels as a clean linear ladder, this chapter shows what happens when the ladder meets messy reality — how the *same* MRL number means different things, and demands different evidence, in science-and-technology (S&T), sustainment, single-system, ship, and industry contexts.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **MRL Users Guide** — a Microsoft Excel–based companion to the Deskbook, built by the MRL Working Group, that carries most of the information needed to run an assessment at any point in the acquisition or product-development life cycle. Introduced as the operational tool that turns the criteria into a usable instrument.
- **The six Users Guide worksheets** — named tabs that structure the tool: *Introduction* (operating instructions), *Users Guide* (the interactive digital matrix with click-to-open pop-ups), *Definitions* (acquisition and assessment terms), *Acronyms* (manufacturing/acquisition acronyms), *MRL Printable Matrix* (the whole matrix on one printable sheet), and *Questionnaire* (the full question set, tailorable per assessment).
- **The pop-up dialog box** — the help mechanism behind each cell of the digital matrix. Selecting a cell shows the sub-thread (e.g., A1, B2) and maturity level (MRL 1–10), the matrix criterion for reference, and six help fields: Purpose, Sources of Information, Examples of Objective Evidence, Questions, Additional Considerations, and Lessons Learned.
- **The MRL Questionnaire** — the question bank derived from the criteria matrix; meant to be copied, sorted, and trimmed to the item under assessment, focused on the target MRL or one level below.
- **The S&T phases as MRL anchors** — Basic Research (MRL 1–3), Applied Research (MRL 4), and Advanced Technology Development / ATD (MRL 5–6), each named with the band of MRL criteria that typically captures the manufacturing knowledge expected there.
- **Sustainment Maturity Levels (SML)** — drawn from the DoD Product Support Manager (PSM) Guidebook; a sustainment-side maturity scale that correlates directly to MRL criteria (depicted in the source's Figure 8-1) and maps to commercial maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO).
- **Depot Activation** — the process triggered when a military depot must take on unplanned product support; it pulls in major elements of both the SML and MRL processes.
- **Single or limited system acquisition** — a defined acquisition class in which the first unit built is also the unit that goes operational (for example, a large-scale radar, a ship class, or a small family of satellites), handled by remapping MRLs to decision points.
- **The Navy "2-pass, 6-gate" process** — the Department of the Navy's governance overlay for ship acquisition, with three requirement gates and three acquisition gates, against which ship MRL expectations are pinned.
- **Gated Product Development** — the industry analogue (the source's Figure 8-5), where companies substitute business or engineering gates for DoD milestones and embed the MRL criteria into their own new-product-introduction process.

## Key Concepts

- **The Users Guide is the criteria made operable.** The matrix alone is a reference; the Users Guide adds instructions, definitions, acronyms, a printable view, and — most usefully — an interactive matrix where every cell opens contextual help. It is the difference between a standard and a working tool.
- **Six fields per cell, not just a criterion.** Each pop-up gives the assessor far more than the bare criterion: *why* this sub-thread is assessed here, *what data* to collect, *what objective evidence* looks like, *which questions* to ask, *additional considerations* from prior programs and the Services, and *lessons learned* from past assessments. Lessons-learned content is explicitly expected to grow as practitioners accumulate experience.
- **The questionnaire is meant to be cut down, not used whole.** The default move is to copy the question set and tailor it to the specific system, subsystem, or component, limiting it to the target MRL or one level lower. Tailoring starts at the question level before it ever reaches the criteria level.
- **Most applications need only minor adaptation.** Across two decades of use, assessments using the MRL criteria have fit the great majority of programs with small adjustments. The friction points are terminology — phrases like "production-relevant," "production-representative," "pilot line," and "rate tooling" carry different meanings for ship, space, S&T, ground-vehicle, aircraft, or electronics programs — which is why the Deskbook supplies notional definitions to pin intent.
- **Tailoring out a criterion creates a documented known-unknown.** Because each criterion is its own risk area, eliminating one without assessment buries that risk. The rule is not "skip what's hard" but "assess to what's appropriate for the phase," and when a criterion genuinely cannot be assessed, write down the reason and give it to the transition customer.
- **The S&T tension: funding stops short of the criteria.** Many MRL 5–6 criteria use acquisition language — cost models, budget estimates for Milestone B or C, target yields, make/buy evaluations for pilot line / LRIP / FRP — that an S&T effort funded only through ATD has no basis to assess. References to a follow-on program the S&T effort does not fund are generally not feasible to consider, and that must be documented and passed along.
- **But S&T should still harvest the feasible criteria.** Criteria covering quality, design, materials, facilities, and workforce are both valuable for de-risking transition and more assessable in S&T. The right move is to adapt — capture the achievable risk reduction rather than burn resources chasing maturation that isn't feasible.
- **Modeling and simulation is scaled to need.** Sub-thread E.1 (Modeling & Simulation, product and process) should be sized to the application: sometimes extensive M&S is required, sometimes a simple spreadsheet calculation satisfies the criterion.
- **"Production-relevant environment" is a stakeholder agreement, not a fixed place.** Before an MRL 6 assessment, stakeholders and trained SMEs should agree what counts as production-relevant for that application, guided by the Deskbook's Section 2.4 definition. A lab can qualify if it carries enough production-line realism to demonstrate readiness or expose process risk.
- **Sustainment maturity rides on manufacturing maturity.** SMLs correlate to MRL criteria, and existing depot processes deserve the same rigor of manufacturing-maturity evaluation as new ones. Understanding the manufacturing maturity behind a sustainment activity is how you size the risk to depot or program objectives — especially for Depot Activation.
- **Single-system acquisition flips the milestone logic.** When the first article built is the first operational unit, manufacturing maturity is front-loaded. For single/limited systems (except ships), system-level maturity must reach MRL 8 at the CDR decision point, with subsystems/components at MRL 8 or 9, because First Build follows shortly after CDR and may be the only production build. An MRL 7 assessment can serve as a mid-development waypoint between PDR and CDR.
- **Space systems demand the strictest reading.** Where on-orbit hardware can't be repaired or replaced, initial (EMD) units must meet full mission requirements, which forces complete as-built documentation and traceability of every flight unit to support later anomaly analysis. Quality and reliability dominate space-vehicle assessments.
- **Definitions tighten under single-system risk.** Some criteria require a more stringent reading for single/limited builds — e.g., in sub-thread D.1 (Materials Maturity), "sufficient" at MRL 7 means fully characterized, and "adequate" at MRL 8 means fully proven and validated, because LRIP here is the actual production EMD system.
- **Ships are a system-of-systems with their own gate map.** Major ship subsystems should be fully characterized or in production (MRL 8/9) before ship CDR; overall ship design targets MRL 7 by Milestone B (typically 3–6 months after CDR). The Navy's 2-pass, 6-gate process anchors these: at SD1 Final Design Review (≈PDR) system maturity should be MRL 6; post-Gate 4/5 with SD2 (≈CDR) at Milestone B it should be MRL 7; the winning shipyard drives toward MRL 8 for PRR before the lead-ship keel-laying decision. Any subsystem that cannot be replaced or retrofitted must be at MRL 8.
- **Industry can own and improve the criteria.** Companies adapt the DoD criteria by swapping in business vernacular (business/engineering gates for milestones), embedding the full criteria and process into a single spreadsheet or dashboard, and standardizing report formats to build management buy-in. Ownership lets a firm resolve interpretation disputes quickly and fold its own lessons learned back into the criteria (e.g., adding an ESH thread).

## Mental Models

- **Same number, different evidence.** An MRL is not self-defining across domains. "MRL 6" for an electronics program and "MRL 6" for a satellite ask for the same maturity *position* but very different proof, because the meaning of terms like production-relevant shifts with the product. Tailoring is mostly the work of agreeing what the number requires *here*.
- **Tailoring is subtraction with a receipt.** You may remove a criterion you can't assess — but only by writing down why and handing that justification to whoever inherits the risk. A dropped criterion without documentation is a hidden liability; a dropped criterion with documentation is a managed, transparent decision.
- **The substitute-criterion move.** When one criterion can't be assessed (often because it reaches into a follow-on program the effort doesn't fund), look within the same thread or sub-thread for a sibling criterion that *can* be assessed. The thread as a whole is never simply ignored even when individual criteria are infeasible.
- **Front-load maturity when there's no second chance.** The fewer units you build — one radar, one satellite, a lead ship — the earlier and higher the manufacturing maturity must be, because there is no later production run to absorb the learning. Single-system acquisition pulls MRL 8 forward to CDR precisely because First Build is effectively production.
- **Strictness scales with irreversibility.** The less repairable or replaceable a unit (a flight article, a non-retrofittable ship subsystem), the more stringently the criteria and their key terms must be read. Risk tolerance tightens as the cost of being wrong rises.
- **Assess to the phase, not to perfection.** Especially in S&T, the goal is not to complete every criterion but to surface risk as early as possible so it can be acted on. An assessment that finds and flags real risk is succeeding even when many criteria are deferred.
- **Sustainment is a day-to-day activity, not an afterthought.** The PSM Guidebook's early-planning posture mirrors early manufacturing planning. Treating SML/MRL alignment as routine — rather than a crisis response to a lost supplier — is what makes Depot Activation manageable.

## Anti-patterns (only the source names them)

- **Being quick to tailor out criteria that merely *look* out-of-scope, infeasible, or too hard.** The source explicitly warns against this reflex: rather than dropping such criteria, assess to what is appropriate for the phase and the unique reality of the effort. Eliminating a criterion without thorough assessment converts it into a buried known-unknown risk that can surface in a later phase or after transition to an acquisition program.
- **Ignoring an entire thread or sub-thread because some of its criteria aren't feasible.** Most threads carry multiple criteria; the infeasibility of one does not license abandoning the whole thread. The feasible criteria within it still must be assessed.
- **Dropping criteria silently.** Criteria judged not feasible — and their justifications — must be documented and provided to the transition customer for transparency. Quietly omitting them defeats the point.
- **Spending scarce S&T resources on maturation that isn't feasible.** The source frames this as the opposite failure mode: PMs should adapt the criteria to capture achievable risk reduction instead of pouring resources into manufacturing-maturation efforts the effort cannot realistically accomplish.

## Key Takeaways

- The MRL Users Guide is the Excel-based working instrument — six worksheets, an interactive matrix with six-field pop-ups (Purpose, Sources of Information, Examples of Objective Evidence, Questions, Additional Considerations, Lessons Learned), and a tailorable questionnaire focused on the target MRL or one level below.
- Tailoring is legitimate and expected, but bounded by three rules: keep traceability to the criteria, document and justify any criterion you can't assess, and lean on trained SMEs to judge intent and find substitute criteria.
- Each criterion is its own risk area; tailoring one out without assessment creates a known-unknown that can stay hidden until a costlier phase. Assess to the phase rather than skipping what looks hard.
- S&T mapping: Basic Research ≈ MRL 1–3, Applied Research ≈ MRL 4, ATD ≈ MRL 5–6 — with acquisition-language criteria (budgets, Milestone B/C estimates, pilot-line/LRIP/FRP yields) often infeasible for efforts funded only through ATD, while quality/design/materials/facilities/workforce criteria remain both valuable and assessable.
- Sustainment ties in through Sustainment Maturity Levels (from the PSM Guidebook), which correlate to MRL criteria; Depot Activation pulls in SML and MRL processes, and unplanned depot capability targets MRL 5/6/7 depending on how much engineering data exists, with MRL 8 (and SML 8) requiring all data and processes in place.
- Single/limited systems front-load maturity: system-level MRL 8 at CDR (subsystems/components MRL 8–9), with stricter readings of key terms; space systems add full as-built traceability and a quality/reliability emphasis.
- Ships, as a system-of-systems, follow the Navy 2-pass, 6-gate map — MRL 6 at SD1/PDR, MRL 7 by Milestone B/SD2/CDR, MRL 8 for PRR before keel-laying — with non-retrofittable subsystems required at MRL 8.
- Industry adapts the same criteria via business vernacular, dashboard embedding, and standardized reporting; ownership lets a company resolve interpretations fast and fold in its own lessons (e.g., an ESH thread), much as adopting ISO 9000/9001 and AS6500 improves operations.
