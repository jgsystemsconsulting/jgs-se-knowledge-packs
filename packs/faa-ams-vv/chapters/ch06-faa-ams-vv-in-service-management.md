# Chapter — V&V in In-Service Management

## Core Idea
Verification and validation do not stop once a system enters service. Under the FAA Acquisition Management System (AMS), In-Service Management (ISM) is the lifecycle phase where fielded assets — systems, products, services, and facilities — are run, maintained, kept secure, and sustained continuously so they keep delivering the National Airspace System (NAS) services that air traffic control and other FAA missions depend on. During this phase the central V&V question shifts from "did we build the right thing" to "does the fielded thing still do its job, and does every change to it remain valid." Any modification must be verified against new or revised requirements, and any new or revised requirement or operational concept must itself be validated. The guiding obligation is that the product keeps fulfilling its intended purpose for as long as it stays in service, and every major work product tied to a modification is subject to V&V.

## Frameworks Introduced
The guideline structures ISM V&V around **Table 6, "V&V in In-Service Management"**, which is the organizing artifact for this phase. For each major decision point, Table 6 enumerates four things: the minimum set of items requiring V&V (spanning work products, product components, and end products); the criteria each item is checked against; and the stakeholder accountable for the activity. Two decision points anchor the table:

- **Decision to Implement an Improvement or Change** — the gate at which a change to a fielded baseline is authorized. Use it to scope what V&V is required before committing to the change.
- **Decision to Deploy an Improvement or Change** — the gate at which the authorized change is released into operational service. Use it to confirm the change has been verified and validated before it reaches users.

Within those gates, Table 6 names the concrete criteria the source treats as required evidence, including: updates to **Enterprise Architecture** products and their amendments; the **NAS Segment Implementation Plan**; **NAS Requirements Documents**; **Operational Analysis Reports**; the **NAS Integrated Systems Engineering Framework**; **Post-Implementation Reviews**; **Operational Analyses**; the **Final Program Requirements Document** (carrying its Critical Operational Issues and Critical Performance Requirements); **NAS Change Proposals (NCPs)**; the **Functional Description Document**; and **System Support Directives**. Governing policy and form references appear as verification criteria in their own right — **FAA Form 1800-2** (the Case File / NCP form), **FAA Order 1800.66** (Configuration Management policy), and **FAA Order 1320.58A** (which governs how notices, maintenance technical handbooks, and system support directives are to be written). The table also points to the **Implementation Strategy and Planning Document** and its template, plus underlying **AMS Policy**, as criteria for validating and verifying updates to implementation strategy and planning. The guideline is explicit that Table 6 reflects AMS policy as of publication and that the current detailed templates and ISM lifecycle guidance should be consulted for authoritative direction.

## Key Concepts
- **In-Service Management (ISM):** the phase covering operation, maintenance, security, and sustainment of deployed assets in real time, plus periodic monitoring and evaluation. ISM feeds performance data back into ongoing Mission Analysis (MA) and Investment Analysis (IA) so the need to keep sustaining an asset can be revalidated or a different action taken.
- **Sustainment activity set:** ISM explicitly spans configuration management, preventive and corrective maintenance, training, infrastructure and logistics support, emergency sustainment actions, and the removal or disposal of obsolete assets. It also covers sustaining flight inspection, aircraft certification, and regulatory requirements.
- **Verification vs. validation in service:** verification confirms a modified work product conforms to its specification and governing policy (e.g., a System Specification, a configuration-management form, an order on how the document must be written). Validation confirms the requirement, concept, or implemented change is the right one and still serves the operational mission (e.g., Operational Analyses, Post-Implementation Reviews, operational procedures).
- **Decision-point–driven evidence:** V&V obligations are not generic; they attach to the implement/deploy decisions and produce a defined minimum set of V&V'd items at each.
- **Major V&V activities in ISM:** the source flags ISM developmental test (DT) and operational test (OT) activities, including Independent Operational Assessment (IOA) where applicable, as the major V&V activities for implemented baseline changes.
- **Stakeholder accountability:** responsibility recurs through the Service Team — including functional organizations such as systems engineering — with the Enterprise Architecture Control Board (for NAS and Mission Support) and ANG Engineering Services accountable for validating Enterprise Architecture updates.

## Mental Models
- **Treat in-service V&V as "prove the change, re-prove the purpose."** Every modification carries a double burden: verify the change against its new or modified requirements, and validate that the requirement or operational concept driving the change is itself sound. Neither half stands alone.
- **Read Table 6 as a four-column contract.** Decision point → required work products → V&V criteria → responsible stakeholder. If you can answer all four for a given change, you have a defensible ISM V&V case; if any column is blank, you have a gap.
- **Distinguish "conforms to policy" from "serves the mission."** A change can pass verification (it matches the System Specification and was documented per FAA Order 1320.58A) yet still fail validation if a Post-Implementation Review or Operational Analysis shows it no longer serves the intended operational need. Both passes are mandatory.
- **Use feedback as the trigger for revalidation, not just monitoring.** Performance data gathered in service is not collected for its own sake; it flows back to MA and IA to decide whether continued sustainment is justified or another course of action is warranted.

## Key Takeaways
1. **V&V is a lifecycle obligation, not a pre-deployment milestone.** ISM keeps the product under V&V so it continues to fulfill its intended purpose throughout its service life.
2. **Two decision gates frame ISM V&V** — the decision to implement a change and the decision to deploy it — and each carries a defined minimum set of work products to verify and validate.
3. **All major modification work products are in scope.** During ISM, every major work product associated with a product modification is subject to V&V, not just the end item.
4. **Verification leans on specifications, forms, and orders; validation leans on operational evidence.** System and lower-level specifications, FAA Form 1800-2, FAA Order 1800.66, and FAA Order 1320.58A serve as verification criteria; Post-Implementation Reviews, Operational Analyses, and operational procedures serve as validation criteria.
5. **Enterprise Architecture changes have their own control authority.** Validating updates to Enterprise Architecture products and amendments is accountable to the Enterprise Architecture Control Board (NAS and Mission Support) and ANG Engineering Services, not the Service Team alone.
6. **DT, OT, and IOA are the heavy V&V lifting in ISM.** Implemented baseline changes are exercised through ISM developmental and operational test activities, with independent operational assessment where applicable.
7. **Table 6 is policy-as-of-publication.** The guideline directs practitioners to current detailed templates and ISM AMS lifecycle guidance for authoritative, up-to-date direction.

## Connects To
- **FAA AMS lifecycle phases (this pack):** ISM is the operational/sustainment counterpart to the earlier acquisition phases; the same verification-versus-validation distinction carries forward, now applied to changes against an established baseline rather than initial development.
- **Configuration management:** FAA Order 1800.66 and the NCP/Case File mechanism (FAA Form 1800-2) tie ISM V&V directly to formal change control of the fielded baseline.
- **Mission Analysis and Investment Analysis:** in-service performance data feeds MA and IA so the need to sustain deployed assets can be revalidated or redirected.
- **NASA SE Handbook Phase E (Operations and Sustainment):** both frameworks treat post-deployment as architecture-constrained evolution where systems engineering stays engaged to validate changes and resolve issues within the existing system; changes exceeding that scope reopen earlier lifecycle work.
- **ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 "Operation" and "Maintenance" processes:** ISM corresponds to the operation-and-support stages of the system lifecycle, where deployed systems are run, maintained, and assessed for continued fitness for purpose.
