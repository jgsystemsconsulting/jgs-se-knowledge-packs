# FAA System Safety Handbook — Glossary

Key terms from the FAA System Safety Handbook (Dec 30, 2000), alphabetical, with chapter
references. Definitions are synthesized in original wording, not reproduced from the source.

**Acquisition Management System (AMS)** — the FAA's agency-wide acquisition policy; its
system-safety section makes a safety program mandatory per Order 8040.4 and reports status at
each Joint Resources Council. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Allocation of Safety Objectives and Requirements (ASOR)** — process that turns a hazard's
severity into a paired *objective* (desired probability) and *requirement* (minimum acceptable
probability) against a Target Level of Safety, then writes them as testable "shall" statements.
(Ch 2)

**Comparative Safety Assessment (CSA)** — a genuine risk assessment (severity and likelihood)
that ranks competing alternatives, including the no-action baseline, for management decision; a
decision tool and a planning tool. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Common Cause Failure Analysis (CCFA)** — an extension of fault-tree analysis that examines the
primary events of minimal cut sets for shared coupling factors, testing whether redundancy is
genuine. (Ch 4)

**Contributory hazard** — an unsafe act or unsafe condition that, combined with others along a
sequence, leads from an initiating hazard to a primary (catastrophic/critical) event. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Cut set / minimal cut set** — a combination of basic events whose joint occurrence fails the
system; the minimal form is reduced to remove redundant fault paths. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**DAMP (Facility Damage and Personnel Injury) analysis** — launch-safety input that estimates
casualty expectation from debris and overpressure; feeds the Maximum Probable Loss analysis. (Ch 6)

**Energy Trace** — a hazard analysis built around controlled and uncontrolled energy sources; it
complements rather than replaces other methods and seeds fault-tree top events. (Ch 4)

**Expected casualty** — the space industry's public-risk measure: average human casualties
(fatality or serious injury) expected per launch mission. (Ch 6)

**Failure Modes and Effects (and Criticality) Analysis (FMEA / FMECA)** — bottom-up reliability
analyses; FMECA adds a purpose-specific criticality figure of merit. Considers only hardware/
software failures, so it is not a complete hazard analysis. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Fault Hazard Analysis** — a safety-focused, depth-limited member of the FMEA/FMECA family that
tracks only the safety-relevant effects of failures. (Ch 4)

**Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** — a deductive, top-down logic model that starts from one defined
undesired event and branches down through AND/OR/INHIBIT gates to cut sets. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Flight Operations Quality Assurance (FOQA)** — a voluntary, non-punitive program collecting and
analyzing routine flight-recorder data to improve safety. (Ch 7)

**Global Aviation Information Network (GAIN)** — an industry-owned international infrastructure for
voluntary collection, analysis, and sharing of safety information. (Ch 7)

**Hazard** — per Order 8040.4, any condition, event, or set of circumstances with the potential to
cause, or contribute toward, an unplanned or undesired outcome; distinct from risk. (Ch 1)

**Hazard Tracking and Risk Resolution (HTR)** — the closed-loop mechanism that carries medium- and
high-risk hazards to verified closure, gated by documented management risk acceptance. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Health Hazard Assessment (HHA)** — design-phase analysis identifying and quantifying hazardous
materials and physical agents, with life-cycle-cost-aware controls. (Ch 3)

**Initiating hazard** — the event that starts an accident sequence by pushing a balanced system
toward imbalance. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Integrated System Hazard Analysis** — the logical combining of separate hazard analyses into one
coherent risk evaluation around human–machine–environment interactions; not a stapling of reports.
(Ch 3)

**Integrated System Safety Program Plan (ISSPP)** — the SSPP-modeled plan for large multi-
subcontractor programs, adding an integration manager and an integrated working group. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Managing Activity / Managing Authority (MA)** — the responsible FAA organization for a program;
approves procedures, resolves safety-vs-design and inter-contractor conflicts. (Ch 1)

**Maximum Probable Loss (MPL)** — launch-licensing methodology setting the financial-responsibility
(insurance) amount from probable (not possible) third-party and government-property loss. (Ch 6)

**Operating and Support Hazard Analysis (O&SHA)** — analysis of procedurally controlled operation
and support across the life cycle; usually the last design-phase analysis completed. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Operational Risk Management (ORM)** — a six-step, repeatable decision method for managing risk
during operational use, run pre-emptively (e.g., before each flight). (Ch 7)

**Operational Safety Assessment (OSA)** — the pre-investment, system-level safety-requirements
assessment (OSED + OHA) for CNS/ATM systems; not a classical risk assessment. (Ch 2)

**Order 8040.4 (FAA)** — the originating 1998 policy mandating disciplined, documented safety risk
management across every Line of Business. (Ch 1)

**Preliminary Hazard Analysis (PHA)** — the foundational design-phase analysis that frames all
later analyses; lands before preliminary design review. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Preliminary Hazard List (PHL)** — the early seed list of hazards carried into the RFP, contract,
and specifications; enables a proactive program. (Ch 1, Ch 3)

**Requirements Hazard Analysis (RHA)** — analysis translating hazards and generic standards into
design requirements; substantially complete by the allocated baseline. (Ch 3)

**Residual risk** — the risk remaining after controls; must be knowingly accepted by the
appropriate authority and documented before a hazard is closed. (Ch 1, Ch 7)

**Risk** — the coupling of severity of consequence and likelihood of occurrence; both must be
characterized. (Ch 1)

**Risk Acceptability Matrix** — the severity-by-likelihood matrix producing High/Medium/Low bands
that serves as management's risk-acceptance criterion. (Ch 1)

**Safety Action Record (SAR)** — the tracking record carrying each hazard's description, causes,
initial/residual risk, controls, verification, and the concurrences needed to close it. (Ch 1)

**Safety Order of Precedence** — the fixed priority for treating hazards: design out, then safety
devices, then warning devices, then procedures and training. (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 3)

**SHEL(L) model** — a system-description model (Software, Hardware, Environment, Liveware) stressing
that interface matches/mismatches matter as much as the elements. (Ch 1)

**Sneak Circuit Analysis (SCA)** — a method finding latent topological conditions that misbehave
without any component failing; classified as sneak paths, timing, labels, and indicators. (Ch 4)

**Software Hazard Criticality Matrix (SHCM)** — pairs a software control category with severity to
rank how much analysis and test a software contributor needs. (Ch 5, Ch 6)

**Subsystem / System Hazard Analysis (SSHA / SHA)** — SSHA examines how a component affects the
whole; SHA examines how the whole and its interfaces affect subsystem safety. (Ch 3)

**System Safety Program Plan (SSPP)** — the contractually binding plan defining how a program meets
its safety requirements; its MIL-STD-882 Section 4 core is mandatory and non-tailorable. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Systematic Software Safety Process (SSSP)** — the organized, phase-mapped review of safety-
critical software for a launch operation. (Ch 6)

**Target Level of Safety (TLS)** — the inverse coupling of severity and tolerable probability used
to allocate requirements: the more catastrophic the outcome, the rarer it must be made. (Ch 2)

**5M model** — a system-description model (Mission, Man, Machine, Management, Media) used to capture
the interactions among people, hardware, procedures, and environment. (Ch 1, Ch 3, Ch 7)
