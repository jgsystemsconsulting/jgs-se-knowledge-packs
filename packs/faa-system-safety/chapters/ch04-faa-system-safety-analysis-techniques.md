# Chapter — Safety Analysis Techniques (FTA, FMECA, Common-Cause, Sneak Circuit, Energy Trace)

> Source: FAA System Safety Handbook, Chapter 9 ("Analysis Techniques"), December 30, 2000. This chapter paraphrases and synthesizes that material; it is not a verbatim reproduction. Use the FAA original as the authoritative text — reprint commentary added by third-party redistributors is not public domain.

## Core Idea

A safety program needs more than one analytical tool because hazards present themselves from two opposite directions. Some questions are *event-driven* — "what set of conditions could produce an airplane crash or an airspace encroachment?" — and others are *consequence-driven* — "if the pilot is overloaded during taxi, or a pump shaft bearing seizes, what follows?" The handbook organizes its techniques around this split, and the practical lesson is that the two framings catch different hazards. A top-down deductive method that starts from a defined accident can overlook a non-obvious top event, while a bottom-up inductive method that walks every component failure can drown the program in failures that never lead to an accident. No single technique is complete; they are chosen and combined to cover each other's blind spots.

The chapter's deeper idea is that hazard analysis is an exercise in disciplined modeling under uncertainty. Each technique imposes a structure — a tree, a matrix, a topographical node map, an energy inventory — that forces the analyst to reason systematically about how a system fails rather than guessing. But every structure carries assumptions, and the handbook is unusually candid that the math is only as good as the data feeding it: chasing exact numbers from inexact inputs is named as one of the field's recurring pitfalls.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

The handbook names and characterizes the following techniques in Chapter 9. The exact source labels are preserved.

- **Fault Hazard Analysis** (Section 9.2). A deductive method that can run purely qualitatively or be extended to a quantitative form. It is described as a member of the FMEA/FMECA family of reliability analyses, distinguished only by depth: where FMEA/FMECA examine all failures and their effects, the Fault Hazard Analysis is concerned only with the safety-relevant effects. Use it when you want a safety-focused subset of an FMEA, for design concept selection, "designing out" critical failure modes, operational planning, and feeding management risk-control efforts.

- **Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)** (Section 9.3). A deductive, top-down graphical logic tool that begins from a single defined undesired event (the example used is "engine failure") and works downward through the combinations of faults and events that could cause it. Qualitative trees come first and are always the starting point; quantitative (Boolean) treatment is layered on when the added value justifies the cost. The handbook points to Figure 8-5 for the standardized FTA symbology and uses Figure 9-1 (the sample engine-failure tree) to illustrate. Use FTA when interrelationships between causes matter and a graphical "map of failure paths" aids communication.

- **Common Cause Failure Analysis (CCFA)** (Section 9.4). Presented explicitly as an *extension* of FTA. After a fault tree is built, CCFA examines the primary events of the minimal cut sets to find "coupling factors" — shared environment, location, secondary causes, human error, or quality-control links — that make supposedly independent failures dependent. Its purpose is to test redundancy for whether it is "real." Use it after a detailed FTA exists and component, layout, operator, and inspector data are available.

- **Sneak Circuit Analysis (SCA)** (Section 9.5). A specialized method for electrical/electronic circuits that recognizes recurring topological patterns. It hunts for latent ("sneak") conditions that block intended functions or trigger unintended ones *without any component having failed*. Schematics are converted to topographical drawings (Figures 9-2 and 9-3 show an automobile sneak-circuit example and its node-topograph equivalent), and the work is labor-intensive enough that it is best done with special-purpose software. Use it on high-reliability, high-criticality systems — the handbook cites nuclear plant safety subsystems, ordnance handling, and spacecraft as analogues, and suggests FAA autopilot-type systems that remove human control as candidates.

- **Energy Trace** (Section 9.6). A hazard analysis built around energy: it addresses all sources of controlled and uncontrolled energy that could cause an accident, whether tied to the product/process, the resource being protected, or the surrounding items and conditions. It is explicitly complementary — its outputs can seed top events for a fault tree or check the completeness of a Fault Hazard Analysis — and it does not replace FTA, SCA, event trees, or FMEAs. The handbook lays out fourteen procedural steps and references Figure 9-4 (energy sources and transfer modes) and the precedence in Chapter 3, Table 3-1, for controlling identified energy hazards.

- **Failure Modes, Effects, and Criticality Analysis (FMECA)** (Section 9.7). A reliability tool that supplies data usable by the system safety program. An FMEA is the first step; the FMECA is produced by adding a criticality figure of merit. It is typically bottom-up (though it can run either direction), considers only hardware and software failures, and is generated from a different question set than a hazard analysis — "if this fails, what is the system impact, can I detect it, and will it cause anything else to fail?" Figure 9-5 gives a sample FMECA worksheet built around a coolant-pump bearing. Use the FMECA version, not bare FMEA, for hazard-control work.

- **System Safety Analysis Handbook (Stephens & Talso)** (Section 9.8). Not an FAA technique but the cited external catalog: the System Safety Society's *System Safety Analysis Handbook* (2nd Edition, August 1999), which summarizes 106 safety methodologies. Chapter 9's Table 9-1 reproduces the subset the FAA judges applicable, each with a one-line summary and a note on applicability and use.

## Key Concepts

**Deductive versus inductive direction.** The handbook keeps returning to this axis. FTA and Fault Hazard Analysis are deductive — they reason from a defined undesired event back toward causes, considering *combinations* of events in the cause path. FMEA/FMECA are characteristically inductive and bottom-up, walking from a low-level failure forward to its effects. The trade-off is stated plainly: a top-down approach like FTA can miss a non-obvious top-level hazard, while a bottom-up approach generates many failures that never become accidents.

**Qualitative first, then quantitative.** For both FTA and the Fault Hazard Analysis, the qualitative pass is the foundation and is described as cost-effective and invaluable on its own. Quantitative treatment multiplies the value but is more expensive and often difficult; for FTA it requires assigning probabilities to the lowest events, forming a Boolean equation for the top event, and comparing the result to a system-level requirement, with corrective action ranging from redesign to refining the analysis itself.

**FTA symbology and event vocabulary.** An "event" is any occurrence — normal or abnormal. Rectangles denote events built from lower-level events; circles denote events needing no further development; triangles denote events developed further because they depend on lower events; diamonds denote undeveloped events, usually for lack of information; and a "house" denotes a normal (rather than failure) event, such as the presence of electrical power. The handbook also distinguishes *primary* events (a pump failing from a bad bearing) from *secondary* events (a pump failing because ice formed when antifreeze was omitted), and *faults* (an ignition turned off at the wrong time) from *failures* (an ignition switch that will not conduct current). AND gates and OR gates are the Boolean operators; the engine example uses an AND gate for redundant ignition systems (both must fail) and OR gates for alternative fuel-flow causes.

**Cut sets and minimal cut sets.** A cut set is a collection of basic events whose joint occurrence fails the system; a minimal cut set has been reduced to remove redundant fault paths. CCFA works on the primary events of these minimal cut sets, which is what lets it test whether redundancy is genuine or only apparent.

**The CCFA procedure in brief.** Establish "Critical Tree Groups" by combining FMECA, FTA, and SCA — FTA identifies critical functions, FMECA identifies critical components, and SCA exposes hidden interrelationships. Then find common components within those groups (redundant processors sharing one power source; redundant hydraulic lines fed by one pump or routed physically adjacent), identify credible failure modes, narrow to credible *common-cause* failure modes using system knowledge, lessons learned, and historical data, and summarize with corrective actions.

**Four categories of sneak condition.** SCA classifies what it finds into sneak paths (current flowing along an unsuspected route), sneak timing (functions inhibited or occurring unexpectedly), sneak labels (incorrect stimuli initiated), and sneak indicators (ambiguous or false displays). Beyond sneak circuits proper, SCA also surfaces drawing errors (reported incrementally on Drawing Error Reports, typically disagreements within or between input documents) and design-concern conditions such as unsuppressed or improperly suppressed inductive loads, excess components, lack of redundancy, and failure points. Its three reported products are sneak-circuit, design-concern, and drawing-error conditions.

**Energy-trace as a completeness check.** The energy method's purpose is to ensure all hazards and their immediate causes are identified so they can serve as fault-tree top events or verify the completeness of a Fault Hazard Analysis. Causal factors are traced independent of probability — the governing question is simply whether the factor *can* occur or exist — and a top-level factor (excessive stress) may have subordinate factors beneath it (inadequate design, material flaws, poor welds, excessive loads). The fourteen steps run from identifying the resource being protected, through operational phases and operating states, energy sources and release mechanisms, causal factors, potential accidents and consequences, hazard category, down to recommending controls and specifying verification.

**FMECA worksheet logic.** For each low-level component or function, list every reasonable failure mode, then record its effect at the local, intermediate, and system levels. The coolant-pump bearing example runs "shaft won't turn" (local) → "pump won't circulate coolant" (intermediate) → "engine overheat and fail" (system). The analyst then documents the fault-detection method (monitoring pump-motor power or coolant temperature), which has real safety value because it can warn a crew before a catastrophic event, and identifies compensating provisions (redundant pumps, combined cooling) before assigning severity. Criticality — the figure of merit that turns an FMEA into an FMECA — is a mathematical combination of severity and probability of occurrence, and the index must be defined for the *purpose*: a high-failure-rate part scores high for reliability and a long-lead-time part scores high for supportability, yet neither may matter for safety, so a safety analysis needs its own criticality equation.

## Mental Models

**Two doors into the same building.** Event-driven and consequence-driven framings are two entrances to the hazard space. Walk in through "what could cause this accident?" and you build downward (FTA). Walk in through "if this fails, what happens?" and you build upward (FMECA). Neither door lets you see the whole building; that is why the handbook treats the techniques as a set, not a menu of substitutes.

**The tree as a shared map.** The handbook's claim for FTA is communicative as much as analytical: a non-technical person can read a completed tree, with minimal training, and see the combinations and alternatives that lead to a hazard. The graphical format is judged superior to a tabular matrix *for showing interrelationships* — but the matrix is still needed to carry severity, criticality, family-tree, probability, and cause information. Hold both: the tree for relationships, the matrix for attributes.

**Redundancy is a hypothesis, not a fact.** CCFA exists because two "independent" channels can share a hidden coupling — one power supply, one pump, or merely adjacent routing. Treat every claimed redundancy as a hypothesis to be falsified by looking for the common cause, rather than a property you can assume from the block diagram.

**Failure without failure.** The defining intuition of SCA is that a system can misbehave with every component working exactly to spec — the fault lives in the topology, not in any part. When a system "shouldn't be able to do that" yet does, suspect a sneak condition before suspecting a broken component.

**Energy wants out.** Energy-trace reframes accidents as energy escaping control. Once you inventory where energy is stored and how it transfers, the control strategies follow a hierarchy: prevent accumulation (limit voltage, pressure, speed, stored combustibles), prevent release (containment, insulation, lockouts), modify the release (shock absorbers, relief valves, blowout panels), separate assets from the energy in time or space, interpose barriers (guards, shields, protective equipment), raise the damage threshold (stronger design), and finally plan contingency response (early detection, first aid, recovery procedures).

**Detection buys time.** In the FMECA model, the fault-detection column is not bookkeeping — it is the difference between a frozen bearing that is caught by a temperature monitor and one that is discovered as an engine failure. Designing the detection is designing the chance to act before the accident.

## Anti-patterns

The handbook explicitly names the following as limitations or pitfalls.

- **Over-precision in the mathematics.** Called out as one of the greatest pitfalls in fault hazard analysis and other techniques: analysts extract "exact" numbers from inexact data and spend more effort polishing the analysis than eliminating the hazard.

- **Tracking every failure in the safety program.** A subsystem has many failures that never cause accidents; following all of them in the System Safety Program is costly and inefficient. The handbook's remedy is to combine the Fault Hazard Analysis with a reliability-program FMEA/FMECA rather than duplicating the effort.

- **Neglecting human factors.** The method concentrates on hardware, treats software less, and often gives inadequate attention to human factors. A switch with a negligible failure rate may be dropped from consideration even though its *placement* causes accidents — the handbook's example is a power switch and a light switch of similar design placed adjacent to each other, inviting operator error.

- **Considering environmental conditions without their probability.** Environmental stresses are usually considered, but the probability that those conditions actually occur is rarely considered, which can lead to applying controls for unrealistic events.

- **Ignoring latent manufacturing defects.** Probability-of-failure reasoning for hardware hazards tends to ignore latent defects introduced by substandard manufacturing, so some hazards are missed.

- **Misunderstanding physics of failure.** Effort can be wasted trying to control a non-realistic hazard — the handbook's example is that most resistors cannot fail shorted, so analyzing a resistor short squanders effort. It similarly warns that a single component failure rate (as in the cited MIL-HDBK-217) often covers all of a component's failure modes at once, so care is needed that the probability used actually matches the specific failure mode being evaluated.

- **FTA missing non-obvious top events.** Because FTA is top-down and starts from a postulated undesired event, it can miss top-level hazards that were never postulated — a blind spot the inductive Fault Hazard Analysis and FMECA help cover.

## Key Takeaways

- Hazard analysis splits into event-driven (top-down, deductive) and consequence-driven (bottom-up, inductive) framings; the techniques are complementary and chosen to cover each other's blind spots, not interchangeable.
- The Fault Hazard Analysis is a safety-focused, depth-limited member of the FMEA/FMECA family — it tracks only safety-relevant effects.
- FTA builds a graphical Boolean logic model downward from one undesired event; do the qualitative tree first, quantify only when the value justifies the cost, and remember the tree communicates relationships while a matrix still carries severity/criticality/probability.
- CCFA extends FTA by attacking the primary events of minimal cut sets to find coupling factors, testing whether redundancy is real; it leans on FTA, FMECA, and SCA together to scope the "Critical Tree Groups."
- SCA finds latent conditions in electrical/electronic systems that misbehave without any component failing, classified as sneak paths, timing, labels, and indicators; it is expensive and reserved for high-criticality systems.
- Energy-trace inventories controlled and uncontrolled energy and complements rather than replaces other methods — its outputs feed fault-tree top events and verify Fault-Hazard-Analysis completeness; control follows a prevent/modify/separate/barrier/threshold/contingency hierarchy.
- The FMECA adds a purpose-specific criticality figure of merit (severity combined with probability) to an FMEA, considers only hardware/software failures, and earns its safety value largely through the fault-detection and compensating-provisions columns.
- Guard against the named pitfalls: false precision, tracking non-safety failures, neglecting human factors, ignoring condition probabilities and latent manufacturing defects, misjudging physics of failure, and over-trusting a single top-down view.
