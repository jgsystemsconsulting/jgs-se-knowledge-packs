# Chapter 2: Safety in the Acquisition Lifecycle — Pre/Post-Investment Assessments and Contracting

## Core Idea
System safety runs as a continuous thread through the FAA's Acquisition Management System (AMS) lifecycle, changing form at each gate. Before the investment decision it generates early requirements and objective comparison data so decision makers can choose among alternatives; after the investment decision it becomes a contractually binding program defined by the System Safety Program Plan (SSPP); and at contract award it becomes a set of explicit specification, Statement of Work, and data-deliverable provisions — because anything not written into the contract is something the contractor has no obligation to do. The discipline's payoff is timing: a hazard caught during concept costs a design change, the same hazard caught after deployment may only be controllable through procedures and training, at far higher long-term cost.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Operational Safety Assessment (OSA)**: The pre-investment, system-level safety-requirements assessment for CNS/ATM systems, built on the RTCA/SC-189 framework. It is composed of two parts — an Operational Services & Environment Description (OSED) describing the system, environment, air-traffic services, and procedures (ground and air), and an Operational Hazard Assessment (OHA) that qualitatively classifies the operational hazards by severity. When to use: during Mission Analysis and Investment Analysis (before JRC-1 and JRC-2) to establish early, top-level safety requirements. Note: the OSA is explicitly *not* a classical risk assessment — its job is to surface requirements early, though it feeds the follow-on risk assessments required by FAA Order 8040.4.
- **Allocation of Safety Objectives and Requirements (ASOR)**: The process that converts hazard severity into two tiers — an *objective* (the desired probability) and a *requirement* (the minimum acceptable probability) — by mapping each classified hazard to a Target Level of Safety. When to use: after the OHA, to write testable "shall" statements that allocate controls across the air and ground elements of a system.
- **Comparative Safety Assessment (CSA)**: A genuine risk assessment (severity *and* likelihood) performed to rank competing alternatives — design, procedure, or program changes — for management decision making. One alternative is frequently the "no change" baseline. When to use: in trade studies and option evaluations where the decision maker must see the relative safety merit of each choice.
- **System Safety Program Plan (SSPP)**: The contractually binding agreement on how the contractor will meet the specified safety requirements; it documents scope, organization, milestones, requirements, hazard analyses, safety data, verification, audit, training, and reporting. When to use: prepared in preliminary form before the investment decision and again after JRC-2, then submitted as a deliverable for managing-activity (MA) approval, typically 30–45 days after contract start.
- **Integrated System Safety Program Plan (ISSPP)**: An SSPP-modeled plan (rooted in MIL-STD-882C) for large or complex multi-subcontractor programs, adding a System Safety Integration Manager, an Integrated System Safety Working Group (ISSWG), and integrated scheduling, requirements, hazard tracking, verification, audit, and reporting. When to use: when many parts, subsystems, partners, or contractors must be logically combined under one integrator.
- **Safety Order of Precedence**: The fixed priority sequence for controlling risk — (1) design for minimum risk, (2) incorporate safety devices, (3) provide warning devices, (4) develop procedures and training. When to use: whenever selecting controls; falling back to procedures/training for severe hazards normally requires concurrence of authority.
- **Contracting Process (AMS)**: The acquisition sequence — planning, documenting detailed requirements, communicating them to industry, evaluating proposals, negotiating/selecting a source, and managing the award. When to use: across Solution Implementation, with the safety hooks (specification, SOW, CDRL, bidders' instructions) inserted at each step.

## Key Concepts
- **Pre-investment dual purpose**: Safety work before the investment decision exists to (1) seed the foundational safety and system-engineering requirements and (2) hand decision makers objective, data-driven safety inputs for choosing among alternatives early, when changes are cheap.
- **OSED / OHA split**: The OSED is essentially a 5M-model system description that bounds what is and is not analyzed; the OHA derives a Preliminary Hazard List from the system functions, then assigns each hazard a worst-credible severity per MIL-STD-882 and FAR/AMJ 25.1309.
- **Target Level of Safety (TLS)**: The inverse relationship between severity and tolerable probability — as severity rises, the required probability of occurrence falls (e.g., a "hazardous"/severity-2 hazard requires a demonstrated probability of "extremely remote" with an objective of "extremely improbable").
- **Function → hazard → control → requirement chain**: The handbook walks NAS functions into hazards, hazards into existing/recommended controls, and controls into measurable "shall" requirements allocated across air and ground elements — the OSA being a primary source of early safety requirements.
- **Hazard identification toolset**: Operations analysis, the "what-if" tool, scenario process, logic diagrams, and change/cause-and-effect analysis are offered to survey hazards across all operational phases and prioritize the highest-risk areas; in low-hazard cases the Preliminary Hazard Analysis may be the final identification step.
- **CSA principles**: A CSA must be objective, unbiased, and complete; assumptions are used only when data is missing and must be conservative and clearly flagged; severity is judged independently of likelihood, while likelihood is the probability of an *accident* (not merely the hazard) occurring at the stated severity.
- **SSPP as a baseline contract instrument**: Once approved, the SSPP becomes the SSP baseline that the FAA uses to track contractor progress and that protects against downstream methodology disputes; MIL-STD-882 Section 4 requirements are the only mandatory, non-tailorable core.
- **Safety-manager independence**: The SSPP must place a system safety manager as a staff function reporting directly to the program manager, with unrestricted access to all management levels; organizations that route safety authority through lower management tiers are deemed unacceptable.
- **Tailoring**: Every SSP is tailored to the system's criticality, acquisition size, and lifecycle phase; programs are categorized Low / Moderate / High risk, and the category decision is revisited at each phase as better information emerges.
- **Special acquisition cases**: Government-Furnished Equipment (the contractor owns interface safety but not the GFE's inherent risk) and COTS/NDI (requires an intended-use safety assessment before purchase, with the MA setting minimum analysis requirements because COTS suppliers often have no SSP) each get distinct tailoring guidance.
- **Contract primacy**: Because the contract is the legal interface, unspecified safety analyses or tests are simply not delivered — the IPT leader must define safety requirements early enough to negotiate them in. The specification (health/safety criteria, safety requirements, functional-area characteristics, quality-conformance inspections), the SOW system-safety section, the CDRL, and the bidders' instructions are the four solicitation vehicles that shape the final SSP.

## Mental Models
- **Two assessments, two questions**: An OSA answers "what must this system be required to do to be safe?" (sets the target); a CSA answers "which of these alternatives is safest?" (measures current risk). Use the OSA to write requirements, the CSA to rank choices.
- **Severity drives probability, not the reverse**: When allocating requirements, fix severity first from worst-credible outcome, then read across the TLS chart to the probability the design must achieve — the more catastrophic the consequence, the rarer it must be made.
- **Tie deliverables to milestones, not dates**: Schedule safety analyses and SSPP tasks relative to design reviews (e.g., 30–45 days before a review) rather than calendar dates, so master-schedule slips do not trigger costly contractual revisions or schedule-date waivers.
- **Earlier is cheaper**: Treat the design timeline as a cost gradient for hazards — eliminate by design first; the later a hazard surfaces (test, production, deployment), the more it forces permanent risk into temporary procedural patches.
- **Fix the size of an uncertain analysis at negotiation**: When a downstream design decision will swing analysis cost (e.g., how many fault trees a fly-by-wire vs. mechanical choice demands), negotiate a fixed count both parties agree to populate later, so the task tailors to the eventual design without cost or performance surprises.
- **Compliance matrix as the evaluation spine**: Key the bidder's response to specific specification and SOW paragraphs through an RFP-required compliance matrix and price the SSP against the Work Breakdown Structure, so safety cost and coverage stay visible.

## Anti-patterns
- **Vague SOW safety statements**: A bare one-line clause directing the contractor merely to run an SSP to identify and control accident risk is called out as inadequate — a contractor could read it as no more than cautioning its designers to watch for hazards. The SOW system-safety section must say exactly what kind of SSP is required.
- **Letting cost-competitive procurement starve safety**: The handbook warns that the system safety effort can become "the victim of a cost competitive procurement"; requiring SSPP approval (and sometimes a preliminary SSPP with the proposal) gives the MA the control to prevent under-costed safety programs.
- **Budget-driven design without risk assessment**: The handbook observes that budget pressure commonly steers design decisions without a proper risk assessment; removing safety representation lets short-term cost cuts raise accident risk while ignoring the far larger economics of an accident.
- **Burying safety authority in lower management**: Organizations that demonstrate safety responsibility only through lower management levels are explicitly judged ineffective and unacceptable.
- **Over-tailoring a small program to zero**: Small-program risks can be as severe and likely as a major program's; tailoring must optimize, not gut, the SSP — over-reducing scope is a named failure.
- **Citing whole standards wholesale**: Invoking an entire document or design handbook and expecting blanket compliance is called unrealistic; safety requirements should be specific, with acceptable probability numbers assigned where practical for the most severe hazard categories.
- **Penalizing bidders for unstated requirements**: Evaluators are cautioned not to mark a contractor down for omitting something not explicitly or reasonably implicitly in the stated requirements.

## Key Takeaways
1. Pre-investment safety has two products — early requirements and objective decision data — delivered through the OSA (which sets targets) and the CSA (which ranks alternatives, baseline included).
2. The OSA is deliberately not a risk assessment; it exists to pull requirements forward, then feeds the FAA Order 8040.4 risk assessments via its OSED, hazard list, and severity codes.
3. ASOR translates severity into a paired objective/requirement against a Target Level of Safety, then writes those controls as measurable "shall" statements allocated across air and ground elements.
4. After the investment decision, safety becomes a contract: the SSPP is the binding baseline, its MIL-STD-882 Section 4 core is mandatory and non-tailorable, and it is normally due 30–45 days after contract start (sometimes preliminarily with the proposal).
5. Control selection follows a fixed Safety Order of Precedence — design, then safety devices, then warnings, then procedures/training — with falling back to procedures for severe hazards generally needing concurrence of authority.
6. Complex multi-contractor efforts escalate to an ISSPP with an integration manager and ISSWG; the prime is not always the integrator — sometimes the MA integrates several associate contractors.
7. The contract is the only enforceable interface: define safety in the specification, SOW, CDRL, and bidders' instructions early, or the contractor is not obligated to deliver it.
8. Tailoring is mandatory and phase-revisited; GFE and COTS/NDI acquisitions get their own rules because inherent-risk ownership and supplier maturity differ from developmental items.
9. Tie deliverables to milestones rather than dates, fix the scope of cost-volatile analyses at negotiation, and keep deliverables to the minimum needed to support the review-and-approval cycle.

## Connects To
- **MIL-STD-882**: Source of the mandatory SSP core (Section 4), the Safety Order of Precedence, severity definitions, and the personnel-qualification baseline; the handbook notes MIL-STD-882 definitions differ from FAA usage, so the SOW must state that FAA definitions supersede them.
- **RTCA/SC-189**: The internationally developed framework underlying the OSA's OSED/OHA structure for CNS/ATM systems.
- **FAA Order 8040.4**: The order requiring the follow-on safety risk assessments that the OSA feeds.
- **FAR/AMJ 25.1309**: Cited alongside MIL-STD-882 for determining worst-credible hazard severity.
- **FAA System Engineering Manual**: Referenced for functional analysis, verification and validation, and the Design Analysis Report.
- **AMS lifecycle (JRC-1 / JRC-2 gates)**: the five phases that anchor when each safety assessment is performed — Mission Analysis, then Investment Analysis, then Solution Implementation, then In-Service Management, and finally Service Life Extension.
- **Hazard Tracking / Risk Resolution (closed-loop)**: The Safety Action Record-based process that the SSPP and ISSPP carry forward into program execution.
