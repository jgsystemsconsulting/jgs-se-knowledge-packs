# Chapter — System Software Safety, Test & Evaluation Safety, and Facilities Safety

## Core Idea
Software, test campaigns, and physical facilities are each a domain where system-safety discipline must be applied, but none of them is a self-contained safety problem. The Handbook's recurring thesis across these three chapters is that software safety is a *systems* issue solved with good systems-engineering practice (not a programming specialty), that testing is both a *source* of safety evidence and a *hazard* in its own right, and that a facility is a major subsystem whose risks are managed over a full acquisition life cycle from site selection through demolition. In all three cases the same engine runs underneath: identify hazards, trace causes to contributors, derive requirements that eliminate or control the risk per the order of precedence, verify by analysis or test, and track every hazard to closure with documented residual-risk acceptance.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names)
- **Software Safety Process Steps (five-step process, Section 10.3)** — Planning and Management → Assign Software Criticality → Safety-Critical Requirements Derivation → Design and Analyses → Testing. The Handbook adapts this process from the **JSSSC Software System Safety Handbook** (Dec 1999), borrowing concepts from **RTCA DO-178B** — the Dec 1992 standard on software considerations for certifying airborne systems and equipment. Use it as the spine of any program where software performs safety-significant functions.
- **Software Safety Working Group (SwSWG)** — a sub-group of the System Safety Working Group, chaired by the safety manager, responsible for monitoring and controlling the software safety program, resolving risks with software contributors, interfacing with the other IPTs, and performing the final safety assessment of the software design. Stood up as the System Safety Program is established.
- **Software Hazard Criticality Matrix (SHCM, Figure 10-4)** — pairs a software control category against a severity level to produce a risk ranking that drives how much analysis and test a given software contributor needs. The example matrix is extracted from MIL-STD-882C for illustration only.
- **Software control-capability categories** — two named listings the team reviews and tailors: **MIL-STD-882C** (categories I, IIa, IIb, IIIa, IIIb, IV, ordered by how autonomously the software acts and whether intervention is possible) and **RTCA DO-178B** (levels A through E, ordered by the failure-condition severity the software's anomalous behavior could cause). Both express the software's *control capability over* a hazard, not a software failure probability.
- **Software Safety Requirements Derivation (Figure 10-5)** — the flow PHL → PHA → Safety Requirements Criteria Analysis (SRCA) → SSHA/SHA → Software Safety Assessment Report, used to derive system-specific safety-critical software requirements and trace them to test.
- **System Safety Assessment Report (SSAR / SAR, Section 10.4)** — typically a CDRL deliverable that encapsulates all analyses and tests, the criteria and methodology used to rank software-related hazards, residual-risk hazards and their assessment, significant hazards with their precautions, and a closing risk-acceptance statement signed by the program safety lead engineer.
- **Test and Evaluation Safety tasks and results (Sections 11.4.1–11.4.2)** — a named task list (coordinate with test planning, schedule safety review of test plans, analyze test equipment and instrumentation, identify test-unique hazards and controls, review results for new hazards, document and track every hazard) and a named product list (hazard analysis reports, test safety analysis reports, a hazard tracking and risk resolution system, lists of hazards/controls/required data/warnings, and safety inputs to training and operations manuals).
- **Facility Acquisition Life Cycle (Figure 12-1)** — four phases: Site Selection (pre-construction), New Facility (design and construction), Existing Facility (re-engineering of structure and equipment), and Facility/Equipment Decommissioning. System-safety technique is applied across all four.
- **Facility System Safety Program Plan (SSPP, Section 12.4.2)** — the road map prepared by the SSWG tailoring the system-safety effort to the project: risk-acceptance criteria, task list with responsible parties, milestone schedule, hazard-tracking and residual-risk procedures, lessons-learned capture, and a change-evaluation mechanism. Detailed against the Chapter 5 SSPP template.
- **Operational / Occupational Risk Management Group (ORMG, Sections 12.4.4–12.4.7)** — a multi-discipline group (fire protection, system safety, environmental and industrial engineering, industrial hygiene, hazardous-materials management) that executes a nine-element process: develop system knowledge → hazard identification (master matrix) → hazard control & analysis → requirements cross-check → risk assessment → hazard tracking & risk resolution → document in the initial Safety Engineering Report (iterative) → system-safety monitoring → update the SER → progress to the next system.
- **Facility analytical techniques (Section 12.5)** — Change Analysis (worst-case effects of each modification from a baseline, often using a system walk-down), Preliminary Hazard List (PHL), Preliminary Hazard Analysis (PHA), Operating and Support Hazard Analysis (O&SHA), Job Safety Analysis (JSA, formerly Job Hazard Analysis), and Physical Aviation Risk Analysis.
- **Facility risk-analysis methodology (Section 12.6)** — Risk Assessment (severity × likelihood, prioritized), Hazard Control Analysis, Requirements Cross-Check Analysis (relating each hazard control to OSHA/FAA/NFPA/Human-Factors requirement references), and Hazard Tracking & Risk Resolution (validation + verification to closure).
- **Hazardous Material Management Program (HMMP, per NAS 411)** — a program complementing the SSPP: HMMP plan, life-cycle cost analysis of material alternatives, documented trade-off analyses, training, and an HMMP report.

## Key Concepts
- **Software does not fail like hardware.** It does not wear out, break, or drift in tolerance; its "failures" are overwhelmingly requirements errors (failing to anticipate conditions that lead to a hazard) or implementation errors (coding, misinterpreted design). Because software-error occurrence cannot yet be reliably predicted or quantified, the traditional hardware-oriented Hazard Risk Index is insufficient on its own for hazards with software causal factors.
- **Severity is invariant to cause; probability is not.** Whatever the contributor — hardware, software, human error, or software-influenced human error — the *consequence* of a hazard is the same, so the Chapter 3 severity tables still apply. What changes is how likelihood is reasoned about: software contribution is assessed via *control capability* and combined with hardware and human-error likelihoods (Figure 10-2).
- **Software failure mechanisms** the analyst evaluates: failing to perform a required function; performing an unrequired function (wrong answer, wrong command, right action under wrong conditions); timing/sequencing faults; failing to recognize a hazardous condition or a safety-critical function; or producing an intended-but-inappropriate response. The most common underlying causes are **specification errors** (the single largest cause), design/coding errors, hardware/computer-induced errors, documentation errors, and debugging/change-induced hazards.
- **Software safety is a team sport.** An isolated safety engineer cannot solve software-caused hazards alone; the team should include the safety engineer, software engineer, system engineer, software quality engineer, the relevant "-ility" engineers (configuration management, T&E, V&V, reliability, human factors), and the subsystem domain engineer.
- **Trace, don't just analyze.** System-specific software safety requirements come from flowing hazard controls down into subsystem requirements with an audit trail linking each requirement to its risk and to the affected modules of code. PHA-phase causes are defined at least to the CSCI level; SSHA and SHA analyze causes down to the algorithm level where appropriate. Verification is organized through SSR verification trees that link each safety-significant SSR to a Safety-Critical Function (SCF) to a hazard.
- **Verify by test where you can.** The Software Safety Engineer chooses analysis or test per the requirement's criticality, but the Handbook's stated preference is testing for verification whenever possible. Two analyses bracket the test phase: analysis *before* the fact (to ensure the tests are valid and cover Must-Work-Functions) and analysis *of* the results.
- **100% test coverage is usually unattainable.** The permutation space of program states and the indeterminate number of environmental variables defeat exhaustive testing; white-box testing applies at the module level, and statistical methods such as Monte Carlo simulation help plan worst-case credible scenarios. For high-value, high-risk software an Independent Verification and Validation (IV&V) organization participates as an independent group in validating test analysis.
- **Testing cuts both ways.** Tests run *specifically for safety* prove whether hazards exist, whether mitigations work, or whether the hazard analysis is correct (flammability, extinguisher effectiveness, PPE, RF radiation characteristics). Tests run *for other purposes* (performance verification, failure-rate determination, interface checks, vendor QC) still yield safety data — but only if safety engineering defines its data needs up front so test planners collect and document the right information. Separately, the **test itself is a hazardous activity** requiring its own hazard analysis and controls.
- **A facility is a subsystem.** "Facility" spans the structures, surrounding areas, and operations in or near them, and its risks (structural, HVAC, electrical, hydraulic, pressure/pneumatic, fire protection, water treatment, material handling, routine and special operations) are managed with the same MIL-STD-882 process used for airborne and ground systems, tailored to scope and complexity and scaled to the degree of risk.
- **Facility directive hierarchy.** The effort starts with two FAA orders — 1600.46 and the Occupational Safety and Health Program order, 3900.19. The Handbook is explicit that every effort should begin from 3900.19 rather than from narrower related orders (e.g., FAA Order 6000.15) or specifications (e.g., FAA-G-2100), since those carry only a fraction of the safety-and-health requirements that 3900.19 and the OSHA standards hold.
- **Three facility risk categories.** Low-risk (housing, administrative — usually no further hazard analysis), medium-risk (maintenance facilities, heating plants, ATC buildings — PHA appropriate, possibly SHA/SSHA), and high-risk (high-energy facilities, fuel storage, aircraft maintenance — unique hazards best assessed by an experienced user who prepares the PHA as well as the PHL). Categorization weighs people exposed, inherent hazard, criticality to the NAS, vulnerability, and cost.
- **Closure has three legal forms.** A logged safety risk is closed when it is (1) eliminated/controlled by design, (2) controlled by procedures (closed but annotated so SOPs get written and handed to the user), or (3) accepted as-is or with partial controls (closed with risk-acceptance documentation). The hazard tracking system carries this status across the whole facility life cycle.
- **Listing is not approval.** Certification by a nationally recognized testing laboratory (e.g., UL) only attests conformance to a specific product test standard; it does not make a product "OSHA-approved" or guarantee acceptable risk in a given application. COTS / non-developmental items therefore require the same system-safety analysis as developmental items — the canonical example being a 110-VAC computer that is perfectly UL-compliant yet lethal if used beside a swimming pool.
- **Decommissioning is a hazard phase.** Disposition surfaces hazardous materials (PCB capacitors/transformers, lead/nickel-cadmium/lithium batteries, beryllium heat sinks, CRT lead and mercury, lead in circuit boards, mercury switches, lead/cadmium paint, asbestos) governed by a thick stack of regulation — RCRA, CERCLA/Superfund, SARA, NEPA, TSCA, the Federal Facilities Compliance Act of 1992, CERFA, DOT and OSHA HAZCOM rules — where non-compliance can mean fines and personal penalties against federal officials.

## Mental Models
- **Hazards down to contributors, contributors up to requirements.** The productive direction of software safety analysis is to start from system hazards and failure modes, identify which are caused or influenced by software (including software that misleads an operator into a wrong decision), and convert each contributor into a design requirement to eliminate or control the risk. This is faster and adds engineering utility because most of it can be done early enough to shape preliminary design.
- **Control capability ≠ failure probability.** When you label a software contributor with a MIL-STD-882C category or a DO-178B level, you are ranking *how much autonomy the software has over the hazard*, not how likely the code is to be wrong. Autonomous software linked to catastrophic outcomes earns the most analysis and test; software touching low-severity risks earns the least.
- **The product is influence on the design, not a report.** The Handbook repeatedly insists the output of the safety effort is a *safer system entering production*, not a hazard-analysis document. The SSAR/SER and the tracking log are records of that influence, not the goal.
- **Same engine, three casings.** Software, test, and facilities each reuse the identical loop — identify, analyze causes, derive controls per the order of precedence (substitute → design out → safety devices → warning devices → administrative procedures/training), verify, and track to closure. Once you see the engine, the three chapters are one process applied to three domains.
- **The order of precedence is the default decision rule.** Whenever a control must be chosen, descend the precedence: try to eliminate the risk by substitution, then design it out, then add safety devices, then warning devices, and only then fall back to procedures and training.
- **Tailor effort to risk, everywhere.** Facility low/medium/high categories, software SHCM rankings, and DO-178B levels are all expressions of one rule: spend analysis and test resources in proportion to severity and control capability, with managing-activity approval needed to *reduce* coverage on higher-risk items.
- **Conformance is a floor, not a ceiling.** Meeting codes, standards, and laws is treated as the *minimum* system-safety requirement; the actual risk lives in the application, so hazard analysis (e.g., O&SHA) is still required to reach acceptable risk even for fully code-compliant, NRTL-listed, or COTS items.

## Anti-patterns
- **"Wait and see."** The Handbook recounts that in the 1970s, believing traditional methods no longer applied to software, much of the safety community defaulted to a wait-and-see posture and left useful tools undeveloped — the lesson being that traditional methods proved *partially* effective and should have been adapted rather than abandoned.
- **Specification by omission.** Software developed "correctly" against its specification can still be wrong from a systems perspective; omitted, misunderstood, or incorrect specifications are named as probably the single largest cause of software failures.
- **Oversimplifying the control-category lists to nothing.** The Handbook warns that developers often want the control-capability list as short and simple as possible; the danger is losing the very purpose of the activity — categorizing software causal factors to drive risk likelihood and the depth of design, code, and test needed to mitigate them.
- **Treating an NRTL listing (or COTS provenance) as proof of safety.** Relying on a single UL standard, or assuming COTS-NDI is OSHA-compliant, in place of a real workplace hazard analysis is called out explicitly as inappropriate.
- **Testing with the wrong people.** A system whose safe operation depends on trained personnel should not be tested without appropriately trained personnel; using design engineers in the operational environment yields only limited validation data, and a sound OT&E program trains operators in normal, support, and emergency procedures including the residual hazards (high voltage, RF energy, hot surfaces, toxic materials).

## Key Takeaways
1. **Software safety is a systems-engineering problem.** It is solved within good SE principles by a multi-discipline team, traced from hazards to software contributors to requirements to code, and verified preferably by test — not treated as a programming specialty.
2. **Use the five-step software safety process** (plan/manage → assign criticality → derive requirements → design & analyze → test), grounded in the JSSSC Software System Safety Handbook and DO-178B, governed by a Software Safety Working Group, and closed out in a System Safety Assessment Report.
3. **Rank software by control capability, not failure probability.** Tailor the MIL-STD-882C categories or DO-178B levels and combine them with severity in the Software Hazard Criticality Matrix to decide how much analysis and test each contributor warrants.
4. **Testing is both evidence and hazard.** Harvest safety data from all testing (define your data needs early), and analyze the safety *of the test itself*; verify safety requirements through test where practical, knowing 100% coverage is usually unattainable and IV&V applies to high-risk software.
5. **A facility is a major subsystem** managed across the Facility Acquisition Life Cycle (site selection → new → existing/re-engineering → decommissioning) under FAA Order 3900.19 and MIL-STD-882, with effort scaled to its low/medium/high risk category.
6. **Drive facility risk through the ORMG and the analytical toolkit** — Change Analysis, PHL, PHA, O&SHA, JSA, and the requirements cross-check — feeding a Safety Engineering Report and a hazard tracking log that records validation, verification, and one of the three closure forms.
7. **Conformance is the floor.** Codes, NRTL listings, and COTS provenance never substitute for hazard analysis; risk lives in the application, and controls follow the order of precedence to an acceptable, documented residual risk.
8. **Plan for the end.** Decommissioning is a distinct hazard phase dominated by hazardous-material identification and a heavy regulatory stack with personal liability for non-compliance.
