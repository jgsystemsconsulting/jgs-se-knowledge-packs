# Chapter — Operational Risk Management and Human/Organizational Safety Factors

> Source: FAA System Safety Handbook (December 30, 2000), Chapter 15 (Operational Risk Management), Chapter 16 (Operational Safety in Aviation), and the opening of Chapter 17 (Human Factors Engineering and Safety). This is a public-domain U.S. Government work; cite the FAA original, not any third-party reprint or commentary, which is not public domain.

## Core Idea

Earlier chapters of the handbook treat safety as something engineered into a system before it is fielded. This chapter pivots to the other end of the lifecycle: managing risk while the system is actually being operated, by the people who operate it, in the conditions they face on a given day. The vehicle for that is **Operational Risk Management (ORM)** — a structured, repeatable decision-making method meant to be used during operational use rather than during development. Where an Operational and Support Hazard Analysis is performed as the system is built, ORM is run in the field — the handbook's own example is performing one before each flight.

The framing is deliberately pre-emptive rather than reactive. The handbook argues that waiting for an accident and only then working out how to stop a recurrence is both irresponsible and wasteful. Risk, in this view, is unavoidable in every FAA operation and even in ordinary daily life, so the point is not to chase zero risk but to balance risk against benefit and pick the most effective course of action. ORM formalizes that everyday balancing into a discipline that does not depend on any one person's experience or instinct.

The second half of the material widens the lens from the individual decision to the organization. The 5-M model treats the operation as a system of interacting elements — people, hardware, environment, the operation itself, and management — and locates the human as the area of greatest variability. The operational-safety and human-factors material that follows reinforces the same message at organizational scale: voluntary, non-punitive data sharing, a positive safety culture, and human factors integrated from the earliest project phases are what actually move the risk needle over time.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Operational Risk Management (ORM)** — the chapter's central method. Introduced in §15.1 as a decision-making tool to systematically identify operational risks and benefits and choose the best course of action; characterized as a six-step process applied during operational use, in contrast to development-phase analyses.

- **The four ORM Principles** (§15.2) — governing axioms applied before, during, and after every task by people at all levels: *Accept No Unnecessary Risk*, *Make Risk Decisions at the Appropriate Level*, *Accept Risk When Benefits Outweigh the Costs*, and *Integrate ORM into Planning at all Levels*.

- **The ORM 6-Step Process** (§15.3, Figure 15-2) — the operational loop: (1) Identify the Hazards, (2) Assess the Risks, (3) Analyze Risk Control Measures, (4) Make Control Decisions, (5) Implement Risk Controls, (6) Supervise and Review. Used as the spine for the rest of the chapter.

- **Types of Risk taxonomy** (§15.5.1, Figure 15-3) — a vocabulary distinguishing *identified*, *unidentified*, *total*, *acceptable*, *unacceptable*, and *residual* risk, where residual risk is what remains after management effort and comprises acceptable plus unidentified risk.

- **The 5-M Model** (§15.9, Figure 15-4) — a systems framework adapted from military ORM, with elements *Man*, *Machine*, *Media*, *Management*, and *Mission*. Introduced as the way to view the composite of hardware, procedures, and people as one system so its interactions can be analyzed. ("Man" denotes the human regardless of gender; "Mission" is the military word for what civil aviation calls the operation.)

- **Three Levels of Risk Management** (§15.10) — *Time-Critical* (an on-the-run mental or verbal review, usually unrecorded), *Deliberate* (the full process, most effective done as a group using experience and brainstorming), and *Strategic* (the deliberate process deepened with research, analysis tools, formal testing, and long-term tracking, reserved for high-priority or high-visibility risks).

- **The Primary Family of Hazard Identification Tools** (§15.11.1, Figure 15-4) — the seven primary tools named for Step 1: Operations Analysis, Preliminary Hazard Analysis, the "What-If" Tool, the Scenario Process Tool, the Logic Diagram, Change Analysis, and the Cause-and-Effect Tool. Strategic tools (accident analysis, cause-and-effect diagrams, multi-linear event sequence, interface analysis, failure mode and effect analysis, mapping, energy trace and barrier analysis) are named for deeper work.

- **Levels of User Involvement in Risk Controls** (§15.11.1, Figure 15-7) — a ladder from weakest to strongest engagement: Robot, Comment and Feedback, Coordination, Input, Team Member, Co-Ownership, and User Ownership. Used to gauge and raise the "involvement factor" behind a control.

- **Levels of Command Involvement** (Figure 15-8) — a companion ladder of leader-support actions ordered by strength, from signing directives up through sustained, consistent behavior, used to signal management backing for a control.

- **The Global Aviation Information Network (GAIN)** (§16.1) — an FAA-proposed, industry-owned international infrastructure for the voluntary collection, analysis, and sharing of safety information, with a Steering Committee, four Working Groups (A: Aviation Operator Safety Practices; B: Analytical Methods and Tools; C: Global Information Sharing Prototypes; D: Reducing Impediments), a Program Office, and a planned Government Support Team.

- **Flight Operations Quality Assurance (FOQA)** (§16.2) — a voluntary program for collecting, analyzing, and sharing routine flight-operation data derived from flight data recorders, paired with assurance that the data will not be used for punitive enforcement.

- **The Aircraft Performance Risk Assessment Model (ASPRAM)** (§16.3.1) — an FAA/NASA model that uses empirical recorder data and expert judgment, encoded as knowledge-based rules, to identify non-accident precursor events and quantify the risk of incidents and accidents.

- **The Operator's Flight Safety Handbook (OFSH)** (§16.4) — a GAIN Working Group A product, adapted from an Airbus handbook into a generic worldwide guide for standing up a flight-safety function inside an operator's organization, intended to be tailored to each operator.

- **The Heinrich Pyramid / accident ratio concept** (§16.0, Figure 16-1) — the classical observation that reported accidents sit atop a far larger base of incidents and unreported occurrences (Heinrich's roughly 1-in-300 ratio, later Frank Bird's roughly 1-in-600), motivating the reporting of near-misses.

## Key Concepts

**Risk defined as probability and severity.** The handbook treats risk as the combined probability and severity of an accident or loss arising from exposure to hazards — spanning both harm to people and loss of resources. A hazard, in its turn, is any actual or potential condition capable of producing degradation, injury, illness, death, or damage to equipment and property. Risk assessment (Step 2) brings quantitative and qualitative measures to bear so that probability and severity can be pinned down against the exposure of people or assets.

**The three components of every risk.** Step 3 rests on the claim that any risk breaks into three parts — how probable the occurrence is, how severe the hazard is, and how much people and equipment are exposed — and that an effective control has to cut or remove at least one of those three.

**The seven risk-control options.** Step 3's Action 1 names the menu of control types: rejection, avoidance, delay, transference, spreading, compensation, and reduction. Controls are generated starting from the highest-assessed risk and traced back to the listed causes.

**Standard order of precedence.** The ideal control is to plan or design for minimum risk; less preferred, in order, are adding safety devices, adding warning devices, then changing procedures and training. The handbook adds an operational caveat: this ordering makes sense while a system is still on the drawing board, but once fielded, redesign is often not cost-effective, so revising operational or support procedures is frequently the lowest-cost route — it does not remove the risk but can quickly cut the likelihood or severity.

**Residual risk and acceptability.** Acceptable risk is the portion of identified risk allowed to persist after controls; risk becomes acceptable when further reduction would degrade the chance of mission success or when diminishing returns set in. The handbook is explicit that some risk must always be accepted, that quantifying risk does not by itself ensure safety, and that risk is often a matter of perspective. How much is accepted is the prerogative of the defined decision authority — and that acceptance decision should be coordinated with affected personnel where practical and then documented so the reasoning survives.

**Root-cause focus.** Step 1's third action (List Causes) pushes the analyst toward the root cause — the first link in the chain leading to degradation, injury, death, or damage — because controls applied at the root are the ones that actually break the chain.

**Hazard identification as the foundation.** The chapter repeatedly calls hazard identification the foundation of the entire process and insists the steps run in sequence: until all hazards are identified, the remaining steps are not effective. Identification draws on task analysis across the 5-Ms, the named tool families, and a wide net of information sources — accident/incident reports, operational personnel, outside experts, current guidance, surveys, and inspections — with brainstorming led by a facilitator singled out as one of the best.

**Implementation is a people problem.** Step 5 reframes implementation around acceptance: make the directive clear (examples, pictures, job aids, a vision of the end state), establish accountability at the right level, and provide visible management support plus a feedback mechanism. The handbook's review of the historical record finds that many risk controls never reach their potential, and the primary cause of shortfalls is failing to involve the people the control actually affects.

**Supervise and Review as a closed loop.** Step 6 has three actions — Supervise, Review, Feedback — and turns on measurement. Because accident rates are too slow and too coarse to judge a control quickly, the handbook prescribes directly measuring the degree of risk in the system through direct measures of behavior, direct measures of conditions, measures of attitudes, measures of knowledge, and structured loss-control reviews. The seat-belt-use sample and the foreign-object-on-the-flight-line count are its worked examples.

**Voluntary, non-punitive data sharing as an organizational control.** Chapter 16 extends the logic from the single operation to the enterprise. GAIN and FOQA both rest on the premise that operators will only share routine safety data if reporting is "jeopardy-free" — protected from enforcement, litigation, and public-disclosure exposure. GAIN Working Group D exists specifically to reduce those impediments, including pursuing changes to ICAO Annexes and legislation that protects reporters.

**The total-system / human-factors handoff.** The Chapter 17 opening defines human factors as a multidisciplinary effort to match human capabilities and limitations to equipment, procedures, environments, training, and staffing, and stresses the Total System Concept: people tend to fixate on hardware and software and forget that the "user" varies in aptitude, training, and operating conditions. FAA Order 9550.8 is cited as directing that human factors be integrated into planning and execution at the earliest project phases — the same "front-load the risk work" principle ORM applies operationally.

## Mental Models

- **Risk versus benefit as a balance to be tilted, not eliminated.** Successful performance comes from shifting the cost-versus-benefit balance toward benefit by controlling risk — running ORM whenever anything changes so the accident chain never forms. The goal is the most effective course of action, not the safest conceivable one.

- **The 5-M system view.** Treat any operation as Man, Machine, and Media interacting under Management to produce a Mission. The human is the most variable element and the largest source of risk; management sets the rules governing the interactions but cannot fully control them (weather is not under management control, and individual decisions affect people more than policy does). When an operation fails, re-examine the inputs and interactions across all five M's — and note the chapter's citation that management processes figure in as many as 80 percent of reported accidents per the National Safety Council.

- **Match the rigor to the situation (Time-Critical / Deliberate / Strategic).** Don't apply strategic depth to a snap decision or a snap mindset to a complex new system. Pick the level by available time, stakes, and how well the hazards are understood.

- **User ownership beats compliance.** Move every control as far up the involvement ladder as possible — from "Robot" (ordered to comply) toward "User Ownership" (operators empowered to develop the control themselves). The people exposed to a risk usually know best what works, and controls they own survive; controls imposed on them tend to fail.

- **Measure the risk, not the body count.** Because accidents are rare and slow to accumulate, judging a control by accident rates wastes a year and a lot of effort. Sample behavior, conditions, attitudes, and knowledge directly to learn quickly whether a control is changing anything.

- **The near-miss iceberg.** Treat every reported accident as the visible tip of a much larger base of incidents and unreported occurrences. Reporting and investigating near-misses is how you learn before the fatal event, not after.

- **Document the decision so the reasoning outlives the people.** Careful documentation of each ORM step is what makes risk communication and the rationale behind risk decisions auditable later — especially when a manager chooses to accept risk, so that a future reviewer can reconstruct why.

## Anti-patterns (only if the source names them)

The source names specific failure modes for risk controls and risk management:

- **Indiscriminate application of controls.** Applying risk controls across an entire organization rather than only to the activities and people actually at risk — which wastes resources and needlessly irritates busy operational personnel.

- **The named shortfalls of risk controls.** The handbook catalogs why controls fall short of their potential: the control is a poor fit for the problem; the operators resent it; the managers resent it; sustaining it proves too expensive; competing priorities overwhelm it; people misunderstand it; and progress goes unmeasured until it is too late. Nearly all of these trace to a single root failure — neglecting to involve the people the control actually touches.

- **Culture-incompatible controls.** A rigid, centrally directed control imposed on a decentralized, flexible culture (or a decentralized control dropped into a top-down organization) will not take. The handbook directs that controls be developed to fit the organization's existing culture.

- **Treating the cookbook as the method.** Setting clear objectives and parameters beats a cookbook approach. In the same vein, the handbook insists that hazard analysis and risk assessment are not a substitute for sound judgment — they sharpen it — and that there are no "safety problems," only management problems that turn into accidents when left unresolved.

- **Interrupting hazard identification to chase one hazard.** Breaking off Step 1 to fixate on controlling a single hazard risks overlooking other, more important ones; the steps must be completed in sequence.

## Key Takeaways

1. ORM is a six-step, repeatable decision tool for the operational phase — Identify, Assess, Analyze, Decide, Implement, Supervise/Review — meant to be run pre-emptively, including before each flight, not pulled out after an accident.

2. Four principles govern it: accept no unnecessary risk, decide at the level that controls the resources, accept risk only when benefits outweigh costs, and integrate the process into planning at every level — because changes made late in planning or execution cost the most.

3. Risk decisions are about residual risk, not zero risk; the defined decision authority chooses how much to accept, coordinates that with affected personnel, and documents the rationale.

4. The 5-M model (Man, Machine, Media, Management, Mission) frames the operation as one interacting system in which the human is the most variable element and management is frequently the controlling factor in success or failure.

5. Controls have a standard order of precedence (design for minimum risk first), but for fielded systems, revising procedures and training is often the fastest, cheapest effective option.

6. Most control failures are people failures. User ownership and visible management support, gauged on the involvement and command-involvement ladders, are what make controls stick.

7. Judge controls by directly measuring risk, behavior, conditions, attitudes, and knowledge — not by waiting on accident statistics — and close the loop with feedback so lessons (and analysis errors) are captured and shared.

8. At the organizational scale, the same logic appears as voluntary, non-punitive, jeopardy-free data sharing (GAIN, FOQA, ASPRAM, OFSH) and as early-and-integrated human factors per FAA Order 9550.8 and the Total System Concept.

## Connects To

- **Earlier handbook chapters on hazard analysis (PHA, FMEA, O&SHA).** ORM reuses the handbook's analysis tools but applies them in the field; ORM is explicitly positioned as the operational counterpart to the development-phase O&SHA, and the named hazard-identification and strategic tools (Preliminary Hazard Analysis, failure mode and effect analysis, interface analysis, energy trace and barrier analysis) are drawn from the broader handbook toolkit.

- **Chapter 17, Human Factors Engineering and Safety.** The 5-M model's "Man" element and the chapter's framing of the human as the area of greatest variability hand directly to the human-factors discipline, the Total System Concept, and FAA Order 9550.8's mandate to integrate human factors at the earliest phases.

- **Chapter 16's operational-safety programs.** GAIN, FOQA, ASPRAM, and the OFSH operationalize ORM's "supervise, review, feedback" loop at industry scale, and the OFSH's listed elements (senior-management commitment, positive safety culture, immunity-based reporting, lessons-learned exchange, human-factors training, the SHEL model, Crew Resource Management) prefigure the safety-management-system thinking that later FAA and ICAO guidance formalizes.

- **Safety Management Systems and Just Culture (later FAA/ICAO doctrine).** The chapter's insistence on non-punitive, jeopardy-free reporting, documented risk acceptance, and culture-fit controls is an early articulation of the just-culture and SMS principles that became standard in subsequent regulatory frameworks.

- **Systems-engineering risk and decision processes generally.** ORM's identify-assess-control-monitor loop, its residual-risk vocabulary, and its cost-versus-benefit framing align with risk-management processes across the SE canon, while the 5-M systems view and root-cause emphasis connect to systems thinking and accident-causation modeling more broadly.
