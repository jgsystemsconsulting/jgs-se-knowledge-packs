# GAO Technology Readiness Assessment Guide Patterns & Techniques

Reusable patterns from the GAO TRA Guide (GAO-20-48G). Each has When / How / Trade-offs.

## Pattern 1: Identify CTEs from the WBS and Requirements

**When to use**: at the start of any TRA, before assigning a single TRL.

**How**: walk the work breakdown structure and the system's performance requirements. For each technology, apply the CTE test: is it new/novel (or used in a new/novel way) **and** needed to meet operational requirements within cost and schedule? Keep the ones that pass both; document why you screened the rest out. Have an independent reviewer hunt specifically for *missing* CTEs.

**Trade-offs**: thorough CTE identification takes time and domain expertise — but a missed CTE means unassessed risk that surfaces late, and an over-stuffed list buries the genuinely critical few. Bias review toward catching omissions.

---

## Pattern 2: Assign a Defensible TRL (Evidence First)

**When to use**: when rating each CTE's maturity (step 3).

**How**: assemble the evidence — what was demonstrated, in what environment, against what criteria — *then* let the TRL fall out of it. Name the environment explicitly (laboratory / relevant / operational) and confirm the demonstration actually matched the claimed fidelity. Record assumptions and gaps. Never pick the TRL first and back-fill evidence.

**Trade-offs**: evidence-first assessment is slower and sometimes yields a lower, unwelcome number — but a number-first rating collapses under independent scrutiny exactly when it matters (defensibility).

---

## Pattern 3: Run an Independent, Credible Assessment

**When to use**: when forming the assessment team (step 1).

**How**: staff the TRA with qualified experts who are **independent** of the developers whose technology is being rated. Define scope, criteria, environments, and methodology in the TRA plan up front. Capture dissent. Independence and a clear plan are what make the result credible and defensible later.

**Trade-offs**: independent assessors cost more and may take longer than letting the developer self-assess — but self-assessment defeats credibility, and a non-credible TRA gives false confidence.

---

## Pattern 4: Build a Technology Maturation Plan for Immature CTEs

**When to use**: whenever a CTE is below the TRL required for the next decision.

**How**: for each immature CTE, document current TRL, target TRL and the decision it supports, the maturation activities/tests, resources and funding, a realistic schedule, the risks, and **off-ramps** (alternative technology, design change, descope). Track the TMP's milestones as a risk burn-down. Size the plan by the TRL gap.

**Trade-offs**: a resourced TMP commits time and money up front — but "hope" is not a plan, and a critical technology with no maturation plan and no off-ramp is how immaturity becomes a cost/schedule crisis.

---

## Pattern 5: Judge an Existing TRA Against the Four Characteristics

**When to use**: when you must decide how much to trust a TRA someone else produced.

**How**: test it against the four characteristics. Comprehensive — are *all* CTEs covered against full criteria and the right environment? Well-documented — can you trace and repeat each rating from the report? Credible — were the assessors qualified and independent, the evidence sufficient? Defensible — would the conclusions survive a hostile independent review? A "no" on any one discounts the whole.

**Trade-offs**: a rigorous critique takes effort and may force a re-do — but relying on an unreliable TRA means making a major commitment on maturity the program does not actually have.

---

## Pattern 6: Set Maturity Thresholds at Milestones

**When to use**: when defining entry criteria for an acquisition phase or review.

**How**: tie required TRLs to decisions — e.g., critical technologies demonstrated in a **relevant** environment (TRL 6) by the preliminary design point, and in an **operational** environment (TRL 7) before committing to product development. Use these as both the go/no-go test and the target a TMP must reach by the milestone.

**Trade-offs**: holding firm to a TRL threshold can delay a milestone — but committing with immature critical technology reliably costs more later in overruns and slips. The honest delay is cheaper than the hidden risk.
