# Chapter 6: Technology Maturation Plans

## Core Idea
A TRA that finds an immature Critical Technology Element is not the end — it is the trigger for a **Technology Maturation Plan (TMP)**. The TMP lays out the steps, resources, schedule, and level of effort required to raise an immature CTE to the TRL needed for the upcoming decision, plus the alternatives or off-ramps if it cannot mature in time. The TMP turns a maturity finding into managed action.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Technology Maturation Plan (TMP)** — the program's documented response to immature CTEs: how each will be matured to the required TRL, by when, with what resources.
  - When to use: whenever a CTE is below the TRL required for the next milestone.
- **Required-TRL-by-decision** — the maturity threshold a CTE must reach before a given commitment (e.g., demonstrate in a relevant environment before preliminary design; in an operational environment before product development).
  - When to use: to set the *target* a TMP must reach and the deadline it must meet.
- **Off-ramps / alternatives** — fallback options (alternative technology, design change, descope) if a CTE cannot mature on the program's timeline.
  - When to use: as part of every TMP for a high-risk CTE, so the program is not held hostage to one immature technology.

## Key Concepts
- **What a TMP contains**: for each immature CTE — the current TRL, the target TRL and the decision it supports, the specific maturation activities and tests, the resources and funding, the schedule with milestones, the risks, and the alternatives/off-ramps.
- **Gap-driven**: a TMP is sized by the gap between current TRL and required TRL — a CTE one level short needs a lighter plan than one several levels short.
- **Linked to risk management**: immature CTEs are program risks; the TMP is the mitigation, and its milestones are tracked like any other risk burn-down.
- **Realistic schedules**: maturing technology takes time and money that optimistic schedules routinely underestimate; a TMP that assumes maturation "will happen" without resourced activities is not a plan.
- **Decision coupling**: a TMP is only meaningful relative to a decision — "mature to TRL 7 before committing to product development" — not as open-ended R&D.

## Mental Models
- A TMP converts a red light into a route: the TRA says "not ready"; the TMP says "here is exactly how, by when, and at what cost we get ready — and here is what we do if we can't."
- Always carry an off-ramp for a critical, immature technology: betting the program on a single technology maturing on schedule, with no alternative, is how immaturity becomes a cost/schedule crisis.
- The TMP's deadline is set by the decision, not the lab: maturation that finishes after the milestone it was meant to support has failed, however good the technology eventually becomes.

## Anti-patterns
- **Hope as a plan**: assuming an immature CTE will mature without resourced, scheduled activities.
- **No off-ramp**: committing to an immature technology with no alternative if it slips.
- **Decoupled maturation**: open-ended technology development not tied to the TRL needed by a specific decision.
- **Optimistic schedules**: underestimating the time and money to cross the lab→relevant→operational environment gaps.

## Key Takeaways
1. An immature CTE triggers a **Technology Maturation Plan**.
2. A TMP specifies activities, resources, schedule, and **target TRL tied to a decision** — plus alternatives/off-ramps.
3. The plan is **sized by the gap** between current and required TRL.
4. A TMP is a **risk mitigation**: its milestones are tracked like a risk burn-down.
5. Always carry an **off-ramp** for a critical, immature technology — never bet the program on one maturing on time.
6. The TMP's deadline is set by the **decision it supports**, not by the lab.
