# Chapter 7: Using TRAs in Decisions

## Core Idea
The point of a TRA is to inform **knowledge-based** acquisition decisions: commit to the next phase only when critical technologies have demonstrated the maturity that phase requires. Best practice ties specific TRL thresholds to milestones — demonstrate CTEs in a *relevant* environment before preliminary design, and in an *operational* environment before committing to product development. TRAs also serve oversight and audit, and they fail when they are calendar-driven, optimistic, or non-independent.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Knowledge-based acquisition** — make major investment commitments only when the requisite knowledge (including technology maturity) is in hand; each phase requires more demonstrated knowledge than the last.
  - When to use: as the decision philosophy a TRA serves.
- **TRL thresholds at milestones** — maturity gates: e.g., critical technologies at **TRL 6** (relevant environment) by the preliminary design point and **TRL 7** (operational environment) before committing to product development.
  - When to use: to decide whether maturity is sufficient to proceed, and to set TMP targets.
- **TRA in governance and oversight** — TRAs give oversight bodies and auditors an objective basis to approve, delay, or challenge commitments.
  - When to use: at milestone reviews and in independent audits of program risk.

## Key Concepts
- **Maturity before commitment**: the central best practice — entering a phase with immature critical technology pushes unmanaged risk into execution, where it surfaces as cost growth and schedule slip. GAO's body of program assessments documents this pattern repeatedly.
- **The TRL 6 / TRL 7 lines**: demonstrating CTEs in a relevant environment (TRL 6) supports preliminary design; demonstrating in an operational environment (TRL 7) supports the decision to commit to product development. These are the most consequential thresholds.
- **TRAs inform, decisions are made by leaders**: the TRA supplies evidence; the decision authority weighs it against cost, schedule, and mission need. A TRA does not "approve" anything.
- **Oversight value**: an independent, reliable TRA lets governance bodies and auditors see technology risk objectively, rather than relying on the program's own optimism.
- **Recurring pitfalls**: calendar-driven assessments (timed to a date rather than to evidence), optimistic ratings (environment inflation, self-assessment), and non-independent teams — each undermines the TRA's value exactly when it matters.

## Mental Models
- The decision question is not "what is the TRL?" but "is this TRL, on this evidence, enough maturity for *this* commitment?" Sufficiency is relative to the decision.
- Maturity is bought before the gate, not after: the cheapest place to discover and fix immaturity is before the commitment, while alternatives are still open.
- An optimistic TRA is worse than none: it converts unknown risk into false confidence, and the program acts on a maturity it does not have.

## Anti-patterns
- **Committing with immature CTEs**: starting product development before critical technologies reach the required TRL — the canonical cause of overruns.
- **Calendar-driven TRA**: producing a maturity rating to meet a date rather than because the evidence supports it.
- **Optimism by environment inflation or self-assessment**: ratings that overstate maturity and collapse under scrutiny.
- **Treating the TRA as the decision**: outsourcing the go/no-go to a number instead of weighing maturity against cost, schedule, and need.

## Key Takeaways
1. TRAs exist to enable **knowledge-based acquisition** — commit only when the required maturity knowledge is in hand.
2. Best-practice thresholds: CTEs at **TRL 6 (relevant environment)** by preliminary design, **TRL 7 (operational environment)** before committing to product development.
3. **Sufficiency is relative to the decision** — the question is whether this maturity is enough for *this* commitment.
4. TRAs **inform** decisions and **support oversight/audit**; they do not make decisions.
5. **Maturity is bought before the gate** — the cheapest place to fix immaturity is before commitment.
6. The recurring failures are **calendar-driven, optimistic, and non-independent** assessments.
