# Chapter 3 — Technology Maturation and Risk Reduction (TMRR) Phase

*Source: the DoD M&Q BoK (Manufacturing and Quality Engineering Body of Knowledge), Chapter 3 — v3.0, dated July 2025 — from OUSD(R&E)'s office for Systems Engineering and Architecture (within Research and Engineering). Approved for public release; DOPSR Case # 25-T-2448. This chapter synthesizes the TMRR slice (roughly pp. 3-1 to 3-216); the BoK as a whole is large (~1,658 pp) and is meant to be consulted by phase rather than read straight through.*

## Core Idea

The Technology Maturation and Risk Reduction (TMRR) phase exists to drive technology, engineering, integration, and life-cycle-cost risk down far enough that a program can commit, with confidence, to a contract for Engineering and Manufacturing Development (EMD). The source frames TMRR as the bridge between concept selection (the Materiel Solution Analysis phase that precedes it) and the heavy development investment of EMD that follows. It runs a blend of activities — design and requirements trades, maturation and validation of capability requirements, finalization of affordability caps, and competitive prototyping — and culminates in a Preliminary Design Review (PDR) and source selection for EMD. A program may exit TMRR once an affordable increment of useful military capability has been identified, its technology and manufacturing processes have been demonstrated in a relevant environment and judged mature enough, manufacturing risks have been identified and assessed, and the increment can be developed for production within a short window.

For the manufacturing-and-quality (M&Q) practitioner, TMRR carries a sharpened version of the recurring theme that runs through every chapter of the BoK: M&Q's job is to *influence the design* before the design hardens. The source states this is critical because of how early design decisions lock in cost — it reports that studies show roughly 80 percent of a program's life-cycle cost is determined by the time the PDR is held, even though only a small fraction of cumulative spending has occurred by then. TMRR is therefore framed as the window of maximum leverage: the time when a program or contractor has the greatest opportunity to bend the life-cycle-cost curve. M&Q personnel exercise that leverage by sitting on the technical Integrated Product Team (IPT), feeding producibility, feasibility, and risk judgments into the program's documents and reviews — they do not have authority to sign contracts, but they submit M&Q inputs for consideration.

## Frameworks Introduced

The chapter is organized as twelve parallel "threads," lettered A through L, each a topic area built on the "5 Ms" (Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement), on Manufacturing Readiness Level criteria, and on DoD-unique functions not found in commercial industry (the acquisition, contracting, and surveillance systems). The threads, by letter: **A** the DoD Acquisition System, **B** Defense Contracting, and **C** Surveillance (the DoD-unique trio); then **D** Technology and the Industrial Base, **E** Design, **F** Cost and Funding, and **G** Materials Management; and finally **H** Process Capability/Control, **I** Quality Management, **J** the Manufacturing Workforce, **K** Facilities, and **L** Manufacturing Management/Control. Within each one the BoK lists its Activities, the Tasks that M&Q staff lead or support, and the available Tools and Resources. The named frameworks this slice introduces, and when/how to apply them:

- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) / DoDI 5000.02.** The governing policy. The BoK documents the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) path in detail but says its practices apply to the other AAF pathways as well. *Use:* place the program on the correct pathway, then tailor the MCA activities to fit.

- **Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) Rapid Prototyping.** Presented explicitly as an "advanced version of TMRR" with a hard constraint: a prototype must be fielded and demonstrated in an operational environment, leaving a residual operational capability, within five years of program start. *Use:* because the clock is short, the manufacturing processes for the final configuration must be substantially mature — the source recommends starting no lower than MRL 4 and reaching MRL 7 before the operational demonstration, with critical processes matured enough to support fielding. When this option is chosen the usual TMRR requirements may be truncated and a tailored program initiated, but M&Q must still support a Manufacturing Readiness Assessment and a Production Readiness Review before production.

- **Analysis of Alternatives (AoA), revisited.** Carried forward from MSA, the AoA is the analytical comparison, across alternatives that could meet the Initial Capabilities Document need, of their operational effectiveness, their suitability, and their life-cycle cost. It has three products — AoA Study Guidance (developed/approved by the Director of Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation, DCAPE), the AoA Study Plan (the road map for conduct), and the AoA Final Report — and the source notes the AoA should be updated in each phase. *Use:* M&Q analyzes each alternative for industrial and manufacturing impact, using prior Industrial Base Assessments to judge whether a solution can be produced with existing capability while meeting quality, rate, cost, and schedule.

- **JCIDS and the Capability Development Document (CDD).** The Joint Capabilities Integration and Development System supports the Joint Requirements Oversight Council's validation of joint warfighting requirements. The CDD specifies the system's operational requirements as attributes with thresholds and objectives. *Use:* M&Q has a limited but specific role — supporting CDD sections on KPP/KSA/APA development, technology readiness, and program affordability, and supplying manufacturing-feasibility and capability inputs.

- **Acquisition Strategy and Systems Engineering Plan (SEP).** The Acquisition Strategy is the comprehensive, integrated plan describing the business, technical, and support strategies; the SEP implements the technical and engineering aspects of it, and the M&Q strategies are a subset of the Acquisition Strategy. *Use:* M&Q embeds a Manufacturing Plan and Quality Plan in the SEP, supplies an updated industrial-base capability analysis required by DoDI 5000.02, and develops M&Q exit-criteria metrics — including planned MRL targets for system, subsystems, components, and items — for the phase decision points. Outputs of the SRR, SFR, and PDR drive substantive SEP updates; a final revision is required before the Development RFP Release Decision and Milestone B.

- **Sixteen Systems Engineering processes.** The source states SE comprises sixteen processes — eight technical management processes and eight technical processes — providing a structured way to raise a system's technical maturity while balancing mission performance against cost, schedule, risk, and design constraints. *Use:* M&Q participates across the processes; manufacturability is described as an important consideration early within them.

- **Integrated Product Team (IPT) structure.** Three types are named: the Overarching IPT (OIPT, strategic guidance and issue resolution), the Working-level IPT (WIPT, identifying and resolving program issues and finding improvement opportunities), and the Program-level IPT (PIPT, program execution, possibly including government and industry after award). A best practice is to stand up a Cyber IPT or working group early. *Use:* M&Q must participate in all program IPTs; the source flags that major programs run a core design team of roughly 20–50 contractor engineers that makes 90–95 percent of critical decisions, most before production — so M&Q must be a primary concern of that team, not a late add-on.

- **Digital engineering and the digital thread.** The source describes the digital thread feeding requirements analysis, architecture, design optimization, integration, cost estimating, test, and sustainment. It introduces Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) for end-to-end design-to-delivery data, the Technical Data Package (TDP) as the digital technical description of an item, and Product Manufacturing Information (PMI) — non-geometric attributes embedded in the 3D CAD model such as bill of materials, geometric dimensioning and tolerancing (GD&T), surface finish, weld symbols, and material specs. *Use:* M&Q performs manufacturing data analysis on digital artifacts (producibility analysis, manufacturability analysis, factory layout and flow, capacity and line balancing, quality control). The DE Body of Knowledge (DEBoK) and Industry 4.0 are referenced as the broader context.

- **Technical reviews and audits: SRR, SFR, PDR.** TMRR runs a sequence of event-driven (not calendar-driven) reviews whose entrance criteria are documented in the SEP. The System Requirements Review (SRR) checks that performance requirements are defined and bidirectionally traceable to the draft CDD and that verification methods and risk mitigations exist. The System Functional Review (SFR) confirms a balanced, technically achievable functional baseline. The PDR establishes the *allocated baseline* and assesses producibility, manufacturing cost, and manufacturing risk; M&Q feeds it MRL-assessment results and entry/exit criteria per IEEE 15288. *Use:* M&Q supplies inputs at each review to confirm the manufacturing system can meet effectiveness and suitability within budget and schedule.

- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA) and the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM).** Per 10 USC 2448b, ITRAs are conducted for all Major Defense Acquisition Programs before Milestones A, B, and C and before a Full-Rate Production decision; OUSD(R&E) policy directs they follow DTRAM. The DTRAM focus areas span mission capability and technology; development and integration of the system; the Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA); software; cybersecurity and security; manufacturing; and reliability/availability/maintainability with sustainment. *Use:* the ITRA leverages ongoing program activities — TRAs, MRAs, SE technical reviews — but renders an *independent* judgment, so its result may not correlate directly with external measures such as Technology Readiness Levels.

- **Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA) and Critical Technology Elements (CTEs).** A metrics-based evaluation that identifies Critical (Enabling) Technology Elements, assesses schedule realism and cost/performance risk, reviews the WBS and technology roadmaps for redundancy and integration impacts, and assesses maturity-growth plans for the next phase. *Use:* M&Q supports the program TRA and the parallel MRL assessment; together they identify any critical technologies or manufacturing processes not yet demonstrated in a relevant environment.

- **Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs) and the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment (MRA).** MRL criteria and assessments are the BoK's named best practice for identifying and mitigating M&Q risk across the life cycle. *Use:* M&Q conducts MRL assessments to support the PDR and the Milestone B decision, applying system-level criteria at the level appropriate to the phase and separate criteria for subsystems, items, and components.

- **Industry standards stack.** Repeatedly specified: SAE AS6500 (Manufacturing Management Program), ISO 9001 and AS9100 (Quality Management Systems), AS9103 (Variation Management of Key Characteristics), AS9145 (APQP/PPAP), and the IEEE 15288 family (.1 Application of SE on Defense Programs, .2 Technical Reviews and Audits). *Use:* named in the Acquisition Strategy as the contractor's required Manufacturing Management System (MMS) and Quality Management System (QMS); where standards are not specified, the program develops program-specific Manufacturing and Quality Management Plans instead.

- **Process Capability and Control framework.** Built on AS6500, which requires analyzing capability for all critical processes, using statistical tools to reduce variation, and computing capability indices. It defines Key Characteristics (KC), Key Manufacturing Processes (KMP, which create or affect a KC), and Critical Characteristics (CC, where variation could cause loss of life or catastrophic failure). Capability is measured with Cp/Cpk (established, in-control processes, predicting conformance) and performance with Pp/Ppk (how a process actually performed over time, using the whole-population standard deviation). *Use:* set Cpk goals for each KMP, run Statistical Process Control (e.g., X-bar and R charts) to confirm a process is both *capable* and *in control*, and stand up a Variability Reduction Program with Process Control Plans using FMEA, Six Sigma, and DMAIC. The source notes there is no single universal capability metric — KCs and CCs deserve the most focus.

- **Yield framework.** Three measures of uniformity: First Pass (throughput) yield, Rolling Throughput Yield (yield at each step), and Final Yield (parts passing inspection over total produced). *Use:* manufacturing risk assessments must surface gaps in M&Q processes as risks with yield impact and plan the time and resources to mature them.

- **Quality Management framework.** Quality is the degree to which a product's attributes and characteristics satisfy a need; quality management coordinates quality policy, objectives, planning, assurance, and improvement within a Quality Management System (QMS), with quality assurance being the part focused on giving confidence that requirements will be met. *Use:* most DoD programs require contractors to run a basic QMS (ISO 9001 or AS9100); requirements stem from FAR Part 46 / 52.246 and DFARS. M&Q audits the contractor's QMS, performs Measurement System Analysis (MSA), and updates the program Quality Strategy for EMD entry after the PDR.

- **Advanced Product Quality Planning (APQP), Lean, and the eight dimensions of quality.** APQP (AS9145, with PPAP) is a structured product/process design framework running five phases: first plan and define; then develop the product design; then develop the process design; then validate both product and process; and finally gather production feedback. Lean manufacturing targets the seven wastes — transportation, inventory, motion, waiting, overproduction, over-processing, and defects (TIMWOOD) — with an eighth (wasted human potential) sometimes added. The Quality Strategy is tied to the classic dimensions of quality — a product's performance and features, its reliability and conformance, its durability and serviceability, and its aesthetics and perceived quality. *Use:* the contractor's quality strategy should link corporate goals to operations and tie quality metrics to KPPs, KSAs, Measures of Performance, and Technical Performance Measures.

## Key Concepts

- **The PDR as the cost-lock moment.** The single most distinctive idea in the TMRR chapter is that the PDR sits at the point where roughly 80 percent of life-cycle cost is already determined, even though little cumulative cost has been spent (the source attributes this to the DoD O&S Cost Management Guidebook). This is *why* M&Q influence has to land before the PDR, not after.

- **"Can it be built?" as the recurring feasibility test.** Manufacturing feasibility — whether a proposed solution can be produced with existing capability while meeting quality, rate, cost, and schedule — runs through the AoA, Acquisition Strategy, design, and process-capability threads as the central M&Q question.

- **The "relevant manufacturing environment."** A defining TMRR concept. Prototypes are built with *elements of production realism* present on the line — moving beyond the lab onto a shop floor where manpower, materials, machines, methods, measurement systems, facilities, and tooling are identified and used at least in a limited way. The demonstration must give the program confidence that cost, schedule, and performance targets are achievable. This is distinct from the later, fuller "production representative environment" targeted by CDR in EMD.

- **Allocated baseline.** The PDR establishes the allocated baseline (preliminary designs of hardware, software, and procedures under configuration management, satisfying the functional baseline with no major open or "To Be Determined" items), as the SFR before it established the functional baseline.

- **The Milestone B demonstration requirement.** Per the NDAA for FY 2017, Section 807 (referencing 10 USC 2366b), ahead of Milestone B approval, M&Q personnel must flag any manufacturing process that has *not* yet been shown to work in a relevant environment. Milestone B is the commitment to design for production, and it requires a final demonstration that technology, engineering, integration, manufacturing, sustainment, and cost risks have all been mitigated.

- **Influence, not authority.** Reiterated explicitly: M&Q cannot sign contracts; the contractor "follows the government's lead," so if the government shows concern for producibility early, the contractor mirrors that concern.

- **Process must be both capable and in control.** A process is *capable* when it produces parts meeting the drawing's specification and *in control* when it is statistically predictable; contractors are expected to drive processes to be both. SPC is the technique for confirming statistical control.

- **Key Characteristics, Key Manufacturing Processes, Critical Characteristics.** The vocabulary for variation management — KCs (variation significantly affects fit, form, function, performance, service life, or producibility), KMPs (processes that create or affect a KC), and CCs (variation could cause catastrophic failure or loss of life) — anchors process-capability and quality work throughout the phase.

- **Competition as cost lever and complexity source.** Competition is described as a major contributor to reducing weapon-system cost, but the source is candid that it can also add M&Q complexity and must be carefully planned, including data and data-rights strategy for dual sourcing.

## Mental Models

- **TMRR is a risk-burn-down run toward a financing decision.** Think of the phase as deliberately spending engineering effort to retire risk so that the Milestone B decision — committing real money to EMD — can be made with confidence. Everything M&Q produces is evidence that technology and manufacturing risk has been bought down to an acceptable level.

- **PDR is the leverage point; after it, the curve is set.** Because design decisions lock in ~80 percent of life-cycle cost by PDR, treat the pre-PDR window as the one chance to make a producible, affordable design cheap to build. M&Q effort delivered after the allocated baseline freezes is largely cleanup.

- **The factory-floor reality check.** The "relevant environment" is the mental discipline of refusing to believe a process is mature until it has been run with real production elements present — not in a pristine lab. If a critical process has not been demonstrated this way, it is, by the Section 807 rule, an unresolved Milestone B risk.

- **Two baselines, two reviews.** SFR locks the *functional* baseline (what the system must do); PDR locks the *allocated* baseline (how requirements are allocated to a preliminary design). Reading the review sequence this way clarifies what evidence M&Q owes at each gate.

- **Capability vs. performance indices.** Use Cp/Cpk to *predict* whether an established, in-control process will conform; use Pp/Ppk to *describe* how an ongoing process actually performed. Mixing them up confuses a forecast with a post-mortem.

- **Rapid Prototyping is TMRR with the clock turned up.** When a program takes the MTA Rapid Prototyping path, the same risk-reduction logic applies but the five-year fielding limit forces manufacturing maturity much earlier (MRL 4 rising toward MRL 7), collapsing the usual leisurely maturation timeline.

## Key Takeaways

- TMRR's purpose is to reduce technology, engineering, integration, and life-cycle-cost risk enough to contract for EMD with confidence; it ends at the PDR and EMD source selection.
- M&Q's role is to influence the design through the technical IPT before the allocated baseline freezes — roughly 80 percent of life-cycle cost is set by PDR.
- The phase is built on twelve threads (A–L) grounded in the 5 Ms, MRL criteria, and DoD-unique acquisition/contracting/surveillance functions.
- The "relevant manufacturing environment" — production realism on a shop floor — is the bar for judging process maturity in this phase, and Section 807 requires naming any process not demonstrated there before Milestone B.
- Process-capability work runs on AS6500: identify KCs/KMPs/CCs, set Cpk goals, run SPC to confirm processes are capable and in control, and reduce variation with FMEA/Six Sigma/DMAIC.
- The ITRA (per 10 USC 2448b, following DTRAM) provides an independent risk judgment across eight focus areas and may not match external maturity measures like TRLs.
- Quality work centers on auditing the contractor QMS (ISO 9001 / AS9100), performing Measurement System Analysis, and updating the Quality Strategy for EMD after the PDR.
- MTA Rapid Prototyping is a truncated, time-compressed TMRR demanding earlier manufacturing maturity (MRL 4 → MRL 7) and still requiring an MRA and PRR before production.

## Connects To

- **MSA phase (Chapter 2).** TMRR inherits the preferred concept, the initial M&Q strategies, and the AoA from MSA, and carries the same "Can it be built?" test and twelve-thread structure forward. The Milestone A risk-reduction decision authorizes entry into TMRR.
- **EMD phase (Chapter 4).** TMRR's PDR and risk demonstrations set up the Milestone B commitment to design for production; the relevant-environment process demonstrations of TMRR are deliberately maturing toward the production-representative environment targeted at CDR in EMD.
- **Production and Deployment (Chapter 5).** The MRL targets, process-capability indices, and Manufacturing Maturation Plans seeded in TMRR are the foundations that LRIP and Full-Rate Production later prove out.
- **Standards and policy.** Anchors to DoDI 5000.02 (the AAF) and 5000.85 (MCA), to 5000.88 (engineering of defense systems) and 5000.97 (digital engineering); to 10 USC 2366b / 2448b and NDAA FY2017 Sec. 807; and the AS6500 / ISO 9001 / AS9100 / AS9103 / AS9145 / IEEE 15288 stack.
- **Companion SE references.** The DoD Systems Engineering Guidebook (sixteen SE processes, technical reviews), the Engineering of Defense Systems Guidebook, the MRL Deskbook, MIL-HDBK-896 (Manufacturing Management Program Guide), the Digital Engineering Body of Knowledge, and the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM) all back the frameworks invoked in this phase.
