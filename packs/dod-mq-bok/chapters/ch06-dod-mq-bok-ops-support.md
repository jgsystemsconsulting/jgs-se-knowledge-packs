# Chapter — Operations and Support (O&S) Phase

*Source: the DoD M&Q BoK (Manufacturing and Quality Engineering Body of Knowledge), Chapter 6 — v3.0, dated July 2025 — from OUSD(R&E)'s office for Systems Engineering and Architecture (within Research and Engineering). Approved for public release; DOPSR Case # 25-T-2448. This chapter synthesizes the O&S slice (roughly pp. 6-1 to 6-234); the BoK as a whole is large (~1,658 pp) and is meant to be consulted by life-cycle phase rather than read straight through.*

## Core Idea

Operations and Support (O&S) is the phase where a fielded system is sustained in service and, eventually, retired. The source frames it around two distinct efforts: **Sustainment** of operational systems and **Disposal/Demilitarization** at end of useful life. The governing document is the **Life Cycle Sustainment Plan (LCSP)** / Product Support Strategy, prepared by the Program Manager (PM) and approved by the Milestone Decision Authority; it is the basis for every activity in the phase. A defining feature of O&S is that production work often shifts away from the prime contractor to government-owned-and-operated industrial sites — depots, arsenals, shipyards, and maintenance-repair-and-overhaul (MRO) facilities — though some sustainment still occurs at contractor plants.

The chapter is written from the manufacturing-and-quality (M&Q) practitioner's vantage point inside a Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) program. It is explicit that many O&S activities have a *logistics* center of gravity (supply, inventory, transportation, maintenance and repair), but it deliberately scopes itself to **program-office** work — managing system upgrades, modifications, spares production, and obsolescence — rather than logistics "shop floor" functions. The recurring engineering job is to keep fielded articles operating, to feed improvements and modifications into the production system with minimal disruption and cost, and to start disposal/demilitarization planning early enough to control cost, risk, and statutory compliance. A successful program, in the source's framing, meets sustainment performance requirements, stays affordable, and keeps pursuing cost reductions (e.g., should-cost management) across the whole phase.

A second structural point: O&S overlaps the Production and Deployment (P&D) phase for years. O&S activities begin when the first system is fielded, while production can continue long after Initial Operational Capability (IOC). The phase ends only when the system is demilitarized and disposed of.

## Frameworks Introduced

Like every M&Q BoK chapter, this one is organized as **twelve "threads" lettered A through L**, each a topic area drawn from the **"5 Ms" (Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement)**, from **Manufacturing Readiness Level (MRL)** criteria, and from DoD-unique functions not found in commercial industry. The threads, by letter: **A** the DoD Acquisition System, **B** Defense Contracting, and **C** Surveillance (the DoD-unique trio); then **D** Technology and the Industrial Base, **E** Design, **F** Cost and Funding, and **G** Materials Management; and finally **H** Process Capability/Control, **I** Quality Management, **J** the Manufacturing Workforce, **K** Facilities, and **L** Manufacturing Management/Control. Within each one the BoK lists its Activities (the gray boxes in the chapter figures), the Tasks that M&Q staff lead or support, and the available Tools and Resources.

Named frameworks, standards, and methods this slice introduces and tells you when/how to apply:

- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) / DoDI 5000.02.** The governing acquisition policy. The BoK documents the MCA path in detail but states the practices are tailorable to the other AAF pathways (Urgent Capability Acquisition under 5000.81, Middle Tier of Acquisition under 5000.80, etc.). It places every O&S activity in its milestone/decision-point context.

- **Life Cycle Sustainment Plan (LCSP) and the Integrated Product Support (IPS) Elements.** The LCSP is the principal sustainment-governing document after IOC; the source says programs update it whenever the product support strategy changes or at least every five years, whichever comes first. The backbone of the product support package is the **12 IPS Elements** (per the IPS Element Guidebook), which the BoK groups into three areas: *Life cycle management* (Product Support Management; Supply Support; Packaging, Handling, Storage, and Transportation; Maintenance Planning and Management); *Technical management* (Design Interface; Sustaining Engineering; Technical Data; Computer Resources); and *Infrastructure management* (Support Equipment; Training and Training Support; Manpower and Personnel; Facilities and Infrastructure).

- **Product Support Strategy and the twelve-step Product Support Model.** M&Q personnel support development of the Product Support Strategy and the resulting product support package, with the strategy described as performance-based and the basis for all sustainment efforts.

- **Independent Logistics Assessment (ILA).** A multi-disciplined product-and-process assessment of the in-service health of the fielded system. For Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAPs), ILAs occur before Milestones B and C and the Full-Rate Production decision; after IOC, DoD Components conduct them at a minimum interval of every five years. The Component Acquisition Executive (CAE) charters each one, and it is run by experts in logistics, program management, and business drawn from outside the program office; the source says M&Q experts should participate. ILAs must specifically assess O&S costs and adopt strategies to reduce cost growth.

- **In-Service Review (ISR).** Described as the major review of the O&S phase — a multidisciplined assessment that characterizes in-service technical and operational health of the deployed system and its enabling elements (training, manuals, documentation), producing risk, readiness, technical-status, and trend information in measurable form to substantiate in-service support budget priorities. ISRs typically recur ahead of each fiscal year's O&S budget-determination cycle.

- **Manufacturing Readiness Assessment (MRA) and MRLs.** Used as needed during O&S to support ongoing risk assessment; the MRA assesses the 12 threads, and the Interactive MRL Users Guide and MRL Deskbook (dodmrl.org) are the working tools.

- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA).** Required by 10 U.S.C. §2448b for MDAPs prior to Milestones A, B, and C and before an FRP decision. The slice stresses that an ITRA leverages ongoing activities (TRAs, MRAs, SE technical reviews) but renders an *independent* judgment, so its conclusions may not correlate directly with the raw readiness-level numbers.

- **AS6500, Manufacturing Management Program**, implemented via **MIL-HDBK-896, Manufacturing Management Program Guide.** The named industry best practice for manufacturing management; it supplies requirements for process-capability analysis, variability reduction, modeling and simulation, and the definitions of key/critical characteristics.

- **AS9100 / ISO 9001, Quality Management Systems.** The quality-system standards used to assess and structure the contractor's (or organic facility's) Quality Management System (QMS). The source is emphatic that compliance with a management-system standard does *not* by itself guarantee product or service quality.

- **DoD Risk, Issue, and Opportunity (RIO) Management Guide.** Supplies the proactive risk process reused for industrial-base, materials, and manufacturing risk throughout the phase.

- **DoD Systems Engineering process model (Table 6-1).** Sixteen processes — eight technical management (Technical Planning, Decision Analysis, Technical Assessment, Requirements Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, Technical Data Management, Interface Management) and eight technical (Stakeholder Requirements Definition, Requirements Analysis, Architecture Design, Implementation, Integration, Verification, Validation, Transition) — that M&Q personnel support, per the DoD Systems Engineering Guidebook.

- **National Technology and Industrial Base (NTIB) analysis (10 U.S.C. §4820).** Requires considering the NTIB — the people and organizations doing national-security and dual-use R&D, production, maintenance, and related work across the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia — in acquisition planning for each MDAP, including production rates/quantities, supply-chain risks, single points of failure, resilience, and surge/contraction capacity.

- **FAR/DFARS contracting machinery.** FAR Part 10 (Market Research), the **Contracting Cone** (the DAU graphic spanning FAR and non-FAR strategies), FAR 16.104 contract-type selection factors, FAR Subpart 16.4 (incentive contracts), FAR 15.101 best value, FAR 15.305 proposal evaluation, and Sections L (instructions to offerors) and M (evaluation criteria) of the RFP — guided by MIL-HDBK-245E for the Statement of Work. The **Early Manufacturing and Quality Guide, Appendix F** is named as the source of recommended contracting approaches for M&Q activities.

- **Supply Chain Operations Reference (SCOR) Model.** The source notes DoD supply-chain materiel management (DoDM 4140.01) uses the SCOR processes — Plan, Source, Make/Maintain, Deliver, and Return — as the framework for material-management activities.

- **Aerospace Industry Competency Model.** Cited as the basis for defining the M&Q workforce-requirement competencies (management, occupational, and sector competencies).

- **Material Management and Accounting System (MMAS)** (DFARS 252.242-7004 / Subpart 242.72) and the **counterfeit-avoidance standards** AS5553 (counterfeit electronic parts) and AS6174 (counterfeit materiel), plus SD-19 (Parts Management) and SD-22 (DMSMS).

## Key Concepts

**Two efforts, one phase: sustainment and disposal.** Sustainment deploys the product support package and monitors its performance against the LCSP — which may include time-phased transitions among commercial, organic, and partnered product support providers — and requires the program to acquire the intellectual-property (IP) deliverables, with their license rights, plus the tools, equipment, and facilities each level of maintenance needs — and to stand up organic depot maintenance capability as statute requires. Disposal/Demilitarization (DeMil) is the phase's terminal activity: the system is demilitarized and disposed of in accordance with all legal, regulatory, safety (including explosives safety), security, and environmental requirements, with demilitarization and controlled-inventory-item coding handled per DoDM 4160.28. The source repeatedly stresses starting demil planning with enough lead-time before the first asset retires to cut cost and risk.

**The production-to-spares transition is fragile.** A central O&S concept is that shifting from production to spares provisioning sharply reduces future spares-procurement opportunities if production facilities are reconfigured, materials change, or special-purpose tooling is removed. The source lists the conditions that drive up later spare-parts prices: smaller order quantities, earlier-configuration units needing special documentation, parts requiring special-purpose tooling, unique or scarce materials, lost production capability (out of business, discontinued facilities, no capacity), and special handling/packaging/shipping needs. This motivates the **"smart shutdown"** concept — using a Work Breakdown Structure framework to identify the tasks that stop Full-Rate Production and convert to a limited spares-production capability in an orderly way.

**Update Program Documentation and the CDRL/DID mechanism (Thread A).** M&Q personnel stay engaged in developing and updating the Acquisition Strategy (with its Product Support / Manufacturing / Quality Strategies), the SEP (with Manufacturing and Quality Plans), the TEMP, IMP/IMS, LCSP, CDD, RFP, and Source Selection Plan. The source enumerates the specific Data Item Descriptions to request — e.g., the Integrated Program Management Report (DI-MGMT-81861), the Integrated Master Schedule (DI-MGMT-81650), a Critical Manufacturing Process Description (DI-SESS-81012F), the Manufacturing Plan whose DID is DI-MGMT-81889A, a Manufacturing Risk Assessment Report under DI-SESS-81974, the Quality Program Plan (DI-QCIC-81722), and the SEMP (DI-SESS-81785A). These deliverables are the BoK's way of turning oversight intent into enforceable contract artifacts.

**The reviews that gate O&S budgets (Thread A.2 / Thread C).** The phase runs on a recurring cadence of formal reviews and audits: MRAs, TRAs, ITRAs, ISRs, ILAs, Industrial Capabilities Assessments, Follow-on Test and Evaluation (FOT&E), and even residual Full-Rate Production decisions. M&Q provides assessments into each — e.g., into the ILA across all 12 IPS elements plus ESOH, and into the ISR covering system-hazard risk, operational-readiness impacts of M&Q risk, and the effect of M&Q on reliability, maintainability, and operational readiness.

**The contracting thread re-runs for sustainment (Thread B).** Even in O&S, the program may re-compete sustainment work, so the full contracting sequence reappears: Market Research (strategic vs. tactical, per FAR Part 10), Contract Strategy (type, competition, incentives), Source Selection Plan, RFP, and Contract Evaluation and Award. The source devotes extensive detail to award-fee/incentive-fee criteria M&Q should propose — MRL progress against targets, Cpk improvements on key/critical processes, variability reduction, learning-curve progress, DMSMS mitigations, domestic-source qualification, and timely CDRL/DID delivery, each with metrics for partial achievement, recovery, and penalty. Sections L and M of the RFP get explicit M&Q content: MRL demonstrations against a target MRL, manufacturing planning (AS6500A management system, assembly-sequence and process flow, integrated build schedule, facilities/manpower), quality systems (assure quality, achieve stable/capable processes, prevent defects, drive root-cause/corrective action), and supplier management (selecting/managing key suppliers, integrating their activities, mitigating supplier risk, and preventing counterfeit-part intrusion).

**Surveillance continues even after work moves organic (Thread C).** Contract administration ensures the contractor performs to the agreement; DFARS Part 242.2/242.3 and PGI 242.3 define roughly seventy Contract Administration Service (CAS) functions, many requiring M&Q support. CAS may be delegated to DCMA via Memorandum of Agreement or Letter of Delegation (DCMA-INST 205, FAR 42.302(a)). The source is careful to note that when M&Q functions move to government depots/MROs where CAS surveillance may not be available, the oversight obligations in the FAR/DFARS still apply — and that if a contractor exits the business or is replaced, a pre-award survey is needed to re-establish capability, capacity, and financial stability.

**Industrial base risk is the dominant O&S program risk (Thread D).** The source states plainly that the potential loss of design or manufacturing capability at planned cost and schedule is the major program risk during O&S. Industrial Base Assessments (per 10 U.S.C. §4820 and DFARS Subpart 207.1) examine capability, capacity, and financial stability. When a capability is in danger of being lost, the program selects a government-action option from DoD Handbook 5000.60-H: take no action, buy from a foreign source, find/develop an alternative source, make a lifetime buy, execute a smart shutdown, or maintain the current capability. ManTech projects are the named mechanism for creating and maintaining competitive sources for critical items.

**Design work in O&S is "influence," not authorship (Thread E).** M&Q personnel are explicitly *not* design engineers; their role is supporting — assessing whether a design is manufacturable and inspectable/testable. The source's vivid framing: the existing factory floor is a "capability," so a design that cannot be produced on it either requires a design change to match the floor or new processes that yield uniform, defect-free, affordable product. Designs should be stable and mature before production, with changes limited to continuous improvement; all Key Characteristics should be stable and under control. O&S design activity centers on product improvement — procurement, installation, retrofit, modernization, upgrade, or rebuild that improves reliability/availability/maintainability, extends useful life, enhances safety, lowers maintenance cost, or adds capability.

**Cost and funding as a quantitative discipline (Thread F).** Life Cycle Cost Estimates (LCCEs) attempt to capture all costs from initiation through disposal and phase them for budget submission, feeding the PPBE process (POM and Budget Estimate Submission). The program WBS (tailored per MIL-STD-881) frames cost estimating and performance measurement. **Cost modeling** is described as a parametric estimating method based on cost drivers and cost-estimating relationships, used when there is insufficient actual-cost data. The **Integrated Program Management Data Analysis Report (IPMDAR)** measures contractor cost/schedule/technical performance and consists of the Contract Performance Dataset, the schedule (Schedule Performance Dataset plus the native Integrated Master Schedule), and the performance narrative. Cost estimating is repeatedly characterized as both a science and an art.

**Material vs. Materiel, and the material-science tetrahedron (Thread G).** The source draws an explicit distinction the O&S phase makes salient: *Material* management (acquisition's concern — materials/supplies used in manufacturing) versus *Materiel* management (the logistics/sustainment community's concern — supplies, equipment, and weapon systems supporting the military). Material characterization sits at the intersection of materials science and materials engineering and is depicted as a "material science tetrahedron" relating structure, properties, processing, and performance. Materials risk spans maturity/availability, long-lead and single/sole/foreign sourcing, DMSMS and obsolescence, counterfeit parts, and ESOH/HAZMAT constraints. The MMAS is assessed against benchmarks the source cites — e.g., a bill-of-materials accuracy target of 98% and a master-production-schedule accuracy target of 95%, with material costing meeting Cost Accounting Standards (48 CFR 9904.411-50(b)).

**Process capability and control (Thread H).** A **process capability study** measures inherent process variability against the contractual specification and lets an organization "qualify" a process for production. AS6500 requires analyzing capability for all critical processes, setting **Cpk goals**, feeding capability data back to designers, and managing the data to improve low yields. The thread defines **Key Characteristics (KC)** as features whose variation significantly affects fit, form, function, performance, service life, or producibility and therefore require specific control actions. **Yield** is a uniformity measure — First Pass (throughput) yield, Rolling Throughput Yield, and Final Yield — and a variability-reduction/yield-enhancement program uses Process Control Plans, FMEA on key/critical processes, and Six Sigma/DMAIC tools. Modeling and Simulation (M&S) lets manufacturers virtually test production/assembly/inventory/transportation before buying tooling or reserving capacity, reducing cost, scrap, and rework; Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) and digital-engineering advances (model-based definition, MBSE, Industry 4.0) extend this up and down the supply chain. **Demonstrating manufacturing maturity** means showing the 5 Ms are matured at the right time and place via process demonstrations that reflect design intent while remaining repeatable and affordable.

**Quality management (Thread I).** Quality is defined as the degree to which a product's attributes, performance features, and characteristics satisfy a need; the QMS is the part of the organization's management system focused on quality results, with quality assurance the part that gives confidence requirements will be met. During sustainment, a contractor or government remanufacturing facility (depot/MRO) should run an effective QMS, invoked through FAR 46.202-4 as a higher-level quality requirement on the contract. The contractor's plan for executing that work is its Quality Control Plan (QCP); the government's **Quality Surveillance Plan (QSP)** establishes how the government monitors and evaluates contractor performance. The source notes subcontractors make up 60–80% of material content on many programs, so prime control of supplier QMS/plans is essential, and it calls for counterfeit-avoidance training to AS5553.

**Workforce (Thread J), Facilities and Tooling (Thread K).** Workforce planning is framed as a supply/demand/gap discipline — Strategic Direction, Demand Analysis, Supply Analysis, Gap Analysis, and Solution Identification — built on competency models such as the Aerospace Industry Competency Model, with explicit attention to an aging M&Q workforce and chronic skills shortages. Facilities management covers all permanent/semi-permanent real property over the life cycle, with quantitative analysis (size, workstations, layout, floor space) and qualitative analysis (process types). The three classes of special equipment — special tooling (ST), special test equipment (STE), and the special inspection equipment (SIE) used to verify product — are flagged as significant cost/schedule drivers and quality influencers (the source cites the B-1 program's tooling exceeding $1 billion and multi-year lead times), favoring condition-based or total productive maintenance and pointing again at smart shutdown as rates and budgets decline.

**Manufacturing management and industrial cybersecurity (Thread L).** Manufacturing management orchestrates manufacturing planning, cost estimating/scheduling, engineering, fabrication/assembly, installation/checkout, demonstration/testing, product assurance, and shipment — best accomplished by putting AS6500 (with MIL-HDBK-896) on contract. A Manufacturing Strategy is part of the SEP and Acquisition Strategy and may use co-production and competition to reduce cost and risk. **Industrial cybersecurity** is treated as a first-class risk: the convergence of Information Technology (IT) and Operational Technology (OT) improves productivity but exposes industrial control systems (ICS)/OT to nation-states, criminals, and insiders who can compromise data integrity, ransom or damage machinery, or injure workers. Controls draw on DFARS 252.204-7012, NIST SP 800-37 (Risk Management Framework), 800-82 (ICS Security), and 800-171 (CUI), extended through the supply chain, with workforce cyber training for the production environment. Manufacturing risk is defined as anything with potential to harm cost, schedule, or performance, assessed continuously through PRRs, MRAs, MFAs, PCRs, and ITRAs.

## Mental Models

- **The factory floor is a capability, not a given.** A design that the existing floor cannot build forces a choice — change the design to fit the floor, or develop new processes that yield uniform, defect-free, affordable product. O&S decisions about upgrades and spares all hinge on this constraint.

- **Material is not Materiel.** O&S is where the two communities collide: acquisition manages the materials that go into manufacturing; logistics manages the supplies, equipment, and systems that support the warfighter. Keeping the distinction sharp prevents talking past sustainment counterparts.

- **Spares are produced under different economics than the original run.** Once production winds down, the cost structure inverts — smaller quantities, special documentation and tooling, scarce materials, and lost capability all push later spares prices well above the original procurement. The cure is to plan the spares posture (and any lifetime buys) *before* the capability disappears.

- **Smart shutdown is an engineered transition, not an event.** Stopping Full-Rate Production and converting to limited spares production is itself a WBS-managed task set — the source treats the wind-down as something to design, not let happen by default.

- **Loss of the source is the worst-case O&S risk.** The explicit framing — potential loss of design or manufacturing capability at planned cost and schedule is the major O&S program risk — orients industrial-base, materials, and facilities work toward keeping a source of supply alive.

- **Readiness is a number; judgment is separate.** MRLs and TRLs give defensible scales, but the ITRA exists precisely because an independent team may reach different conclusions than the readiness numbers suggest. Treat the level as evidence, not the verdict.

- **A standard is a basis, not a guarantee.** Complying with AS6500, ISO 9001, or AS9100 only sets up a capable process system; the source warns that implementing a standard is of little use if, say, the financial system rewards delivering nonconforming product — all management systems must pull toward the same goals.

- **M&Q influences, it does not command.** M&Q staff supply inputs to documents and reviews owned by others (they cannot sign contracts), so their leverage comes from being embedded early on the technical IPT and from translating manufacturing reality into the contractual and review artifacts decision-makers act on.

## Anti-patterns

The source names several recurring O&S failure modes:

- **The "identical facility" and "same-site" fallacies.** The M&S discussion explicitly cautions against two false assumptions: that identical production facilities will experience identical problems, and that a line that ran smoothly in one location will run smoothly after a move — variability from disassembly, relocation, and reassembly occurs even with the same workforce. This is especially acute in O&S as work migrates from prime plants to depots/MROs.

- **Letting production capability lapse before securing spares.** The source warns that if extended production runs did not build a spares inventory, parts produced later can cost significantly more — and lists the specific drivers (small quantities, special documentation/tooling, scarce materials, lost capability, special handling). Treating the production-to-spares transition casually is a named cost trap.

- **Standard-compliance as a stand-in for quality.** The BoK states that compliance with AS6500, ISO 9001, or AS9100 does not guarantee product or service quality, and that a standard is worthless if other management systems (e.g., financial incentives that reward nonconforming delivery) create conflicting or dysfunctional behavior.

- **Deferring demilitarization and disposal planning.** The source repeatedly insists demil planning (including controlled-inventory-item coding per DoDM 4160.28) start with sufficient lead-time before the first asset retires; doing it late raises cost, risk, and the chance of statutory/regulatory non-compliance.

## Key Takeaways

- O&S sustains the fielded system and ultimately retires it; the LCSP (updated when the support strategy changes or at least every five years) governs the phase, and work frequently migrates from prime contractors to government depots, arsenals, shipyards, and MROs.
- The chapter is organized as twelve M&Q threads (A–L) spanning acquisition, contracting, surveillance, industrial base, design, cost, materials, process control, quality, workforce, facilities, and manufacturing management.
- The defining reviews are the In-Service Review (the major O&S review) and the Independent Logistics Assessment (every five years post-IOC), supported by MRAs, ITRAs, FOT&E, and Industrial Capabilities Assessments; these substantiate in-service support budgets.
- The product support package is built on the 12 Integrated Product Support elements, grouped into life-cycle, technical, and infrastructure management; M&Q feeds assessments into each element.
- The major O&S program risk is loss of design or manufacturing capability at planned cost and schedule — driving industrial-base assessment (NTIB per 10 U.S.C. §4820), ManTech investment, DMSMS/counterfeit management, and the smart-shutdown / lifetime-buy decision set in DoD Handbook 5000.60-H.
- Core standards remain AS6500 (manufacturing management via MIL-HDBK-896) and AS9100/ISO 9001 (quality systems), with process capability held via Cpk goals, KC control, yield measures, and Six Sigma/DMAIC variability reduction.
- O&S re-runs the contracting machinery for sustainment (Market Research, Contract Strategy, SSP, RFP Sections L/M, Award/Incentive Fee), and continues DCMA-delegated surveillance even where work has moved organic.
- Industrial cybersecurity (DFARS 252.204-7012, NIST 800-37/800-82/800-171) is a first-class production risk as IT and OT converge across the supply chain.
