# Chapter — Pre-Materiel Development Decision (Pre-MDD) Phase

> **Source authority note:** This chapter synthesizes Chapter 1 of the DoD M&Q BoK (the Manufacturing and Quality Engineering Body of Knowledge), v3.0 dated July 2025, issued by OUSD(R&E) Systems Engineering and Architecture. The BoK is a compilation of best practices and lessons learned; it does not supersede DoD policy, guidance, or law. The 2025 revision was rewritten to align with the Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) pathways, and the chapter follows the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) path while noting that the practices apply to the other AAF pathways as well.

## Core Idea

Pre-MDD is the front end of the defense acquisition process — the work that happens *before* a program formally exists. Its purpose is to reach a clear understanding of user needs, surface a range of technically feasible materiel solutions, consider near-term interim responses, and lay out a resourced plan for the next phase. All of this knowledge feeds the Materiel Development Decision (MDD): the Milestone Decision Authority's (MDA) call on whether to let the effort enter the formal acquisition life cycle and chase a materiel solution.

The chapter's central argument is that manufacturing and quality (M&Q) considerations must begin *this early* — before the Analysis of Alternatives (AoA), during concept development — rather than being deferred until a design exists. The reasoning is leverage: design decisions made at the front end drive the bulk of downstream life-cycle cost, so the cheapest moment to influence producibility, reliability, and manufacturing risk is when the solution space is still being shaped. A key Pre-MDD job is narrowing the field of candidate solutions to a reasonable set for the AoA, while keeping that set broad enough to avoid prematurely discarding viable options. The BoK cites a Government Accountability Office finding that programs examining a broad range of alternatives tended to fare better on cost and schedule than those that looked narrowly.

M&Q practitioners in this phase work almost entirely in a *supporting* role on the technical Integrated Product Team (IPT). They do not own contracts, requirements, or milestone decisions, but they inject manufacturing-feasibility and quality-risk judgment into the documents and reviews that shape those decisions — chiefly the draft Initial Capabilities Document (ICD), the AoA Study Guidance, and the inputs to the MDD.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)** — introduced in the BoK introduction as the structure (implemented by DoDI 5000.02) within which the chapter operates. The BoK describes M&Q activities along the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) path but states the practices are tailorable to the other pathways, including Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA), Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) rapid prototyping/fielding, and others.
- **Twelve M&Q Threads (A through L)** — the organizing scheme used in every chapter, introduced in the BoK introduction. The threads cover DoD-unique functions (DoD Acquisition System, Defense Contracting System, Surveillance System) plus functions built around the "5 Ms" — Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement — together with Technology and Industrial Base, Design, Cost and Funding, Materials Management, Process Capability and Control, Quality Management, Manufacturing Workforce, Facilities, and Manufacturing Management and Control. Each thread decomposes into numbered Activities, Tasks, Tools, and Resources.
- **Joint Capabilities Integration and Development System (JCIDS)** — introduced in Thread A.2 as the process supporting the Joint Requirements Oversight Council's (JROC) duty to validate joint warfighting requirements. Its steps run from Capabilities-Based Assessment (CBA) to the Initial Capabilities Document (ICD) to the Capability Development Document (CDD).
- **Analysis of Alternatives (AoA)** — defined in Thread A.1 as an analytical comparison of operational effectiveness, suitability, and life-cycle cost across candidate materiel solutions. Its three products are AoA Study Guidance (developed/approved by the Director of Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation, DCAPE), the AoA Study Plan, and the AoA Final Report.
- **Mission Engineering (ME)** — introduced in Thread A as a top-down approach that bridges JCIDS requirements definition into early systems analysis, architecture, and the SE process, producing mission-based outputs that guide design options and investment decisions.
- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA) and the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM)** — introduced in Thread A under DoDI 5000.88 and 10 USC 2448b. The DTRAM focus areas named here span mission capability and technology; development and integration of the system; the Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA); software; cybersecurity and security; manufacturing; and reliability/availability/maintainability with sustainment.
- **Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs)** and **Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs)** — recurring assessment frameworks, with MRL criteria and the Interactive MRL Users Guide cited as the best practice for identifying and mitigating M&Q risk across the life cycle.
- **DOTMLPF analysis** — referenced in Thread A.2 as the basis on which the ICD documents why non-materiel changes alone cannot close the capability gap, framing whether a materiel approach is warranted.
- **AS6500, Manufacturing Management Program; MIL-HDBK-896, Manufacturing Management Program Guide; ISO 9001 / AS9100 (Quality Management Systems)** — named as the fundamental industry/DoD planning and management standards M&Q practitioners should know.
- **Sixteen Systems Engineering processes** — introduced in Thread E (Design) as eight technical management processes and eight technical processes that structure technical maturation; M&Q is one of the specialty engineering disciplines supporting them.
- **Detection to Prevention (D2P)** — DCMA's surveillance strategy, introduced in Thread C, that shifts emphasis away from redundant end-product inspection toward process capability, risk assessment, and data-driven verification of contractor systems.
- **DoD Risk, Issue, and Opportunity (RIO) Management process** — introduced in Thread K.5 with its five steps: Risk Planning, Risk Identification, Risk Analysis, Risk Mitigation, and Risk Monitoring; when targets are missed, programs develop a Manufacturing Maturation Plan (MMP).

## Key Concepts

- **Three Pre-MDD M&Q objectives.** First, make M&Q part of the design process so the design is producible (manufacturing's role) and reliable/robust (quality's role). Second, assess manufacturing feasibility and quality risk for each identified materiel solution. Third, support Knowledge-Based Acquisition by reducing M&Q risk and demonstrating producibility.
- **Manufacturing feasibility before the AoA.** M&Q analysis begins before the AoA so that the manufacturing-feasibility and quality risks tied to each candidate solution are understood and written into the study guidance for the next phase.
- **Three categories of early research.** The BoK distinguishes Basic Research (6.1 budget activity — fundamental understanding without specific application), Advanced Research (6.2 — knowledge to meet a recognized need, connecting technology to a user), and Advanced Technology Development (6.3 — moving hardware toward field experiments and tests). Service labs, ManTech offices, Centers of Excellence, universities, and industry IRAD all feed this early technology pool.
- **The JCIDS document chain.** CBA frames the military problem and recommends solutions; the draft ICD defines the capability gap and captures the first cut of the Key Performance Parameters (KPPs), the Key System Attributes (KSAs), and any Additional Performance Attributes (APAs); the CDD later specifies operational requirements with thresholds and objectives. M&Q's role here is limited but real — providing inputs to four of the six mandatory KPPs (Force Protection, System Survivability, Sustainment, and Energy) and to manufacturing-feasibility judgments.
- **AoA trade space.** The AoA exists to define the trade space across cost, schedule, and performance and to provide the analytic basis for the performance attributes in the JCIDS documents. M&Q personnel staff the AoA's working groups (Technology and Alternatives, Risk Assessment, Effectiveness Analysis, Cost Analysis) and seed initial M&Q Measures of Effectiveness for each solution.
- **Critical Technology Element (CTE).** A technology element is "critical" when the system depends on it to meet operational requirements at acceptable cost, schedule, and risk, *and* the element (or its application) is new or novel or poses major technological risk during design or demonstration.
- **Producibility.** Defined as the relative ease of producing a product — the composite of design and production-planning characteristics that yield the most effective and economical fabrication, assembly, inspection, test, installation, checkout, and acceptance. The BoK frames producibility as answering "Can it be built?" and identifies it as a leading determinant of product cost. DoD policy makes producibility-risk consideration a requirement in the Acquisition Program Baseline before technology development starts.
- **The digital thread and digital engineering.** The chapter weaves digital engineering throughout: the digital thread links requirements, architecture, design, production, V&V, sustainment, and cost estimating; Technical Data Packages (TDPs), Product Lifecycle Management (PLM), and Product Manufacturing Information (PMI, including GD&T, BOMs, and material specs embedded in 3D CAD) are the carriers of that data.
- **National Technology and Industrial Base (NTIB).** Defined by statute (10 USC) as the people and organizations engaged in national-security and dual-use R&D, production, and maintenance across the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia. Acquisition plans for Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAPs) must consider the NTIB, including supply-chain risk, single points of failure, surge capacity, and resilience.
- **Materials management and the materials science tetrahedron.** Materials characterization examines the interrelationships among structure, properties, processing, and performance. The chapter classifies properties as mechanical, physical, and chemical, and stresses that poorly characterized new materials carry production and field-failure risk.
- **Process capability and control.** Capability studies (Cp/Cpk, Pp/Ppk, X-bar/R and control charts, SPC) assess whether a process can meet drawing and specification requirements. The goal recurs throughout: a uniform, defect-free product with consistent performance at an affordable cost.
- **Quality, quality management, and the QMS.** Quality is the degree to which a product's attributes and characteristics satisfy a need. A Quality Management System (QMS) — typically ISO 9001 or AS9100 — is the formalized system documenting processes, procedures, and responsibilities for meeting quality and regulatory requirements.
- **Industrial cybersecurity.** The IT/OT convergence that boosts productivity also exposes industrial control systems (PLCs, SCADA, DCS, MES) to attack; manufacturing is described as the most-targeted industry. DFARS 252.204-7012 obligates contractors to follow NIST SP 800-171, safeguard covered defense information, and report cyber incidents; NIST SP 800-37 supplies the definition of operational technology.
- **The "5 Ms" and the supporting role.** Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, and Measurement organize the factory-floor view of manufacturability. Across every thread, M&Q personnel support rather than direct — submitting inputs to contracts, requirements, and decisions they do not have authority to execute.

## Mental Models

- **Front-loaded leverage.** Picture an inverted cost curve: the influence M&Q can exert on life-cycle cost is highest at Pre-MDD and falls sharply as the design freezes. Spending analytic effort here is cheap; recovering from a non-producible design later is not.
- **Funnel, not a sieve.** Pre-MDD narrows many candidate concepts down to a defensible AoA set — but the funnel must stay wide at the top. Eliminate concepts only when constraints and technical-feasibility analysis genuinely rule them out (not timely, not sustainable, not affordable); over-narrowing courts the cost-and-schedule penalty the GAO finding warns about.
- **Inputs, not authority.** Treat the M&Q practitioner as an advisor seated at the table. The deliverable is not a signature but credible manufacturing-and-quality input embedded in someone else's document — the ICD, the AoA Study Guidance, the written determination for the MDD.
- **Document chain as a relay.** CBA hands to ICD, ICD informs AoA Study Guidance, the AoA feeds the MDD, and M&Q strategy threads through the SEP into the Acquisition Strategy. A weak link anywhere — an unassessed feasibility risk, a missing producibility consideration — propagates forward.
- **Maturity ladders.** TRLs and MRLs are parallel yardsticks: TRLs gauge technology readiness, MRLs gauge manufacturing readiness. The Pre-MDD job is to set target MRLs for key milestones and identify which technologies and processes need a maturation roadmap before they are safe to carry into acquisition.
- **Risk lives in the supply chain.** Since a large share of a weapon system's content comes from subcontractors and vendors, problems often originate deep in the supply chain and only surface at integration. Look below the first tier, where most programs lose visibility.
- **Same plant, different problems.** The BoK explicitly warns against the assumption that identical facilities behave identically or that a line that ran smoothly in one place will run smoothly when relocated — variability from disassembly, movement, and reassembly is real, even with the same workforce.

## Anti-patterns

The source names several failure modes to avoid:

- **Ignoring producibility.** The BoK warns that ignoring producibility can lock a program into design solutions achievable only at unnecessarily high cost, or into designs carrying substantial technical, cost, and schedule risk. Producibility is treated as too consequential to defer.
- **Over-narrowing the alternatives.** Citing the GAO finding, the chapter cautions that programs examining only a narrow scope of alternatives tended to have worse cost and schedule outcomes than those that considered a broad range — so prematurely shrinking the AoA solution space is a recognized mistake.
- **The "identical facilities" assumption.** Managers are explicitly cautioned against the false impression that identical production facilities will experience identical problems, or that a smoothly running facility will run just as smoothly after a move.
- **Stopping supply-chain oversight at the first tier.** The BoK observes that program offices and contractors often identify and manage problems at first-tier suppliers but do not do well below that level — leaving lower-tier risks to surface late, at integration.

## Key Takeaways

- Pre-MDD is the acquisition front end; its objective is to understand user needs, identify a feasible range of materiel solutions, weigh near-term interim options, and produce a resourced plan that supports the MDA's MDD.
- M&Q work must start before the AoA, during concept development, because early design decisions dominate life-cycle cost — manufacturing influences producibility and quality influences reliability and robustness.
- The three Pre-MDD M&Q objectives are: make M&Q part of design, assess manufacturing feasibility and quality risk for each solution, and support Knowledge-Based Acquisition by reducing risk and demonstrating producibility.
- M&Q strategy should appear in the Capabilities-Based Assessment, the draft ICD, and the AoA Study Guidance, and should thread through the Systems Engineering Plan into the Acquisition Strategy.
- The chapter is organized into twelve threads (A–L) spanning DoD-unique functions and the 5-M factory functions; in this phase the practitioner's role is overwhelmingly supportive, providing inputs rather than exercising authority.
- Feasibility analysis at Pre-MDD must address industrial-base development, new materials and novel processes, additional R&D, manufacturing-technology and capital-equipment needs, special test/inspection equipment and tooling, new or expanded facilities, and new skill sets.
- Standards and yardsticks recur throughout: AS6500 and MIL-HDBK-896 for manufacturing management; ISO 9001/AS9100 for quality; MRL and TRL criteria for readiness; DTRAM/ITRA for independent technical risk; and the DoD RIO process (with a Manufacturing Maturation Plan when targets are missed) for risk management.
- A broad-but-disciplined AoA solution space, early producibility consideration, supply-chain visibility below the first tier, and realistic facility assumptions are explicit guardrails the source flags against common failures.
