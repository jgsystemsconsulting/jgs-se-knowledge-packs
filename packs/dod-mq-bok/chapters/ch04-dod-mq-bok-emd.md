# Chapter 4 — Engineering and Manufacturing Development (EMD) Phase (Post-Milestone B)

## Core Idea
EMD is the phase where a chosen concept becomes a buildable, tested product. Its purpose is to develop, build, and test the system to verify that all operational and derived requirements are met, and to provide the evidence base for the Milestone C production decision. The phase asks manufacturing and quality (M&Q) engineering to do three things: influence the design for producibility (culminating at the Critical Design Review), prepare for production through planning, and execute the M&Q plans. EMD spans the completion of all hardware and software detailed designs, the mitigation and closure of open risks, and the building and testing of prototypes and first articles — anchored by the mid-phase CDR, where the initial product baseline is set and configuration control passes to the program. The phase then proves manufacturing maturity by building initial systems on a pilot line, so that producibility risk is acceptable, supply-chain qualifications are complete, and the manufacturing processes for Key Characteristics and critical characteristics are under statistical process control before the Milestone C production decision. As in every chapter, the work is organized into twelve parallel threads (A–L) that each repeat an activity-overview / M&Q-tasks / tools / resources pattern.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Twelve M&Q Threads (A–L).** The chapter's organizing scaffold, identical in structure across the BoK. By letter: **A** the DoD Acquisition System, **B** Defense Contracting, and **C** Surveillance (the DoD-unique trio); then **D** Technology and the Industrial Base, **E** Design, **F** Cost and Funding, and **G** Materials Management; and **H** Process Capability/Control, **I** Quality Management, **J** the Manufacturing Workforce, **K** Facilities, and **L** Manufacturing Management/Control. The threads are built on the "5 Ms" (Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement), on Manufacturing Readiness Level (MRL) criteria, and on three DoD-unique functions not found in commercial industry (the acquisition, contracting, and surveillance systems). *Use:* a tailorable checklist of M&Q topic areas for an EMD program.

- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) and DoDI 5000.02.** The phase is defined under DoD Instruction 5000.02, Operation of the Adaptive Acquisition Framework. The BoK details the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) path but states the practices apply to the alternative pathways as well. *Use:* EMD-equivalent work under the **Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA) Rapid Fielding** path (begin production within six months, complete fielding within five years) and the **Urgent Capability Acquisition (UCA)** path (field within two years) — both treated as compressed, tailored versions of EMD that demand manufacturing maturity start at MRL 8, with Rapid Fielding achieving MRL 8 before fielding and UCA achieving MRL 9, and a tailored Manufacturing Readiness Assessment plus a Production Readiness Review recommended before entering production.

- **Three M&Q roles in EMD.** The chapter frames the entire phase around three duties for M&Q managers: *Influence the Design* (for producibility), *Prepare for Production* (planning), and *Execute the M&Q Plans* (execution). *Use:* a coarse mental partition of the dozens of tasks in the twelve threads.

- **Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA) and the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM).** USD(R&E) policy, per 10 USC 2448b, requires an ITRA to back each milestone and production decision on a Major Defense Acquisition Program — one before each of Milestones A, B, and C, and one before the Full-Rate Production (FRP) decision. ITRAs follow DTRAM, whose focus areas span mission capability and technology; development and integration of the system; the Modular Open Systems Approach (MOSA); software; cybersecurity and security; manufacturing; and reliability/availability/maintainability (RAM) with sustainment. The ITRA leverages ongoing activities (TRAs, MRAs, technical reviews) but renders an *independent* judgment that may not correlate with those external measures. *Use:* the independent risk lens applied at the Milestone C gate.

- **EMD technical reviews and audits.** The phase's review backbone: the **Critical Design Review (CDR)** at mid-phase (establishes the initial product baseline, transfers configuration control, and freezes design changes to the formal Engineering Change Proposal process); the **Test Readiness Review (TRR)**; the **System Verification Review (SVR)** and **Functional Configuration Audit (FCA)**, often run together to verify the system against the functional baseline; the **Physical Configuration Audit (PCA)**; and the end-of-phase **Production Readiness Review (PRR)** before transition to LRIP. Supporting assessments include the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment (MRA), Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA), ITRA, and Independent Logistics Assessment (ILA). *Use:* M&Q contributes documented inputs to each; the chapter notes TRR, certification reviews, and SVR/FCA occur sequentially while the pilot line can run in parallel, but all must complete before the PRR.

- **Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs) and their EMD targets.** MRL criteria and the MRL Deskbook are named the best-practice maturity yardstick. The chapter sets explicit EMD targets: **MRL 7 system-level for CDR** (with subsystems/items/components at MRL 8 and/or 9), and **MRL 8 system-level for PRR and Milestone C** (with subsystems/items/components at MRL 9). The chapter notes that some organizations run the PRR against MRL 8 criteria directly, so they do not treat the MRL assessment and the PRR as a separate pair. *Use:* read every maturity claim against the phase-and-level-appropriate target.

- **Producibility framework.** Producibility planning is decomposed into Organizing for Producibility, Producibility Planning, Producibility Engineering, Process Capability, and Process Measurement and Improvement. Producibility is defined as the composite of design and production-planning characteristics that lead to the most economical fabrication, assembly, inspection, test, installation, checkout, and acceptance — and is framed as an engineering function aimed at answering "Can it be built?" *Use:* the Producibility Plan guides the design effort, is integrated into the SEP, and is updated by M&Q for realism, completeness, and clarity throughout EMD.

- **Key Characteristics (KC) framework, per AS9100/AS9103.** A characteristic is a measurable feature defined by design data; a Key Characteristic is a selected geometric, material, functional, or cosmetic feature whose variation control is necessary to meet customer requirements (a *process* KC is a measurable process parameter whose control manages part/system KC variation). The chapter sets a capability target of **Cpk ≥ 1.33** (or as the customer specifies), links KC selection to the **Pareto principle** (a few features dominate performance), and lays out a six-stage KC management cycle: understand the KC and required performance, plan a capable process, operate to generate data, analyze the data, take improvement action, and continue monitoring. *Use:* AS9103 is the named best practice for KC identification and control; KCs flow down to subcontractors and into the Verification Cross-Reference Matrix (VCRM).

- **Process Capability and Control toolkit.** Process measurement and control use Statistical Process Control (SPC), Design of Experiments (DOE), Measurement System Analysis (MSA), Process Capability Studies, and Lean/Six Sigma to reduce lead time, eliminate non-value-added activity, reduce variation, and improve efficiency. The wider producibility toolset adds FMEA (Design/Process/Criticality variants — DFMECA, SFMECA, PFMEA), Quality Function Deployment (QFD), Advanced Product Quality Planning (APQP), Production Part Approval Process (PPAP), Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DFMA), Design for Six Sigma, Tolerance Analysis, Value Stream Mapping, and Root Cause Analysis. *Use:* M&Q evaluates the contractor's producibility plans for application of these tools and uses them itself as a Design IPT participant.

- **Pilot line vs. production-representative environment.** Two distinct demonstration environments. A **production-representative environment** is used pre-CDR to demonstrate manufacturing with as much realism as the design maturity allows. A **pilot line** brings together *every* element of production realism — the production equipment, the trained personnel and their skill levels, the facilities, the real materials and components, the work instructions, the processes and tooling, and even the ambient conditions (temperature, cleanliness, lighting) — to build production-configuration items at low rate, leaning on FRP processes as far as is practical. *Use:* the pilot line is where manufacturing maturity is demonstrated; its First Article Inspection (FAI) / First Article Test (FAT) articles validate that processes are stable, in control, and ready for LRIP.

- **Quality Management System (QMS) framework.** An effective QMS compliant with ISO 9001 or AS9100 is named the foundation for producing conforming products and for ensuring the as-delivered configuration matches the as-designed and as-tested configuration. *Use:* in early EMD, M&Q (with DCMA support) audits the contractor's QMS for alignment with program M&Q strategy via process audits of the contractor and supply chain.

- **Industry standards stack.** Recurring named standards: SAE **AS6500** (Manufacturing Management Program — the benchmark for assessing the contractor's manufacturing management system, and the source defining a critical manufacturing process as one that creates or affects a key/critical characteristic), **AS9100/ISO 9001** (QMS), **AS9103** (Variation Management of Key Characteristics), **AS9145** (APQP/PPAP), **IEEE 15288** family (.1 Application of SE on Defense Programs, .2 Technical Reviews and Audits), and **MIL-HDBK-896 / MIL-STD-881 (WBS)**. *Use:* specified in the RFP, source-selection criteria, and contract as the contractor's required management systems.

- **DoD Risk, Issue, and Opportunity (RIO) management.** A joint government/contractor RIO management system that identifies, analyzes, mitigates, tracks, and controls M&Q risks across performance, technical, cost, schedule, sustainment, and programmatic areas. *Use:* the recurring engine across the threads; outputs feed the SEP and TEMP and inform the ITRA.

- **DCMA surveillance.** Defense Contract Management Agency support provides monitoring, tracking, and reporting of contractor and supply-chain performance and compliance, including multi-disciplined audits and First Article / Production Lot testing (DCMA-INST 302). *Use:* the program's oversight arm for verifying application of M&Q best practices (AS6500, AS9100, IEEE 15288.2) at the contractor and in the supply chain.

## Key Concepts

- **CDR is the design freeze and the configuration-control handoff.** The CDR occurs mid-EMD, brings detailed-design paths to closure, and establishes the system initial product baseline; from that point, changes proceed only through a formal Engineering Change Proposal (ECP). CDR completion yields an established product baseline, an updated EMD risk assessment, an updated Cost Analysis Requirements Description (CARD), an updated fabrication/test/coding schedule with critical-path drivers, and an approved Life Cycle Sustainment Plan. By CDR, all the design information needed to plan detailed manufacturing operations should exist, and CDRs are conducted at each supply-chain level from component up to system.

- **The Technical Data Package (TDP) as the product baseline content.** At CDR the initial product baseline transferred for configuration control includes all drawings and specifications with tolerances and test points; all KC, Critical Safety Item (CSI), and Critical Application Item (CAI) specifications; and may include process control plans and work instructions — enough that every component and hardware item — including its embedded software — can be made across the supply chain and on the pilot line. The TDP is finalized for LRIP after pilot-line demonstrations.

- **KCs, critical characteristics, and the M&Q traceability chain.** A *critical* characteristic (per AS6500) is one whose defect would create or increase a hazard to human safety or cause a system function to fail; a *significant* characteristic is one whose variation could affect fit, function, durability, satisfaction, or manufacturability. Products perform better with less variation on key and critical characteristics. M&Q keeps a traceability chain that runs from each KC up through the Critical Manufacturing Process (CMP) that drives it, the relevant Technical Performance Measure (TPM), the allocated-baseline requirement, and on to the governing KPPs and KSAs — and it makes sure every KC, CSI, and CAI is captured in the VCRM for verification.

- **Pilot-line success validates the design and the processes.** A successful pilot-line build validates that the system design is complete; its FAI/FAT articles confirm the M&Q processes are stable, under control, and ready for LRIP. Pilot-line data give current yield and rate figures and validate all M&Q learning curves for the system and subsystems.

- **Demonstrated maturity is the entry ticket to production.** Per DoDI 5000.02, the Milestone C decision requires no significant manufacturing risks, available industrial production capabilities, and an assessment that critical manufacturing processes are affordable and executable. If processes are not capable, in control, and affordable, the program must keep maturing them. DoDI 5000.88 requires manufacturing readiness and risk to be assessed and documented in the SEP and managed throughout the life cycle.

- **The 5 Ms as the manufacturability lens.** Manufacturability focuses on factory-floor activity — Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement — with each brought to maturity in the right place and at the right time, and that maturity then proven. Manufacturability is positioned as an early consideration within the eight SE technical and eight technical management processes.

- **Capability and control are two separate tests.** Process capability comes from robust product and process design plus process-control activity and continuous improvement to remove sources of variation. A process must be both capable (meets specification, e.g., Cpk ≥ 1.33) and in statistical control (predictable) — pilot-line capability studies confirm both for all KC and critical-characteristic processes throughout the supply chain.

- **SVR/FCA verifies what the pilot line actually produced.** The system-level FCA assesses performance against the functional baseline using collected data, test and analysis results, and modeling-and-simulation output after development testing and pilot line; the SVR addresses all changes since CDR (ECPs, specification change notices, interface control changes, and M&Q process changes) so the as-tested pilot-line product reflects every change.

- **Manufacturing key performance indicators.** Pilot-line and production-representative assessments collect OEE (overall equipment effectiveness), cycle time, takt time, yields, and rates as the operating metrics that quantify maturity and feed resource loading and facilities planning for later phases.

- **Design maturity is judged against the SE processes, not a single number.** The chapter notes design maturity is not crisply defined in DoD guidance; it is inferred when a design and product meet cost, schedule, and performance targets (tied to MOEs, MOPs, KPPs, KSAs, TPMs) and satisfy the user, and it is managed across the eight technical and eight technical-management processes. Cost analysts use the MIL-STD-881 Work Breakdown Structure as the basis for estimates, and M&Q establishes cost traceability to the WBS work-package level.

## Mental Models

- **EMD is "design freeze, then proof."** The phase has two halves hinged on CDR. Before CDR, M&Q influences the design for producibility and works to reduce and finalize the KC list. After CDR, the work shifts to proving — pilot-line builds, capability studies, FAI/FAT — that the now-frozen design can actually be produced. Treat CDR as the moment the question changes from "should it be this way?" to "can we make it this way, repeatably?"

- **Reduce the KCs, then lock them.** Before CDR the KC list may shrink as producibility activity refines the design to make characteristics less sensitive to variation; as KCs finalize at CDR, the corresponding list of critical M&Q processes completes too. Fewer, well-controlled KCs are better than many fragile ones — the Pareto principle says a small number of features dominate performance.

- **Two demonstration environments, two maturity bars.** A production-representative environment (pre-CDR, MRL 7 system-level target) is "as real as the immature design allows"; a pilot line (pre-Milestone C, MRL 8 system-level target) is "everything real, using FRP processes." Don't conflate them — the pilot line is the production decision's evidence, and its data, not the representative environment's, validate learning curves and finalize the TDP for LRIP.

- **Build the inputs once, propagate everywhere.** The same M&Q feasibility, producibility, and industrial-base analysis should flow consistently into the Acquisition Strategy (with its Manufacturing and Quality Strategies), the SEP (with its Manufacturing and Quality Plans), the TEMP, the RFP and Source Selection Plan for the P&D contractor, and the CDR/PRR review packages. The threads are different windows onto one coherent risk-and-readiness argument.

- **Cost and learning curves get re-grounded in actuals.** Learning curves established in TMRR are refined through CDR and then *validated* by pilot-line data; LRIP cost estimates should rest on the completed design, known processes, executed M&Q operations, and actual system-level pilot-line costs rather than projections.

- **M&Q influences but does not command.** As in earlier phases, M&Q personnel sit on the technical IPT and submit inputs for consideration; they do not sign contracts or own the reviews. The leverage is being an integral element of the Design IPT, the FCA/SVR, and the source-selection inputs for the P&D RFP.

- **Maturity is a system-versus-supplier gradient.** At any review, the system level lags its subordinate levels: at CDR the system targets MRL 7 while subsystems/items/components target MRL 8–9; at PRR/Milestone C the system targets MRL 8 while the lower levels target MRL 9. Read each maturity claim against the level it applies to.

## Anti-patterns

- **The GAO cost-growth triad.** The Cost/Funding thread (F.4) cites numerous GAO reports on weapon-system cost growth and ties it to three recurring maturity failures: critical technologies given too little time/funding and demonstrated late; designs that were unstable with too many changes for the point in time (e.g., at CDR with production set to begin); and critical manufacturing processes not demonstrated in a timely manner that, once in production, were *not under statistical process control*. The chapter frames timely process demonstration and SPC as the direct countermeasures.

- **Ignoring producibility.** The Producibility thread (E.4) warns that ignoring producibility may lock the acquisition into design solutions achievable only at unnecessarily high cost, and/or into designs entailing substantial technical, cost, and schedule risk.

- **Immature processes as a dominant risk source.** The Process Capability threads (H.4/H.5) state that immature processes are a major source of risk during EMD, when the design is still maturing and production is just emerging — and that process maturity grows in importance as the program advances toward the production and FRP decisions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **EMD exists to build, test, and prove a producible product for the Milestone C decision.** The phase ends when the design is stable, validated capability requirements are demonstrated by developmental and initial operational testing per the TEMP, manufacturing processes are effectively demonstrated and under control, software sustainment is functioning, industrial production capability is reasonably available, and EMD exit / Milestone C entrance criteria are met.

2. **CDR is the pivot.** It establishes the initial product baseline, transfers configuration control, locks the TDP content (drawings, specs, tolerances, KC/CSI/CAI), and converts further change into formal ECPs. M&Q ensures the baseline is complete and configuration-controlled enough to manufacture throughout the supply chain.

3. **Finalize and trace the Key Characteristics.** Reduce KCs through producibility work, finalize them at CDR with their critical M&Q processes, hold them to Cpk ≥ 1.33 per AS9103, and trace KC → CMP → TPM → allocated baseline → KPP/KSA, with every KC/CSI/CAI in the VCRM.

4. **Demonstrate maturity in the right environment at the right MRL.** Use a production-representative environment for CDR (system-level MRL 7) and a pilot line using FRP processes for PRR/Milestone C (system-level MRL 8), collecting capability studies, yields, rates, and FAI/FAT evidence.

5. **Capable *and* in control.** Pilot-line process capability studies must confirm both that processes meet specification (Cpk) and are statistically stable, across the supply chain, for all KC and critical-characteristic processes — the explicit Milestone C prerequisite.

6. **Carry the M&Q argument across all documents and reviews.** The Acquisition Strategy, SEP, TEMP, RFP/SSP for the P&D contractor, and the CDR/TRR/SVR-FCA/PRR packages should reflect one consistent M&Q strategy, plan, and risk picture.

7. **Standards and the QMS are the contractual levers.** AS6500, AS9100/ISO 9001, AS9103, AS9145, and IEEE 15288 are specified for the contractor's manufacturing management system and QMS; M&Q (with DCMA) audits the QMS in early EMD for alignment with program strategy.

8. **Use the independent and surveillance lenses at the gate.** ITRA (per 10 USC 2448b, via DTRAM) supplies an independent risk judgment for Milestone C, while DCMA surveillance and multi-disciplined audits supply current, predictive insight into contractor and supply-chain compliance and process capability.

9. **Re-ground cost and learning curves in pilot-line actuals.** Validate learning curves with pilot-line yield/rate data and base LRIP cost estimates on the completed design and actual system-level pilot-line costs, with cost mitigation plans folded into the Manufacturing Maturation Plans.

10. **Tailor for compressed pathways.** Under MTA Rapid Fielding and UCA, truncate EMD but raise the manufacturing-maturity bar — start at MRL 8 (achieving MRL 8 for Rapid Fielding, MRL 9 for UCA before fielding) and run a tailored MRA plus a recommended PRR before production.

## Connects To

- **DoDI 5000.02 (Operation of the Adaptive Acquisition Framework)** — the governing instruction defining the EMD phase, Milestone C decision, and the Rapid Fielding / UCA pathways; cited as the anchor for every thread and for the Milestone C maturity prerequisites.
- **DoDI 5000.85 (Major Capability Acquisition), 5000.88 (Engineering of Defense Systems), 5000.89 (Test and Evaluation), 5000.97 (Digital Engineering)** — the policy set the threads reference for acquisition, engineering, test, and digital practice; 5000.88 is the source of the requirement to assess and document manufacturing readiness in the SEP.
- **10 USC 2448b and the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology (DTRAM)** — the statutory and methodological basis for the ITRA conducted at Milestone C and the FRP decision.
- **DoD Systems Engineering Guidebook and Engineering of Defense Systems Guidebook** — the SE process backbone (the eight technical and eight technical-management processes, CDR/TRR/SVR-FCA/PRR) into which the EMD M&Q activities plug; the SE Guidebook's producibility chapter is a named reference.
- **Manufacturing Readiness Level (MRL) Deskbook and MIL-HDBK-896 (Manufacturing Management Program Guide)** — the maturity-assessment criteria (MRL 7 at CDR, MRL 8 at Milestone C) and manufacturing-management reference used phase-wide; Manufacturing Maturation Plans live here.
- **SAE AS6500, AS9100, AS9103, AS9145; ISO 9001; IEEE 15288.1/.2** — the contractor management-system, KC-variation, APQP/PPAP, QMS, and technical-review standards specified in the RFP, SSP, and contract.
- **DoD Risk, Issue, and Opportunity Management Guide** — the RIO process underpinning the joint risk system that feeds the SEP, TEMP, and ITRA.
- **DCMA instructions (e.g., DCMA-INST 302, First Article and Production Lot Testing)** — the surveillance and FAI/FAT framework supporting the pilot-line and Milestone C evidence base.
- **MIL-STD-881 (Work Breakdown Structure) and the CAPE Cost Estimating Guide** — the WBS framework and cost-estimating reference for design-maturity cost traceability and the CARD update at CDR.
- **Preceding and following BoK chapters (Chapter 3, TMRR; Chapter 5, P&D; Chapter 6, O&S)** — EMD inherits TMRR learning curves and PDR results, and hands a stable design, finalized TDP, validated pilot-line processes, and the P&D RFP/SSP forward into Production and Deployment.
