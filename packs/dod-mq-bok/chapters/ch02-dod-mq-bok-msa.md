# Chapter 2 — Materiel Solution Analysis (MSA) Phase

## Core Idea
The MSA phase is the first acquisition window in which manufacturing and quality (M&Q) engineering can shape a future weapon system, and the Body of Knowledge frames the entire phase around one governing question: "Can it be built?" The phase runs the analytical and engineering work needed to choose a product concept and culminates in the Milestone A risk-reduction decision — an investment decision to commit resources to mature technology and reduce risk before development. M&Q's role here is influence rather than authority: practitioners feed feasibility, producibility, affordability, and industrial-base judgments into the Analysis of Alternatives (AoA) and into the foundational program documents (Acquisition Strategy, Systems Engineering Plan, Test and Evaluation Master Plan), so that a chosen concept is producible, not merely performant. The chapter organizes all of this into twelve parallel "threads" (A–L), each repeating a consistent pattern of activity overview, M&Q tasks, tools, and resources.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Twelve M&Q Threads (A–L).** The chapter's organizing scaffold. The threads, by letter: **A** the DoD Acquisition System, **B** Defense Contracting, and **C** Surveillance (the DoD-unique trio); then **D** Technology and the Industrial Base, **E** Design, **F** Cost and Funding, and **G** Materials Management; and finally **H** Process Capability/Control, **I** Quality Management, **J** the Manufacturing Workforce, **K** Facilities, and **L** Manufacturing Management/Control. The introduction explains the threads are built on the "5 Ms" (Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, Measurement), on Manufacturing Readiness Level criteria, and on DoD-unique functions not found in commercial industry (the acquisition, contracting, and surveillance systems). *Use:* as a checklist of M&Q topic areas to tailor for a given program in this phase.

- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF) and DoDI 5000.02.** The phase is defined under DoD Instruction 5000.02. The BoK follows the Major Capability Acquisition (MCA) path but states the practices also apply to alternative AAF pathways such as Urgent Capability Acquisition (under 2 years to field) and Middle Tier of Acquisition (rapid prototyping or rapid fielding). *Use:* to locate the program on the right pathway and tailor MCA activities accordingly.

- **Analysis of Alternatives (AoA).** An analytical comparison, across candidate alternatives that could meet the Initial Capabilities Document (ICD) need, of their operational effectiveness, their suitability, and their life-cycle cost. It has three primary products: AoA Study Guidance (developed/approved by the Director of Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation, DCAPE), the AoA Study Plan (the road map for conduct), and the AoA Final Report. *Use:* M&Q supplies feasibility and industrial-base inputs across all three products; the MDA must certify to Congress that an AoA consistent with DCAPE guidance was completed.

- **JCIDS and the requirements documents (ICD, CDD).** The Joint Capabilities Integration and Development System supports the Joint Requirements Oversight Council's validation of joint warfighting requirements. The ICD identifies enabling capabilities; the Capability Development Document (CDD) specifies the system's operational requirements with attributes expressed as thresholds and objectives. *Use:* M&Q translates validated KPPs/KSAs into Key Characteristics and supports CDD sections on technology readiness, affordability, and KPP/KSA development.

- **Acquisition Strategy and Systems Engineering Plan (SEP).** The Acquisition Strategy is described as the top-most planning document; the SEP and Contracting Strategy feed into it, and M&Q strategies should be a component of the SEP. The SEP is the "blueprint" for technical execution from conception to disposal, updated for MDA approval at each milestone beginning with Milestone A. *Use:* M&Q embeds a Manufacturing Plan and Quality Plan, exit-criteria metrics, and MRL targets into both documents.

- **Sixteen Systems Engineering Processes.** Table 2-1 lists eight technical management processes (Technical Planning, Decision Analysis, Technical Assessment, Requirements Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, Technical Data Management, Interface Management) and eight technical processes (Stakeholder Requirements Definition, Requirements Analysis, Architecture Design, Implementation, Integration, Verification, Validation, Transition). *Use:* M&Q supports each process; design maturity is assessed against completion of all sixteen.

- **Manufacturing Readiness Levels (MRLs) and Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA).** MRL criteria and assessments are named the best practice for identifying and mitigating M&Q risk across the life cycle. The TRA is a metrics-based process for evaluating maturity of Critical Technology Elements (CTEs): identify the CTE, assess it, prepare and conduct the TRA, then develop and manage a CTE Maturation Plan. *Use:* during MSA, manufacturing processes should reach MRL 4 (lab environment) to be considered mature; ManTech projects start no lower than MRL 3 and end no lower than MRL 4.

- **DoD Risk, Issue, and Opportunity (RIO) five-step process.** Risk Planning, Risk Identification, Risk Analysis (likelihood × consequence), Risk Mitigation (accept, avoid, transfer, or control), and Risk Monitoring. *Use:* the recurring engine for managing M&Q, materials, supply-chain, and industrial-base risks throughout the phase.

- **Industry standards stack.** The BoK repeatedly names SAE AS6500 (Manufacturing Management Program), AS9100 / ISO 9001 (Quality Management Systems), AS9103 (Variation Management of Key Characteristics), AS9145 (APQP/PPAP), and the IEEE 15288 family (.1 Application of SE on Defense Programs, .2 Technical Reviews and Audits). *Use:* specified in RFPs, source-selection criteria, and contracts as the contractor's required management systems.

- **Contracting frameworks.** Market Research (FAR Part 10, strategic vs. tactical), the Contracting Cone (full spectrum of FAR and Non-FAR strategies), contract-type selection per FAR 16.104, the Source Selection Plan (SSP), and RFP Sections L (instructions to offerors) and M (evaluation). *Use:* M&Q injects MRL demonstrations, manufacturing planning, quality-system, and supplier-management criteria into Sections L and M.

- **DCMA surveillance frameworks.** Contract Administration Service (CAS) functions per FAR 42.302 / DFARS Part 242, delegated via Memorandum of Agreement (MOA), Letter of Delegation (LOD), or Quality Assurance Letter of Instruction (QALI). DCMA's current posture is the "Detection to Prevention (D2P)" strategy, emphasizing process capability, risk mitigation, and data-driven action over end-item inspection. *Use:* program offices maximize DCMA data and delegate CAS activities where authority and expertise exist.

- **Producibility framework.** Producibility planning is decomposed into Organizing for Producibility, Producibility Planning, Producibility Engineering, Process Capability, and Process Measurement and Improvement. *Use:* integrated into the SEP and Manufacturing Management Plan, beginning at concept development.

- **Process Capability and Control framework.** Built on AS6500 requirements: identify Key Characteristics (KC), Key Manufacturing Processes (KMP), and Critical Characteristics (CC); compute capability indices Cp/Cpk (established processes) and performance indices Pp/Ppk (actual performance over time); run Statistical Process Control; and implement a Variability Reduction Program with Process Control Plans. *Use:* AS9103 sets a KC capability target of Cpk ≥ 1.33 (or as the customer specifies).

- **Cost estimating framework.** Estimate types (ICE, Component Cost Estimate, Program Office Estimate, Component Cost Position, Cost Capability Analysis, IGCE, Should Cost Estimate) and four techniques (Expert Opinion, Analogous, Parametric, Engineering "bottom-up"), structured by a Work Breakdown Structure per MIL-STD-881 and tracked via Earned Value Management and the IPMDAR. *Use:* M&Q identifies cost drivers and feeds the Cost Analysis Requirements Description (CARD) and the ASR.

- **MSA technical reviews.** The Alternative Systems Review (ASR) is the primary MSA review, conducted before Milestone A to confirm the preferred solution can be affordable, producible, operationally effective and suitable. Supporting reviews include the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment (MRA), Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA), Independent Technical Risk Assessment (ITRA, per 10 USC 2448b, conducted via the Defense Technical Risk Assessment Methodology / DTRAM), and Independent Logistics Assessment (ILA). *Use:* M&Q provides producibility and risk inputs to each.

## Key Concepts

- **"Can it be built?" as the unifying test.** Manufacturing feasibility — answering whether a proposed materiel solution can be produced with existing capabilities while meeting quality, rate, cost, and schedule — recurs as the central M&Q question across the AoA, Acquisition Strategy, design, materials, and producibility threads.

- **Influence, not authority.** The introduction is explicit that M&Q personnel cannot sign contracts; their job is to be on the technical IPT and submit M&Q inputs for consideration. The phase is framed as M&Q's first real opportunity to influence design before commitments harden.

- **Translating KPPs/KSAs into Key Characteristics.** Validated Key Performance Parameters and Key System Attributes (and Additional Performance Attributes) are converted by M&Q into Key Characteristics — the features whose variation most affects fit, form, function, service life, or producibility — and into Critical Characteristics where human safety or catastrophic failure is at stake.

- **The National Technology and Industrial Base (NTIB).** Per 10 USC 4820, the NTIB comprises national-security and dual-use R&D, production, and maintenance organizations across the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia. Industrial Base Assessments evaluate capability, capacity, and financial stability and surface single points of failure (sole, single, fragile, or foreign sources).

- **Critical Technology Elements (CTEs).** A technology element is "critical" if the system depends on it to meet operational requirements and it is new, novel, or used in a novel way posing major technological risk. CTEs anchor the TRA and ManTech investment decisions.

- **The "5 Ms" as the manufacturability lens.** Manpower, Machines, Materials, Methods, and Measurement organize industrial-base capability and capacity evaluation and define the factory-floor scope of producibility and manufacturability.

- **Material science tetrahedron.** Material characterization is framed as the interrelationship of structure, properties, processing, and performance — material scientists focusing on structure/properties, material engineers on performance/processing — and drives the assessment of material maturity, mechanical/physical/chemical properties, and counterfeit-part risk.

- **Yield and capability vocabulary.** First Pass (throughput) yield, Rolling Throughput Yield, and Final Yield measure uniformity; processes must be both *capable* (meeting specification) and *in control* (statistically predictable).

- **Digital engineering and the digital thread.** Digital models, the Technical Data Package (TDP), and Product Manufacturing Information (PMI) embedded in 3D CAD enable producibility analysis, factory simulation, and life-cycle cost modeling; Industry 4.0 / IIoT capabilities extend this to the plant floor.

## Mental Models

- **The phase is a funnel ending in a gate.** MSA starts with a broad AoA trade space and narrows to a single preferred concept that must clear the Milestone A gate. Treat the ASR as the dress rehearsal and Milestone A as the investment commitment; everything M&Q does is evidence that the chosen concept is producible at acceptable risk.

- **Documents are a dependency tree, not a checklist.** The Acquisition Strategy sits at the top; the SEP, Contracting Strategy, Manufacturing Strategy, and Quality Strategy feed up into it. Build M&Q inputs once and propagate them — the same feasibility and industrial-base analysis should appear in the AoA, the SEP, the Acquisition Strategy, the RFP, and the ASR.

- **Cost is locked early, even though it is estimated late.** Detailed M&Q cost estimates usually cannot be finalized in MSA, but cost drivers can be identified now — and producibility decisions made now determine EMD, P&D, and O&S costs later. Ignoring producibility can lock the program into a design achievable only at unnecessarily high cost.

- **The government sets the tone.** The chapter observes that a contractor's core design team makes 90–95 percent of critical decisions, mostly before production. If the government shows concern for M&Q in early design trade-offs, the contractor follows that lead; if M&Q is deferred to secondary teams, cost/schedule/performance problems follow.

- **Maturity is measured against a phase target.** A process at MRL 4 (demonstrated in a lab) is "mature" for MSA but not for later phases. Read every maturity claim against the phase-appropriate MRL/TRL target rather than as an absolute.

## Anti-patterns

- **Deferring M&Q until late.** The Defense Contracting System thread warns that a large share of programs put off thinking about M&Q management requirements until EMD or later — even though the M&Q risks that matter are emerging, and need to be controlled, in the early phases.

- **Reactive Key Characteristic identification.** The Design Maturity thread states that companies have historically identified KCs only after experiencing cost problems in the plant and the field; proactive ("robust") engineering identifies KCs early in the design phase.

- **Assuming identical facilities behave identically.** The Process Capability thread warns against the false impression that identical production facilities experience identical problems, and that a smoothly running facility will run smoothly after a move — even with the same workforce, variability from disassembly, movement, and reassembly occurs.

- **Ignoring producibility.** The Producibility thread cautions that ignoring producibility may lock the acquisition into design solutions achievable only at unnecessarily high cost and/or designs entailing substantial technical, cost, and schedule risk.

## Key Takeaways

1. **MSA exists to choose a producible concept and clear Milestone A.** The phase ends when a Component completes the analysis needed to support proceeding to TMRR, EMD, or P&D; M&Q's deliverable is confidence that the preferred solution can be built at acceptable risk.

2. **The AoA is M&Q's primary point of leverage.** Feasibility and industrial-base inputs to the AoA Study Guidance, Study Plan, and Final Report quantify the differences between alternatives and drive concept selection; M&Q sits on the study team and the supporting working groups.

3. **Translate requirements into Key Characteristics and carry them forward.** Validated KPPs/KSAs become KCs and CCs that thread through the CDD, SEP, RFP, and the contractor's QMS, with AS9103 setting Cpk ≥ 1.33 as the KC capability benchmark.

4. **Build the documents as one coherent argument.** The Acquisition Strategy, SEP (with its Manufacturing and Quality Plans), Contracting Strategy, TEMP, and CARD should reflect a single, consistent M&Q risk-and-feasibility narrative.

5. **Standards and readiness metrics are the contractual levers.** AS6500, AS9100/ISO 9001, AS9103, AS9145, IEEE 15288, MRLs, and TRLs are specified in RFP Sections L and M and in source-selection criteria so the contractor is held to industry best practice and to phase-appropriate maturity targets.

6. **Industrial-base and supply-chain risk are first-order concerns.** IBAs, CTE identification, ManTech planning, NTIB analysis, DMSMS/obsolescence and counterfeit-part mitigation, and the RIO five-step process together protect the program from sole/single/fragile/foreign source failures.

7. **Use DCMA and the surveillance system as data sources.** Delegated CAS functions and DCMA's Detection-to-Prevention posture give the program current, predictive insight into contractor cost, schedule, process capability, and supply-chain health.

8. **Process capability work starts before metal is cut.** Modeling and simulation, capability studies, yield/rate estimates, and lab-environment process demonstrations (to MRL 4) reduce manufacturing risk and feed the Acquisition Strategy and SEP.

- **JCIDS (CJCSI 5123.01I), the ICD, and the CDD** — the requirements lineage that feeds the materiel solution and the Key Characteristics M&Q derives.
