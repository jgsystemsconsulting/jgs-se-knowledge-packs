# Chapter — Timing and Time-Awareness in CPS

## Core Idea

Timing is not an optional refinement in a cyber-physical system; it is the seam where the cyber world meets the physical one, and getting it wrong is a correctness failure rather than a performance shortfall. NIST SP 1500-202 (Volume 2), in the report of the Timing Sub-group, builds its entire treatment on one tension: every timing capability ultimately rests on a *physical* signal that obeys the laws of physics, yet modern data systems — processors, software stacks, networks — were deliberately optimized by abstracting that physical timing away. Computers, operating systems, and networks gain their efficiency from asynchrony, isolating timing processes so that data can be moved and crunched as fast as possible. That very design choice strips them of time-awareness, which is exactly the property a CPS needs to coordinate processes, timestamp events, measure latency, and run real-time control.

The source's central argument is that today's CPS work around this conflict with brute force — dedicated hardware and bespoke software for the timing-critical paths, deliberately keeping data processing out of the way of anything that needs strong temporal determinism. That workaround is expensive and brittle: a hardware or software upgrade can force re-calibration or recertification of the whole system because timing was never a first-class, hardware-independent abstraction. The chapter's forward-looking thesis is "time-correctness by design" — making time an explicit, designer-controllable property throughout the stack so that systems can be shown correct by construction rather than verified after the fact.

A second core idea is that timing requirements are best expressed as constraints on **time intervals (TIs)** — each TI being how long elapses between two instants measured against one common timescale — bounding the gap between pairs of significant system events. A motor-control loop might require that the interval between sensing and actuation be 100 µs within ±1 µs; a monitoring system might require a bound on how long it takes a measurement to become available. Framing requirements this way gives a clean vocabulary for reasoning about deadlines, determinism, and accuracy, and it is the backbone of how the rest of the report classifies and manages timing.

The third core idea is that timing is also a **security and resilience** problem, not just a precision problem. Because a timing signal has both a physical-signal component and an associated data component, securing it means securing both the channel and the data. A remote user needs assurance that a signal genuinely originated from the legitimate source, and separately that nobody has tampered with the transit delay, on top of the ordinary data-integrity concerns — and timing signals can be denied in many distinct ways.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **CPS Framework Timing Aspect** — the report (Section 4 of Volume 2) is the working-group elaboration of the Framework's *timing aspect*. Section 4.1 lays the conceptual groundwork, 4.2 examines where time is present or absent in the components used to describe and operate a CPS, 4.3 addresses managing timing and bounded latency, and 4.4 treats secure and resilient time. It repeatedly defers detailed metrics and tables to a companion **Timing Annex** (Volume 3, Framework for Cyber-Physical Systems: Volume 3), which the report treats as the authoritative reference for timescale definitions, uncertainty, and the orders-of-magnitude span of timing requirements.

- **Time-Interval (TI) classification: Bounded, Deterministic, Accurate** — introduced in Section 4.2 as a way to categorize timing constraints by their degree of time-awareness. A **Bounded TI** stays under a stated maximum (and sometimes above a minimum); a **Deterministic TI** holds within a stated error of an application-specified target, on a system-local timescale; an **Accurate TI** is a deterministic TI whose timescale is traceable to international standards such as UTC or TAI. Use bounded TIs for deadline-driven behavior, deterministic TIs for repeatable precise timing relative to the system's own clock, and accurate TIs where spatial extent or legal/regulatory traceability demands an internationally referenced timescale.

- **CPS Time Domain** — defined in Section 4.3 as a logical grouping of CPS nodes and bridges forming a network with its own timing master. The domain runs on a primary (locally synchronized) timescale that gives applications a strong monotonically increasing clock for I/O and time-based scheduling. A key design rule: the master must not introduce a time discontinuity once time-sensitive transfer has begun within the domain, even if it briefly loses its global source such as GPS. A node that also needs internationally traceable time can run a second **Global Traceable Timescale** and maintain the offset between the two, which lets timescales from multiple domains be correlated.

- **CPS Network Manager (CNM)** — defined in Section 4.3 as the workstation or node responsible for managing and monitoring each node's state and configuration across one or more time domains (or across an SoS). It works with a schedule generator and a path-computation engine — often via a centralized network controller — to compute transmission schedules and bridge gate events that guarantee bounded latency and reserved bandwidth per time-sensitive stream. The report notes standardization efforts to formalize this role under the name **Centralized User Configuration (CUC)**, citing Time-Sensitive Networking work (IEEE 802.1Qcc) and AVnu industrial interoperability efforts, both described as early-stage at the time of writing.

- **Time-triggered architecture** — named as the principal industrial mechanism for time-awareness. A controller initiates a fixed, pre-planned group of physical-world interactions on a periodic sense–compute–actuate cycle, and in a distributed setting inter-node communication is scheduled against a common timescale (commonly IEEE 1588, NTP, or proprietary protocols). The report highlights its design value: by separating timing at the cyber/physical boundary, it can deliver deterministic or accurate timing right at that interface while the compute phase needs to satisfy only a *bounded* TI.

- **"Time-correctness by design"** — introduced in Section 4.2 as the capability the field currently lacks. It means designers can specify timing as a hardware-independent abstraction (much as other system behavior is abstracted), and tooling then determines whether chosen target hardware can meet that timing and, ideally, generates the implementation. The report frames this as aspirational — much work remains — but positions it as the route to designs that survive hardware and software upgrades without timing recertification.

- **PTIDES** — cited as an example of a distributed-programming modeling tool that is better suited to CPS because it can map a logical-time model onto appropriate hardware, assuming synchronized time and giving the language explicit timing semantics. Model boundaries to sensors and actuators carry explicit latency annotations, and at implementation time the model's "logically synchronized" clock maps to the physical synchronized clock, making the specified latency verifiable. The report pairs this with the **Y-chart** approach for exploring multiple architectural mappings of one model.

## Key Concepts

- **The three synchronization quantities — frequency, phase, time**: A single clock can supply accurate frequency, but phase and time synchronization always require transporting signals (and often data) and compensating for transmission delay to the needed accuracy. The report's GPS illustration makes the scale vivid: a roughly 70 ms signal-transit delay must be removed to a few nanoseconds to achieve meter-level ranging — accuracy on the order of one part in twenty million.

- **On-time marker and accompanying data**: A physical time signal (the on-time marker) marks *when* the time is correct, while the actual date and time-of-day travel separately as data that can tolerate noise and latency, provided it remains clear which marker the data refer to. The node must know or cancel transmission delay in proportion to its accuracy requirement, typically using a two-way time-transfer protocol — NTP and PTP (IEEE 1588) are the named common protocols — to cancel network delay.

- **Timescale, epoch, and rate**: A timescale is characterized by its epoch (the time-zero origin) and the rate at which time advances (the definition of the second). The SI second is defined via cesium energy levels by the BIPM; a clock is accurate in frequency to the degree its rate matches that definition, and time is accurate if traceable to UTC or TAI. The report stresses that any real-time UTC or TAI value is only a prediction, since both are post-processed scales, and that UTC is discontinuous because of leap-second insertions that keep it near UT1.

- **Self-consistent vs. traceable timescales**: Many CPS need only an internally self-consistent timescale with no obligation to agree with an international one — the epoch may be an application event (such as power-on) and the rate a count of a local oscillator. Other systems must agree with UTC or TAI and therefore must use the SI second. The pervasive connectivity of the IoT means some traceable time is often available even when the system's own precision needs exceed it.

- **Predictability and the two meanings of determinism**: The classic computer-science sense is functional determinism — identical inputs and state yield identical outputs. CPS additionally need **temporal determinism**: identical or near-identical timing behavior, achievable only when identical input, identical initial architectural state, and absence of external interference can be guaranteed — a hard ask on high-performance processors with variable execution time. **Timing predictability** means behavior can be predicted within the parameters a system requires.

- **Uncertainty: Type A and Type B**: Following the BIPM method, the report splits uncertainty into Type A (statistical) and Type B (deterministic bias). In PTP, packet delay variation (jitter) is a Type A effect, while asymmetry between the two directions of timing-packet transfer produces a constant, invisible error captured as Type B uncertainty — it cannot be revealed by the measurement spread alone.

- **Event-driven vs. time-triggered execution**: Both require that interactions with the physical world be bounded so the controller can meet its deadlines. Time-triggered systems schedule interactions and inter-node communication on a common timescale; event-driven systems permit external or controller-initiated events but must impose application-specific limits on event number and frequency to avoid overwhelming resources. Real systems may combine both.

- **Ordering of timestamps**: Two timestamps that fall within their resolution or within the synchronization error of the timescales cannot be confidently ordered — a general consequence of comparing physical measurements, worsened when the two scales differ in resolution. When ordering cannot be resolved, an arbitrary tie-break (such as lexical order on variable name) is used. The report points to Kopetz's sparse-time proposal as a way to make timestamps fine-grained enough to avoid confounding closely spaced events.

- **Position and time are coupled**: Because phase and time require delay compensation, the end device's position is bound up with time transfer; conversely, navigation relies on synchronized time signals from known locations. GNSS supplies both but is weak, blocked by buildings, and vulnerable to interference, so many CPS must obtain timing through networks. The report extends this to spatiotemporal reasoning — a **time-space region** can be defined for cooperating nodes by enumeration or discovery, used to exclude interlopers, and used to advertise latency guarantees among nodes inside it.

- **Why microprocessors are no longer time-aware**: Timers and interrupts are the main explicit timing primitives, yet execution time of a code segment generally cannot be specified or controlled, and is often not even repeatable for identical inputs because of scheduling, caches, pipelining, and speculative execution. The consequence cascades: operating systems and mainstream programming languages also lack time-awareness, and modern general-purpose processors cannot, by themselves, meet deterministic or accurate TI requirements. Where exact constraints are required, designers fall back to FPGAs, ASICs, or custom logic in which time is explicit.

- **Hardware moving toward time-awareness**: Newer Ethernet PHYs carry hardware support for synchronized clocks via protocols such as IEEE 1588; PCI-SIG added Precision Time Measurement (PCIe-PTM) so peripherals and network time can be correlated across the CPU subsystem; and Intel added an Invariant Timekeeping instruction to measure the offset between the timestamp counter and a synchronized source more precisely. The report's recommendation is to push time-awareness down to the network and hardware layers and to adopt COTS parts that expose explicit time as they appear.

- **Logical vs. physical time**: Models reason in *logical time*, where a monotonically increasing software register orders events by causal relation rather than by wall-clock instant. At run time, logical time must be mapped to physical time on hardware that can enforce the real timing relationships — the essence of time-correctness by design and the gap that tools like PTIDES aim to close.

- **CPS Time Domain primary timescale behavior**: The domain's primary timescale must keep running monotonically and continuously through brief loss of its global reference, so that scheduled, time-sensitive transfer is never disrupted by an upstream GNSS outage — holdover, not a jump, is the required behavior.

- **Converged time-sensitive and best-effort traffic**: Time-awareness in standard Ethernet lets bounded-latency, time-sensitive streams share a network with traditional best-effort traffic. A time-aware device model carries timestamp units in the PHY/MAC, separate transmit/receive queues for time-sensitive versus best-effort data, a scheduled (time-triggered) transmit unit fed by the CNM's schedule, and application-layer pieces including timing/sync protocols (IEEE 1588, IEEE 802.1AS) and time-sensitive data mapping (the report cites CANopen as an example mapping protocol).

- **Secure timing rests on assurance plus resilience**: Section 4.4 frames secure time around source channel assurance, source data assurance, user-provided assurance, predictable failure, and availability/diversity. Source-side assurance means the channel's expected behavior is well characterized so deviations are caught quickly and the data prove the timing was not forged; user-provided assurance covers measures a user adds (such as anti-spoof GNSS techniques) when the source is unassured.

- **Byzantine fault tolerance for time**: Fault-tolerant timing must synchronize the non-faulty processes despite conflicting, erroneous, or missing temporal information — collectively Byzantine faults. Achieving it requires integrity and authenticity, predictable failure, and diverse, redundant traceable sources and paths, with each node having multiple paths to each source. Predictable failure depends on prompt, accurate fault detection (timeliness plus controlled true/false-positive rates), fault containment, and ideally self-stabilization that converges back to a correct state within bounded time and bounded slew rate.

- **GNSS jamming and spoofing**: Civil GNSS is the dominant worldwide timing source and is inherently vulnerable. Jamming denies the signal by illegally radiating energy in the navigation band — cheap low-power "personal privacy device" jammers are widely available — and robust receivers should enter predefined holdover or failure modes when it is detected. Spoofing injects counterfeit or recorded signals to falsify data or induce delay; the report notes that spoofing research and software-defined spoofers have been spreading. Navigation Message Authentication (NMA), digitally signing satellite data (advanced by Galileo, and considered for modernized civil GPS), is presented as an affordable, backwards-compatible baseline of source assurance.

- **Network-time attack vectors**: Packet-based distribution (PTP, NTP) faces common network threats, typically modeled as a Man-in-the-Middle or injector. The report's threat table names packet manipulation/replay, spoofing, rogue-master (Byzantine master) attacks, interception-and-removal that manipulates the best-master-clock election, packet-delay manipulation, flooding-based and interruption-based denial of service, master-time-source attacks (including GNSS jamming/spoofing), and cryptographic-performance attacks that exhaust a master with expensive validation work. Threats are passive (interception) or active (interruption, insertion, modification), and detecting passive precursors is itself a defense.

- **Network-time mitigations and their limits**: NTP uses the AutoKey protocol for end-to-end authentication, integrity, and replay protection; PTP, being hop-by-hop through transparent/boundary clocks for higher precision, is harder to secure but offers an experimental Annex K for group authentication, integrity, and replay protection. MACsec (hop-by-hop) and IPsec (end-to-end) can complement PTP. Key management at scale and residual exposure to cryptographic spoofing and insider attacks remain open problems, with standards work underway for source channel and data assurance in NTP and PTP.

## Mental Models

- **Treat time as a physical signal first, data second.** The report's foundational move is to remember that all timing carries a physical signal, optionally accompanied by descriptive data. Security, accuracy, and delay reasoning all split along this seam — channel versus data — so any timing design or threat analysis should be done on both halves separately.

- **Pick the weakest TI class that meets the requirement.** Reach for a Bounded TI when only a deadline matters (lowering the landing gear: failing late is failure, finishing early is fine). Escalate to a Deterministic TI when the *relationship* between events must repeat precisely on the system's own clock (fuel-injection timing tied to shaft position; acoustic or electromagnetic ranging where precision matters but absolute accuracy does not). Escalate to an Accurate TI only when spatial scale or regulation forces an internationally traceable scale (continental synchrophasor measurement against a TAI-synchronous reference; medical certification against international metrics). Over-specifying the class wastes hardware (e.g., demanding SI-traceable accuracy where a quartz oscillator at 0.1 % would do).

- **Push time down the stack, then verify upward.** The recommended architecture infuses time at the network and hardware layers — on-path clock-synchronization support in bridges and routers, time-aware PHYs and CPUs — so that the application can rely on time it did not have to reconstruct. The payoff is analyzability: when a scheduled converged network is realized with FPGAs alongside time-aware CPUs, architecture-specific tools can derive static upper (and sometimes lower) bounds on execution, because time is foundational throughout.

- **Decouple logical from physical time, enforce only at the boundary to physics.** Design and reason in logical time for ordering and causality, and require physical-time enforcement only where the system touches sensors and actuators. This is what lets a design persist across hardware upgrades, and it is the mechanism the report credits to modeling tools that map logical models onto synchronized hardware clocks.

- **For domains, prefer holdover over discontinuity.** A CPS Time Domain master should ride through a brief loss of its global source without introducing a time jump, because a discontinuity mid-operation is worse than running slightly free on a strong monotonic local clock. Correlate to international time, when needed, by maintaining an *offset* to a separate Global Traceable Timescale rather than by disciplining the primary scale directly.

- **Assume time will be attacked; design for predictable failure.** The resilient posture is multiple independent, assured, traceable sources with safe, predictable behavior when time is denied or visibly manipulated. Where assured and unassured sources coexist with common traceability, validate the unassured against the assured. The realistic near-term goal is to keep timing inside application tolerance for the *duration* of a compromise, detected early enough that the system rides through without performance impact.

- **Let the presence of system-wide time change the design, not just the implementation.** The report uses Google's Spanner — a worldwide database that replaces commit message-passing with reasoning over timestamps from synchronized clocks — to show that abundant, accurate time can shift design philosophy outright, echoing Liskov's earlier observation that NTP spurred using time to improve mainstream algorithms. The lesson for CPS: trading message-passing (which consumes bandwidth) for timestamp reasoning is a design lever, not merely a tuning knob.

## Anti-patterns

- **Timing-correctness as an afterthought rather than by design.** The report explicitly flags that the industry's current toolkit — time-triggered architectures, TDMA, PROFINET, IRIG-B, GNSS, IEEE 1588, FPGAs for local timing, and worst-case-execution-time (WCET) analysis — has a "conspicuously absent" member: timing-correctness *by design*. Relying entirely on after-the-fact verification is named as the gap, not the solution.

- **Treating execution time as a performance metric instead of a correctness criterion.** The report calls out that on modern general-purpose processors, execution time is at best construed as performance rather than as a correctness condition. Carrying that mindset into a CPS that has deterministic or accurate TI requirements is precisely the failure mode it warns against.

- **Ordering events from timestamps that are too close to distinguish.** Comparing two timestamps that lie within their resolution or within the timescales' synchronization error and then asserting an order is identified as a trap; the source insists another method (or finer, sparse-time granularity) must be used instead of trusting indistinguishable timestamps.

- **Isolating time behind a firewall without accounting for drift.** Using a network firewall to protect a CPS's time from the external network can let the internal and external clocks drift apart, degrading performance and in some cases causing failure at one or more levels — a named consequence of cutting time off in the name of security.

- **Naively "restoring" time after a compromise.** When GNSS or network time sources are compromised and synchronization errors appear, the report warns that trying to normalize or restore time services carries a high risk of introducing timing discontinuities and alignment problems — so recovery must itself be bounded and predictable rather than abrupt.

## Key Takeaways

1. Time is the bridge between cyber and physical, and the two worlds are fundamentally at odds: data systems earn efficiency through asynchrony, which is precisely what strips them of the time-awareness a CPS needs.
2. Express timing requirements as constraints on time intervals between significant events, then classify each as Bounded, Deterministic, or Accurate and use the weakest class that satisfies the requirement.
3. Frequency, phase, and time are distinct synchronization quantities; phase and time always require transporting signals and compensating for transmission delay, which is why position and time are coupled.
4. Choose a self-consistent local timescale unless spatial scale, cross-domain correlation, or legal/regulatory traceability forces an internationally referenced scale such as UTC or TAI.
5. Modern general-purpose microprocessors, operating systems, and languages are not time-aware; explicit, accurate timing today lives in FPGAs, ASICs, time-aware PHYs/CPUs, and time-triggered architectures.
6. "Time-correctness by design" — making time a hardware-independent abstraction, with logical time mapped to physical time enforced only at the boundary to physics — is the report's target for designs that survive upgrades and are correct by construction.
7. Bounded latency at scale needs structure: a CPS Time Domain with a continuous primary timescale and a CPS Network Manager that computes schedules and reserves bandwidth so time-sensitive and best-effort traffic can converge on one network.
8. Secure and resilient time is a first-class requirement built on source channel and data assurance, user-provided assurance, predictable failure, and availability through diverse, redundant sources and paths — assuming Byzantine faults and active attacks.
9. GNSS jamming and spoofing, and network-time attacks on PTP/NTP, are known critical-infrastructure vulnerabilities; mitigations (NMA, AutoKey, PTP Annex K, MACsec/IPsec, holdover, failover) exist but leave open problems in detection, key management, and insider threats.
10. The realistic near-term security goal is to detect compromise early and hold timing within application tolerance for its duration; recovery itself must be bounded to avoid introducing new discontinuities.
