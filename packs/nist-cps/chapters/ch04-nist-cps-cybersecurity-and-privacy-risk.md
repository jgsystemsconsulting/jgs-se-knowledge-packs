# Chapter — Cybersecurity and Privacy: Cross-Property Risk Management for CPS

> Source: NIST SP 1500-202 (the CPS Framework's Volume 2 of working-group reports), Section 2 — the Cybersecurity-and-Privacy Sub-Group's report on trustworthiness (Sections 2.1–2.3.5). All claims below are grounded in that slice.

## Core Idea

Cyber-physical systems bridge the digital and physical worlds out of heterogeneous, often distributed, co-engineered components. That bridging makes them *trustworthy* only in the broadest sense — reliable, resilient, secure, private, and safe all at once — and the classic move of treating each of those concerns as a separate engineering discipline no longer works. The sub-group's central argument is that the traditional, siloed handling of safety, reliability, privacy, cybersecurity, and resilience must give way to **cross-property risk management**: an integrated approach in which the interactions among these properties are reasoned about together, across the whole system lifecycle, because a compromise in one can directly defeat the others.

The reason this matters more for CPS than for pure IT is physicality. When a CPS is attacked, the consequences can be physically catastrophic — damaged capital equipment, harmed people, modified human behavior — not merely lost data or revenue. A cyber interface can become the path by which an attacker overcomes safety, reliability, privacy, security, and resilience provisions that were each designed in isolation. The Stuxnet worm is the canonical demonstration: a cyber payload reached into a physical process and tore it apart while feeding falsified status back to operators. Managing CPS risk therefore demands that cybersecurity be treated as one risk-management property among several co-equal ones, with trade-offs evaluated holistically against the system-of-systems objective rather than optimized property-by-property.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **The Trustworthiness Aspect of the CPS Framework.** Introduced as one of the nine Aspects within the CPS Framework defined in Volume 1 (NIST SP 1500-201). The source explains that this Aspect *emerged* from the Cybersecurity and Privacy sub-group's work once that group expanded its focus beyond security and privacy to include the intersection with safety, physical security, and reliability. Trustworthiness is defined as the demonstrable likelihood that a system performs to its designed behavior under any set of conditions, evidenced by characteristics such as safety, security, privacy, reliability, and resilience.

- **Cross-property risk management.** Named explicitly as the approach the document advocates (citation [1] in the source). It is framed as leveraging and extending the risk-management approaches from historically separate areas of expertise, so that design trade-offs and complementary decisions can be made across properties rather than within them.

- **The five top-level Trustworthiness management properties.** Section 2.3.1 defines them precisely: Cybersecurity (security), Privacy, Safety, Reliability, and Resilience. The sub-group's analysis of illustrative examples concluded that these five applied most broadly across the diverse breadth of CPS. Additional properties (e.g., physical security) may be added depending on system functionality and operational needs.

- **The risk-budget model.** Introduced in Section 2.3.3 as a co-design device. Rather than meting out a separate, fixed share of allowable performance impact to each property, the source argues the budget should be treated as a *common resource* on which every property can draw, with trade-offs required when the budget cannot cover all concerns. Allocation is situation-dependent and need not be apportioned equally.

- **The physical / analog / cyber component decomposition.** Section 2.3.3 frames CPS as composed of three kinds of elements — physical (materials science), analog (mechanical/electrical/kinetic, typically sensing), and cyber (logical/mathematical/computational). Co-design is defined as the correct allocation of requirements across these three element types to reach an optimum balance of cost, benefit, and risk.

- **Referenced principle-based privacy approaches.** The source names the **Fair Information Practice Principles** and **Privacy by Design** as the principle-based approaches organizations have used, and observes that while helpful, they have not delivered a consistent, repeatable methodology for identifying specific privacy risks — which is why NIST, MITRE, and others began developing privacy-engineering methodologies. The source also calls for establishing and professionalizing the **discipline of Privacy Engineering**.

## Key Concepts

**Risk versus uncertainty — reliability versus resilience.** The source draws a sharp definitional line. Reliability engineering addresses *risk*: situations where the distribution of possible outcomes from the system-environment interaction is known, and the system is expected to behave predictably in its anticipated operating conditions. Resilience addresses *uncertainty*: situations where that distribution is NOT known, often because the relevant environmental conditions are poorly understood — frequently because a malefactor has deliberately manipulated them. The document's pivotal observation is that a great many cybersecurity and privacy problems belong to resilience's domain of unknown distributions rather than to reliability's domain of known ones. (A footnote clarifies that throughout the document, "risk management" is used to cover both risk and uncertainty.)

**The CPS continuum.** Rather than a clean taxonomy, the source asks the reader to picture all CPS as a continuum. At one end sit safety-critical systems — those whose failure could cause loss of life, major property damage, or environmental harm (medical devices, aircraft flight control, weapons, nuclear systems). These are typically highly regulated, physically protected, and the product of deliberate design and heavy capital investment. At the other end sit consumer convenience and entertainment devices — cheap enough to be treated as disposable, assuming little restriction on access, and built in varied and sometimes unstructured environments. The unsettling point is that capabilities are converging across this continuum: a wearable or implantable medical device is safety-critical and somewhat regulated, yet has limited physical protection, is almost always accessible, and is produced like a consumer good.

**Three families of CPS-specific challenge.** The source organizes cybersecurity challenges into distinct buckets:
- *Overarching issues* — resilience as the dominant challenge; the system-of-systems nature of CPS (boundaries hard to pin down, upstream/downstream dependencies, shared ownership and shared responsibility); and extreme scalability driven by IoT growth that dwarfs current IT networks.
- *Interaction with the physical world* — attacks can be physically catastrophic; recovery time scales differ enormously (a website DoS may be recoverable in minutes, while a DoS against a power-generation control system can cause irreparable equipment damage taking months or years to replace); components often cannot all be physically secured; and processes are time-aware and deadline-sensitive, so security must fit within hard timing constraints and largely rules out human-in-the-loop response.
- *Operational constraints* — resource-constrained platforms demand lightweight security; in-place business processes (e.g., shared passwords on a shop floor for rapid emergency access across shifts) defeat conventional "strong password" hygiene; "always on" requirements make rebooting and patching non-viable; and dynamic domains of use (transportation, emergency response) break traditional key management when the valid-user population changes constantly.

**Lifecycle issues.** Some OT and infrastructure CPS run for 30 years or more and are hard to change; verification cost can lock owners into old technology. The source flags **orphaned devices** (no firm provides required support such as OS upgrades or patches) and **orphaned code** (code the system no longer uses but that remains present) as standing risks to any network they touch, and warns against **throwaway systems** left in the environment past their intended lifespan where they can be co-opted in an attack. The conclusion is that lifecycle threats demand a systems-engineering approach spanning the manufacturing process, supply chain, commissioning, operation, and decommissioning.

**Privacy as a distinct, physical-harm-capable concern.** The source insists privacy risk must be analyzed across the entire data lifecycle, from creation/collection through disposal — though it notes privacy may not be implicated at all in some cases (e.g., monitoring an industrial control system). The single most significant privacy factor is the sheer volume of data generated and analyzed: data that is not personal in isolation may become identifying once correlated. Crucially, a CPS privacy violation can lead to *physical* harm — inaccurate medical sensor readings, an incorrect medication dose from a compromised insulin pump, malfunctioning smart-car braking or acceleration. The source also surfaces data **leakage or "exhaust"**: information leaked simply by operating in a CPS environment, with high-frequency smart-meter data given as a worked example (collection moved from roughly monthly to every few minutes, which incidentally reveals occupancy, sleep, cooking, TV use, and even specific appliance signatures including medical equipment).

**Opportunities created by physicality.** The same physicality that complicates CPS security also enables defenses unavailable to open IT systems. Because physics often constrains CPS behavior, the normal operating range is frequently well understood, which can make anomaly detection easier. CPS tend to have well-defined network dynamics — stable user populations, regular communication patterns, limited protocols — that can be modeled to detect compromised nodes. Distributed CPS often provide multiple observations of the same phenomenon, which can be leveraged for data-integrity schemes. Newly added components can monitor, protect, or wrap older "comrades," and the redundancy already designed into safety-critical systems for reliability can be repurposed for resilience.

**The four design-response principle areas.** Section 2.2.4 organizes the design response around four areas: *Threats to Resilience* (threat vectors cannot all be known; cyber threats are co-adaptive and intelligent; supply chains are global and uncontrolled; obfuscation buys resilience against malicious threats but added complexity adds brittleness against non-malicious ones), *Cyber-Physical* (minimize shared interdependencies and localize process/security dynamics to prevent cascading failures), *Cognitive* (tailor information to the benign operator's role; build a model of expected behavior; obfuscate information accessible to a malicious human), and *Cyber-Physical-Cognitive* (quantify degradation within the distributed architecture; treat trust as earned through conformance metrics, not granted, with the highest correlation reserved for the highest-consequence functions).

**Cybersecurity's dual role.** Section 2.3.4 makes the subtle point that adding cyber components introduces new loci of faults and new threat vectors *and* a new locus of protection mechanisms ("smarts") that could never have been instantiated in the physical domain. Cybersecurity is necessary for privacy but not sufficient — privacy intrusions have causes beyond security breaches, and cybersecurity measures themselves (log retention, system monitoring) can create privacy risk. The source decomposes the attack surface that resilience-oriented cybersecurity must defend into availability, integrity (including the recognition that attacking the cyber subsystem can make the physical subsystem execute the wrong instructions), and confidentiality.

**Reliability versus cybersecurity, reframed.** Reliability mechanisms concentrate on detecting, protecting against, and mitigating component failures (fault tolerance) within a *predicted* set of conditions. Cybersecurity concentrates on detecting, preventing, and mitigating attacks and compromises — call it threat tolerance — whose full scope lies beyond what can be foreseen. The source argues that the seamless convergence of these two — reliability's fault tolerance and cybersecurity's threat tolerance — is what yields CPS resilience and the required level of safety.

## Mental Models

**The cyber component as the dominant draw on the system risk budget.** Physical and analog components can be tested rigorously with directly observable results; cyber components are hard and costly to test across the variety of configurations and conditions they may face. The mental model the source offers is that cyber components are therefore likely to consume a far larger share of the overall system risk budget than analog or physical ones — a fact engineers, owners, and operators should internalize when assessing risk and planning mitigations.

**The risk "blob" over the physical/analog/cyber elements.** The source's conceptual risk-model picture (its Figure 5) is worth carrying as a mental image even without the figure: a risk model has a shape representing *where risks are incurred* and a separate shape representing *where risk is assigned within the budget*. These assignments must rest on thorough knowledge of each component type's tolerances. A system-of-systems whose top-priority goal is safety should carry a risk model that visibly *favors* safety; the model also helps decide which element type — physical, analog, or cyber — should bear a given protection.

**Different component types, different risk tolerances — and the missing physical interlock.** A recurring model is that physical, analog, and cyber components have genuinely different tolerances for different sources and impacts of risk, and a designer can exploit that asymmetry. The source's own counterfactual on Stuxnet crystallizes it: had the centrifuges carried a physical or analog control that physically prevented over-speed regardless of any digital command, the cyber attack would have failed. The lesson is to push protection onto the component type best suited to enforce it.

**Per-property metrics, one holistic process.** A compact model for what each property "measures": security looks at operational and reputational risk, safety at error rates, reliability at failure rates, privacy at unwanted disclosure rates, and resilience at recovery rates. The point of listing them together is that the complexity, interconnectivity, and dynamism of CPS demand a single process spanning all of these — not five separate optimizations.

**Worked risk profiles across example systems.** Section 2.3.4 reasons through several systems to show how the property priorities shift:
- *Implanted medical device* — high safety and reliability; meaningful privacy (PII tied to the device); strong cybersecurity needed on command-and-control paths but lesser needs on reporting paths (where privacy protection may already cover the gap); and resilience that *cannot* rely on the usual redundancy/fail-over because of size and power limits — pushing toward frequent monitoring, scheduled replacement, or early degradation detection.
- *Chemical manufacturing plant* — high safety (process safety and equipment safety, e.g., keeping reactor pressure below burst limits), leaning on reliability and security; only cybersecurity can provide detection and protection against integrity (sensor-manipulation) attacks, DoS, and attacks on situational awareness (the HMI console); resilience that extends withstand time buys operators the window to intervene; privacy requirements low (little PII present).
- *Wearable computing and IoT* — requirements depend heavily on the application: fitness/life-tracking has low risk-property requirements but potentially significant privacy risk, while police or military uses demand high safety, security, and resilience.

The transferable model: there is no universal priority ordering; the profile is a function of consequence and context, and the design must reflect that profile.

## Anti-patterns

The source frames most of its cautions as challenges and recommendations rather than as named anti-patterns, but a few are stated sharply enough to read as named pitfalls:

- **Siloed (discipline-specific) risk management.** The source explicitly states that the properties have, for the most part, evolved within distinct silos, with property-specific leads holding discrete viewpoints and risk management conducted within each silo rather than across them — and names this as exactly what CPS design must move away from. Conducting risk-management planning and operations within discipline-specific silos is presented as the thing to break.

- **Dividing the risk budget into fixed per-property shares.** The source warns against meting the budget out as a separate share to each concern; the explicit corrective is to treat it as a common resource each property can draw on.

- **Orphaned devices, orphaned code, and lingering throwaway systems.** Named directly: devices no firm supports, unused code still resident in the CPS, and disposable-by-design systems never removed from the environment — each a standing risk that cannot be hardened against emerging threats and can be co-opted in an attack.

- **Relying on privacy notices and posted policies for transparency and consent.** The source observes that the efficacy of this traditional approach is already questioned even in conventional systems, and that the system-of-systems complexity of CPS makes such transparency even more limited — a method whose adequacy it explicitly doubts.

## Key Takeaways

- Trustworthiness in CPS is greater than the sum of trustworthy parts; it requires reasoning about safety, security, privacy, reliability, and resilience *together*, not as separate disciplines.
- Cross-property risk management is the named successor to classic, single-property cybersecurity: leverage and extend the risk approaches from formerly disparate fields, and make complementary cross-property design decisions.
- Five top-level properties — cybersecurity, privacy, safety, reliability, resilience — apply most broadly across CPS; additional properties may be added per system.
- Reliability handles known distributions of outcomes (risk); resilience handles unknown ones (uncertainty), and many security and privacy challenges live in the resilience domain.
- Physicality cuts both ways: it raises the stakes (catastrophic physical consequences, long recovery times, hard timing constraints that exclude humans-in-the-loop) but also enables physics-constrained anomaly detection, multi-viewpoint integrity checks, and reuse of safety redundancy for resilience.
- Treat the system risk budget as a shared resource, expect cyber components to consume the largest share of it, and allocate protections to the component type — physical, analog, or cyber — best able to enforce them.
- Cybersecurity is necessary but not sufficient for privacy; security measures can themselves create privacy risk, so privacy must be analyzed independently across the full data lifecycle.
- The Stuxnet case is the load-bearing illustration: a cyber payload manipulated a physical process to self-destruct while falsifying operator feedback, defeating safety, reliability, privacy, security, and resilience provisions that had been engineered in isolation. A simple physical over-speed interlock would have stopped it.
- CPS cybersecurity is no longer just confidentiality/integrity/availability prioritized and applied; it is a trade-off of risks, achievable only through co-design of cybersecurity (including privacy) with safety, reliability, and resilience.
- The recommended next steps point research toward attack-aware control and estimation algorithms (modeling deception and DoS attacks), detection of compromised sensors *and* controllers using the physical-system model, autonomous real-time response for safety-critical timing, adversary-model-based security definitions, joint estimation of system and network state, and combining physical/analytical redundancy with security principles like diversity and separation of duty.
