# Chapter — Assessment Reference: Auditor's Key Questions, Effects, and Common Agile Frameworks

## Core Idea

The reference appendices of the GAO Agile Assessment Guide (GAO-24-105506) translate the guide's best practices into something an evaluator can actually use at a desk: a structured bank of probing questions paired with explicit statements of what goes wrong when a practice is missing. The guide frames this as a *starting place* rather than a comprehensive checklist — auditors are told to first collect organizational, programmatic, and team-level documentation, familiarize themselves with the local Agile practices, and only then begin comparing observed conditions against criteria. A recurring procedural caution opens the whole section: before interviewing anyone, the audit team should agree among themselves on common terminology, because Agile vocabulary varies widely across organizations and programs.

The structure is deliberately two-sided. For each best practice the appendix supplies "Key considerations and questions" (what to ask) and "Likely effects if criteria are not fully met" (why it matters). This pairing is the chapter's organizing logic: a question without a consequence is just curiosity, and a consequence without a question gives the auditor nothing to investigate. The frameworks appendix then grounds all of this by cataloging the common Agile and Agile-adjacent frameworks a program might actually be running, so an auditor knows what "normal" looks like for the method in front of them. A final appendix dismantles ten persistent myths that auditors and program staff alike tend to carry into an assessment.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

The source's framework appendix opens by noting that the Agile Manifesto was published in 2001, that several frameworks predating it may have shaped it, and that some frameworks arrived in the 1990s — most notably the presentation of Scrum in 1995. After 2001, Kanban began folding in lean-manufacturing principles. The guide is candid that "Agile frameworks" is used loosely here as an umbrella that includes Agile-related approaches, some of which do not honor every Agile principle. The frameworks it details, in the order presented:

- **DevOps** — combines development and operations so a single team carries software from requirement through deployment and ongoing support, lowering the historical barrier between dev and ops teams. Its driving aim is frequent, small releases. The guide lists DevOps principles including automation of processes, a standardized environment (to reduce interoperability problems when new code meets the operations environment), microservices (small, decoupled components), and monitoring of the operational software. The guide also introduces **DevSecOps** as the extension that makes security a shared responsibility integrated into every stage of the workflow rather than a separate end-stage team.

- **Disciplined Agile (DA)** — a scaling framework meant to address the full IT product delivery process from program initiation through production deployment. The guide describes it as a hybrid, goal-driven process that borrows and tailors strategies from several methods at once — among them Scrum, XP (Extreme Programming), AM (Agile Modeling), AUP (Agile Unified Process), and Kanban. It defines primary roles (stakeholder, product owner, team member, team lead, architecture owner) and secondary roles (specialist, independent tester, domain expert, technical expert, integrator) used mainly when programs scale. Notably, DA's "stakeholder" splits into four sub-groups: customers, principals, partners, and insiders. Among its principles: putting people first, staying hybrid, covering delivery end-to-end, being goal-driven, and remaining enterprise aware.

- **Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)** — created in 1994; brings control and quality through an emphasis on transparency and communication, and can scale Agile for larger multi-team programs. It uses a defined four-phase life cycle: feasibility, foundations, evolutionary development, and deployment. The guide attributes the **MoSCoW** prioritization technique (must have, should have, could have, won't have this time) to DSDM, along with facilitated workshops and modeling. DSDM has eight principles, among them: focus on the business need, deliver on time, collaborate, never compromise quality, build incrementally from firm foundations, develop iteratively, communicate continuously and clearly, and demonstrate control.

- **eXtreme Programming (XP)** — short, iterative cycles with frequent releases; the guide credits Kent Beck with originating XP at Chrysler in 1996 and later publishing *eXtreme Programming Explained*. XP prescribes no formal roles, relying on self-organized, cross-functional teams that include the customer. Its activities are planning, managing, designing, coding, and testing, and it is the source of practices such as pair programming, small releases, simple design, collective code ownership, test-first development, and frequent integration. Its five values are communication, simplicity, feedback, courage, and respect.

- **Kanban** — focuses on smoothing the flow of work from commitment to delivery and on relieving teams and systems of overburdening. It uses a Kanban board (physical or virtual) and limits work-in-progress so bottlenecks become visible. The guide gives Kanban three basic principles: visualize what you do today (workflows), limit work-in-progress, and focus on flow (backlog prioritization). It traces Kanban's inspiration to lean manufacturing and notes no prescribed roles.

- **Lean Software Development** — combines lean-manufacturing and IT principles; the guide notes there is no single lean process and attributes the principles to Mary and Tom Poppendieck's book *Lean Software Development*. It characterizes Lean as related to but not a subset of Agile, and lists seven principles: eliminate waste, amplify learning, deliver fast, defer commitment, empower the team, build integrity in, and optimize the whole. The guide adds there are 22 practices/tools, citing value stream mapping, feedback from iterations, and pull systems with queuing theory as examples.

- **Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)** — a governance model for aligning modest-to-large numbers of teams across team, program, large-solution, and portfolio levels; the guide notes it references SAFe v6.0 as of July 2023. Teams at the program level form a "release train." Roles include scrum master/team coach, product owner, development team (three to nine contributors), Release Train Engineer, product management, system architect/engineer, and business owners. SAFe has ten principles, including take an economic view, apply systems thinking, assume variability and preserve options, build incrementally with fast integrated learning cycles, base milestones on objective evaluation of working systems, make value flow without interruptions, apply cadence and synchronize, unlock intrinsic motivation, decentralize decision-making, and organize around value.

- **Scrum** — described as the most widely used Agile framework. The guide traces its lineage to a 1986 Takeuchi and Nonaka text ("The New New Product Development Game") and notes Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland named it "Scrum" in the early 1990s and authored the Scrum Guide. Core structure: Scrum teams with three roles (product owner, development team, scrum master), self-organizing and cross-functional, working in time-boxed sprints with a sprint backlog drawn from the product backlog, daily meetings, and a burn down chart of remaining work. Scrum's three pillars are transparency, inspection, and adaptation.

- **Scrum@Scale** — a framework for managing multiple Scrum teams while minimizing bureaucracy. It groups four to five Scrum teams into a "Scrum of Scrums," repeats the pattern into a "Scrum of Scrum of Scrums" as the organization grows, and defines an executive action team responsible for building the Agile organization and setting its rules. It inherits Scrum's three principles.

- **Scrumban** — combines Scrum and Kanban, typically pairing the Scrum team structure with Kanban process principles; the guide frames it as more flexible than Scrum but more structured than Kanban. It borrows iterative planning, requirements prioritization, and structured teams from Scrum, and the pull system, WIP limits, and work visualization from Kanban.

The frameworks appendix sources its descriptions to GAO analysis of agency and private-sector material (Department of Justice, FAA, Booz Allen Hamilton, the Scrum Guide, Scaled Agile Inc., and the DSDM Consortium, among others).

## Key Concepts

**The question-and-effect pairing.** Every best practice in the auditor's appendix is treated as a testable claim. The "key considerations" are interview prompts; the "likely effects" are the program risks an auditor should expect to surface if the practice is weak. For example, against the practice that teams are self-organizing, the effect statement warns that non-self-organizing teams can become inefficient, raising cost and slipping schedule.

**Product owner empowerment as a single point of failure.** The slice repeatedly returns to the product owner. The questions probe whether one has been identified, how many teams they serve, whether they can prioritize the backlog, define acceptance criteria, judge whether criteria are met, and approve completed work — and whether they are reliably available. The effect statements are blunt: without a clearly identified, authoritative product owner, developers receiving conflicting signals may not know which features are priorities, delaying delivery; and a non-dedicated product owner forces developers to make assumptions that, if wrong, drive significant rework.

**INVEST user stories and relative estimation.** The questions on user stories explicitly ask whether stories are Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable, and whether they carry acceptance criteria and a clear definition of "done." On estimation, the auditor is steered toward relative-complexity techniques, affinity estimation, locking estimates once an iteration begins to study variance, and using prior estimates to inform future ones. The corresponding risk: teams that don't compare current sizing to historical completed work tend to over- or under-estimate.

**Backlog prioritization by value.** The guide names MoSCoW (must-have, should-have, could-have, would-like-to-have) as one prioritization approach and asks whether value is tracked, whether feature usage or customer satisfaction is measured, and whether the team compares value expected against value delivered. The risk of skipping this: developers build features that are not "must haves," delivering software that may go unused and contribute to cost and schedule overruns.

**Repeatable engineering processes.** Continuous integration, automated build and test, mechanisms for code quality, management of technical debt, daily standups, end-iteration demonstrations, and retrospectives are all probed as a single "repeatable processes" cluster. The guide defines technical debt as the accumulation of deficiencies over time and warns that, as a code base grows, additional functions come to rely on deficient code, degrading performance and pulling teams away from new features to clean up errors.

**Program controls compatible with Agile.** Critical (mission, architectural, safety) features and dependencies must be defined and incorporated early; non-functional requirements, including security, must be defined; and teams must maintain a sustainable development pace tracked through velocity. The guide warns that establishing critical architectural features late risks substantial rework and integration trouble, and that a team without a consistent pace cannot reliably use historical velocity to estimate future work.

**Organizational and acquisition enablers.** The appendix extends past the team to the enterprise: documented but tailorable acquisition and development processes, policies that allow changing requirements and frequent small deployments, goal alignment from the IT strategic plan down to the program, sponsorship that cascades through the organization, a supportive culture, incentives tied to working software rather than ancillary documents, and acquisition policies and contracts structured for Agile. A notable governance point: both the contracting officer's representative (COR) and the product owner must be government employees so they are empowered to make day-to-day decisions — if the product owner were not a government employee, they could not be empowered in that way, causing delays.

**Requirements development and management in Agile.** A separate cluster covers eliciting and prioritizing requirements (including non-functional ones), refining and discovering requirements over time, ensuring requirements are complete/feasible/verifiable (shared definition of done, acceptance criteria, definition of ready), balancing customer and user needs against constraints, testing and validating the system as it is built, managing change, and maintaining traceability from source requirements to lower-level requirements and back. The traceability effect is sharp: if a user story cannot be traced upward to the high-level requirements it serves, the program has no way to show oversight bodies that its commitments are being met or that the work is adding value.

**Contracting for Agile.** Modular contracting, flexibility in contract requirements, contract structure and type, Agile-aligned contract data requirements lists and metrics, oversight reviews tied to the Agile cadence, training of program-office and contracting staff, and clear roles all appear. A federal constraint recurs: the FAR and agency supplements require that contractors not perform inherently governmental functions, so responsibilities must be carefully delineated in the solicitation.

**Agile metrics.** The metrics chapter asks whether metrics address technical management, program management, and Agile methods; whether they are quantifiable, meaningful, repeatable, and actionable; whether they are delineated across organization, program, and team levels; and whether they are established early and aligned with incentives. It covers velocity and flow metrics, quality metrics such as post-deployment defects, the cost of technical debt, burn up/burn down charts, and the use of automated tools and dashboards. A standout caution: forcing a program to report Waterfall-based metrics impedes Agile adoption and gives an inaccurate view of the software development process, and management must itself demonstrate commitment to using metrics or others will not treat them as useful.

**Monitoring and control by reference.** For cost estimating, scheduling, and earned value management the appendix does not restate the checklists; it points to the companion GAO Cost Guide (GAO-20-195G) and GAO Schedule Guide (GAO-16-89G), where the detailed best-practice checklists and effect statements live.

## Mental Models

**Conditions versus criteria.** The auditor's whole method is to establish what the program actually does (conditions), gathered from documentation and interviews, and compare it to what good practice requires (criteria, supplied by these questions). The "likely effects" are the bridge that lets the auditor reason from a gap to a consequence the program owner will care about.

**Structure-and-flexibility as a tension to balance, not resolve.** The contracting and requirements material repeatedly frames the goal as balance: a contract needs enough structure to achieve mission outcomes while offering enough flexibility to adapt requirements within the agreed scope; a requirements-change process that is too rigid becomes a change-*prevention* process that starves user needs, while one that is too loose invites boundless development that never delivers the value required.

**The umbrella of "Agile frameworks."** Treat the framework catalog as a spectrum, not a taxonomy of pure types. The guide explicitly calls "Agile frameworks" a loose term that includes Agile-adjacent approaches (Lean, DevOps) which may not honor every Agile principle. SAFe can run Scrum, XP, or Lean underneath it; Scrumban is a deliberate blend; DA tailors strategies from several methods at once. An auditor should expect hybrids in the field.

**Lagging versus leading indicators.** The myths appendix supplies a useful model for schedule: working software is the primary measure of progress, so burn down/burn up trends are lagging indicators of schedule health rather than the schedule itself.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these explicitly through its "likely effects" and its myth-debunking, and they function as anti-patterns an auditor should watch for:

- **Frequently shifting people between teams.** The guide warns this undoes learning, shifts team dynamics and skills, and diminishes the team's ability to meet commitments.
- **Treating the daily standup as a management status update.** If used for status reporting instead of focusing on progress and impediments, the meeting can run too long.
- **Policy-mandated Agile imposed too early.** Using a mandate to force adherence to Agile principles does not produce healthy adoption; putting policies in place before transition mechanisms are solidified yields basic compliance without the cultural and mindset change a real transition requires.
- **Building every user story regardless of relative value.** If the product owner ignores relative value, all stories can end up developed just before deployment — a sign of not prioritizing — and this collapses badly if funding is cut mid-iteration or mid-program.
- **Delaying integration to just before release.** Teams that postpone integration will likely run out of time to test adequately, fix defects, and prepare infrastructure.
- **Adversarial acquirer–developer relationships.** Because federal acquisition is built on strong oversight, traditional acquisition can breed adversarial relationships; an Agile environment instead needs a climate of trust.

The myth-debunking appendix names ten misconceptions directly, each treated as a false belief to correct: Agile requires no documentation; requires no planning; requires no oversight; works only co-located; works only for small single-team programs; any framework guarantees success; requires no architecture; requires no risk analysis; cannot support a reliable schedule baseline; and is incompatible with earned value management. In each case the guide affirms a tempered version — Agile uses an appropriate level of documentation at time-boxed milestones, spreads planning across the life cycle, needs governance "guard rails," can succeed when distributed if carefully managed, scales by dividing larger systems among teams, must be chosen program-by-program, simplifies rather than eliminates upfront design, still requires explicit risk management, can use a baseline created alongside rolling-wave planning, and can use a tailored EVM approach.

## Key Takeaways

- The appendices convert best practices into an operational audit instrument: paired interview questions and explicit consequence statements, intended as a starting point and preceded by agreeing on shared terminology.
- The product owner's authority and availability is the most heavily probed risk in the entire question bank; its absence cascades into wrong priorities, developer assumptions, and rework.
- User stories should be INVEST-shaped with clear acceptance criteria and a definition of done; estimation should be relative and informed by historical data; backlogs should be prioritized by value (MoSCoW being one named technique).
- Repeatable processes — continuous integration, automated testing, technical-debt management, standups, demos, retrospectives — are audited together, and technical debt is explicitly defined as a compounding liability.
- Assessment extends well beyond the team into goal alignment, sponsorship, culture, incentives, acquisition policy, contract structure, and metrics; the COR and product owner must both be government employees to hold real decision authority.
- The framework catalog (DevOps/DevSecOps, DA, DSDM, XP, Kanban, Lean, SAFe, Scrum, Scrum@Scale, Scrumban) gives auditors the baseline of what each method normally looks like; "Agile frameworks" is a loose umbrella that includes hybrids and Agile-adjacent approaches.
- Metrics must be meaningful, repeatable, and actionable across organization/program/team levels, established early, aligned with incentives, and never forced into Waterfall reporting molds.
- The ten myths supply ready counters to the most common objections an auditor will hear, from "Agile needs no documentation" to "EVM is incompatible with Agile."
