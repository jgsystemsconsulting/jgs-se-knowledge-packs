# Chapter — Agile and Program Monitoring and Control: WBS, Cost, Schedule, and EVM

## Core Idea

Program monitoring and control exist so that legislators, agency officials, and the public can judge whether a government program is meeting its goals. Most of the established controls — the work breakdown structure (WBS), cost estimating, scheduling, and earned value management (EVM) — grew up around Waterfall development, where the big picture is planned in detail up front. Agile flips that emphasis: the most attention and detail go to the current iteration, and the long view is deliberately left coarse. The Guide's argument is that this is a tension to be managed, not a contradiction that excuses abandoning oversight. Every federal program, whatever its development framework, must establish a budget, be accountable for delivering a value-based outcome, and pass through the federal budgeting process. So the traditional controls still apply — they just have to be *adapted* to where Agile keeps its stability.

The unifying mechanism is the WBS. It is the framework agencies use to break work into manageable pieces, and it is the common, product-oriented structure that feeds all three downstream controls: cost, schedule, and EVM. The Guide's repeated, load-bearing move is to push monitoring up to the **epic and feature levels**, where Agile work is stable enough to baseline, while letting user stories and tasks churn underneath as quantifiable backup data. Detailed checklists live in the companion guides; this chapter shows how each control maps onto Agile artifacts already produced by the team — the road map, the prioritized backlog, velocity, and burn up/down charts.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **Work breakdown structure (WBS) in an Agile environment.** The product-oriented, hierarchical parent/child decomposition of the total program scope. The Guide ties it to the Agile hierarchy of **epics, features, and user stories** and insists the WBS be derived from existing Agile artifacts (e.g., the road map) to preserve traceability between program controls and Agile planning. Figures 13 and 14 (source exhibits, not reproduced here) show the WBS gaining detail over time and its relationship to the road map.
- **GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (the "Cost Guide," GAO-20-195G)** and its **12-step cost estimating process.** Cited as the consistent, best-practice methodology federal agencies use to develop, manage, and evaluate a cost estimate; the chapter walks all 12 steps and maps each to an Agile cadence in its Table 10.
- **The four characteristics of a reliable cost estimate** — *well-documented, comprehensive, accurate, credible* — drawn from the Cost Guide and re-read for Agile in the chapter's Table 11, which pairs each characteristic with example Agile artifacts.
- **GAO Schedule Assessment Guide (the "Schedule Guide," GAO-16-89G, 2015)** and its **10 scheduling best practices**, collapsed into four characteristics of a sound schedule — *comprehensive, well-constructed, credible, controlled.* The chapter's Table 13 maps all ten practices to the Agile environment and supporting artifacts.
- **Integrated master schedule with rolling wave planning.** The Guide names the integrated master schedule as the vehicle that must capture both Agile and non-Agile tasks across the whole program, planned in detail only for near-term work.
- **The 13 fundamental EVM activities** (from the Cost Guide) and the **three characteristics of a high-quality, reliable EVM system** — *comprehensive, accurate, informative* (the chapter's Table 14). Table 15 maps the 13 activities onto Agile execution.
- **Value Stream Mapping (VSM)** — introduced as a named alternative performance-tracking method: a flowchart of the full sequence of steps, people, systems, and information flows used to deliver value to a customer, used to reduce hand-offs and cycle time. Attributed to GAO analysis of DOD and Scaled Agile, Inc. information.
- **The Section 809 Panel** (the Advisory Panel on Streamlining and Codifying Acquisition Regulations, authorized under the FY2016 NDAA). The Guide notes the Panel recommended eliminating EVM *requirements* for Agile programs while still insisting on "appropriate project monitoring and control methods."
- **Earned value measurement techniques named for Agile use:** the **percent complete** method at the feature level and the **0/100 method** at the user-story level (full credit only when a story is 100 percent done — which the Guide notes aligns naturally with the Agile definition of "done").
- **Quantifiable backup data (QBD)** — defined as the information used to gauge a capability's progress from the technical completion of each feature, judged against that feature's acceptance criteria. This is the bridge between volatile story-level Agile data and feature-level EVM.
- **The NDIA Integrated Program Management Division's 2022 industry practice guide** (published Dec. 9, 2022) covering how to run Agile on earned-value-management programs — the source for the baseline-change examples and the **freeze period** concept. The **DoD EVM System Interpretation Guide (EVMSIG)** is cited alongside it on the importance of performance-measurement-baseline stability during that period.

Three case studies anchor the chapter and are summarized under Anti-patterns and Mental Models: **Case Study 18 (FEMA Grants Management Modernization, GAO-19-164)**, **Case Study 19 (F-35 Joint Strike Fighter Block 4, GAO-23-106047)**, and the **Agile in Action** vignettes on DHS simple function point estimating and NNSA G2 CPIC reporting. ## Key Concepts

- **The WBS is the shared spine.** One product-oriented, hierarchical WBS containing the total program scope feeds the cost estimate, the schedule, and the EVM system. It decomposes the effort into discrete, measurable, outcome-focused work products and shows how the Agile development effort relates to the rest of the program — program management, hardware, software licenses, and so on. As more is learned (e.g., during iteration or release planning), features are added under the right parent level and tied back to Agile documents, deepening traceability.
- **Three Agile levels, three roles.** An **epic** captures a high-level capability, is broad and short on detail, and usually spans more than one or two iterations before being split into smaller stories. A **feature** is a chunk of work completable within one or two reporting periods, described in enough detail to stay stable through development — and it is the level that should be tracked in the life cycle cost estimate and schedule, fully traceable to the road map. A **user story** is the smallest, most volatile unit; it can be added or removed without changing overall feature scope, is weighted with story points, and only enters the WBS after release/iteration planning, traceable to the prioritized backlog.
- **Monitor high, let the bottom churn.** Because user stories and tasks are flexible and unstable by design, the Guide directs that the majority of traditional monitoring-and-control practice be maintained at the *epic or feature level*. Lower-level data is not discarded; it becomes quantifiable backup data supporting the feature-level numbers.
- **The 12-step cost process is mostly sequential but partly iterative.** Steps 3 through 7 — defining the program, settling the estimating structure, fixing ground rules and assumptions, gathering data, and producing the point estimate (then checking it against an independent estimate) — can be done in varying order or concurrently. The Guide stresses that Agile's iterative cadence is an *advantage* here: every iteration generates fresh data to keep the estimate current. Even though Agile lowers technical risk through incremental delivery, sensitivity, risk, and uncertainty analyses still belong; even though Agile values working software over documentation, the cost estimate's assumptions still must be documented.
- **Consistent sizing versus relative estimating.** Developers use relative methods (story points, t-shirt sizing) to plan iterations, but those vary team to team and even within one program, so they cannot be normalized into a program cost estimate. Cost estimators use *consistent sizing* (source lines of code, function points) at a higher level for cross-program comparison. The two coexist: after a release, estimators establish the size and productivity achieved, then work with developers to relate delivered features back to the consistent metric — building, over time, an Agile program database for future estimates. The Guide prefers using two sizing methodologies over one when feasible.
- **Velocity is a refinement input, not a target.** Velocity measures how much work a team delivers per iteration. Because it is team-specific, it must never be used to dictate how much a team *should* do — but a team's traceable velocity is a legitimate input to refine a cost estimate once development is under way.
- **Schedule the whole program, in detail only near-term.** Agile plans the next iteration in detail and leaves the rest coarse, but the program still needs a vision and a release plan. The integrated master schedule must capture *all* planned features (government and contractor, Agile and non-Agile) using rolling wave planning, so it can serve as summary, intermediate, and detailed schedule at once. Without all features planned for the program's duration, the schedule loses horizontal and vertical traceability, a valid critical path cannot exist, and schedule risk analysis becomes invalid.
- **Time-boxing is not the same as constraints.** Fixed-length iterations (typically 2-4 weeks) tempt schedulers to model start/end dates with hard date constraints — but constraints reduce the schedule's value as a coordination tool, can cause unrealistic resource allocations, break horizontal traceability, and interfere with deriving a valid critical path. Use constraints only when necessary and justified in the documentation; otherwise let dates respond dynamically.
- **Critical path and float live at epic/feature level.** For an Agile program the critical path may run across multiple epics and features and include non-Agile tasks; it is managed during iteration planning and daily standups. Total float — the amount an activity can slip before the program finish date moves — is likewise tracked at the epic and feature levels, not at individual user stories.
- **EVM is not mandatory everywhere, but the concept travels.** EVM is often required above a threshold (DoD requires compliance for cost or incentive contracts at or above $20 million, per the FAR and OMB Circular A-11), but, just as it is not applied to every traditional program, it should not be forced onto *small* Agile programs where the implementation cost outweighs the value. On medium and large programs it can and should be tailored in.
- **EVM mechanics for Agile.** The WBS for EVM should sit at the feature (sometimes epic) level, mirroring the integrated master schedule. The performance measurement baseline's smallest building block is the **control account**, each based on a feature or group of features, with a control account manager owning the work — consolidated during iteration/release planning. Earned value is taken at the feature level via percent complete, derived from completed user stories and their story-point weight using the 0/100 method per story. The worked example: a feature of 16 story points is 31 percent complete once a 5-point story finishes (5 of 16).
- **Variances and estimates-at-complete in effort terms.** When the WBS is at the feature level, cost variance is the gap between planned and actual hours for the feature (a feature planned at 100 hours that takes 200 has a −100-hour cost variance); schedule variance is the gap between planned value and value earned over the planned iterations (50 of 100 hours earned after three iterations is a −50-hour schedule variance). The Guide presents the estimate-at-complete with cost and work replaced by *effort*: completed work plus remaining work divided by an efficiency index, where for a feature the efficiency index is total hours expended over total story points for completed iterations. Program-level EAC can roll up epic-level estimates, or use program velocity (completed weighted story value across teams divided by iterations completed). Multiplying effort hours by an average labor rate converts effort to cost. These roll-ups assume consistent story-point schemes across teams; where schemes differ, prefer feature-level data.
- **Baseline change control protects measurement, not Agile flexibility.** A disciplined process to manage baseline changes preserves the integrity of the performance baseline so credible measurement can occur. The Guide is explicit that a *properly designed* change process does not restrict reprioritizing the backlog from iteration to iteration. Its three worked examples: (1) moving an unstarted feature to a future release re-plans it and returns its stories to the backlog — but if the feature's baseline start is inside the **freeze period**, control mechanisms apply; (2) carrying unfinished stories into the next release is usually *not* a baseline change, though missing the planned release creates a schedule variance and possibly a cost variance; (3) the product owner removing scope from a feature *is* a baseline change — finish the reduced feature and reassign the freed budget.
- **Rework needs its own budget.** Modifying or revising existing code (without changing functionality) is real work; the performance measurement baseline and integrated master schedule should carry adequate budget and schedule for rework so it appears in EVM. Some programs put rework in a separate planning package; alternatively, earned-value adjustments can reflect incomplete features or ongoing rework.
- **Reporting can ride on data you already have.** Much of the data Agile generates can support required EVM and oversight reporting (e.g., OMB CPIC), so reporting need not be onerous — though, as the NNSA G2 vignette shows, mapping an Agile cadence onto a project-based reporting structure can require deliberate proration and clear narrative explanation of resulting variances.

## Mental Models

- **The WBS is a living tree, not a one-time chart.** It starts coarse — high-level capabilities from the road map — and grows downward as iteration and release planning reveal features. Because it is built from Agile artifacts rather than alongside them, traceability is a property of how it is constructed, not an audit done afterward.
- **Stability lives at the top, change lives at the bottom.** Picture a waterline across the WBS: above it (epic/feature) the work is stable enough to baseline, schedule, and earn value against; below it (stories/tasks) the work flexes freely. Push every control up to that line and treat the churning detail beneath as backup evidence, not as the thing you baseline.
- **"Just-in-time" estimating.** Cost estimating for an Agile program is an ongoing activity tightly integrated with developers and engineers, not a one-shot deliverable. Fidelity is low until teams find a rhythm and improves release over release as productivity data accumulates. A rough order of magnitude estimate early is fine; precision is earned.
- **Proximity with professional distance.** Estimators should attend Agile ceremonies — standups, retrospectives, release planning — to understand team dynamics and the backlog, *and* retain objectivity through independent, alternate estimating methods, especially when the estimate will judge contractor performance or cost-proposal reasonableness. Integration improves quality; distance preserves credibility.
- **A baseline that moves with actuals stops measuring anything.** The F-35 Block 4 case (GAO-23-106047) is the cautionary image: an EVM system that "generally followed best practices" was still hard to interpret because frequent baseline changes from new scope meant the program was measuring against a moving target. The PMB grew 56 percent (about $2.49B to $3.88B) and the completion date slipped two years; the metrics distorted, and forecasting outcomes became difficult. Scope flexibility for an *iteration* is healthy; an unstable *program* baseline defeats EVM's purpose.
- **Flexibility for the iteration, fixity for the program.** EVM still informs Agile decisions because, in government programs, scope flexes within an iteration or release but is *not* freely flexible for the program as a whole. Reframe the report accordingly: instead of monthly projected variances, fold an EVM read into the release retrospective showing the estimated cost and schedule overrun *if all backlog work is completed.* This reframing is what lets traditional EVM concepts land in an Agile rhythm.
- **The cut-off line makes trade-offs visible.** For a fixed budget and schedule, project a "cut-off" point showing which features can be accomplished (the Guide's figure has releases 1 and 2 fitting and release 3 falling outside funding). The value is not the line itself but the conversation it forces between customer and product owner about balancing scope, schedule, and budget to still meet minimum viable product needs.
- **Welcome change without becoming undisciplined.** Rolling wave planning embodies Agile's "welcome change," but the Guide is firm that welcoming change does not license ad hoc delivery. The vision fixes high-level cost, schedule, and scope goals and anchors the schedule baseline; product owners reprioritize *within* that frame at the end of each iteration. The baseline is the boundary inside which flexibility is safe.

## Anti-patterns

The source names these failure modes, chiefly through its two embedded case studies and the F-35 / FEMA findings:

- **Treating Agile as an excuse to skip schedule rigor** — In Case Study 18 (FEMA Grants Management Modernization, GAO-19-164), officials were "uncertain about the level of rigor" to apply to the schedule *because* they were using Agile. GAO's correction: program schedules must meet all the scheduling practices regardless of the development method. The program's September 2020 delivery date was an unsubstantiated, imposed date rather than one derived from a realistic assessment of development activities — leaving leadership unable to judge whether the goal was achievable or what cost increase or scope reduction delivery would require.
- **An estimate that no longer reflects the program** — Also in the FEMA case, key assumptions changed after the May 2017 cost estimate was approved (technical approach, more development personnel, data-migration delays and complexity), so the once-reliable estimate stopped reflecting the current approach and a cost breach followed. The lesson the Guide draws: the estimate must be refreshed as assumptions, milestones, and contracts change.
- **Measuring against a continuously changing target** — In Case Study 19 (F-35 Block 4, GAO-23-106047), frequent baseline changes from added scope, plus a concurrent stop-work order, produced baseline volatility that distorted EVM metrics; the program measured against a moving baseline rather than a static one, making cost variances hard to document or explain and program outcomes hard to forecast.
- **Tracking too low in the WBS** — Because Agile's lower levels change frequently, baselining and earning value at the user-story or task level yields volatile, low-value data and can mask serious performance issues. Keep EVM (and the integrated master schedule) at the feature or epic level; use story-level data only as quantifiable backup.
- **Over-using date constraints to model iterations** — Hard date constraints may look like a tidy way to fix iteration start/end dates, but they reduce the schedule's coordination value, can drive unrealistic resource allocations, break horizontal traceability, and prevent a valid critical path.
- **Defining EVM scope and budget below the feature level** — The Guide warns this introduces too much volatility and can mask serious performance problems; work-package scope and budget should be anchored at the feature level.

## Key Takeaways

- One product-oriented WBS, derived from Agile artifacts and built around epics → features → user stories, is the shared foundation for cost, schedule, and EVM; keep monitoring at the epic/feature level and treat story-level data as backup.
- Federal accountability does not bend to development method: every program needs a reliable cost estimate, a high-quality schedule, and — above the appropriate threshold — EVM, regardless of Agile.
- The GAO 12-step cost process, its four reliability characteristics, the Schedule Guide's 10 best practices (four characteristics), and the 13 EVM activities (three EVM-system characteristics) all map cleanly onto Agile artifacts — road map, prioritized backlog, velocity, burn up/down charts, retrospectives — so the controls are adapted, not abandoned.
- Cost estimating is a just-in-time, integrated, collaborative activity; integrate estimators with developers for quality while keeping independent methods for objectivity, and reconcile relative estimating (story points) with consistent sizing (function points, SLOC) for a defensible program estimate.
- EVM works in Agile when it is integrated with technical performance, tailored to the program, and reported in an Agile cadence (e.g., a retrospective view of total-backlog overrun), with feature-level percent-complete/0-100 measurement and effort-based EAC formulas.
- A disciplined baseline-change process — including a freeze period for in-progress work — is what reconciles Agile reprioritization with credible measurement; an unstable program baseline, not iteration-level flexibility, is what destroys EVM's value.
- Detailed best-practice checklists for cost, EVM, and scheduling live in the companion GAO guides (GAO-20-195G and GAO-16-89G).
