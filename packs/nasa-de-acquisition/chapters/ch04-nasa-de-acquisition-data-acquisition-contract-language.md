# Chapter — Data Acquisition Contract Language and Statement-of-Work Provisions

> Source: NASA-HDBK-1004, Appendix B ("Data Acquisition Contract Language"). This chapter pairs with NASA-HDBK-1009A; the legend marking, data-rights, and intellectual-property provisions summarized here are intended to be read alongside that companion handbook rather than as standalone legal text, and the Appendix itself directs all binding language through Legal and Procurement review before use.

## Core Idea
NASA repeatedly lost cheap, early leverage over contractor-produced data by leaving data requirements out of contracts and then trying to buy access back at the end of the period of performance. Appendix B's answer is to treat data as an asset and bake achievable, template-based data requirements into the front end of a procurement—principally the Statement of Work (SOW), the Data Requirements Description (DRD), and the Cost Volume—so that delivery formats, rights, metadata, transmittal methods, and end-of-contract transfer are priced and obligated at award rather than negotiated after signature when the government has no competitive lever left.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Data Acquisition Contract Language (NASA-HDBK-1004, Appendix B)** — A menu of selectable, tailorable contract clauses spanning rights, protection, format, metadata, transmittal, viewing, storage, workflow, administration, integration, and non-functional requirements. *When/how:* The Appendix explicitly tells the user to pick the categories and individual requirements that apply to a given contract and assemble them into the SOW; science-core and engineering-core contracts use data differently, so the options are meant to be chosen, not adopted wholesale.
- **Needs Assessment** — A survey-and-focus-group exercise that determined whether NASA's data-acquisition problems were Agency-wide or program-specific, and whose findings feed the drafting of future contract language. *When/how:* Run before contract language is finalized to establish high-level data requirements across the program life cycle.
- **Request for Proposal (RFP) data-language placement model** — A three-locus rule that the same data approach must appear in the **SOW** (functional/technical requirements, typically Section C of the government request package), the **DRD** (a template demanding the details the SOW asks for, submitted with the proposal), and the **Cost Volume** (data cost as a separate line item). *When/how:* Used during RFP/RFQ development; the rules belong in both the SOW and DRD because Contracting Officers often default to the SOW as the governing rule set.
- **Procurement Information Circular (PIC)** — An instrument that pushes the data-language requirement to all NASA procurements that have data requirements (excluding pure hardware/software buys), so Contracting Officers know to include it. *When/how:* The PIC routes responsibility to board chairs/members rather than the Contracting Officer, and ships with a guidelines document describing how and when to apply the language.
- **Intellectual Property Strategy** — An early-developed plan ensuring all technical data, computer software, and associated license rights needed for procurement and sustainment are available across the system's life cycle. *When/how:* Developed early in program/project management; supports sustainment activities such as re-bid, maintenance, repair, modification, interoperability, and technology insertion.
- **Model-Based Enterprise (MBE) Plan** — A contractor-provided, government-approved plan documenting the data-management infrastructure put in place and providing periodic reporting on data produced; required for contracts with data deliverables. *When/how:* Submitted on contracts requiring data deliverables (the Appendix cross-references its Appendix J).
- **Table 3 — Specific Contract Delivery Requirements** — A rights-category matrix mapping each license category to the data types it covers, the criteria that trigger it, and permitted uses inside and outside the government. *When/how:* Consulted when specifying delivery, because the standard FAR clauses do not by default compel delivery of technical data or software—specific delivery requirements must be written into each contract.
- **Earth Science Data and Information Policy (adopted language)** — Externally sourced policy promoting full and open data sharing, which the team reviewed and reused. *When/how:* Carried into the Appendix as guidance for ongoing Earth science contracts and as a template for open-sharing clauses.

## Key Concepts
- **Data (as scoped here):** The Appendix uses "data" as an umbrella term covering data, information, and knowledge held in an information system; clauses deliberately reach knowledge artifacts like work procedures, not just files.
- **Technical Data (TD) vs. Computer Software (CS):** Data rights split into two intellectual-property categories—TD (recorded scientific/technical information such as design or maintenance data and software documentation) and CS (executable code, source, listings, design detail, processes, flow charts, and related material).
- **License-rights ladder:** Unlimited Rights, Government Purpose Rights, Limited Rights (TD only), Restricted Rights (CS only), Specifically Negotiated License Rights, SBIR Data Rights, and Commercial TD/CS licenses—each gated by who funded development and whether the item is commercial or noncommercial.
- **Funding-source determinism:** The government's license rights generally track how much the government funded the development; some categories (form-fit-function data, and operation/maintenance/installation/training data, excluding detailed manufacturing and process data) qualify for Unlimited Rights regardless of funding source.
- **Delivery is not automatic:** FAR clauses do not by default require TD or CS delivery; the contract must state delivery requirements explicitly, and mere access may not protect the government's interests.
- **Deferral instruments:** Priced options, the deferred delivery clause (DFARS 252.227-7026), the deferred ordering clause (DFARS 252.227-7027), and the Data Accession List (DAL) let the government postpone delivery or ordering until needs are known, while still locking the option in at award.
- **Single authoritative source:** Delivered data should land in one authoritative source acquired through contract, with DRDs specifying named electronic formats instead of "contractor format acceptable"—and with format/transaction definitions kept independent of the access or delivery method.
- **Standardization targets:** Solicitations should drive consistency across systems, minimal redundant collection, audit-trail accessibility and rapid retrieval, effective metrics analysis, and improved acquisition timelines—triggered when reuse, interoperability, data-relationship needs, or contract closeout are in play.
- **Open-data alignment:** Public-access and reuse provisions tie to the Open Data Policy, "Managing Information as an Asset" (OMB M-13-13), with release governed by the OCIO's internal clearance processes.
- **Format obligations:** Proprietary-format data requires the native file plus a commonly readable representation (e.g., PDF/A, PNG); structured data requires schema, database type/version, and W3C XML-family or OMG XMI standards; documents require text-searchable PDF/A or Office formats; metadata must be machine-readable, cataloged, and standards-based (the Appendix names ISO 10303-233 and ISO 10303-239 as metadata foundations and requires metadata be defined 90 days before first use).
- **Transmittal modes:** Five selectable mechanisms—view-in-place in the contractor system, direct entry into a NASA system, upload to a NASA repository, system-to-system connection (REST/Web API, SOAP middleware, or approved standards-based API), and secured transmittal using encryption per the NIST 800 series.
- **Security baselines:** Categorization via NIST SP 800-60, controls via NIST SP 800-53 and NPD/NPR 2810.1, platform compatibility via NASA-STD-2804, authentication passed with at least 256-bit encryption, no anonymous or impersonated actions, and full backups with roll-back.
- **Acceptance Data Package (ADP) vs. Technical Data Package (TDP):** ADPs are working, assemblable data views; TDPs are explicitly tagged, treated as static historical documents, and carry creation metadata—a "view" form of TDP can store construction criteria without freezing content so it refreshes dynamically.

## Mental Models
- **Buy the data while the bidders are still competing.** The cheapest moment to obtain access, rights, and transfer terms is at the RFP/front end, when offerors compete against each other; once the contract is signed and the contractor controls the data, every later request is a sole-source negotiation. The Appendix's recurring theme is that omitting data requirements converts a competed commodity into an end-of-contract ransom.
- **Put the rule in two places or lose it.** Because Contracting Officers tend to treat the SOW as the binding rule set and may underweight the DRD, the same data approach must live in both the SOW and the DRD; relying on the DRD alone risks the obligation being ignored.
- **Price the data line so it survives cost-cutting.** Data deliverables historically vanished during cost-cutting exercises. Forcing data cost into the Cost Volume as a separate line item fixes the price at award and protects it—critical on fixed-price contracts where later changes are most expensive.
- **Separate "what the data is" from "how you get it."** Define data formats and transaction sets independently of access or delivery method, so the same data definition can be viewed in place, uploaded, or pushed system-to-system without re-specifying the content.
- **Pick the lowest license category that still meets the mission.** Use Table 3 to match each data type to a rights category by funding source and commerciality, and negotiate special license rights only where the standard categories fail both parties—never accepting less than Limited Rights in noncommercial TD or Restricted Rights in noncommercial CS.
- **Assert restrictions up front, then evaluate.** Resolve data-rights friction by requiring offerors to assert all restrictions on deliverable TD and CS in their proposals, evaluating what is offered, and negotiating special licenses where needed—rather than discovering proprietary claims at closeout.

## Anti-patterns
- **Omitting data requirements entirely.** The Appendix names this as the primary lesson learned: simply leaving data requirements out of a contract makes access during the contract and at its end obtainable only at extra cost.
- **Accepting "best available" or "existing contractor data."** Contracts that specify the most economic-seeming "best available data" or accept existing contractor data to the maximum extent (as the Launch Services Program did) often turn out proprietary or costly to extract when the government later needs it.
- **"Contractor format acceptable" language.** DRDs that accept contractor format instead of named electronic formats undermine reuse and interoperability; the Appendix directs specifying delivered transaction scope, formats, metadata, naming, and exchange standards instead.
- **Letting contractors classify deliverables as proprietary or trade-secret to avoid sharing.** The ISS Payload Processing example—work procedures developed under contract but deemed to contain "trade secrets" and never turned over—shows how unaddressed proprietary claims defeat closeout transfer; proprietary and IP rights must be addressed so data cannot be labeled proprietary purely to avoid sharing.
- **Deferring data decisions to the end of the contract.** End-of-contract changes cost significantly more; the SSP spent millions researching and obtaining data because design data and drawings were never made contractual deliverables.
- **Treating mere access as protection.** Relying on access alone may not protect the government's interests; without explicit delivery requirements (and a priced option where needs are uncertain), access can evaporate.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Embed data requirements at the front end.** The most efficient and least expensive route to obtaining data is achievable requirements in the RFP or contract front end; everything deferred to closeout costs more.
2. **Acquire the minimum essential, integrated set.** NASA should acquire only the essential contractor-originated data needed for the program life cycle, integrating safety, reliability, quality, logistics, test, and verification specialties (and sub-projects) to remove redundancy and inconsistency.
3. **Triangulate SOW, DRD, and Cost Volume.** Put the functional/technical data requirements in the SOW, demand the details in the DRD, and force the cost into the Cost Volume as a separate line item so price is set at award.
4. **Specify rights deliberately using Table 3.** Match each TD/CS deliverable to a license category by funding source and commerciality; require up-front assertion of all restrictions and negotiate special licenses only where standard categories fail.
5. **Mandate named, standards-based formats and machine-readable metadata.** Require specific electronic formats with schemas, database type/version, and standards-based metadata (with ISO 10303-233/-239 as a foundation and metadata defined ahead of first use) rather than accepting contractor format.
6. **Obligate end-of-contract transfer explicitly.** Require contractor applications to be NASA-owned and fully transferable (source code and documentation included), and schedule a pre-closeout meeting to plan the transfer of all data, information, and knowledge.
7. **Anchor protection to recognized security baselines.** Tie categorization, controls, encryption, platforms, backup, and access to NIST SP 800-60/800-53, the NIST 800 series, NASA-STD-2804, and NPD/NPR 2810.1, with no anonymous or impersonated actions.
8. **Institutionalize the requirement via a PIC and trained POCs.** A Procurement Information Circular pushes the language across applicable procurements, accompanied by guidelines and Center CIO points of contact trained to guide the boards.

## Connects To
- **NASA-HDBK-1009A** — The companion handbook this Appendix is meant to be read alongside; the rights, legend-marking, and IP provisions summarized here are partial and should be paired with that handbook plus Legal/Procurement review before any binding use.
- **FAR / NFS clauses (e.g., 48 CFR §52.227-11 through -20; FAR 52.227-14 Alts II & III)** — The Appendix supplements existing FAR and NASA FAR Supplement clauses and requires marking less-than-unlimited-rights data with the appropriate legend; government drafters must distinguish FAR guidance from clauses incorporated by reference that are contractually binding.
- **DFARS 252.227-7026 / 252.227-7027 and the Data Accession List** — Deferred delivery and deferred ordering clauses, plus the DAL, used to postpone delivery or ordering of TD/CS until government needs are known.
- **OMB M-13-13, Open Data Policy ("Managing Information as an Asset")** — Governs public-access, reuse, and clearance provisions for data released through the OCIO's internal clearance processes.
- **NIST SP 800-60, SP 800-53, and the NIST 800-series** — Drive security categorization, recommended controls, and encryption requirements for systems holding the acquired data.
- **NPD 2810.1 / NPR 2810.1 and NASA-STD-2804** — NASA information-security policy and the Minimum Interoperability Software Suite that protection and platform-compatibility clauses reference.
- **ISO 10303-233 (AP233, Systems Engineering) and ISO 10303-239 (AP239, Product Life Cycle Support)** — Named foundations for defining common program and systems-engineering metadata.
- **NASA Earth Science Data and Information Policy** — Source of the full-and-open-sharing language carried forward as guidance for ongoing Earth science contracts (no exclusive-access period, non-discriminatory access, release of standard products with source code and ancillary data).
