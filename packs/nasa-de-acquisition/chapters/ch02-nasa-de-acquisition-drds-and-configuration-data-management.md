# Chapter — Data Requirements Descriptions: DRD Framework, Technical Data Package, MBx Models, and Configuration & Data Management

## Core Idea
A digital or model-based acquisition succeeds or fails on whether the data a contractor delivers can flow into the receiving organization's tools with little to no rework. NASA-HDBK-1004 Appendix A answers this need with a library of reusable, example Data Requirements Descriptions (DRDs): each one is a single contractual instrument that specifies exactly what a deliverable data item is, why it exists, what it must contain, what format it takes, what compliance standards govern it, how it is maintained, and when it is submitted. The DRD is the contract-level translation of "we want models, not just documents" into enforceable obligations. Appendix A bundles a baseline set covering the technical product description (the Technical Data Package and its constituent MBx engineering models) together with the configuration-and-data-management machinery (planning, status accounting, audits, change control, and the trees and lists that tie them together) so an acquirer can assemble a coherent, neutral-format, model-based data acquisition rather than a pile of incompatible vendor exports. The whole appendix is explicitly a starter kit: every DRD and its content must be selected and tailored by the acquiring program or project, and acquirers are pushed to favor Agency-approved or endorsed standards and neutral formats over proprietary vendor formats wherever they can.

## Frameworks Introduced

- **Data Requirements Description (DRD)** — the atomic unit of Appendix A (Section A.2). A DRD fully describes one deliverable data item (a model, database, object, document, report, and so on) by stating its purpose, content, format, compliance standards, maintenance approach, and submittal requirements that bind the contractor. *When/how:* invoke each DRD through relevant Statement of Work (SOW) or Performance Work Statement (PWS) language in the matching section; use it for contract data requirements, or tailor it for an inter-Agency (Supplier) agreement. The design goal is that the delivered item can be assimilated into the receiver's systems with little or no post-receipt processing beyond verification.

- **Data Procurement Document (DPD)** — the assembly that gathers all DRDs for one contract into a single package (Section A.2). *When/how:* the DPD is attached to the contract and acts as a companion to the SOW or PWS. Each DRD carries a unique DRD number built from the three- or four-digit DPD number, a two-letter Data Category designation, and a sequential three-digit number within that category.

- **Data Category Designations (Table 1)** — a two-letter taxonomy that classifies every DRD by discipline (Section A.2). *When/how:* used to construct the DRD number and to route the item to the right organization. Categories include CD (Contractual Data), CM (Configuration Management), DE (Design and Development Engineering), DM (Data Management), SE (Systems Engineering), SW (Software), QE (Quality Engineering), SA (Safety), and others; categories may be tailored per task.

- **Data Types for Contractor Deliverables (Table 2)** — a five-level scale (Types 1–5) that sets the level of Government control over a delivered item (Section A.2). *When/how:* Type 1 requires written Government approval before any release or use, and is the designation to choose when contractor data must enter the Center baseline under Configuration and Data Management (CDM) control. Type 2 gives NASA a time-limited right (default 45 calendar days, tailorable) to disapprove in writing before release. Type 3 is delivered as the contract requires and needs no approval, but still must meet all contractual requirements on time. Type 4 is retained by the contractor and delivered only on written NASA request (and needs FAR 48 CFR § 52.227-16 in the contract to compel delivery). Type 5 is incidental data the contractor keeps, accessible to the Contracting Officer or their Representative on inspection.

- **Technical Data Package (TDP)** — the DRD (Section A.3, DRD STD-TDP) that defines the complete technical description of an item: its as-designed product configuration, performance requirements, and the procedures needed to assure performance. *When/how:* the TDP leans heavily on the DoD standard MIL-STD-31000 to set requirements for the deliverable data products. It is delivered in maturing levels keyed to life-cycle reviews — Conceptual, Developmental, Product, and Commercial — and its data are ingested into the Government's digital environment to seed the as-received ("as-sustained") Product Baseline.

- **Engineering Model-Based x (MBx) Models and Associated Data** — the DRD (Section A.4, DRD STD-MBx) that delivers engineering digital data built on model-based definition (MBD) inside a model-based enterprise (MBE). *When/how:* it specifies the design, analysis, and manufacturing models — CAD, CAE (including FEM/FEA and SysML), and CAM — sufficient to build the detailed configuration of every system, subsystem, and component level, plus an indentured Product Structure for each configuration item. Delivered as part of the TDP, typically four weeks before each major review.

- **Configuration and Data Management Plan (CDMP)** — the DRD (Section A.6, DRD STD-CDMP) describing how the supplier will implement configuration and data management across the product life cycle. *When/how:* it must satisfy SAE GEIA-859 (Data Management), SAE EIA-649 (the Configuration Management Standard), and SAE EIA-649-2 (CM Requirements for NASA Enterprises), and it spans how CDM is planned and organized, how configurations are identified, how change and interfaces are controlled, how configuration status is accounted for, and how configurations are verified and audited — along with the access controls and security wrapped around the data.

- **Configuration and Data Management Process Audit (CDMPA)** — the DRD (Section A.5, DRD STD-CDMPA) for the joint NASA/Supplier audit that verifies the supplier's CDM implementation is compliant and that baselines are correctly defined, controlled, accounted for, and verified. *When/how:* conducted against a program/project-specific CDM Process Audit Plan; discrepancies are written up as Findings or Observations with corrective action assigned to each.

- **Configuration Status Accounting (CSA)** — the DRD (Section A.7, DRD STD-CSA) that delivers accurate, timely information about a product and its product configuration information across the life cycle. *When/how:* meets SAE EIA-649 and EIA-649-2, and is delivered as a defined set of reports — Dispositioned Change Activity, Pending Change Activity, Configuration Identification, Change Incorporation Status, As-designed/As-built Comparison, and Software CSA reports.

- **Specification and Drawing Trees (SDT)** — the DRD (Section A.8, DRD STD-SDT) providing top-down (generation-breakdown) listings of the specifications and drawings that allocate the requirements of a configuration item. *When/how:* specification trees are due before PDR, drawing trees before CDR, and both are updated for the Physical Configuration Audit.

- **Engineering Models, Drawings, and Associated Lists (EMDAL)** — the DRD (Section A.9, DRD STD-EMDAL) that delivers the engineering definition needed for manufacture, test, and logistics support, in 2D/3D CAD as primary information. *When/how:* governed by a stack of ASME Y14 standards (Y14.100 drawing practices, Y14.5 dimensioning and tolerancing, Y14.41 digital product definition, Y14.47 model organization, Y14.34 associated lists, Y14.24 drawing types), the Global Drawing Requirements Manual (10th edition) for electrical drawings, and MIL-STD-130 for part marking.

- **Change Request (CR)** — the DRD (Section A.10, DRD STD-CR) used to put forward an engineering change so the responsible Configuration Approval Authority can evaluate it and decide its disposition. *When/how:* meets SAE EIA-649-2 and carries a defined minimum content set (number, revision, title, originator, change description and classification, impacts on cost/schedule/risk/technical, affected baselines and configuration items, and recommended priority of routine, urgent, or emergency).

- **FCA/PCA Documentation (AD)** — the DRD (Section A.11, DRD STD-AD) supporting the Functional Configuration Audit and Physical Configuration Audit. *When/how:* the FCA verifies performance of the CI/CSCI against approved configuration documentation; the PCA verifies that the as-built configuration matches the as-designed configuration. Both follow SAE/EIA-649-2 and certify that configuration items meet the criteria for establishing the Product Baseline.

## Key Concepts

- **Each DRD is structured as numbered items.** The example DRD form has a fixed skeleton: identifying header fields (DPD/DRL number, DPD status, DRD number, data type, revision date, page, title), a short Description/Use, organizational fields (OPR — the Office of Primary Responsibility; DM — the Data Manager), distribution, an initial-submission date and submission frequency, remarks, and interrelationships, followed by the Data Preparation Information block (Scope, Applicable Items, Contents, Format, Maintenance).

- **Milestone-anchored scheduling.** Submission dates should be expressed as milestones, not calendar dates — for example "30 days after contract award" rather than a fixed date — so the schedule survives program slips. Submission frequency is likewise tied to events such as design reviews.

- **The OPR/DM split.** The Office of Primary Responsibility holds technical or administrative control over the requirement, while the Data Manager (often a Center or project organization code such as ENB, CM, or QUAL) receives, distributes, and processes the actual submittals. The OPR is not necessarily the reviewer — the reviewer is typically a subject matter expert.

- **As-designed versus as-built (and as-sustained).** The TDP defines the as-designed product configuration; the PCA exists to confirm the as-built physical item matches it; and the as-designed/as-built comparison is itself a CSA report. The Government ingests the TDP into its digital environment to form the as-received, "as-sustained" Product Baseline — a thread that ties acquisition, audit, and sustainment together.

- **TDP maturity levels track the life cycle.** A Conceptual TDP (sketches, low-fidelity CAD, text used to test feasibility) gives way to a Developmental TDP (documenting a design approach and prototype, explicitly not adequate for competitive procurement of parts), then a Product TDP (full descriptive documentation enabling competitive procurement of spares or end items), with a Commercial TDP covering contractor-developed, proprietary, or off-the-shelf items. Submission timing is pegged to NPR 7120.5 life-cycle reviews — conceptual updates around SRR/SDR, developmental 90 days before PDR, product 90 days before CDR.

- **Model-based delivery vocabulary.** The MBx DRD introduces a working vocabulary for delivering models, not just files: the Digital Mock-up Unit (DMU); the *simplified representation* (a precise, water-tight contiguous body built from a single surface that stands in for the nominal as-designed geometry — explicitly *not* a native CAD simple representation, though it may be derived from one); the common *skeleton structure definition* used to place major elements at the system-of-systems level; keep-out zones and volume simulators as separate models; and the *light-weight viewable*, a CAD-derived product viewable on a standard PC with web-downloadable software, usable by people with little CAD experience or no CAD licenses, delivered in formats such as 3D PDF and Jupiter Tessellation (JT) alongside the native CAD.

- **Neutral and standardized formats are mandated, not suggested.** The TDP format requirements name ASME Y14.41 and Y14.47, and ISO 10303 (STEP) application protocols — Part 209 (multidisciplinary analysis and design), 238 (numeric controllers), 239 (product life-cycle support), and 242 (managed model-based 3D engineering) — plus lightweight 3D options via ISO 10303-242, ISO 14306 (JT), and ISO 14739-1 (PRC). This operationalizes the appendix-wide push toward neutral formats over proprietary ones.

- **The CDMP is the backbone the rest hang from.** It enumerates the configuration management disciplines — identification (CIs, CSCIs, PCIs, baselines, unique identifiers, release procedures), control and interface management (CCBs, change classification, the link between nonconformance disposition and change management), status accounting, and verification and audits — and it is the document a CDMPA audits against. Several other DRDs (CSA, CR, FCA/PCA) trace back to it and to the SAE EIA-649 family.

- **Configuration baselines: functional, allocated, product.** The CDMP establishes these three baselines and defines the configuration documentation required for each; the FCA/PCA pair ultimately certifies the items that establish the Product Baseline.

- **Interrelationships make the set a system.** DRDs cite one another deliberately: the TDP references MBx, SDT, and EMDAL; EMDAL references the TDP and the Master Records Index; the CR recommends including related MBx models; the CSA references the CDMP. The DRDs are designed to be assembled, not used in isolation.

## Mental Models

- **A DRD is a "data contract" with an API spec attached.** Think of each DRD the way a software engineer thinks of an interface contract: it nails down the schema (Contents), the encoding (Format and named neutral standards), the SLA (submission timing and maintenance), and the access policy (Data Type 1–5 and distribution). The acceptance criterion is integration without rework — the delivered item should drop into the receiver's tools after verification alone.

- **Data Type is a control-and-trust dial, not paperwork.** Reading the Type 1–5 scale as a single dial clarifies intent: turn it up (Type 1) when the data will live in the Government baseline and demands approval before use; leave it in the middle (Type 2/3) for review-with-time-limit or deliver-on-time-no-approval; turn it down (Type 4/5) for contractor-retained data NASA can pull or inspect when needed. Pick the lowest control level that still protects the baseline.

- **The TDP is a maturing photograph of the design, not a one-time dump.** Each life-cycle review demands a sharper image: a fuzzy conceptual sketch at SRR/SDR, a developmental prototype picture before PDR, a fully detailed, procurement-ready portrait before CDR. The level you owe is a function of where you are in NPR 7120.5.

- **Model-based delivery is layered fidelity.** The same product structure is delivered at several fidelities for different consumers: full native models for engineering, simplified single-surface bodies for space-and-weight and integration, keep-out volumes for clearance, and light-weight viewables for stakeholders without CAD. Deliver the right fidelity to the right audience rather than one heavyweight model to everyone.

- **CDM is a closed loop: plan, identify, control, account, verify.** The CDMP plans it, configuration identification names the items and baselines, configuration control (CCBs and CRs) governs change, configuration status accounting records the truth of what changed and when, and the audits (CDMPA for process, FCA/PCA for product) verify the loop actually held. CSA is the system's memory; the audits are its conscience.

- **FCA asks "does it do what the spec says?"; PCA asks "is it built the way the drawings say?"** Keeping these two questions distinct keeps the audits distinct: functional performance against approved documentation (FCA) versus physical as-built against as-designed (PCA). Both feed the same destination — establishing the Product Baseline.

## Anti-patterns

- **Defaulting to "Contractor format is acceptable."** The TDP DRD states this phrase should almost never be used — only when no specific Government format is required and only after advance Government approval. Treating contractor format as the default undercuts the entire neutral-format, ingest-without-rework premise of the appendix.

- **Specifying proprietary or vendor-specific formats when neutral standards exist.** The appendix's purpose statement directs acquirers to emphasize and maximize Agency-approved or endorsed standards and neutral formats and to avoid proprietary vendor formats wherever possible; doing the opposite locks the Government into a single vendor's toolchain.

- **Pinning submissions to calendar dates instead of milestones.** Using a fixed date (the source's counter-example is a specific January date) rather than an event-relative milestone ("30 days after contract award") makes the schedule brittle to program changes; the DRD instructions call for milestones precisely to accommodate slips.

- **Delivering a top-level DMU built from simplified representations where full fidelity is required.** The MBx DRD is explicit that the integrated system/subsystem/element top-assembly DMU must not contain simplified model representations — substituting simplified bodies at that level defeats the purpose of an accurate integrated mock-up.

- **Double-counting TDP sub-parts as separate deliverables.** When the TDP DRD is incorporated in whole or part, DRDs applicable to individual parts of a TDP are not to be incorporated as separate requirements — pulling them out separately creates conflicting, redundant obligations.

## Key Takeaways
1. **The DRD is the contractual lever for digital acquisition.** It converts "deliver usable models and data" into enforceable purpose, content, format, standards, maintenance, and submittal terms, assembled into a DPD that rides alongside the SOW/PWS and is invoked by SOW/PWS language.
2. **Tailor everything; assume nothing is one-size-fits-all.** Appendix A is an example library. The acquirer selects which DRDs to use, edits their content, may need additional DRDs, and should add the program's own neutral-standard and format choices.
3. **Set the control level deliberately with Data Type.** Use Type 1 when data must enter the Center baseline under CDM control; understand the 45-day (tailorable) Type 2 review window; and route contractor-retained data through Types 4 and 5 with the right FAR clause when delivery must be compellable.
4. **Mature the TDP with the life cycle and ground it in MIL-STD-31000.** Conceptual, Developmental, Product, and Commercial levels map to NPR 7120.5 reviews; the TDP seeds the as-sustained Product Baseline once ingested into the Government's digital environment.
5. **Deliver models, with the right fidelity for each consumer.** MBx delivery distinguishes full native models, simplified single-surface representations, keep-out volumes, integrated DMUs, and light-weight viewables — backed by named ASME Y14 and ISO 10303/STEP standards and neutral formats like JT and 3D PDF.
6. **Stand the CDM suite up as a closed loop.** The CDMP plans CDM against SAE GEIA-859 / EIA-649 / EIA-649-2; CSA records the configuration truth; CRs and CCBs control change; and CDMPA (process) and FCA/PCA (product) verify it — with the audits ultimately certifying the Product Baseline.
7. **Use interrelationships to keep the data set coherent.** TDP, MBx, SDT, EMDAL, CSA, CR, and FCA/PCA reference each other on purpose; specifying them as a connected set — not as isolated documents — is what produces an integrated, ingestible digital deliverable.

## Connects To
- **NASA-HDBK-1009A** — this DRD/CDM material is intended to be used together with NASA-HDBK-1009A; treat that companion handbook as the partner reference when applying these example DRDs in a real digital-engineering acquisition.
- **MIL-STD-31000 (Technical Data Package)** — the DoD TDP standard the STD-TDP DRD relies on for the structure, levels (conceptual/developmental/product/commercial), and content of deliverable TDP data products, including the TDP Option Selection Worksheet.
- **SAE EIA-649 and SAE EIA-649-2 (Configuration Management; CM Requirements for NASA Enterprises) and SAE GEIA-859 (Data Management)** — the configuration- and data-management standards the CDMP, CSA, CR, and FCA/PCA DRDs are written to satisfy.
- **ASME Y14 family (Y14.5, Y14.24, Y14.34, Y14.41, Y14.47, Y14.100) and the Global Drawing Requirements Manual (10th edition)** — the drawing, dimensioning, digital-product-definition, model-organization, and associated-list practices governing EMDAL and TDP format.
- **ISO 10303 / STEP application protocols (Parts 209, 233, 238, 239, 242), ISO 14306 (JT), ISO 14739-1 (PRC), ISO 32000-1 (PDF)** — the neutral exchange and lightweight-viewable formats the TDP and MBx DRDs mandate to enable ingest without rework.
- **MIL-STD-130 (item identification marking)** — governs the UID/IUID 2D part-marking requirements called out in the EMDAL DRD.
- **NPR 7120.5 (NASA life-cycle management)** — supplies the Major Milestone reviews (SRR, SDR, PDR, CDR, PCA) that anchor TDP, MBx, SDT, EMDAL, and CSA submission timing.
- **FAR 48 CFR § 52.227-16 (Additional Data Requirements)** — the contract clause needed to compel delivery of Type 4 contractor-retained data.
