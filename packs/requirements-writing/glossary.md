# Requirements Writing Glossary

**Ambiguity** — a requirement with more than one reasonable interpretation; the defect EARS most directly reduces (Ch 5).
**Analysis** — a verification method using models, calculation, or simulation where direct test is impractical (Ch 6).
**Appropriate** — quality characteristic: the requirement is at the right level of abstraction (what, not how) (Ch 1).
**Bidirectional traceability** — links from each requirement up to its parent need and down to design and verification evidence (Ch 6).
**Complete (requirement)** — needs no further amplification; all conditions and tolerances present (Ch 1).
**Complex pattern** — an EARS requirement combining keywords (e.g. While + When) for a compound condition (Ch 3).
**Compound requirement** — two or more needs in one statement; violates *singular* (Ch 5).
**Conforming** — quality characteristic: follows the project's template and style (Ch 1).
**Condition clause** — the optional preamble (When/While/Where/If) that sets when an EARS response applies (Ch 2).
**Constrained natural language** — plain prose limited to fixed templates; the basis of EARS (Ch 2).
**Correct** — quality characteristic: accurately reflects the real stakeholder need (Ch 1).
**Demonstration** — a verification method showing a capability operating without detailed instrumentation (Ch 6).
**EARS** — Easy Approach to Requirements Syntax; templates for unambiguous requirements, by Mavin et al. (2009) (Ch 2).
**Event-driven pattern** — EARS form "When `<trigger>`, the `<system>` shall `<response>`" (Ch 3).
**Feasible** — quality characteristic: achievable within cost, schedule, and physics (Ch 1).
**Implementation in a requirement** — stating *how* when only *what* is needed; over-constrains design (Ch 5).
**Inspection** — a verification method examining the item or documentation (Ch 6).
**Necessary** — quality characteristic: states a real need; removing it leaves a gap (Ch 1).
**Optional-feature pattern** — EARS form "Where `<feature>`, the `<system>` shall `<response>`" (Ch 3).
**Rationale** — the recorded *why* of a requirement; lets future changes judge whether it still applies (Ch 5).
**Response clause** — the part of every EARS requirement: "the `<system>` shall `<response>`" (Ch 2).
**Singular** — quality characteristic: one and only one need per requirement (Ch 1).
**State-driven pattern** — EARS form "While `<state>`, the `<system>` shall `<response>`" (Ch 3).
**Test** — a verification method exercising the system and measuring against a criterion (Ch 6).
**Trigger** — the event, state, feature, or condition that determines which EARS pattern fits (Ch 3).
**Ubiquitous pattern** — EARS form with no trigger: "The `<system>` shall `<response>`" (Ch 3).
**Unambiguous** — quality characteristic: exactly one reasonable interpretation to every reader (Ch 1).
**Unverifiable requirement** — one with no finite, affordable way to confirm it was met (Ch 5, Ch 6).
**Unwanted-behaviour pattern** — EARS form "If `<condition>`, then the `<system>` shall `<response>`"; for faults/safety (Ch 3).
**Validation** — confirming the system meets the real need ("right thing") (Ch 6).
**Verification** — confirming the system meets the requirement ("thing right") via test/analysis/inspection/demonstration (Ch 6).
**Verifiable** — quality characteristic: a finite, affordable method can confirm the requirement (Ch 1, Ch 6).
**Weak words** — untestable terms ("user-friendly", "robust", "fast", "support", "etc.") to avoid (Ch 5).
