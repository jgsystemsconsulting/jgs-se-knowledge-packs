# Chapter — Introduction and the NASA Cost Estimating Process Overview

## Core Idea

NASA's cost estimating exists to inform decisions under scarcity: missions are expensive, resources are limited, and choices about which proposals to fund and whether a project may advance to its next life-cycle phase have to be made on the basis of credible cost information. The Cost Estimating Handbook (CEH) frames cost estimating not as a one-off calculation but as a structured, repeatable process whose purpose is to give decision makers a clear view of a project's cost, the cost of alternatives within it, and — critically — the uncertainty around all of those numbers. A central conviction runs through the introduction: cost should be treated as a design variable, not merely an output reported after the fact, so that major cost drivers stay visible during design and estimates that drift far from historical experience get challenged early.

The handbook then translates that conviction into a defined workflow. NASA's estimating process is organized as a twelve-step sequence grouped into three parts — Project Definition, Cost Methodology, and Cost Estimate. Although the steps are presented in order for teaching purposes, the source is emphatic that real estimating is iterative and rarely linear: steps get revisited as data, opportunity, and need surface, and early results are routinely revised as baselines shift or questions about completeness and sensitivity arise.

## Frameworks Introduced (exact source names, when/how)

- **NASA Cost Estimating Handbook (CEH), Version 4.0** — the document itself, dated February 2015. It defines the cost estimating process that supplies decision makers with cost-risk insight across the project life cycle.
- **The NASA 12-step cost estimating process** — introduced in Section 2 as the start-to-finish "how-to," partitioned into three parts: **Part 1 — Project Definition**, **Part 2 — Cost Methodology**, and **Part 3 — Cost Estimate**. Part 1 clarifies the reason for the estimate and builds understanding of what is being estimated; Part 2 holds four tasks that set the approach and framework; Part 3 holds five tasks covering the conduct, presentation, and maintenance of the estimate.
- **NASA Policy Directive (NPD) 1000.5** — NASA's acquisition policy for funding space missions, cited as the source of policy and process expectations for acquisition. The handbook states every NASA cost estimator should know it.
- **NASA Procedural Requirement (NPR) 7120.5E** — describes how space flight programs and projects are managed throughout their life cycles; the source pairs it with NPD 1000.5 as required familiarity for estimators, and notes Figure 1 (the Project Life Cycle Milestone Chart) is drawn from it.
- **NPR 7123.1B, NASA Systems Engineering Processes and Requirements** — referenced as the policy that lays out the complete entrance and exit criteria for each life-cycle review, defining the activities the estimator's product feeds.
- **GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide (GAO-09-3SP, 2009)** — the U.S. Government Accountability Office's recommended estimating process. The CEH states its own process is substantively consistent with the GAO process but tailored to NASA's needs, and provides a mapping between the two (NASA tasks against the GAO guide's "Associated Tasks" in its Table 2) rather than duplicating GAO's process outright.
- **Project Life Cycle Milestone Chart (Figure 1)** — the roadmap identifying where in the life cycle estimates are needed to support justification, formulation, approval, and implementation.

## Key Concepts

- **Cost as a design variable.** The source argues that system cost should actively shape design decisions, keeping major cost drivers in focus and flagging estimates that deviate strongly from historical norms.
- **Estimates serve many consumers.** The work informs projects, programs, Centers, Mission Directorates, and the Agency, plus external stakeholders (Congress, OMB), auditors (GAO, the NASA Office of Inspector General), and ultimately taxpayers. The handbook enumerates these groups explicitly.
- **Life-cycle gating.** To advance through its phases, a project must present cost estimates at each review leading to the next Key Decision Point; the entrance and exit criteria for those reviews come from NPR 7123.1B.
- **Uncertainty narrows with maturity.** In most cases, schedule and cost uncertainty shrink as the project matures, but the estimator's job is to communicate not just the point estimates but the uncertainty attached to them.
- **Three-part, twelve-step structure.** Project Definition (foundation: reason for the estimate, expectations, Work Breakdown Structure, technical description), Cost Methodology (four tasks building the approach and framework), and Cost Estimate (five tasks for conducting, presenting, and maintaining the result).
- **Ground rules and assumptions as the live wire.** Within Part 2, developing the ground rules and assumptions is named as the most frequently revisited task; as methodologies are chosen and data arrive, the assumptions, methods, and even the cost model may be refined.
- **Adaptable yet principled.** The process must stay flexible while holding firm to the established principles, objectives, and practices of cost estimating.

## Mental Models

- **The estimate as a foundation under construction.** Project Definition lays a base — the Work Breakdown Structure and technical description — that later steps build on, but the foundation can be re-poured as new information arrives. Nothing upstream is frozen.
- **Successive steps for teaching, loops for practice.** Picture the twelve steps as a tidy staircase drawn for instruction, then overlay the reality: a spiral that doubles back whenever baselines change, sensitivity is questioned, or completeness is in doubt. The linear diagram is a convenience, not a description of how the work actually flows.
- **Uncertainty as a deliverable.** Treat the spread around the number as a first-class output the decision maker receives, not a footnote — the estimator owes both the value and its confidence.
- **NASA's process as a tailored derivative of GAO's.** Rather than adopting the GAO guide wholesale, NASA keeps its own process-flow structure and maps it onto GAO, so the two can be cross-walked task-by-task while NASA retains a flow tuned to its own programs.

## Key Takeaways

- The reason cost estimating exists at NASA is decision support under scarce resources and rising system cost; informed decisions depend on credible cost and cost-risk information.
- Cost should be used as a design variable so that drivers stay visible and outlier estimates get challenged against history.
- The process is a 12-step, three-part workflow (Project Definition, Cost Methodology, Cost Estimate) that is iterative and rarely linear in practice.
- Estimators must communicate uncertainty alongside point estimates; uncertainty generally decreases as the project matures.
- Estimators should be fluent in the governing policy: NPD 1000.5 and NPR 7120.5E for acquisition and life-cycle management, and NPR 7123.1B for review entrance/exit criteria.
- NASA's process is deliberately consistent with — but tailored from — the GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide, with an explicit task-level mapping between them.
- Although the CEH focuses on flight projects, the same guiding principles extend to IT, technology development, and Construction of Facilities work.
