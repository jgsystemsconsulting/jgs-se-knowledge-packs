# Chapter 6: Analyses for Decision Support — Sensitivity, Trade Studies, Affordability, and Cost As an Independent Variable

## Core Idea
NASA cost estimators do more than produce a number — they supply analytical decision support across a project's life. When project personnel face choices (a life-cycle-cost analysis, an engineering trade, an architecture selection, an affordability assessment), they turn to the cost-estimating community to inform those choices with data. The handbook organizes the common analytical methods into five categories — **Sensitivity Analysis, Trade Studies, Affordability Analysis, Cost As an Independent Variable (CAIV), and Economic Analysis** — and treats them as the estimator's toolkit for guiding decision makers. The unifying message: cost is not just an output to be reported after design; in the affordability and CAIV mindset it becomes a *constraint and input variable* that actively shapes the design and the requirements themselves.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The five-category decision-support taxonomy** — the chapter's organizing scheme groups the analytical methods into five families: Sensitivity Analysis; Trade Studies; Affordability Analysis; Cost As an Independent Variable (CAIV); and Economic Analysis. *When/how:* used as the menu of analytical methods an estimator selects from when asked to support a project decision. Detailed how-to procedures and supporting references for each live in **Appendix N**.
- **Sensitivity Analysis (five-step procedure)** — a technique for evaluating how changes in system parameters, requirements, ground rules, and assumptions affect system cost. *When/how:* applied when project requirements or assumptions are likely to vary; the five steps are (1) compute the point estimate, (2) select the elements for analysis, (3) determine the range of values for each selected element, (4) determine the cost impact by recomputing at high then low values one element at a time, and (5) graph or table the results. A worked example is in Appendix N.
- **Trade-Study Analysis (cost-performance trade studies)** — systematic, interdisciplinary examinations of the factors affecting system cost, analyzing many system concepts to find acceptable ways to meet performance and requirements at acceptable cost and schedule. *When/how:* most effective in early life-cycle phases to narrow the field of technologies, processes, and operational concepts; candidate solutions are laid out within a multidimensional **trade space** whose axes are a cost element together with one or more performance parameters. Steps are in Appendix N.
- **Make-Versus-Buy Analysis** — a strategic choice between producing an item in-house or buying it from an outside supplier (outsourcing). *When/how:* triggered when the Agency can build a product but faces workforce/supplier trouble, diminishing capacity, or changing demand; strategic factors (quality control, workforce proficiency, protecting proprietary technology) plus operational and cost factors drive the decision. Guidance and an example are in Appendix N.
- **Lease-Versus-Buy Analysis** — performed once the decision to acquire an asset has been made, to compare the economics of leasing versus owning. *When/how:* common in business cases, applying most often to facilities and IT projects; the analyst weighs the life-cycle cost of leasing versus buying plus operations-and-support (O&S) cost, with the most meaningful comparison being lease financing versus debt financing. Governed by **OMB Circular A-94** (the federal benefit-cost guidelines that set discount rates for government programs); methodology in Appendix N.
- **Affordability Analysis** — a continuous, overarching process applied throughout the program/project life cycle, confirming the final system can be delivered, owned, operated, developed, and produced inside the funding (or best-value) limits set earlier while it continues to satisfy every approved requirement. *When/how:* embedded in all programmatic decisions and often used within a trade study; establishing the affordability process and methodology in early phases is emphasized.
- **Cost As an Independent Variable (CAIV)** — a study process ensuring an affordable design solution still meets *threshold* performance requirements. *When/how:* used to evaluate requirements and to assist trade studies; its core tenet is that **design converges on cost rather than cost converging on design**. CAIV was taken up across the U.S. Government in the mid-1990s as a response to large overruns in acquisition and sustainment cost; it has since been adopted across the aerospace industry and NASA contractors. The commercial analog is **target costing / target pricing**.

## Key Concepts
- **Decision support is a deliverable, not a byproduct.** Across a project's life there are many choices project personnel must make, and the estimating community is expected to inform each with data — whether that takes the form of a life-cycle-cost study, an engineering trade, a choice of architecture, or an assessment of affordability.
- **Point estimate.** A deterministic cost estimate where inputs are fixed values, built from the most-likely inputs supplied by experts. It sets the baseline against which sensitivity-driven change is measured.
- **One-at-a-time perturbation.** In sensitivity analysis each change is applied singly: one element is moved to its high value, then its low value, while every other element stays at its baseline. The procedure repeats for each element of interest.
- **Sensitive vs. insensitive elements.** Requirements and parameters that cause large changes in cost are *sensitive*; those that can vary without significantly affecting cost are *insensitive*. **Imbalanced** change occurs when the percentage cost change differs depending on the direction the requirement moves.
- **The right requirement, not just the right input.** The elements selected for sensitivity analysis are usually system parameters and requirements likely to change — not necessarily the values plugged directly into estimating equations, but the requirements that drive those values (the example given: a building requirement to hold at least 500 employees, where capacity changes ripple into square footage and cost).
- **Trade space.** A multidimensional region whose boundaries are set by a cost element together with one or more performance parameters, in which candidate design alternatives are plotted. Figure 15 shows a simplified two-dimensional trade space where, at one point, a large performance gain is bought for a small cost increase. Far-left (low-cost) points may fail even the threshold (minimal) performance requirement; far-right points may exceed the cost boundary only to deliver performance beyond what is required and justified.
- **Objective of a cost-performance trade study.** Not necessarily to minimize system cost, but to achieve an **optimal balance** of performance and cost.
- **Affordability obligations.** An affordable program/project optimizes system performance for total LCC while meeting schedule and managing risk; sets aggressive yet achievable cost objectives and manages them across the full life cycle; balances cost objectives against mission needs and projected out-year resources; maintains cost as a principal input variable in program structure and in design, development, production, operation, and support; and emphasizes cost as more of a constraint and less of a variable once performance and cost targets are set.
- **Where the LCC lands.** For NASA's human space systems, much of the LCC can occur during O&S; for robotic missions, most cost is typically incurred during Phases C and D. This is why early establishment of an affordability process matters — to maximize savings and reduce future operations and sustainment costs.
- **CAIV trades performance and schedule for cost.** Once cost is treated as the independent variable, the requirements on both performance and schedule become tradeable in service of hitting the cost target. CAIV requires a system of performance metrics to track and evaluate progress, and its results can help develop contract incentives for achieving cost-reduction objectives.

## Mental Models
- **Cost as input vs. cost as output.** The traditional flow lets cost emerge from a fixed design (cost converges on design). Affordability and CAIV invert this: fix the cost target and let the design and requirements converge on it (design converges on cost). This inversion is the conceptual heart of the chapter.
- **"Performance is not sacred."** Under CAIV, certain performance requirements may be challenged when significant cost savings are possible, and the process continually challenges requirements whenever affordability is at stake. Threshold performance is the floor that must still be met — but anything above threshold is negotiable against cost.
- **The trade-space sweet spot.** Read a trade-space plot for the leverage points — places where a small cost increase yields a large performance gain (or vice versa). The extremes are traps: cheap alternatives that miss the performance threshold, and expensive alternatives that gold-plate performance past the cost boundary.
- **Move one lever at a time to see what matters.** Sensitivity analysis isolates each requirement against a fixed baseline so the analyst can rank elements by their cost leverage and direct scarce attention to the sensitive few.
- **Two financing comparisons hide inside one acquisition.** In lease-vs-buy the decision is not just "which total LCC is lower" but "lease financing vs. debt financing," weighed against fiscal/budgetary constraints — and, where the sponsor can also build the asset, the make option must be assessed alongside.

## Anti-patterns
- **Mistaking sensitivity analysis for a probability statement.** A named *con*: sensitivity analysis cannot describe the exact likelihood of any value occurring — one outcome may be more likely than another, but its exact probability is unknown.
- **Ignoring dependencies among estimate elements.** A named *drawback*: the one-at-a-time method cannot quantify the impact of dependencies (or their absence) among elements of the estimate, because every other element is held constant while one is varied.
- **Gold-plating past the cost boundary / under-delivering past the performance threshold.** The chapter flags both failure modes in the trade space: far-right alternatives that exceed the cost boundary to provide performance beyond the requirement (not justified), and far-left alternatives that look cheap but fail even the threshold performance requirement.

## Key Takeaways
1. Decision support is organized into **five analytical categories** — Sensitivity Analysis, Trade Studies, Affordability Analysis, CAIV, and Economic Analysis — with detailed procedures and references in **Appendix N**.
2. **Sensitivity analysis** follows five steps and varies one element at a time against a fixed point estimate to separate the *sensitive* requirements from the *insensitive* ones.
3. Its limits are explicit: it gives **no probabilities** and **cannot quantify dependencies** among estimate elements.
4. **Cost-performance trade studies** seek an *optimal balance* of performance and cost — not minimum cost — and are most effective in early life-cycle phases; results are plotted in a **trade space** bounded by cost and performance parameters.
5. **Make-vs-buy** is a strategic in-house/outsource choice; **lease-vs-buy** compares lease vs. debt financing under **OMB Circular A-94** and must also weigh the make option when the sponsor can build the asset.
6. **Affordability** is a continuous, life-cycle process that keeps cost as a principal *input variable* and increasingly as a *constraint*; early establishment matters most because LCC concentrates in O&S (human systems) or Phases C/D (robotic missions).
7. **CAIV** makes design converge on cost while still meeting *threshold* performance; "performance is not sacred," and both performance and schedule may be traded to hit the cost objective. Its commercial analog is **target costing/pricing**.
